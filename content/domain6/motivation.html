Introduction to Work Motivation

Work motivation refers to the psychological forces that determine the direction, intensity, and persistence of work-related behavior. Understanding motivation is essential for the EPPP, as it underpins management practices related to job design, compensation, goal setting, and employee engagement.

Motivation theories are broadly categorized as content theories (what motivates people -- identifying specific needs or drives) and process theories (how motivation occurs -- explaining the cognitive mechanisms of motivation).

Content Theories of Motivation

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Abraham Maslow (1943, 1954) proposed that human needs are arranged in a hierarchy, with lower-level needs generally requiring satisfaction before higher-level needs become motivating.

Maslow's Five Levels (Bottom to Top)

  1. Physiological needs: Basic survival needs (food, water, shelter, sleep) -- in the workplace: adequate salary, comfortable working conditions
  2. Safety needs: Physical and psychological security -- in the workplace: job security, safe working conditions, benefits
  3. Belongingness/love needs: Social connection, affection, acceptance -- in the workplace: positive coworker relationships, team membership, supportive supervision
  4. Esteem needs: Self-respect, achievement, recognition from others -- in the workplace: meaningful titles, awards, promotions, challenging assignments
  5. Self-actualization: Realizing one's full potential, creativity, personal growth -- in the workplace: autonomy, opportunities for creativity, personal development

Key principles include the prepotency principle (lower needs must be substantially satisfied before higher needs become active motivators) and the deficit principle (a satisfied need is no longer a motivator). Maslow later added cognitive needs (knowledge, understanding) and aesthetic needs (beauty, order) between esteem and self-actualization, and transcendence above self-actualization.

EPPP Key Concept

Despite its intuitive appeal and widespread influence, Maslow's hierarchy has limited empirical support. Research has not consistently supported the strict hierarchical ordering of needs or the prepotency principle (Wahba & Bridwell, 1976). Nevertheless, the theory remains widely taught and tested on the EPPP due to its historical significance.

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Frederick Herzberg (1959, 1966) proposed that job satisfaction and dissatisfaction are not opposite ends of a single continuum but rather are influenced by two separate sets of factors.

Herzberg's Two Factors

Motivator factors (intrinsic): Related to the content of the work itself. When present, they produce satisfaction and motivation; when absent, they produce a neutral state (not dissatisfaction).

  • Achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility, advancement, growth

Hygiene factors (extrinsic): Related to the context or environment of work. When inadequate, they produce dissatisfaction; when adequate, they produce a neutral state (not satisfaction).

  • Company policy, supervision quality, salary, interpersonal relations, working conditions, job security

Example: Applying Herzberg's Theory

A company improves its cafeteria, parking, and office space (hygiene factors). Employees stop complaining, but they are not more motivated. To actually increase motivation, the company would need to redesign jobs to include more challenging work, greater responsibility, and opportunities for achievement (motivators). This insight led to the concept of job enrichment.

Herzberg's theory has been criticized for being method-dependent (findings replicate best when using his critical incident technique), and for the assumption that salary is solely a hygiene factor. However, the distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic factors and the concept of job enrichment remain influential.

McClelland's Acquired Needs Theory

David McClelland (1961) proposed that three learned (acquired) needs drive behavior in the workplace, with individuals varying in the relative strength of each:

McClelland's Three Needs

  • Need for Achievement (nAch): Drive to excel, accomplish challenging goals, and meet high standards of success. High-nAch individuals prefer moderate-risk tasks, want personal responsibility, and seek feedback on performance.
  • Need for Power (nPow): Drive to influence, control, or direct others. Personalized power is self-serving; socialized power benefits the organization.
  • Need for Affiliation (nAff): Drive for close interpersonal relationships, approval, and social belonging. High-nAff individuals prioritize harmony and may avoid conflict.

McClelland measured these needs using the Thematic Apperception Test (TAT), coding stories for achievement, power, and affiliation themes. His research found that effective leaders typically have high socialized need for power, moderate need for achievement, and lower need for affiliation. High-nAch individuals make good entrepreneurs but not necessarily good managers of large organizations.

Process Theories of Motivation

Vroom's Expectancy Theory

Victor Vroom (1964) proposed that motivation is a function of three cognitive variables, and that individuals make rational choices about the effort they expend at work:

Expectancy Theory Components

Motivation = Expectancy x Instrumentality x Valence

  • Expectancy (E): The belief that effort will lead to acceptable performance (effort-to-performance link). Ranges from 0 to 1.
  • Instrumentality (I): The belief that performance will lead to a specific outcome or reward (performance-to-outcome link). Ranges from 0 to 1.
  • Valence (V): The value or attractiveness of the outcome to the individual. Can be positive, negative, or zero.

If any component is zero, motivation will be zero. All three must be positive for motivation to exist.

Example: Applying Expectancy Theory

An employee considering whether to work overtime: (1) "Can I actually finish this project on time?" (expectancy), (2) "If I finish, will I get the promised bonus?" (instrumentality), (3) "How much do I value that bonus?" (valence). If the employee doubts any of these, motivation to work overtime decreases.

Adams's Equity Theory

J. Stacy Adams (1963, 1965) proposed that individuals are motivated to maintain a sense of fairness by comparing their input-to-outcome ratio with that of a relevant comparison other.

Equity Theory Formula

My Outcomes / My Inputs = Other's Outcomes / Other's Inputs

Inputs: Effort, skills, education, experience, time

Outcomes: Pay, benefits, recognition, promotions, status

When the ratios are unequal, individuals experience inequity distress and are motivated to restore equity through behavioral or cognitive adjustments.

Methods of restoring equity include:

  • Changing inputs (working harder or less hard)
  • Changing outcomes (requesting a raise)
  • Cognitively distorting inputs or outcomes ("I'm actually less qualified")
  • Choosing a different comparison other
  • Leaving the situation (quitting)

Research generally supports that underpayment inequity (being underrewarded) leads to decreased effort or quality. Overpayment inequity (being overrewarded) may temporarily increase effort, though people more quickly rationalize overpayment.

Locke's Goal-Setting Theory

Edwin Locke (1968; Locke & Latham, 1990, 2002) developed one of the most empirically supported theories of work motivation. The core finding is that specific, difficult goals lead to higher performance than vague or easy goals.

Key Principles of Goal-Setting Theory

  • Specificity: Specific goals ("increase sales by 10%") outperform vague goals ("do your best")
  • Difficulty: Difficult but attainable goals lead to higher performance than easy goals, up to the limits of ability
  • Commitment: Goals must be accepted and committed to -- participation in goal setting can enhance commitment
  • Feedback: Goals combined with feedback are more effective than goals alone
  • Self-efficacy: Belief in one's ability to achieve goals mediates the goal-performance relationship

EPPP Key Concept

Goal-setting theory has exceptionally strong empirical support across hundreds of studies. For the EPPP, know that: (1) specific difficult goals produce the highest performance, (2) "do your best" goals are no better than no goals, (3) goals affect performance through four mechanisms -- directing attention, energizing effort, increasing persistence, and prompting strategy development, and (4) assigned goals can be as effective as participatively set goals, provided the person accepts them.

Self-Determination Theory

Self-determination theory (SDT), developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan (1985, 2000), distinguishes between different types of motivation based on the degree to which behavior is self-determined.

SDT Motivation Continuum

  • Amotivation: Complete lack of intention or motivation
  • Extrinsic motivation: Behavior driven by external contingencies, ranging from:
    • External regulation: Acting to obtain rewards or avoid punishments
    • Introjected regulation: Acting to avoid guilt or enhance ego
    • Identified regulation: Acting because the activity is personally important
    • Integrated regulation: Acting because the activity aligns with core values
  • Intrinsic motivation: Engaging in an activity for its inherent interest and enjoyment

SDT proposes three basic psychological needs that must be satisfied for optimal motivation and well-being:

  • Autonomy: Feeling volitional and self-directed
  • Competence: Feeling effective and capable
  • Relatedness: Feeling connected to and cared for by others

A key finding of SDT research is the overjustification effect: providing extrinsic rewards for intrinsically motivated behavior can undermine intrinsic motivation, particularly when rewards are perceived as controlling rather than informational (Deci et al., 1999).

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Maslow: hierarchy of needs with prepotency principle; limited empirical support
  • Herzberg: motivators (intrinsic) produce satisfaction; hygiene factors (extrinsic) prevent dissatisfaction -- not opposite ends of one continuum
  • Herzberg's theory led to the concept of job enrichment
  • McClelland: nAch, nPow, nAff measured by TAT; high nAch people prefer moderate-risk tasks
  • McClelland: effective leaders have high socialized power, moderate achievement, low affiliation
  • Vroom: Motivation = Expectancy x Instrumentality x Valence; if any = 0, motivation = 0
  • Adams: equity theory compares own input/outcome ratio to comparison other; underpayment produces more reliable effects than overpayment
  • Locke: specific, difficult goals produce highest performance; "do your best" = no goal
  • Goal-setting theory: one of the most empirically supported motivation theories
  • SDT: autonomy, competence, relatedness; overjustification effect undermines intrinsic motivation
