Introduction to Job Satisfaction and Occupational Stress

Job satisfaction and occupational stress are among the most widely studied outcomes in I/O psychology. They influence employee well-being, performance, absenteeism, and turnover. The EPPP tests knowledge of the major theoretical frameworks, the construct of organizational commitment, and the burnout syndrome.

Job Satisfaction

Job satisfaction is an evaluative judgment about one's job that reflects cognitive and affective responses to job experiences. It can be assessed as overall (global) satisfaction or as satisfaction with specific facets (pay, supervision, coworkers, promotion, work itself).

Dispositional Approach

The dispositional approach suggests that job satisfaction is partly determined by stable personality traits. Research shows that satisfaction levels remain moderately consistent across jobs and over time, suggesting a dispositional component.

  • Positive affectivity (PA): Individuals high in PA tend to experience more positive emotions and greater satisfaction across situations
  • Negative affectivity (NA): Individuals high in NA tend to experience more negative emotions and report lower satisfaction
  • Core self-evaluations (CSE): Judge et al. (1997) identified a higher-order trait comprising self-esteem, generalized self-efficacy, locus of control, and emotional stability that predicts job satisfaction

Locke's Value-Percept Theory

Edwin Locke (1976) proposed that job satisfaction results from the perception that one's job fulfills important work values. Satisfaction = (Vwant - Vhave) x Vimportance. Greater discrepancy between what is wanted and what is received leads to dissatisfaction, but only if the dimension is important to the individual.

Hackman and Oldham's Job Characteristics Model

The Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham, 1976) identifies five core job dimensions that produce critical psychological states leading to satisfaction and motivation:

Five Core Job Characteristics

  1. Skill variety: The degree to which a job requires different skills and talents
  2. Task identity: The degree to which a job involves completing a whole, identifiable piece of work
  3. Task significance: The degree to which the job has a meaningful impact on others
  4. Autonomy: The degree of freedom and independence in scheduling and procedures
  5. Feedback: The degree to which the job itself provides clear information about performance

The Motivating Potential Score (MPS) = [(Skill Variety + Task Identity + Task Significance) / 3] x Autonomy x Feedback

Autonomy and feedback are multiplied because if either is zero, motivating potential is zero regardless of other dimensions.

EPPP Key Concept

The Job Characteristics Model includes a moderator: Growth Need Strength (GNS). The model predicts that the relationship between job characteristics and outcomes is strongest for individuals with high GNS -- those who have a strong desire for personal growth, learning, and challenge. Individuals with low GNS may not respond positively to enriched jobs.

Organizational Commitment

Organizational commitment reflects an employee's psychological attachment to and identification with their organization. The most widely accepted model is Meyer and Allen's (1991) three-component model:

Three Components of Organizational Commitment

ComponentBasisReason for Staying
Affective commitmentEmotional attachment and identification"I stay because I want to"
Continuance commitmentPerceived costs of leaving (investments, lack of alternatives)"I stay because I need to"
Normative commitmentSense of obligation and loyalty"I stay because I ought to"

Research consistently shows that affective commitment has the strongest positive relationship with desirable outcomes (job performance, organizational citizenship behavior, attendance) and the strongest negative relationship with turnover. Continuance commitment has weaker or even negative relationships with performance, as employees may stay but not be highly motivated.

Example: Continuance Commitment

A psychologist who has accumulated a generous pension, built a strong referral network, and lives close to her clinic may feel "locked in" despite dissatisfaction. Her years of investment and the perceived difficulty of starting over create high continuance commitment. She stays but may be disengaged -- illustrating why continuance commitment predicts retention but not performance.

Organizational Citizenship Behavior (OCB)

Organizational citizenship behavior (OCB), defined by Dennis Organ (1988), refers to individual behavior that is discretionary, not directly or explicitly recognized by the formal reward system, and that in the aggregate promotes the effective functioning of the organization. OCBs are the "extra-role" behaviors that go beyond formal job requirements.

Organ's Five Dimensions of OCB

  1. Altruism: Helping specific individuals with organizationally relevant tasks or problems (e.g., voluntarily assisting a coworker who is behind on their workload)
  2. Conscientiousness: Going well beyond minimum role requirements in areas such as attendance, punctuality, housekeeping, and use of time (also called generalized compliance)
  3. Sportsmanship: Willingness to tolerate less-than-ideal circumstances without complaining, grievances, or petty resentments -- "rolling with the punches"
  4. Courtesy: Taking actions to prevent problems with others by providing advance notice, reminders, or consultation before acting (e.g., alerting a colleague about a decision that may affect them)
  5. Civic virtue: Responsible, constructive involvement in the political process and governance of the organization (e.g., attending meetings, keeping up with organizational issues, voicing concerns)

Research consistently shows that OCBs are positively predicted by affective commitment, job satisfaction, perceptions of fairness (organizational justice), and transformational leadership. OCBs contribute to organizational effectiveness by enhancing coworker productivity, reducing the need for maintenance functions, and improving coordination.

EPPP Key Concept

OCBs are important because they represent performance that is not captured by traditional job performance measures. For the EPPP, know Organ's five dimensions and that OCBs are most strongly predicted by job satisfaction and organizational justice (particularly procedural justice). Also note the distinction between OCB-I (behaviors directed toward individuals, like altruism and courtesy) and OCB-O (behaviors directed toward the organization, like conscientiousness, sportsmanship, and civic virtue).

Burnout

Burnout is a psychological syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed. Christina Maslach's model (Maslach & Jackson, 1981) is the most widely cited framework.

Maslach's Three Dimensions of Burnout

  • Emotional exhaustion: Feeling drained, depleted of emotional resources, and overwhelmed by work demands. This is considered the core and most prominent dimension.
  • Depersonalization (cynicism): Developing negative, callous, or detached attitudes toward clients, patients, or coworkers. In human service professions, this manifests as treating people as objects.
  • Reduced personal accomplishment (inefficacy): Feeling incompetent, unproductive, and lacking achievement in one's work with people.

Burnout is measured by the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI), which has versions for human services, educators, and general occupations. Risk factors include high workload, lack of control, insufficient reward, breakdown of community, absence of fairness, and value conflicts (Maslach & Leiter, 2008).

EPPP Key Concept

Burnout is particularly prevalent among helping professionals (therapists, physicians, nurses, social workers, teachers). For the EPPP, know that emotional exhaustion is the most central component and develops first. Depersonalization is considered a coping response to exhaustion, and reduced personal accomplishment develops as a consequence. Burnout is distinct from depression, though they share features -- burnout is work-specific, while depression is more pervasive.

Occupational Stress Models

Karasek's Job Demand-Control Model

Robert Karasek (1979) proposed that occupational stress results from the interaction of two factors: job demands and job control (decision latitude).

Four Job Types in the Demand-Control Model

Low DemandsHigh Demands
High ControlLow-strain (relaxed)Active (challenging but manageable)
Low ControlPassive (boring, de-motivating)High-strain (most stressful)

The high-strain condition (high demands, low control) is most detrimental to health and well-being. Later versions added social support as a third dimension -- iso-strain (high demands, low control, low social support) is the most harmful combination.

Effort-Reward Imbalance Model (Siegrist)

Johannes Siegrist (1996) proposed that stress results from an imbalance between effort expended at work and rewards received (salary, esteem, career opportunities, job security). High effort combined with low reward produces distress, particularly for individuals high in overcommitment (excessive striving and need for approval).

Work-Life Balance

Work-family conflict occurs when demands from work and family domains are mutually incompatible. Two directions of conflict are recognized:

  • Work-to-family conflict: Work demands interfere with family responsibilities
  • Family-to-work conflict: Family demands interfere with work responsibilities

Both directions are associated with decreased satisfaction, increased stress, and poorer mental health. Organizational interventions include flexible work arrangements, family leave policies, telework options, and supportive supervision.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Job Characteristics Model: five core dimensions; MPS formula multiplies autonomy and feedback
  • Growth Need Strength moderates the JCM -- enriched jobs work best for high-GNS individuals
  • Three types of organizational commitment: affective (want), continuance (need), normative (ought)
  • Affective commitment has strongest link to positive outcomes; continuance predicts staying but not performance
  • OCB (Organ): five dimensions -- altruism, conscientiousness, sportsmanship, courtesy, civic virtue
  • OCBs are discretionary extra-role behaviors; predicted by satisfaction, affective commitment, and organizational justice
  • Maslach's burnout: emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, reduced personal accomplishment
  • Emotional exhaustion is the core, most central dimension of burnout
  • MBI (Maslach Burnout Inventory) is the standard measurement tool
  • Karasek: high demands + low control = high-strain jobs (most stressful)
  • Iso-strain: high demands + low control + low social support = worst combination
  • Core self-evaluations (self-esteem, self-efficacy, locus of control, emotional stability) predict satisfaction
  • Work-family conflict is bidirectional (work-to-family and family-to-work)
