Introduction to Personnel Selection

Personnel selection refers to the process of choosing individuals for employment based on their potential to perform successfully. Effective selection systems are grounded in job analysis and employ validated assessment methods to predict future job performance.

The EPPP tests knowledge of various selection methods, their relative validity, and the legal framework governing their use. Understanding the strengths and limitations of each method is essential.

Employment Interviews

Interviews are the most commonly used selection method, despite historically being among the least valid predictors when conducted in an unstructured format.

Unstructured Interviews

Unstructured interviews lack standardized questions, scoring criteria, or systematic evaluation procedures. Interviewers ask different questions of different candidates, follow up on topics of personal interest, and rely on global impressions.

Meta-analyses show unstructured interviews have relatively low validity (r = .20-.38) and are susceptible to numerous biases including first impression effects, similar-to-me bias, contrast effects, and non-verbal cue overweighting (McDaniel et al., 1994).

Structured Interviews

Structured interviews use predetermined, job-related questions asked consistently of all candidates, with standardized scoring rubrics. Research consistently demonstrates their superiority.

Types of Structured Interview Questions

  • Behavioral (experience-based) questions: Ask candidates to describe specific past experiences relevant to job competencies (e.g., "Tell me about a time when you had to manage a conflict between team members"). Based on the premise that past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior.
  • Situational questions: Present hypothetical job-related scenarios and ask candidates what they would do (e.g., "What would you do if a client became verbally aggressive during a session?"). Based on Locke's goal-setting theory -- intentions predict behavior.

EPPP Key Concept

Meta-analyses (Huffcutt & Arthur, 1994) show that structured interviews have significantly higher validity (r = .44-.57) than unstructured interviews. Structuring the interview reduces bias, increases reliability, and improves legal defensibility. Both behavioral and situational questions have good validity, though behavioral questions may be slightly better for higher-complexity jobs.

Assessment Centers

Assessment centers are multi-method, multi-assessor evaluation processes that use simulations and exercises to evaluate candidates on job-relevant dimensions. They are most commonly used for managerial and leadership selection and development.

Typical Assessment Center Components

  • In-basket exercise: Candidates respond to memos, emails, and documents that simulate a manager's typical workload, making prioritization and delegation decisions
  • Leaderless group discussion: Candidates discuss a problem or make a decision as a group without an assigned leader, revealing interpersonal and leadership skills
  • Role play: Candidates interact with a trained role player in a simulated scenario (e.g., counseling a problem employee)
  • Oral presentation: Candidates prepare and deliver a presentation on a business-related topic
  • Structured interviews: Conducted as part of the assessment battery

Assessment centers show moderate criterion-related validity (r = .28-.37) for predicting managerial performance (Arthur et al., 2003). However, they face challenges with construct validity -- factor analyses often reveal that ratings cluster by exercise rather than by dimension, suggesting assessors may evaluate exercise performance rather than the intended constructs.

Other Selection Methods

Biodata

Biographical data (biodata) uses systematically scored information about candidates' life history, experiences, and background to predict job performance. Items may cover education, work history, hobbies, interests, and attitudes.

Biodata has demonstrated good criterion-related validity (r = .30-.40) and is one of the best predictors of turnover. It is based on the behavioral consistency principle: past behavior and experiences predict future behavior. A weakness is that the reasons why specific biodata items predict performance are often unclear (lack of construct validity).

Work Samples and Situational Judgment Tests

Work Sample Tests

Work sample tests require candidates to perform actual tasks representative of the job (e.g., a typing test for a clerical position, a coding exercise for a software developer). They have high content validity and criterion-related validity (r = .33-.54), generate positive applicant reactions, and show smaller racial group differences than cognitive ability tests (Roth et al., 2005).

Situational Judgment Tests (SJTs)

Situational judgment tests present candidates with written or video scenarios depicting work situations and ask them to choose or rate response options. SJTs show moderate validity (r = .26-.34) and are useful because they measure a combination of cognitive ability, personality, and job knowledge. They show smaller racial group differences than traditional cognitive ability tests.

Cognitive Ability Tests

General cognitive ability (g) tests are among the strongest single predictors of job performance, with meta-analytic validity coefficients around r = .51 (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). Cognitive ability predicts performance across virtually all jobs, with stronger predictive validity for more complex jobs.

The Validity-Diversity Dilemma

While cognitive ability tests are the strongest single predictor of job performance, they also show the largest racial group differences (approximately 1 standard deviation between White and Black means). This creates a tension between maximizing prediction (validity) and minimizing adverse impact (diversity). Strategies to address this include using tests in combination with other predictors, using banding (discussed in Selection Evaluation), or using alternative predictors with less adverse impact.

Personality Tests in Selection

The Big Five personality traits have been extensively studied as selection predictors. Barrick and Mount's (1991) meta-analysis found that:

An important advantage of personality tests is that they show minimal racial group differences, thus producing less adverse impact than cognitive ability tests. Concerns include faking (socially desirable responding) and relatively modest validity coefficients compared to cognitive ability.

Legal Considerations in Selection

Selection practices must comply with federal employment law. Key legislation includes:

Major Employment Laws

  • Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964): Prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin
  • Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA, 1967): Protects workers aged 40 and older
  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990): Prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities; requires reasonable accommodations; limits when medical examinations can be administered
  • Civil Rights Act of 1991: Allows compensatory and punitive damages; prohibits race-norming of test scores

EPPP Key Distinction: Disparate Treatment vs. Disparate Impact

  • Disparate treatment: Intentional discrimination -- treating individuals differently because of their protected group membership (e.g., asking female applicants about childcare plans but not male applicants). Requires proof of discriminatory intent.
  • Disparate impact (adverse impact): A facially neutral practice that disproportionately affects a protected group (e.g., a physical strength test that eliminates significantly more female applicants). No intent is required -- the focus is on the outcome. The employer must demonstrate the practice is job-related and consistent with business necessity.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Structured interviews are significantly more valid than unstructured interviews
  • Behavioral questions = past behavior; situational questions = hypothetical future scenarios
  • Assessment centers use multiple methods and assessors but may lack construct validity
  • Cognitive ability (g) is the strongest single predictor of job performance but produces largest group differences
  • Conscientiousness is the only Big Five trait that generalizes across all job types
  • Work sample tests have high validity and low adverse impact
  • Disparate treatment = intentional; disparate impact = unintentional but disproportionate effect
  • ADA: cannot administer medical exams until after a conditional job offer
  • Civil Rights Act of 1991 prohibits race-norming (adjusting scores by race)
  • Graphology has near-zero validity for predicting job performance
