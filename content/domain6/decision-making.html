Introduction to Organizational Decision Making

Decision making in organizations involves both individual and group processes for identifying problems, generating alternatives, evaluating options, and selecting courses of action. The EPPP tests knowledge of bounded rationality, group decision-making phenomena (especially groupthink), participative decision models, and cognitive biases that affect organizational decisions.

Bounded Rationality

Herbert Simon (1957, Nobel Prize 1978) challenged the classical economic assumption that decision makers are fully rational. He proposed that human rationality is bounded -- limited by cognitive capacity, available information, and time constraints.

Key Concepts in Bounded Rationality

  • Bounded rationality: Decision makers cannot process all available information, consider all alternatives, or accurately predict all consequences. Instead, they use simplified models of reality.
  • Satisficing: Rather than optimizing (finding the best possible solution), people satisfice -- they search through alternatives until they find one that meets their minimum criteria ("good enough"). This is a rational response to cognitive limitations.
  • Administrative model: Simon's descriptive model of how decisions are actually made, in contrast to the classical rational model of how decisions should ideally be made.

Example: Satisficing vs. Optimizing

A hiring manager using the rational/optimizing approach would interview every qualified candidate, systematically compare all on every dimension, and select the objectively best one. A satisficing manager would interview candidates sequentially and hire the first one who meets predetermined minimum qualifications. Given real-world time and resource constraints, satisficing is often the more practical strategy.

EPPP Key Concept

Simon distinguished between programmed decisions (routine, repetitive problems handled by established procedures, rules, or algorithms) and nonprogrammed decisions (novel, complex, unstructured problems requiring creativity and judgment). Organizations should develop standard procedures for programmed decisions and reserve executive judgment for nonprogrammed decisions.

Groupthink

Irving Janis (1972, 1982) developed the concept of groupthink -- a mode of thinking that occurs in highly cohesive groups when the desire for unanimity and conformity overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. Groupthink leads to defective decision making.

Antecedent Conditions

  • High group cohesiveness: Strong sense of group identity and desire to maintain harmony
  • Insulation of the group: Limited exposure to outside opinions and information
  • Directive leadership: Leader who promotes their preferred solution early in discussions
  • Lack of methodical procedures: Absence of systematic norms for evaluating alternatives
  • High stress with low hope: External threats with limited perceived alternatives
  • Homogeneity of members: Similar backgrounds, values, and ideologies

Symptoms of Groupthink

Janis's Eight Symptoms

  1. Illusion of invulnerability: Excessive optimism and risk-taking
  2. Collective rationalization: Discounting warnings or negative feedback
  3. Belief in inherent morality: Unquestioned belief in the group's rightness, ignoring ethical consequences
  4. Stereotyping of out-groups: Viewing opponents as evil, weak, or stupid
  5. Pressure on dissenters: Direct pressure on members who express dissenting views
  6. Self-censorship: Members withhold doubts and concerns to maintain group harmony
  7. Illusion of unanimity: False perception that everyone agrees (silence = consent)
  8. Self-appointed mindguards: Members who shield the group from information that contradicts the group's view

Classic Examples of Groupthink

Janis analyzed several historical decision-making fiascoes as examples of groupthink: the Bay of Pigs invasion (1961), the failure to anticipate the attack on Pearl Harbor (1941), and the escalation of the Vietnam War. In each case, highly cohesive advisory groups failed to critically evaluate alternatives or consider risks, leading to disastrous outcomes.

Preventing Groupthink

Janis recommended several strategies to prevent groupthink:

  • Leader should encourage open criticism and withhold their own opinion initially
  • Assign one member the role of devil's advocate to deliberately challenge proposals
  • Divide into subgroups to independently develop recommendations, then reconvene
  • Invite outside experts to observe and critique the group's process
  • Hold a "second chance" meeting to reconsider the decision before finalizing
  • Encourage each member to discuss the group's deliberations with trusted associates outside the group

Vroom-Yetton-Jago Decision Model

Victor Vroom and Philip Yetton (1973), later revised with Arthur Jago (Vroom & Jago, 1988), developed a normative model that prescribes how much participation leaders should allow subordinates in making decisions. The model identifies five decision-making styles:

Five Decision-Making Styles

StyleDescriptionParticipation Level
AI (Autocratic I)Leader makes the decision alone using available informationNone
AII (Autocratic II)Leader obtains information from subordinates, then decides aloneMinimal (information only)
CI (Consultative I)Leader shares the problem with individual subordinates, gets ideas, then decidesModerate (individual input)
CII (Consultative II)Leader shares the problem with the group, gets collective ideas, then decidesModerate (group input)
GII (Group II)Leader shares the problem with the group; group generates and evaluates alternatives and reaches consensus. Leader facilitates but does not impose.Full (group decision)

The appropriate style depends on situational factors assessed through a series of diagnostic questions:

  • Is decision quality important?
  • Does the leader have sufficient information?
  • Is the problem structured?
  • Is subordinate acceptance critical for implementation?
  • Would subordinates accept an autocratic decision?
  • Do subordinates share the organizational goals?
  • Is conflict among subordinates likely?

EPPP Key Concept

The Vroom-Yetton-Jago model is a normative (prescriptive) model -- it tells leaders what they should do, not what they typically do. The model demonstrates that no single leadership decision style is universally best: sometimes autocratic decisions are optimal (when the leader has expertise and acceptance is not critical), and sometimes participative decisions are needed (when acceptance is essential and the leader lacks information).

Escalation of Commitment

Escalation of commitment (Staw, 1976) refers to the tendency to continue investing in a failing course of action despite mounting evidence that the decision is not working. Also called the "sunk cost fallacy" or "throwing good money after bad."

Factors Contributing to Escalation

  • Self-justification: The need to justify previous decisions and demonstrate consistency
  • Sunk costs: Focus on what has already been invested rather than future costs and benefits
  • Completion effect: The closer a project is to completion, the more reluctant people are to abandon it
  • Public commitment: Having made the decision publicly increases pressure to follow through
  • Selective perception: Attending to information that supports the original decision while ignoring negative evidence
  • Organizational factors: Political pressures, administrative inertia, and project champions who tie their reputation to the outcome

Example: Escalation in Organizations

A company has invested $50 million in developing a new product. Market research now shows minimal consumer interest, but executives authorize another $20 million to "see it through." Their reasoning focuses on the $50 million already spent (sunk cost) rather than the realistic prospects of future returns. Rational decision making would focus only on whether the additional $20 million investment is justified by expected future returns.

Strategies to reduce escalation include: setting advance limits on investment, separating initial and continuation decisions (different decision makers), establishing clear criteria for abandoning a project, and creating a culture that does not punish failure.

Prospect Theory in Organizations

Prospect theory, developed by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky (1979, Nobel Prize 2002), describes how people make decisions under conditions of risk and uncertainty. It challenges classical expected utility theory with several key insights:

Key Principles of Prospect Theory

  • Reference dependence: People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point (typically the status quo), not in absolute terms
  • Loss aversion: Losses loom larger than equivalent gains -- the pain of losing $100 is greater than the pleasure of gaining $100 (approximately 2:1 ratio). This is the most important concept for organizational applications.
  • Diminishing sensitivity: The marginal impact of gains and losses decreases as their magnitude increases (the difference between $100 and $200 feels larger than between $1,100 and $1,200)
  • Risk attitudes: People are generally risk-averse for gains (preferring a certain $50 over a 50% chance of $100) but risk-seeking for losses (preferring a 50% chance of losing $100 over a certain loss of $50)

Organizational Applications of Prospect Theory

Prospect theory has important implications for organizational decision making: (1) Framing effects: The same decision presented as a potential loss vs. potential gain leads to different choices. Managers can influence decisions by how they frame options. (2) Resistance to change: Loss aversion helps explain why people resist organizational change -- the potential losses feel more significant than equivalent potential gains. (3) Escalation of commitment: When decision makers frame additional investment as a way to avoid acknowledging a loss, they become risk-seeking and more likely to escalate.

Group Decision-Making Techniques

Several structured techniques have been developed to improve group decision quality:

TechniqueDescriptionPrimary Benefit
BrainstormingGroup generates as many ideas as possible without criticism; evaluation comes laterQuantity of ideas (though research shows individuals produce more ideas working alone)
Nominal Group Technique (NGT)Members individually generate ideas in writing, then share in round-robin format, discuss, and vote privatelyReduces production blocking and social loafing; equalizes participation
Delphi TechniqueExperts respond to questionnaires independently through multiple rounds; responses are aggregated and shared; process continues until convergenceEliminates conformity pressure; useful when experts cannot meet face-to-face
Stepladder TechniqueMembers join the group one at a time, presenting their ideas before hearing the group's discussionEnsures each member contributes independently before being influenced by the group

EPPP Key Concept

Research consistently shows that nominal groups (individuals working alone whose ideas are pooled) outperform interacting groups (face-to-face brainstorming) in both quantity and quality of ideas. This is due to production blocking (waiting to speak), evaluation apprehension (fear of criticism), and social loafing (reduced effort in groups). The Nominal Group Technique and Delphi Technique were designed to address these problems.

Group Polarization and Risky Shift

James Stoner (1961) made the surprising discovery that groups tend to make riskier decisions than the average of individual members' pre-discussion preferences. This phenomenon was initially called the risky shift.

Subsequent research revealed that groups do not always shift toward greater risk. Sometimes groups shift toward greater caution (cautious shift). The broader phenomenon was reconceptualized as group polarization (Moscovici & Zavalloni, 1969): after group discussion, the group's position tends to become more extreme in the direction of the pre-discussion tendency. If members initially lean toward risk, the group becomes riskier; if members initially lean toward caution, the group becomes more cautious.

Two Explanations for Group Polarization

  • Persuasive arguments theory: During discussion, members are exposed to novel arguments favoring the already-preferred direction. Since the pool of available arguments is skewed toward the initial tendency, discussion introduces new supporting arguments that further strengthen the dominant position.
  • Social comparison theory: Members want to be perceived as holding socially desirable positions. When they discover the group's average position during discussion, they shift further in that direction to appear more extreme (in a valued direction) than others. People want to be seen as at least as risk-taking (or cautious) as their peers.

EPPP Key Concept

Group polarization is distinct from groupthink. Groupthink involves suppression of dissent and defective decision processes in highly cohesive groups. Group polarization is a general tendency for group discussion to amplify pre-existing preferences, regardless of cohesion. Both can lead to poor decisions, but through different mechanisms. Know that the risky shift was the original finding (Stoner, 1961) and that group polarization is the broader, more accurate concept -- groups shift toward extremity, not necessarily toward risk.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Simon's bounded rationality: cognitive limits prevent fully rational decisions; people satisfice rather than optimize
  • Satisficing = choosing the first "good enough" option; optimizing = finding the absolute best
  • Programmed decisions = routine, handled by procedures; nonprogrammed = novel, requiring judgment
  • Groupthink: cohesive groups prioritize unanimity over critical thinking (Janis)
  • Eight symptoms of groupthink: invulnerability, rationalization, morality, stereotyping, pressure, self-censorship, unanimity illusion, mindguards
  • Antecedents: high cohesiveness, insulation, directive leader, homogeneity, high stress
  • Prevention: devil's advocate, outside experts, leader withholds opinion, subgroups
  • Vroom-Yetton-Jago: normative model with five decision styles (AI, AII, CI, CII, GII) based on situational factors
  • Escalation of commitment: continuing to invest in a failing course of action (driven by sunk costs, self-justification)
  • Prospect theory: loss aversion (losses hurt ~2x more than gains); risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses
  • Nominal groups outperform brainstorming groups for idea generation
  • Delphi technique: experts respond independently in iterative rounds; no face-to-face interaction
  • Risky shift (Stoner, 1961): groups tend to make riskier decisions than individual averages
  • Group polarization: groups shift toward more extreme position in the direction of pre-discussion tendency (not always riskier -- cautious shift also possible)
  • Two mechanisms: persuasive arguments (novel arguments) and social comparison (wanting to be seen as more extreme)
