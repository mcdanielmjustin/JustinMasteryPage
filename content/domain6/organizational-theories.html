Introduction to Organizational Theories

Organizational theories provide frameworks for understanding how organizations are structured, how they function, and how they can be improved. For the EPPP, you must understand the historical evolution of these theories, from classical approaches emphasizing efficiency and structure to modern perspectives that account for complexity, human factors, and environmental contingencies.

These theories form the foundation of industrial/organizational psychology and inform current practices in organizational design, management, and consulting.

Classical Organizational Theory

Classical theories emerged in the early 20th century during the Industrial Revolution, when organizations were growing rapidly and managers sought systematic approaches to improve efficiency and productivity. These theories share an emphasis on rational structure, clear hierarchies, and standardized procedures.

Scientific Management (Frederick Taylor)

Scientific management, developed by Frederick Winslow Taylor (1911), applied scientific methods to analyze and optimize work processes. Taylor believed there was "one best way" to perform any job, and that scientific study could identify it.

Core Principles of Scientific Management

  • Scientific study of tasks: Use time-and-motion studies to determine the most efficient method for each task
  • Scientific selection of workers: Match workers to jobs based on their abilities and training them systematically
  • Cooperation between management and workers: Management plans and supervises; workers execute
  • Equal division of work: Responsibility is shared between management (planning) and workers (execution)

Example: Time-and-Motion Studies

Taylor's famous pig-iron experiment at Bethlehem Steel demonstrated that by scientifically studying the optimal weight of shoveled loads and rest intervals, worker productivity could increase from 12.5 to 47.5 tons per day. Workers received a 60% pay increase while the company's costs per ton decreased.

Taylor's approach was criticized for treating workers as interchangeable parts, ignoring social and psychological needs, and reducing worker autonomy. Nevertheless, his principles laid the groundwork for modern job analysis, performance standards, and incentive systems.

Bureaucratic Theory (Max Weber)

Max Weber (1947) developed the theory of bureaucracy as an ideal organizational form characterized by rationality, predictability, and efficiency. Weber distinguished between three types of authority:

  • Traditional authority: Based on established customs and traditions (e.g., hereditary monarchies)
  • Charismatic authority: Based on the exceptional personal qualities of a leader
  • Rational-legal authority: Based on formal rules and procedures -- the foundation of bureaucracy

Key Features of Weber's Ideal Bureaucracy

  • Division of labor: Specialized roles with clearly defined responsibilities
  • Hierarchy of authority: Clear chain of command from top to bottom
  • Formal rules and procedures: Written policies governing behavior and decisions
  • Impersonality: Decisions based on rules, not personal relationships or feelings
  • Merit-based selection: Hiring and promotion based on qualifications and performance
  • Career orientation: Employment as a long-term career with advancement opportunities

Weber acknowledged that bureaucracies could become rigid ("iron cage") and overly focused on rules at the expense of goals, a phenomenon later termed goal displacement.

Administrative Management (Henri Fayol)

Henri Fayol (1916) approached organizational theory from the perspective of top management, identifying five basic functions of management and 14 principles of administration.

Fayol's Five Functions of Management

  1. Planning: Forecasting future conditions and establishing objectives
  2. Organizing: Arranging resources and tasks to achieve objectives
  3. Commanding: Directing and motivating employees
  4. Coordinating: Harmonizing activities across departments
  5. Controlling: Monitoring performance against plans and correcting deviations

Key Fayol principles include unity of command (each employee reports to one supervisor), scalar chain (clear line of authority), and span of control (limit on the number of subordinates one manager can effectively supervise).

Human Relations Movement

The human relations movement emerged as a reaction to the mechanistic view of workers in classical theory, emphasizing the importance of social factors, employee attitudes, and informal group dynamics in organizational effectiveness.

The Hawthorne Studies

The Hawthorne studies (1924-1932), conducted at the Western Electric Hawthorne Works near Chicago, are among the most famous research projects in organizational psychology. Initially designed to examine the effects of physical working conditions on productivity, they yielded unexpected findings about social and psychological factors.

Major Phases of the Hawthorne Studies

  • Illumination studies: Changes in lighting improved productivity regardless of direction -- both increases and decreases in illumination led to higher output
  • Relay Assembly Test Room: A small group of women showed sustained productivity increases across nearly all experimental conditions, attributed to attention and social factors
  • Interviewing program: Over 21,000 employee interviews revealed the importance of social relationships and employee attitudes
  • Bank Wiring Observation Room: Demonstrated that informal group norms powerfully regulated individual output, often overriding formal incentive systems

EPPP Key Concept: The Hawthorne Effect

The Hawthorne effect refers to the tendency for people to change their behavior simply because they know they are being observed or are receiving special attention. While the original interpretation of the Hawthorne studies has been debated and critiqued (Levitt & List, 2011), the term remains widely used in research methodology. On the EPPP, know that the Hawthorne effect represents a threat to internal validity in research design.

Elton Mayo and Fritz Roethlisberger, the principal researchers, concluded that social factors -- group norms, supervisory style, and employee attitudes -- were more powerful determinants of productivity than physical working conditions or economic incentives alone.

McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

Douglas McGregor (1960) proposed that managers hold implicit assumptions about human nature that profoundly influence their management style. He described two contrasting sets of assumptions:

Theory X (Pessimistic)Theory Y (Optimistic)
People inherently dislike work and will avoid itWork is as natural as play or rest
People must be coerced, controlled, or threatened to workPeople will exercise self-direction toward committed objectives
People prefer to be directed and avoid responsibilityPeople seek and accept responsibility under proper conditions
Security is the primary motivationImagination and creativity are widely distributed
Management by close supervision and external controlManagement by creating conditions for self-motivation

McGregor argued that Theory Y assumptions were generally more accurate and that management practices based on Theory Y (participative management, delegation, enriched jobs) would lead to higher motivation and organizational effectiveness. His work significantly influenced the development of participative management and organizational development.

Systems Theory

Systems theory views organizations as complex, interconnected systems that interact with their environment. Drawing on general systems theory (von Bertalanffy, 1968), this perspective emphasizes that organizations cannot be understood by examining their parts in isolation.

Open Systems Model

Organizations are conceptualized as open systems that take in inputs from the environment, transform them through internal processes, and produce outputs that feed back into the environment.

Key Systems Theory Concepts

  • Inputs: Resources obtained from the environment (raw materials, labor, capital, information)
  • Throughput: The transformation process (production, service delivery, decision-making)
  • Outputs: Products, services, or outcomes returned to the environment
  • Feedback: Information about outputs that influences future inputs and processes
  • Entropy: The tendency of systems to run down and deteriorate without energy input
  • Negative entropy (negentropy): Importing more energy than is expended to survive and grow
  • Equifinality: Multiple pathways can lead to the same end result
  • Homeostasis: Self-regulating mechanisms that maintain stability

Example: Equifinality in Organizations

Two technology companies can achieve similar market success through very different strategies: one through innovation and first-mover advantage, and another through cost efficiency and market follower strategies. This illustrates equifinality -- there is no single "best way" to achieve organizational goals.

Katz and Kahn's Social Systems Model

Daniel Katz and Robert Kahn (1966, 1978) applied open systems theory specifically to organizations, identifying five key organizational subsystems:

  1. Production/technical subsystems: Core transformation activities
  2. Supportive subsystems: Procurement of inputs and disposal of outputs
  3. Maintenance subsystems: Ensuring predictable role performance (HR, training)
  4. Adaptive subsystems: Sensing and responding to environmental changes (R&D, planning)
  5. Managerial subsystems: Coordinating and controlling other subsystems

Contingency Theory

Contingency theory rejects the idea of universal "best practices" in organizational design. Instead, it proposes that the optimal organizational structure depends on (is contingent upon) various situational factors, particularly the organization's environment, technology, and size.

Burns and Stalker: Mechanistic vs. Organic Structures

Tom Burns and G.M. Stalker (1961) identified two contrasting organizational forms suited to different environments:

Mechanistic StructureOrganic Structure
Rigid hierarchy and centralized decision-makingFlat hierarchy with decentralized decisions
Precisely defined roles and responsibilitiesFlexible, broadly defined roles
Formal communication through chain of commandLateral communication and networking
Best suited for stable, predictable environmentsBest suited for dynamic, changing environments

Lawrence and Lorsch: Differentiation and Integration

Paul Lawrence and Jay Lorsch (1967) found that effective organizations match their internal structure to environmental demands. As environments become more complex and uncertain, organizations require greater differentiation (specialized departments) and correspondingly greater integration (mechanisms to coordinate those departments).

EPPP Key Concept

The key insight of contingency theory is that there is no single best way to organize. Effectiveness depends on the fit between organizational characteristics and situational factors. On the EPPP, know that mechanistic structures work best in stable environments, while organic structures are more effective in uncertain, rapidly changing environments.

Woodward's Technology Typology

Joan Woodward (1965) demonstrated that an organization's technology -- specifically, the complexity of its production process -- influences its optimal structure:

  • Unit/small-batch production: Custom products; organic structure with few management levels works best
  • Mass/large-batch production: Standardized products; mechanistic structure with clear hierarchies works best
  • Continuous process production: Automated, continuous flow; organic structure with high skill levels works best
Woodward, J. (1965). Industrial organization: Theory and practice. Oxford University Press.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Taylor's scientific management emphasizes "one best way" through time-and-motion studies
  • Weber's bureaucracy features hierarchy, formal rules, impersonality, and merit-based selection
  • Fayol's five functions of management: planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating, controlling
  • The Hawthorne effect: behavioral change due to being observed (threat to internal validity)
  • Hawthorne studies revealed the importance of informal group norms and social factors over physical conditions
  • McGregor's Theory X assumes workers dislike work; Theory Y assumes work is natural and people seek responsibility
  • Open systems: inputs, throughput, outputs, feedback, entropy, equifinality
  • Contingency theory: mechanistic structures suit stable environments; organic structures suit dynamic environments
  • Distinguish between Fayol's unity of command (one boss) and Weber's hierarchy of authority
