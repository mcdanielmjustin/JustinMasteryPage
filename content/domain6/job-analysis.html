Job Analysis

Task analysis, KSAOs, job descriptions, competency modeling
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Introduction to Job Analysis

Job analysis is the systematic process of collecting and analyzing information about the content, context, and requirements of a job. It serves as the foundation for virtually all human resource functions, including selection, training, performance appraisal, compensation, and legal compliance.

For the EPPP, you must understand the distinction between different approaches to job analysis, the concept of KSAOs, and how job analysis information feeds into performance appraisal systems.

Approaches to Job Analysis

Task-Oriented Job Analysis

Task-oriented analysis focuses on what the worker does -- the specific tasks, duties, and responsibilities that comprise the job. This approach produces detailed descriptions of work activities.

Functional Job Analysis (FJA)

Developed by Sidney Fine, FJA classifies jobs based on the level of complexity in three domains:

  • Data: How workers interact with information (synthesizing, coordinating, analyzing, compiling, computing, copying, comparing)
  • People: How workers interact with others (mentoring, negotiating, instructing, supervising, diverting, persuading, speaking-signaling, serving, taking instructions)
  • Things: How workers interact with objects and equipment (setting up, precision working, operating-controlling, driving-operating, manipulating, tending, feeding-offbearing, handling)

Each domain is arranged hierarchically, with higher levels subsuming lower levels. FJA was used to develop the Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT).

Worker-Oriented Job Analysis

Worker-oriented analysis focuses on the human attributes and behaviors needed to perform the job, rather than the tasks themselves. This approach emphasizes what the worker brings to the job.

Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ)

Developed by McCormick, Jeanneret, and Mecham (1972), the PAQ is the most well-known worker-oriented instrument. It contains 194 job elements organized into six divisions:

  1. Information input: How the worker obtains information (visual, non-visual sources)
  2. Mental processes: Reasoning, decision-making, planning, information processing
  3. Work output: Physical activities and tools used
  4. Relationships with others: Interpersonal requirements
  5. Job context: Physical and social work environment
  6. Other characteristics: Schedules, demands, responsibilities

A major advantage of the PAQ is that it allows quantitative comparison across jobs, making it useful for compensation systems. A limitation is that it requires a college reading level and may be too abstract for some applications.

Job analysis identifies the KSAOs required for successful job performance:

Job Descriptions and Job Specifications

Job analysis produces two primary documents:

Job Description vs. Job Specification

  • Job description: A written summary of the tasks, duties, responsibilities, and working conditions of a job. It describes what is done. Used for recruitment, orientation, and defining role expectations.
  • Job specification: A written summary of the minimum qualifications (KSAOs) a person must possess to perform the job successfully. It describes who can do the job. Used for selection and placement decisions.

Example: Distinguishing Description from Specification

Job description excerpt (Clinical Psychologist): "Conducts psychological assessments, provides individual and group psychotherapy, develops treatment plans, and participates in multidisciplinary treatment team meetings."

Job specification excerpt: "Doctoral degree in clinical psychology from an APA-accredited program, state licensure as a psychologist, minimum 2 years post-licensure clinical experience, knowledge of evidence-based treatments."

O*NET and Modern Job Analysis

The Occupational Information Network (O*NET) is the U.S. Department of Labor's comprehensive online database that replaced the Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT). O*NET uses a content model organized around six domains:

  • Worker characteristics: Abilities, occupational interests, work values, work styles
  • Worker requirements: Skills, knowledge, education
  • Experience requirements: Training, experience, licensure
  • Occupational requirements: Generalized work activities, organizational context, work context
  • Workforce characteristics: Labor market information, occupational outlook
  • Occupation-specific information: Tasks, tools, and technologies

Competency Modeling

Competency modeling is a newer, more strategic approach that identifies the broader competencies (integrated sets of knowledge, skills, abilities, and personal attributes) needed for effective performance across a role, job family, or entire organization. Unlike traditional job analysis, which focuses on specific tasks, competency modeling emphasizes organizational strategy and culture.

EPPP Key Distinction

Traditional job analysis is more detailed, legally defensible, and task-specific. Competency modeling is broader, more future-oriented, and aligned with organizational strategy. Both approaches have value, but for legal defensibility in selection contexts, traditional job analysis is preferred because of its specificity and established methodology.

Performance Appraisal Methods

Performance appraisal is the systematic evaluation of employee job performance. Job analysis directly informs the development of performance criteria. Methods vary in their focus on traits, behaviors, or results.

Rating Scale Methods

Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales (BARS)

Developed by Smith and Kendall (1963), BARS use specific behavioral examples (critical incidents) as anchors for each point on the rating scale. Each performance dimension has its own scale with behavioral descriptions at different effectiveness levels.

Advantages: Reduces ambiguity, provides clear behavioral standards, involves employees in development

Disadvantages: Time-consuming and expensive to develop, specific to each job

Behavioral Observation Scales (BOS)

Developed by Latham and Wexley (1977), BOS ask raters to indicate the frequency with which an employee exhibits specific behaviors, using a 5-point frequency scale (Almost Never to Almost Always). Unlike BARS, which rates typical performance level, BOS measures the frequency of desired behaviors.

360-Degree Feedback

360-degree feedback (multi-source assessment) collects performance evaluations from multiple perspectives: supervisors, peers, subordinates, and sometimes customers and the employee themselves. This comprehensive approach provides a more complete picture of performance.

Example: 360-Degree Feedback Sources

A manager receives feedback from: (1) their supervisor on strategic thinking, (2) peers on collaboration, (3) direct reports on coaching and communication, (4) customers on responsiveness, and (5) self-assessment on all dimensions. Discrepancies between self and others' ratings can highlight blind spots.

Common Rating Errors

Error TypeDescription
Halo effectRating an employee the same across all dimensions based on a general impression
Leniency/severityConsistently rating all employees too high (leniency) or too low (severity)
Central tendencyRating all employees near the middle of the scale
Recency effectOverweighting recent events in the appraisal period
Contrast effectRating influenced by comparison with the previously rated employee
Similar-to-me effectRating employees more favorably who are similar to the rater
First impression errorAllowing initial impressions to bias later ratings (primacy effect)

Frame-of-reference (FOR) training has been shown to be one of the most effective methods for reducing rating errors. FOR training teaches raters to use common standards and behavioral examples when evaluating performance.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Task-oriented analysis focuses on what is done; worker-oriented analysis focuses on who can do it
  • PAQ: 194 items, six divisions, allows quantitative job comparison, requires college reading level
  • Functional Job Analysis: classifies jobs by Data, People, Things complexity
  • KSAOs: Knowledge, Skills, Abilities, Other characteristics
  • Job description = what is done; job specification = who can do it (minimum qualifications)
  • O*NET replaced the DOT as the primary occupational classification system
  • BARS uses behavioral anchors; BOS uses frequency of observed behaviors
  • 360-degree feedback collects ratings from supervisors, peers, subordinates, and self
  • Halo, leniency, central tendency, recency, and contrast effects are common rating errors
  • Frame-of-reference training is most effective for reducing rating errors
  • Competency modeling is broader and more strategic than traditional job analysis
