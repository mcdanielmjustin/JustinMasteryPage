Training

Needs assessment, training methods, transfer of training
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Introduction to Training and Development

Training refers to the systematic acquisition of knowledge, skills, and attitudes that result in improved performance in a specific job or role. Effective training programs follow a systematic process: needs assessment, design and delivery, and evaluation.

For the EPPP, you must understand the three levels of needs assessment, the major training methods, Kirkpatrick's evaluation model, and the factors that influence transfer of training.

Training Needs Assessment

A training needs assessment is the systematic process of determining what training is needed, who needs it, and what conditions are necessary for effective training. It involves three levels of analysis:

Organizational Analysis

Organizational analysis examines the broader context to determine where training is needed within the organization and whether conditions support training effectiveness. Key considerations include:

  • Strategic goals and objectives that require new competencies
  • Available resources (budget, time, facilities) for training
  • Organizational climate and support for training (supervisor support, opportunity to practice)
  • Environmental constraints or changes (new technology, regulations, restructuring)

Task Analysis

Task analysis (also called operations analysis) identifies the specific tasks, knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs) required for successful job performance. It determines what the content of training should be.

Steps in Task Analysis

  1. Identify the job and its component tasks (using job analysis data)
  2. Determine the KSAs required for each task
  3. Identify the performance standards and conditions under which tasks are performed
  4. Prioritize training content based on task importance, frequency, and difficulty

Person Analysis

Person analysis determines which specific employees need training and what kind of training they need. It identifies discrepancies between current and required performance levels.

Example: Three-Level Needs Assessment

A hospital implements a new electronic health records system. Organizational analysis: Strategic goal of digitization requires staff retraining; budget allocated. Task analysis: Identifies specific EHR functions staff must perform (entering patient data, ordering labs, documenting notes). Person analysis: Performance data shows that nurses over 55 have the lowest proficiency on the new system and need the most intensive training.

Training Methods

On-the-Job Training Methods

Common On-the-Job Approaches

  • Job rotation: Moving employees through different positions to broaden skills and understanding. Effective for management development and succession planning.
  • Apprenticeship: Structured combination of on-the-job training under a skilled worker and classroom instruction. Common in trades.
  • Coaching/mentoring: One-on-one developmental relationship with a more experienced person. Mentoring is broader and career-focused; coaching is more task-specific.
  • Job instruction training (JIT): A structured four-step method: (1) prepare the trainee, (2) present the task, (3) have the trainee perform, (4) follow up and provide feedback.

Simulation-Based Training

Simulation training creates realistic replicas of work situations for practice without real-world consequences:

  • Role playing: Trainees act out scenarios to practice interpersonal skills (conflict resolution, customer service, counseling)
  • Business simulations/games: Participants make decisions in a simulated business environment and see the consequences
  • Virtual reality/computer simulations: Technology-based recreations of work environments for high-fidelity practice (surgical simulators, flight simulators)
  • Case studies: Detailed analysis of real or hypothetical situations to develop analytical and decision-making skills

E-Learning and Technology-Based Training

E-learning encompasses training delivered via electronic media, including computer-based training, web-based courses, and mobile learning. Research shows e-learning is generally as effective as classroom instruction for knowledge acquisition, with advantages in cost-efficiency, flexibility, and standardization of content (Sitzmann et al., 2006).

EPPP Key Concept

The most effective e-learning incorporates learner control, practice opportunities, and feedback -- consistent with principles from learning theory. Blended learning, which combines online and face-to-face components, often produces the best outcomes by leveraging the advantages of both approaches.

Kirkpatrick's Training Evaluation Model

Donald Kirkpatrick (1959, 1994) developed the most widely used framework for evaluating training effectiveness. It consists of four levels, each building on the previous:

Kirkpatrick's Four Levels

LevelWhat It MeasuresHow It Is Measured
1. ReactionTrainees' satisfaction with and attitudes toward the trainingPost-training surveys, "smile sheets," satisfaction ratings
2. LearningKnowledge, skills, or attitudes acquired during trainingPre/post tests, skill demonstrations, written exams
3. BehaviorTransfer of training to actual job performanceSupervisor ratings, on-the-job observation, performance data
4. ResultsOrganizational impact (productivity, quality, profits, turnover)Organizational records, ROI analysis, business metrics

EPPP Key Concept

Most organizations evaluate only at Level 1 (reaction), even though higher levels provide more meaningful information. Importantly, the levels are not strongly correlated: positive reactions (Level 1) do not guarantee learning (Level 2), and learning does not guarantee behavioral change on the job (Level 3). The most critical and least measured level is Level 4 (results). A fifth level has been proposed -- return on investment (ROI) -- which calculates the monetary value of training results compared to training costs.

Transfer of Training

Transfer of training refers to the degree to which knowledge, skills, and abilities learned in training are applied on the job and maintained over time. Transfer is the ultimate goal of training.

Types of Transfer

  • Positive transfer: Training improves job performance (the goal)
  • Negative transfer: Training interferes with job performance (e.g., learning to drive on the right after years of driving on the left)
  • Zero transfer: Training has no effect on job performance
  • Near transfer: Applying learned skills to situations very similar to the training context
  • Far transfer: Applying learned skills to situations quite different from the training context (more difficult to achieve)

Factors Affecting Transfer

Baldwin and Ford's (1988) Transfer Model

Transfer of training depends on three categories of factors:

  • Training design: Identical elements (Thorndike), general principles, stimulus variability, practice and overlearning, feedback
  • Trainee characteristics: Ability, personality (self-efficacy, motivation to learn, locus of control), perceived utility of training
  • Work environment: Supervisor support, peer support, opportunity to perform, organizational climate for transfer

Identical elements theory (Thorndike & Woodworth, 1901) predicts that transfer is maximized when the training environment closely matches the job environment. This is the theoretical basis for simulation training and high-fidelity practice environments.

Example: Enhancing Transfer

A therapy training program maximizes transfer by: (1) using role-plays with realistic client scenarios (identical elements), (2) screening trainees for motivation and readiness (trainee characteristics), (3) arranging post-training supervision and case consultation (work environment support), and (4) requiring trainees to set specific goals for applying new techniques (relapse prevention).

Relapse prevention, adapted from Marlatt and Gordon's addiction model by Marx (1982), teaches trainees to anticipate situations where they might revert to old behaviors, develop coping strategies, and maintain new skills. This approach has been shown to improve long-term transfer.

EPPP Focus: Key Testable Concepts

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Three levels of needs assessment: organizational, task, person
  • Organizational analysis determines where training is needed and if conditions support it
  • Task analysis identifies what should be trained; person analysis identifies who needs training
  • Kirkpatrick's four levels: reaction, learning, behavior, results
  • Levels are not strongly correlated -- satisfaction does not ensure learning or transfer
  • Most organizations only measure Level 1 (reaction); Level 4 (results) is most meaningful but least measured
  • Transfer of training: positive, negative, zero, near, far
  • Identical elements theory: transfer improves as training-job similarity increases
  • Baldwin and Ford model: training design + trainee characteristics + work environment = transfer
  • Supervisor support is one of the strongest work environment predictors of transfer
  • Overlearning (practicing beyond mastery) enhances retention and transfer
