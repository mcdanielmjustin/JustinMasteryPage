Introduction to Leadership Theories

Leadership is one of the most extensively studied topics in organizational psychology. The EPPP tests knowledge of the major theoretical approaches to understanding leadership effectiveness, from early trait-based perspectives to contemporary models emphasizing situational factors and transformational processes.

The evolution of leadership theory reflects a progression from asking "Who are leaders?" (trait theories) to "What do leaders do?" (behavioral theories) to "When does a particular style work best?" (contingency theories) to "How do leaders transform organizations?" (transformational theories).

Trait Theories of Leadership

Trait theories represent the earliest systematic approach to studying leadership, based on the assumption that effective leaders possess certain innate personal characteristics that distinguish them from non-leaders. This "Great Man" approach dominated leadership research through the 1940s.

Key Leadership Traits Identified in Research

  • Intelligence: Effective leaders tend to be somewhat more intelligent than their followers, but not excessively so (Stogdill, 1948)
  • Self-confidence: Belief in one's own abilities and judgments
  • Determination: Drive, persistence, and initiative
  • Integrity: Honesty and trustworthiness
  • Sociability: Tendency to seek pleasant social relationships

Ralph Stogdill's (1948) influential review concluded that no single trait or set of traits consistently predicted leadership across situations. However, his later review (1974) was more positive, finding that traits combined with situational factors could predict leadership emergence and effectiveness.

EPPP Key Concept

Modern meta-analyses (Judge et al., 2002) have found that Big Five personality traits do predict leadership: Extraversion is the strongest correlate of leadership emergence, followed by Conscientiousness and Openness to Experience. Neuroticism is negatively related, while Agreeableness shows the weakest relationship. Know that trait approaches are not "dead" but are now seen as one piece of the leadership puzzle.

Behavioral Theories of Leadership

Behavioral theories shifted focus from who leaders are to what leaders do, attempting to identify specific behaviors associated with effective leadership. Two major research programs dominated this era.

Ohio State Leadership Studies

Researchers at Ohio State University (Fleishman, 1953; Halpin & Winer, 1957) used factor analysis of the Leader Behavior Description Questionnaire (LBDQ) to identify two independent dimensions of leadership behavior:

Ohio State Leadership Dimensions

  • Initiating structure: The degree to which a leader defines roles, establishes goals, organizes tasks, and establishes communication channels. This is task-oriented behavior focused on getting the job done.
  • Consideration: The degree to which a leader shows concern for followers' welfare, builds mutual trust, respects followers' ideas, and maintains warm interpersonal relationships. This is relationship-oriented behavior.

A critical finding was that these two dimensions are independent, meaning a leader can be high on both, low on both, or high on one and low on the other. Research generally found that leaders high on both dimensions were most effective.

University of Michigan Leadership Studies

Concurrently, researchers at the University of Michigan (Likert, 1961) identified two similar leadership styles:

  • Production-oriented leaders: Emphasize technical aspects of work and view employees as means to accomplish tasks (similar to initiating structure)
  • Employee-oriented leaders: Emphasize interpersonal relationships, take a personal interest in employees' needs, and accept individual differences (similar to consideration)

Unlike the Ohio State studies, the Michigan researchers initially conceptualized these as opposite ends of a single continuum, though later research supported the two-factor model. Michigan studies generally found that employee-oriented leadership was associated with higher group productivity and job satisfaction.

Blake and Mouton's Managerial Grid

Robert Blake and Jane Mouton (1964) extended the behavioral approach with their Managerial Grid (later called the Leadership Grid), plotting concern for production (x-axis) against concern for people (y-axis), each rated from 1-9:

Contingency Theories of Leadership

Contingency theories propose that leadership effectiveness depends on the interaction between leader characteristics and situational factors. No single leadership style is universally best.

Fiedler's Contingency Model

Fred Fiedler (1967) developed the first comprehensive contingency model of leadership. His model measures leader style using the Least Preferred Coworker (LPC) scale, which asks leaders to describe the person they have worked with least effectively.

Fiedler's Key Components

  • High-LPC leaders: Describe their least preferred coworker in relatively positive terms -- these leaders are relationship-motivated
  • Low-LPC leaders: Describe their least preferred coworker in negative terms -- these leaders are task-motivated

Situational control (favorableness) is determined by three factors in order of importance:

  1. Leader-member relations: Degree of trust and loyalty between leader and group (most important)
  2. Task structure: Degree to which tasks are clearly defined with specific procedures
  3. Position power: Leader's formal authority to reward and punish (least important)

Fiedler's Central Prediction

Task-motivated (low-LPC) leaders are most effective in situations of very high or very low situational control. Relationship-motivated (high-LPC) leaders are most effective in situations of moderate situational control. Crucially, Fiedler argued that leadership style is relatively fixed -- rather than trying to change the leader, organizations should change the situation to match the leader (called "job engineering").

Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Theory

Paul Hersey and Kenneth Blanchard (1969, 1977) developed the Situational Leadership Theory (originally called the "Life Cycle Theory of Leadership"), which proposes that effective leaders adapt their style to the readiness level (also called maturity) of their followers for a given task.

Key Insight

The central proposition is that effective leaders adapt their style to match the follower's developmental level on a specific task. As followers develop from R1 to R4, the leader should shift from telling to selling to participating to delegating. Unlike Fiedler, Hersey and Blanchard assumed leaders can and should flexibly change their style.

EPPP Key Concept

Despite its widespread popularity in leadership training programs, the Hersey-Blanchard model has received limited empirical support. Critics note that the readiness construct is ambiguously defined (combining ability and willingness into a single dimension is problematic) and that the prescribed style-readiness matches have not been consistently validated by research. However, the theory's intuitive appeal makes it one of the most widely used leadership training models in organizations.

House's Path-Goal Theory

Robert House (1971, 1996) developed path-goal theory, which draws on expectancy theory of motivation. The leader's job is to help followers achieve their goals by clarifying the path to rewards and removing obstacles.

Four Leadership Behaviors in Path-Goal Theory

  • Directive leadership: Providing specific guidance, standards, and schedules (effective when tasks are ambiguous)
  • Supportive leadership: Showing concern for followers' well-being and creating a friendly climate (effective when tasks are stressful, boring, or hazardous)
  • Participative leadership: Consulting followers and using their input in decisions (effective when followers are autonomous and have internal locus of control)
  • Achievement-oriented leadership: Setting challenging goals and expressing confidence in followers' abilities (effective when tasks are complex and followers need encouragement)

Unlike Fiedler, House assumed leaders can flexibly adapt their style. The optimal style depends on two contingency factors: subordinate characteristics (ability, locus of control, needs) and task/environmental characteristics (task structure, formal authority, work group).

Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) Theory

LMX theory (Graen & Uhl-Bien, 1995) focuses on the dyadic relationship between a leader and each individual follower, rather than treating all followers the same.

LMX Key Concepts

  • In-group: Followers who have high-quality exchange relationships with the leader, characterized by trust, loyalty, and mutual obligation. They receive more attention, resources, and opportunities.
  • Out-group: Followers who have low-quality exchange relationships, limited to the formal employment contract. Interactions are more transactional.

Research shows that high-quality LMX relationships are associated with greater job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and performance for followers.

Transformational and Transactional Leadership

The distinction between transformational and transactional leadership, first proposed by James MacGregor Burns (1978) and elaborated by Bernard Bass (1985), has become one of the most influential frameworks in contemporary leadership research.

Transactional Leadership

Transactional leaders focus on exchanges between leader and follower -- rewards for compliance, punishment for deviation:

  • Contingent reward: Setting clear expectations and providing rewards when goals are met
  • Management by exception (active): Monitoring performance and taking corrective action when standards are not met
  • Management by exception (passive): Intervening only when problems become serious
  • Laissez-faire: Abdicating responsibility and avoiding decisions (considered non-leadership)

Transformational Leadership (Bass's Four I's)

Transformational leaders inspire followers to transcend their self-interest for the good of the organization. Bass identified four components, known as the Four I's:

The Four I's of Transformational Leadership

  1. Idealized influence (charisma): The leader serves as a role model, demonstrating high ethical standards and earning trust and respect
  2. Inspirational motivation: The leader articulates a compelling vision, communicates optimism, and provides meaning and challenge
  3. Intellectual stimulation: The leader encourages creativity, challenges assumptions, and reframes problems
  4. Individualized consideration: The leader attends to each follower's unique needs, acts as a mentor, and promotes development

These components are measured by the Multifactor Leadership Questionnaire (MLQ) (Bass & Avolio, 1995). Meta-analyses consistently show that transformational leadership is positively associated with follower satisfaction, motivation, and performance across cultures and organizational types (Judge & Piccolo, 2004).

EPPP Key Distinction

Burns (1978) originally conceptualized transformational and transactional leadership as opposite ends of a continuum. Bass (1985) reconceptualized them as separate but complementary dimensions -- effective leaders use both, but transformational leadership "augments" the effects of transactional leadership (the augmentation effect). The EPPP may test this distinction.

Servant Leadership

Servant leadership (Greenleaf, 1970) inverts the traditional hierarchy: the leader's primary role is to serve followers. Key characteristics include listening, empathy, healing, awareness, persuasion (rather than coercion), conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, commitment to growth of people, and building community.

Research supports associations between servant leadership and increased organizational citizenship behaviors, job satisfaction, and team effectiveness (Liden et al., 2008).

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Bass's Four I's: idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration
