Brain Structure & Lateralization

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~45 min read

The Four Lobes of the Cerebral Cortex

The cerebral cortex is the outermost layer of the brain, responsible for higher cognitive functions including reasoning, language, and voluntary movement. It is organized into four major lobes, each with distinct functional specializations that are heavily tested on the EPPP.

Frontal Lobe

The frontal lobe occupies the anterior portion of each hemisphere, extending from the central sulcus posteriorly to the frontal pole anteriorly. It contains the primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus), which controls voluntary movement on the contralateral side of the body. The prefrontal cortex is responsible for executive functions including planning, working memory, impulse control, cognitive flexibility, and social judgment.

Broca's Area (Brodmann areas 44 & 45):

Located in the left inferior frontal gyrus, Broca's area is critical for speech production and language expression. Damage produces Broca's aphasia (expressive/nonfluent aphasia), characterized by effortful, telegraphic speech with preserved comprehension. Patients can understand language but struggle to produce it fluently.

Parietal Lobe

The parietal lobe lies posterior to the central sulcus and is bounded inferiorly by the lateral sulcus. It contains the primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus), which processes touch, pressure, temperature, and proprioception from the contralateral body. The angular gyrus (Brodmann area 39) plays a key role in reading and arithmetic; damage can produce alexia and anomia.

Temporal Lobe

The temporal lobe is located beneath the lateral sulcus and contains the primary auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus). It is essential for auditory processing, language comprehension, and memory formation (via the hippocampus).

Wernicke's Area (Brodmann area 22):

Located in the posterior superior temporal gyrus of the dominant hemisphere, Wernicke's area is responsible for language comprehension. Damage produces Wernicke's aphasia (receptive/fluent aphasia), characterized by fluent but meaningless speech (word salad) with severely impaired comprehension.

Occipital Lobe

The occipital lobe occupies the posterior pole of the brain and contains the primary visual cortex (V1, Brodmann area 17). It processes visual information from the contralateral visual field. Damage to V1 can produce cortical blindness, while damage to association areas can cause visual agnosia.

Cortical Organization: Motor & Sensory Maps

The motor and somatosensory cortices are organized somatotopically, meaning that adjacent body regions are represented in adjacent cortical regions. These representations are depicted by the cortical homunculus, a distorted figure in which body parts are sized in proportion to their cortical representation rather than their actual physical size.

Clinical Example:

On the motor homunculus, the hands, lips, and tongue occupy disproportionately large cortical territories compared to the trunk and legs. This reflects the fine motor control required for speech, facial expression, and manual dexterity. The EPPP may ask which body part has the largest cortical representation — the answer is the hands or face.

The association areas constitute the majority of the cortex and integrate information across sensory modalities. The prefrontal association cortex is involved in executive functioning, the parietal-temporal-occipital association area integrates multisensory information, and the limbic association cortex is involved in emotion and memory.

The Arcuate Fasciculus & Language Circuits

The arcuate fasciculus is a white matter tract that connects Broca's area in the frontal lobe with Wernicke's area in the temporal lobe. This fiber bundle is essential for the repetition of spoken language and the transfer of linguistic information between comprehension and production centers.

Conduction Aphasia:

Damage to the arcuate fasciculus produces conduction aphasia, characterized by fluent speech, intact comprehension, but severely impaired repetition. Patients can understand what is said and can produce spontaneous speech, but they cannot repeat words or phrases accurately. This is a high-yield distinction for the EPPP.

Geschwind, N. (1970). The organization of language and the brain. Science, 170(3961), 940-944. — Geschwind's disconnection model established the neuroanatomical basis for the aphasias and the role of the arcuate fasciculus in language.

Hemispheric Lateralization & Split-Brain Research

Hemispheric lateralization refers to the functional specialization of the two cerebral hemispheres. While both hemispheres contribute to most cognitive functions, certain abilities are predominantly associated with one hemisphere.

Left Hemisphere Specialization

  • Language production and comprehension (in ~95% of right-handers and ~70% of left-handers)
  • Analytical and sequential processing
  • Mathematical computation
  • Logical reasoning
  • Control of the right side of the body

Right Hemisphere Specialization

  • Visuospatial processing and spatial reasoning
  • Emotional prosody (tone of voice)
  • Face recognition
  • Holistic and gestalt processing
  • Musical perception
  • Control of the left side of the body
Split-Brain Studies (Sperry & Gazzaniga):

Roger Sperry's Nobel Prize-winning research on split-brain patients (who had undergone corpus callosotomy for epilepsy treatment) demonstrated that the two hemispheres can operate independently. When visual information was presented exclusively to the left visual field (right hemisphere), patients could not verbally name the object but could select it with their left hand. This confirmed that the left hemisphere is dominant for language, while the right hemisphere processes visuospatial information and can recognize objects non-verbally.

The corpus callosum is the largest white matter commissure in the brain, containing approximately 200 million axons. It connects homologous regions of the left and right hemispheres, enabling interhemispheric communication and the integration of information processed in each hemisphere.

Sperry, R. W. (1968). Hemisphere disconnection and unity in conscious awareness. American Psychologist, 23(10), 723-733.

Contralateral Control & Cerebral Dominance

A fundamental principle of brain organization is contralateral control: each hemisphere primarily controls and receives sensory input from the opposite side of the body. Motor fibers cross at the medullary pyramids (pyramidal decussation), and most sensory pathways cross either at the brainstem or spinal cord level.

EPPP Application:

A stroke affecting the right motor cortex will produce left-sided hemiparesis. A lesion in the left somatosensory cortex will impair sensation on the right side of the body. The EPPP frequently tests this principle by presenting lateralized symptoms and asking examinees to identify the hemisphere involved.

Cerebral dominance for language is overwhelmingly left-lateralized. Approximately 95% of right-handed individuals and 70% of left-handed individuals have left-hemisphere language dominance. The Wada test (intracarotid sodium amobarbital procedure) has historically been used to determine language lateralization prior to neurosurgery.

Apraxia, Agnosia & Other Higher Cortical Deficits

Damage to association cortices and their connections produces characteristic neuropsychological syndromes that are frequently tested on the EPPP.

Apraxia:

Apraxia is the inability to perform skilled, purposeful movements despite intact motor function, sensation, and comprehension. Ideomotor apraxia involves the inability to pantomime gestures on command (e.g., "Show me how you brush your teeth"), while ideational apraxia involves the inability to sequence complex motor acts. Apraxia typically results from left hemisphere damage, particularly the parietal lobe or supplementary motor area.

Agnosia:

Agnosia is the failure to recognize objects, persons, sounds, or stimuli despite intact sensory function. Visual agnosia (inability to recognize objects by sight) is associated with occipitotemporal damage. Prosopagnosia (inability to recognize familiar faces) involves the fusiform face area. Anosognosia (unawareness of deficit) is commonly seen after right parietal damage.

The Thalamus

The thalamus is a large, egg-shaped structure sitting atop the brainstem at the center of the brain. It serves as the brain's primary sensory relay station, routing incoming sensory information to the appropriate cortical areas for processing.

Key Thalamic Nuclei

  • Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN): Relays visual information from the optic tract to the primary visual cortex (V1)
  • Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN): Relays auditory information from the inferior colliculus to the primary auditory cortex
  • Ventral posterolateral (VPL) nucleus: Relays somatosensory information from the body to the primary somatosensory cortex
  • Ventral posteromedial (VPM) nucleus: Relays somatosensory information from the face
  • Dorsomedial nucleus: Connects to the prefrontal cortex; involved in memory and emotion. Damage is implicated in Korsakoff's syndrome.
  • Pulvinar: Involved in visual attention and filtering of visual information
High-Yield Fact:

Olfaction (smell) is the only sensory modality that does not relay through the thalamus. Olfactory information travels directly from the olfactory bulb to the piriform cortex and amygdala. This is a frequently tested fact on the EPPP.

EPPP Application:

The amnesia associated with Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (caused by chronic thiamine/vitamin B1 deficiency, typically from chronic alcoholism) is due to damage to the thalamus (specifically the dorsomedial nucleus) and the mammillary bodies. Intravenous administration of thiamine is the primary treatment for Wernicke's encephalopathy.

The Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus is a small structure (approximately the size of an almond) located below the thalamus. Despite its small size, it is critical for maintaining homeostasis and regulating the endocrine system through its connection with the pituitary gland.

The "Four F's" of the Hypothalamus

A classic mnemonic for the hypothalamus's primary regulatory functions:

  • Fighting (aggression): The ventromedial hypothalamus is involved in aggressive behavior. Electrical stimulation of the medial hypothalamus produces affective (emotional) attack behavior, while stimulation of the lateral hypothalamus produces predatory stalking attack behavior.
  • Fleeing (fear responses): Coordinates autonomic fear responses through connections with the amygdala and brainstem
  • Feeding: The lateral hypothalamus (LH) is the "hunger center" — stimulation induces eating, and lesions cause aphagia (refusal to eat). The ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) is the "satiety center" — stimulation inhibits eating, and lesions cause hyperphagia (overeating) and obesity.
  • Mating (sexual behavior): Regulates sexual behavior and reproductive functions; the sexually dimorphic nucleus differs in size between males and females
EPPP Mnemonic:

Lateral hypothalamus lesions → "Lateral Leanness" (aphagia/weight loss). Ventromedial hypothalamus lesions → "Ventromedial Voluminosity" (hyperphagia/obesity). These mnemonics appear frequently on EPPP study resources and the exam itself.

Other Hypothalamic Functions

  • Thermoregulation: Maintains body temperature through sweating, shivering, and vasoconstriction/vasodilation
  • Circadian rhythms: The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) serves as the master biological clock, entrained by light input from the retina. It controls the sleep-wake cycle by triggering changes in melatonin release by the pineal gland.
  • Endocrine control: Regulates the pituitary gland ("master gland") through releasing and inhibiting hormones. Controls the HPA axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis) for stress responses.
  • Thirst and water balance: Osmoreceptors detect blood osmolarity changes and trigger thirst and ADH release
  • Oxytocin production: The hypothalamus produces oxytocin, which stimulates milk ejection during breastfeeding and plays roles in social bonding. Research shows that increasing oxytocin levels in healthy adults can cause them to become overly sensitive to emotional facial expressions of others.

The Limbic System

The limbic system is a collection of interconnected structures surrounding the thalamus that mediate emotion, motivation, learning, and memory. While the exact boundaries are debated, the following structures are consistently included:

Hippocampus

A seahorse-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe that is essential for the consolidation of new declarative (explicit) memories — the transfer of information from short-term to long-term storage.

  • Bilateral hippocampal damage produces severe anterograde amnesia for declarative information (the case of patient H.M.)
  • Contains place cells (O'Keefe, Nobel Prize 2014) that fire when an animal occupies a specific location, forming cognitive maps for spatial navigation
  • One of only two brain regions where adult neurogenesis has been confirmed (the other is the olfactory bulb/subventricular zone)
  • Particularly vulnerable to damage from chronic stress — stress-induced increases in cortisol are neurotoxic to hippocampal neurons and have been linked to impairments in declarative memory retrieval
  • Neuroimaging studies have linked PTSD to a reduced hippocampal volume, decreased activity in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, and increased activity in the amygdala

Amygdala

An almond-shaped structure anterior to the hippocampus, the amygdala is the brain's "fear center" and is critical for emotional processing, particularly fear conditioning and threat detection.

  • Receives rapid, coarse sensory input via a direct pathway from the thalamus (LeDoux's "low road"), enabling fast fear responses before cortical processing is complete
  • Also receives slower, detailed input from the cortex ("high road") for more nuanced evaluation
  • The consolidation of emotional memories is mediated primarily by the amygdala — amygdala activation enhances hippocampal encoding of emotionally arousing events
  • People with bilateral damage to the amygdala often have difficulty recognizing negative emotions in facial expressions, especially fear
  • Electrical stimulation of certain areas of the amygdala elicits conditioned fear responses: freezing, increased heart rate and blood pressure, and release of stress hormones
Klüver-Bucy Syndrome:

Bilateral lesioning of the amygdala, hippocampus, and temporal lobes (Klüver & Bucy, 1938) produces "psychic blindness" — visual agnosia, reduced fear and aggression, increased docility, dietary changes (hyperorality), and hypersexuality. This syndrome demonstrates the amygdala's critical role in recognizing the emotional significance of stimuli.

Cingulate Cortex

The cingulate gyrus wraps around the corpus callosum and is divided into functionally distinct regions:

  • Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC): Involved in error detection, conflict monitoring, pain perception, and emotional regulation. A meta-analysis of neuroimaging studies found that psychotherapy for major depressive disorder increased activity in the cingulate cortex.
  • Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC): Part of the default mode network; involved in self-referential processing and autobiographical memory. One of the first regions affected in Alzheimer's disease.

Other Limbic Structures

  • Mammillary bodies: Part of the Papez circuit (which plays a fundamental role in emotions and memory). Relay information from the hippocampus to the thalamus. Degeneration occurs in Korsakoff's syndrome and contributes to anterograde amnesia and confabulation.
  • Fornix: Major white matter tract connecting the hippocampus to the mammillary bodies and septal nuclei
  • Septal nuclei: Involved in pleasure, reward, and the inhibition of aggression

Basal Ganglia

The basal ganglia are a group of subcortical nuclei involved in the initiation, execution, and modulation of voluntary movement, as well as procedural learning and habit formation. A number of disorders have been linked to abnormalities in these structures, including Tourette's disorder, ADHD, and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).

Components

  • Caudate nucleus: Involved in cognitive control of movement, goal-directed behavior, and procedural memory
  • Putamen: Primary motor component; involved in the initiation and execution of movement. Together with the caudate, forms the striatum (so named for its striped appearance). Areas of the cerebral cortex involved in planning and execution of movement send excitatory signals to the striatum.
  • Globus pallidus: Output nucleus of the basal ganglia; sends inhibitory signals to the thalamus to regulate motor activity
  • Subthalamic nucleus: Regulates the globus pallidus; lesions can cause hemiballismus (violent flinging movements of contralateral limbs). Deep brain stimulation here can reduce Parkinsonian symptoms.
  • Substantia nigra: Located in the midbrain; its pars compacta produces dopamine sent to the striatum via the nigrostriatal pathway. Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons here is the primary pathology of Parkinson's disease.
Direct vs. Indirect Pathways:

The direct pathway through the basal ganglia facilitates movement (excitatory net effect), while the indirect pathway inhibits movement (inhibitory net effect). Parkinson's disease (too little dopamine) → difficulty initiating movement (hypokinetic). Huntington's disease (loss of GABAergic inhibitory neurons in the caudate and putamen) → excessive, uncontrolled chorea (hyperkinetic).

EPPP Application:

Research has found that people with OCD have increased activity in the basal ganglia. High scores on the Intolerance of Uncertainty Scale have been associated with increased volume of the striatum. These findings highlight the basal ganglia's role beyond motor function — in cognitive and emotional regulation.

The Brainstem

The brainstem connects the cerebral hemispheres to the spinal cord and controls vital autonomic functions. It consists of three major divisions, arranged from superior to inferior:

Midbrain (Mesencephalon)

  • Superior colliculi: Visual reflexes and orienting responses (e.g., turning the head toward a visual stimulus)
  • Inferior colliculi: Auditory reflexes and relay of auditory information to the thalamus
  • Substantia nigra: Dopamine production for motor control (part of basal ganglia circuitry); degeneration causes Parkinson's disease
  • Ventral tegmental area (VTA): Dopamine production for the mesolimbic reward pathway (projects to nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex); critical for reward, motivation, and addiction
  • Periaqueductal gray (PAG): Involved in pain modulation and defensive behaviors

Pons

  • Relays information between the cerebellum and cerebral cortex
  • Contains nuclei for cranial nerves V (trigeminal), VI (abducens), VII (facial), and VIII (vestibulocochlear)
  • Contains the locus coeruleus, the brain's primary source of norepinephrine, involved in arousal, attention, and the stress response. The locus coeruleus has also been identified as "ground zero" for Alzheimer's disease.
  • Involved in regulating sleep, particularly REM sleep transitions

Medulla Oblongata

The medulla oblongata is part of the hindbrain and controls vital autonomic functions. Damage to the medulla can result in death because it regulates breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and digestive reflexes.

  • Controls respiration — an overdose of morphine, heroin, or other opioids can cause respiratory failure because of the drug's effects on opioid receptors in the medulla
  • Contains the raphe nuclei, the brain's primary source of serotonin, involved in mood regulation and sleep
  • Site of the pyramidal decussation, where motor fibers cross from one side to the other (explaining contralateral motor control)
High-Yield Fact:

Opioid-induced respiratory depression is due to the effects of opiates on receptors in the medulla. This is the mechanism by which opioid overdose kills — by suppressing the brainstem's respiratory drive. This is a frequently tested concept linking pharmacology to neuroanatomy.

The Cerebellum

The cerebellum ("little brain") is located posterior to the brainstem and contains more neurons than the rest of the brain combined. Although comprising only 10% of brain volume, it contains approximately 50% of the brain's neurons.

  • Motor coordination: Fine-tunes motor output for smooth, coordinated, accurate movements
  • Balance and posture: Integrates vestibular input to maintain equilibrium
  • Motor learning: Essential for acquiring skilled movements and classical conditioning of motor responses (e.g., eyeblink conditioning). Implicit memories (procedural memory and conditioned responses) are stored in the cerebellum and basal ganglia.
  • Timing: Internal clock for the precise timing of movements and cognitive operations
  • Cognitive functions: Increasingly recognized role in language, attention, and executive function
Cerebellar Damage:

Ataxia is the most common outcome of damage to the cerebellum — a loss of coordination that can also be caused by abnormalities in the sensory or vestibular system. Other symptoms include dysarthria (slurred speech), intention tremor (worsens as the hand approaches a target, in contrast to Parkinson's resting tremor), nystagmus (involuntary eye movements), and gait instability. Importantly, cerebellar damage does NOT cause paralysis.

EPPP Application:

A 17-year-old who exhibits clumsiness, slurred speech, and symptoms resembling alcohol intoxication after a skateboarding accident most likely has damage to the cerebellum. Note that Gerstmann's syndrome (finger agnosia, left-right confusion, agraphia, acalculia) is caused by damage to the dominant parietal lobe, not the cerebellum — ataxia is the hallmark of cerebellar damage.

Reticular Activating System (RAS)

The reticular activating system is a network of neurons extending through the brainstem core from the medulla to the midbrain. It plays a critical role in regulating consciousness, arousal, and the sleep-wake cycle.

  • Filters incoming sensory information, determining what reaches conscious awareness
  • Maintains cortical alertness through diffuse projections to the thalamus and cortex
  • Electrical stimulation of the ascending RAS will wake up a sleeping person and cause an awake person to become more alert
  • Damage to the RAS can produce coma or persistent vegetative state
  • Anesthetic agents exert their effects partly by suppressing RAS activity
Clinical Significance:

The RAS is clinically important for understanding disorders of consciousness. Coma results from bilateral cortical damage or damage to the RAS. Damage to specific brainstem regions can produce "locked-in syndrome," where patients are conscious but unable to move or communicate except through eye movements.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

Cortical Structures

  • Broca's area = left inferior frontal gyrus = expressive/nonfluent aphasia; Wernicke's area = left posterior superior temporal gyrus = receptive/fluent aphasia
  • The arcuate fasciculus connects Broca's and Wernicke's areas; damage causes conduction aphasia (impaired repetition with intact fluency and comprehension)
  • Prefrontal cortex mediates executive functions: planning, working memory, impulse control, and cognitive flexibility. Orbitofrontal damage → impulsivity, emotion dysregulation, lack of empathy
  • Frontal lobe damage causes perseveration (repetition of inappropriate responses) and impairs motivation and judgment but often does NOT reduce IQ scores
  • Contralateral control: each hemisphere controls the opposite side of the body; motor fibers cross at the medullary pyramids
  • Left hemisphere dominance for language in ~95% of right-handers and ~70% of left-handers; right hemisphere specializes in visuospatial processing, emotional prosody, and holistic processing
  • Split-brain research (Sperry & Gazzaniga): severing the corpus callosum demonstrates independent hemispheric function; dichotic listening task also reveals hemispheric specialization
  • The cortical homunculus shows disproportionate representation for hands, face, and tongue
  • Angular gyrus damage → Gerstmann's syndrome (finger agnosia, acalculia, agraphia, left-right confusion)
  • Apraxia = inability to perform skilled movements despite intact motor function; Agnosia = failure to recognize stimuli despite intact sensory function
  • Anosognosia and hemispatial neglect are most commonly associated with right parietal lobe damage

Subcortical Structures

  • Thalamus = sensory relay for ALL senses except olfaction; dorsomedial nucleus damage → Korsakoff's amnesia
  • Hypothalamus = homeostasis + "Four F's"; lateral = hunger center (lesion → aphagia); ventromedial = satiety center (lesion → hyperphagia); SCN = master circadian clock
  • Hippocampus = declarative memory consolidation; bilateral damage → anterograde amnesia (H.M.); vulnerable to cortisol/stress; reduced volume in PTSD
  • Amygdala = fear conditioning and emotional memory; bilateral damage → Klüver-Bucy syndrome; LeDoux's "low road" (thalamus → amygdala) enables rapid fear responses
  • Basal ganglia = voluntary movement + procedural learning; Parkinson's = substantia nigra dopamine loss (hypokinetic); Huntington's = caudate/putamen degeneration (hyperkinetic); OCD linked to increased basal ganglia activity
  • Cerebellum = motor coordination, balance, timing; damage → ataxia, dysarthria, intention tremor (NOT paralysis); stores implicit/procedural memories
  • Medulla oblongata = breathing, heart rate, blood pressure; opioid overdose → respiratory failure via medullary receptors; site of pyramidal decussation
  • Reticular activating system = arousal and consciousness; stimulation increases alertness; damage → coma
  • Locus coeruleus (pons) = primary norepinephrine source; Raphe nuclei (medulla) = primary serotonin source
  • Oxytocin is produced by the hypothalamus; stimulates milk ejection; increased levels → heightened sensitivity to emotional facial expressions
