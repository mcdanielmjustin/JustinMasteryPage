Emotion Theories, Arousal & the ANS

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read

Classic Theories of Emotion

The relationship between physiological arousal, cognitive appraisal, and subjective emotional experience has been debated for over a century. Four major theories of emotion are heavily tested on the EPPP, and understanding their differences is essential.

TheoryKey Theorist(s)Core ClaimSequence
James-LangeWilliam James, Carl LangeEmotional experience follows physiological arousalStimulus → Body response → Emotion (we feel afraid because we run)
Cannon-BardWalter Cannon, Philip BardPhysiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneouslyStimulus → Thalamus → Simultaneous body response + subjective emotion
Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor)Stanley Schachter, Jerome SingerEmotion requires both physiological arousal AND cognitive labeling of that arousalStimulus → Arousal → Cognitive interpretation → Emotion
Lazarus (Cognitive Appraisal)Richard LazarusCognitive appraisal MUST precede emotional experience; appraisal determines the emotionStimulus → Cognitive appraisal → Emotion + body response
James-Lange Theory:

The James-Lange theory proposes that emotions are the subjective experience of physiological changes in the body. We do not cry because we feel sad; we feel sad because we cry. Each emotion is associated with a distinct pattern of physiological arousal. Cannon criticized this theory by arguing that (1) visceral changes are too slow to account for the rapidity of emotional experience, (2) artificially induced physiological arousal does not produce genuine emotion, and (3) the same visceral changes accompany different emotions, making it impossible to distinguish emotions based on physiology alone.

Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory:

The two-factor theory proposes that emotional experience requires two components: (1) physiological arousal and (2) a cognitive label for that arousal. In the classic "bridge study" by Dutton and Aron (1974), men who crossed a fear-inducing suspension bridge were more likely to misattribute their physiological arousal (heart racing, sweaty palms) as romantic attraction to a female experimenter than men who crossed a low, stable bridge. This demonstrates misattribution of arousal — the same physiological state can be interpreted as different emotions depending on the situational context.

Schachter, S., & Singer, J. (1962). Cognitive, social, and physiological determinants of emotional state. Psychological Review, 69(5), 379-399.

Facial Feedback & the Yerkes-Dodson Law

Facial Feedback Hypothesis

The facial feedback hypothesis (Tomkins, 1962; Ekman, 1993) proposes that facial expressions are not merely the result of emotions but can actually influence emotional experience. When individuals are asked to hold a pen between their teeth (which mimics a smile), they rate cartoons as funnier compared to when the pen is held between their lips (which inhibits smiling). This provides partial support for the James-Lange theory — bodily states (including facial expressions) can modulate subjective emotional experience.

Yerkes-Dodson Law

The Yerkes-Dodson law describes the relationship between arousal and performance as an inverted-U function. Performance on any task improves with increasing arousal up to an optimal point, after which further increases in arousal lead to performance deterioration. The critical nuance for the EPPP is that the optimal arousal level depends on task complexity:

  • Simple or well-practiced tasks: Higher arousal levels are optimal (the curve peaks later)
  • Complex or novel tasks: Lower arousal levels are optimal (the curve peaks earlier)
EPPP Application:

A professional basketball player may perform free throws better under moderate game-day arousal than during a calm practice session (simple, well-practiced task benefits from moderate-to-high arousal). However, a student learning a new, complex statistical technique would perform best under conditions of low-to-moderate arousal. High test anxiety (excessive arousal) would impair performance on the complex task. This distinction is frequently tested.

Ekman's Basic Emotions & Universal Facial Expressions

While the theories above address the mechanism of emotional experience, Paul Ekman's research focused on the question: what emotions exist and how universal are they? Ekman's program of cross-cultural research, spanning decades, established that certain emotions are biologically based and universally recognized — a finding with major implications for both clinical assessment and forensic psychology.

The Six Universal Emotions

Ekman (1972, 1992) identified six basic emotions that are universally produced and recognized across cultures through facial expressions:

  • Happiness (Duchenne smile — involves both the zygomatic major and orbicularis oculi muscles)
  • Sadness
  • Anger
  • Fear
  • Disgust
  • Surprise
Cross-Cultural Evidence:

Ekman and Friesen (1971) studied the Fore people of Papua New Guinea, a preliterate culture with minimal exposure to Western media. Participants were able to correctly match facial expressions to emotion-provoking stories at rates significantly above chance, providing strong evidence that basic emotional expressions are not culturally learned but are innate, universal products of human evolution. These findings have been replicated across dozens of cultures worldwide.

The Facial Action Coding System (FACS)

Ekman and Friesen (1978) developed the Facial Action Coding System (FACS), an anatomically based system that identifies and codes every visually distinguishable facial movement in terms of action units (AUs) — individual muscle movements. FACS allows researchers and clinicians to objectively measure facial expressions without relying on subjective judgment. For example, a genuine (Duchenne) smile involves AU 6 (cheek raiser) + AU 12 (lip corner puller), while a social smile involves only AU 12.

Microexpressions

Microexpressions are brief, involuntary facial expressions lasting approximately 1/25th to 1/5th of a second that occur when a person is consciously or unconsciously attempting to conceal or suppress an emotion. Unlike deliberate expressions, microexpressions reveal the person's true emotional state and are extremely difficult to fake or suppress because they are driven by the involuntary facial musculature. Research on microexpressions has been applied in law enforcement, security screening, and clinical settings, though the practical accuracy of microexpression detection in real-world deception remains debated.

Display Rules

Display Rules:

Display rules are culturally specific norms that dictate how, when, and to whom emotions should be expressed. While basic emotions and their associated facial expressions are universal, cultures differ in the rules governing their display. Ekman identified several types of display rules: intensification (exaggerating the expression), de-intensification (minimizing the expression), masking (replacing one expression with another), and neutralization (suppressing the expression entirely). For example, Japanese participants in Ekman's studies displayed the same facial expressions as Americans when watching emotional films alone, but suppressed negative expressions when in the presence of an authority figure — demonstrating that cultural display rules modulate the expression of emotion without altering the underlying emotional experience.

Expanded List & Dimensional Models

Ekman later expanded his list of basic emotions to include contempt (the only additional emotion with a universally recognized facial expression), as well as self-conscious emotions such as embarrassment, shame, guilt, and pride, and other states like amusement, awe, and excitement. However, these expanded emotions lack the same degree of cross-cultural evidence for unique, universally recognized facial expressions.

Categorical vs. Dimensional Models:

Ekman's approach is a categorical (discrete) model of emotion — each basic emotion is a distinct, qualitatively different state with its own neural signature and facial expression. This contrasts with dimensional models, most notably Russell's (1980) circumplex model, which characterizes all emotions along two continuous dimensions: valence (pleasant to unpleasant) and arousal (high activation to low activation). In the dimensional view, "anger" and "fear" are both high-arousal, negative-valence states differentiated by their position in two-dimensional space rather than by categorical boundaries. The EPPP may test the distinction between these approaches. Both contribute valid perspectives: categorical models explain the universal recognition of distinct facial expressions, while dimensional models better capture the continuous, blended nature of everyday emotional experience.

EPPP Application:

An exam question might describe a researcher studying emotional recognition across cultures and ask which theory is supported. If the question emphasizes that people from isolated cultures recognize the same facial expressions, the answer is Ekman's basic emotion theory. If the question describes emotions mapped along axes of pleasantness and intensity, the answer involves the dimensional/circumplex model. Note also that bilateral amygdala damage impairs recognition of fear expressions specifically (as in Patient S.M.), connecting Ekman's work to neuroanatomy.

Ekman, P. (1972). Universals and cultural differences in facial expressions of emotion. In J. Cole (Ed.), Nebraska Symposium on Motivation (Vol. 19, pp. 207-283). Lincoln: University of Nebraska Press.
Ekman, P., & Friesen, W. V. (1971). Constants across cultures in the face and emotion. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 17(2), 124-129.
Russell, J. A. (1980). A circumplex model of affect. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 39(6), 1161-1178.

The Autonomic Nervous System

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) regulates involuntary bodily functions and is divided into two complementary branches that work in dynamic balance.

FeatureSympathetic DivisionParasympathetic Division
General FunctionFight-or-flight; energy mobilizationRest-and-digest; energy conservation
Heart RateIncreasesDecreases
PupilsDilate (mydriasis)Constrict (miosis)
BronchiDilate (more air)Constrict
DigestionInhibitedStimulated
BladderRelaxed (inhibits urination)Contracted (promotes urination)

NeurotransmitterNorepinephrine (postganglionic)Acetylcholine (postganglionic)

Clinical Relevance:

Understanding the ANS is critical for psychopharmacology. Anticholinergic side effects of many psychiatric medications (dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention) result from blockade of parasympathetic (cholinergic) activity. Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) reduce sympathetic activation and are sometimes used off-label for performance anxiety. Biofeedback and relaxation training aim to shift the balance from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance. The vagus nerve (cranial nerve X) is the primary parasympathetic nerve and is targeted in vagus nerve stimulation therapy for treatment-resistant depression.

The Limbic System & Emotional Brain

The limbic system is a collection of interconnected structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and olfaction. Key structures for the EPPP include:

  • Amygdala: Central to fear conditioning, threat detection, and the emotional modulation of memory. Bilateral amygdala damage (as in Kluver-Bucy syndrome) produces placidity, hyperorality, visual agnosia, and altered emotional responses. The amygdala is hyperactive in PTSD and anxiety disorders.
  • Hippocampus: Critical for declarative memory formation; also involved in contextual fear conditioning and stress regulation. Chronic stress and elevated cortisol cause hippocampal atrophy.
  • Hypothalamus: Regulates the autonomic nervous system, endocrine system (via the pituitary), and homeostatic drives (hunger, thirst, temperature, sexual behavior). Contains the suprachiasmatic nucleus (circadian rhythms) and releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) to activate the HPA axis.
  • Cingulate cortex: The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) is involved in error detection, conflict monitoring, and the affective component of pain. It connects emotional processing to executive control.

The HPA Axis & the General Adaptation Syndrome

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the primary neuroendocrine stress response system. When a stressor is perceived, the hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), which stimulates the anterior pituitary to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), which in turn stimulates the adrenal cortex to release cortisol. Cortisol mobilizes energy, suppresses inflammation, and inhibits non-essential functions. Negative feedback from cortisol normally shuts down the axis, but chronic stress can dysregulate this feedback loop.

General Adaptation Syndrome (Hans Selye):

Selye's (1956) General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) describes the body's three-stage response to prolonged stress:

1. Alarm reaction: The body mobilizes resources via sympathetic activation and HPA axis response (fight-or-flight). Resistance to stress is initially lowered.

2. Resistance (adaptation): The body adapts to the continued presence of the stressor. Cortisol levels remain elevated, parasympathetic activity partially returns, and the organism appears to cope. However, physiological resources are being depleted.

3. Exhaustion: If the stressor persists, the body's adaptive resources become depleted. Immune function deteriorates, susceptibility to illness increases, and organ damage or death may occur. This stage is associated with stress-related diseases (cardiovascular disease, immune suppression, depression).

Cortisol & Cognition:

Chronic elevation of cortisol has deleterious effects on the brain, particularly the hippocampus (which has a high density of glucocorticoid receptors). Chronic stress-related cortisol elevation is associated with hippocampal volume reduction, impaired declarative memory, and increased risk for depression. This is clinically relevant in Cushing's syndrome (cortisol excess) and is one mechanism linking chronic stress to cognitive decline in older adults.

Selye, H. (1956). The Stress of Life. New York: McGraw-Hill. — Selye's foundational work introduced the concept of the general adaptation syndrome and defined stress as a nonspecific response of the body to any demand.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • James-Lange: body response comes first, then emotion ("I'm afraid because my heart is racing")
  • Cannon-Bard: body response and emotion occur simultaneously; mediated by thalamus
  • Schachter-Singer (two-factor): emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label; demonstrates misattribution of arousal
  • Lazarus: cognitive appraisal must come before emotion; appraisal determines which emotion is experienced
  • Facial feedback hypothesis: facial expressions can influence (not just reflect) emotional experience
  • Yerkes-Dodson law: inverted-U relationship between arousal and performance; optimal arousal is LOWER for complex tasks, HIGHER for simple tasks
  • Sympathetic NS: fight-or-flight (norepinephrine); Parasympathetic NS: rest-and-digest (acetylcholine)
  • Amygdala: fear conditioning, threat detection; hyperactive in PTSD; Kluver-Bucy syndrome from bilateral damage
  • HPA axis: hypothalamus (CRH) → pituitary (ACTH) → adrenal cortex (cortisol); chronic elevation harms hippocampus
  • General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye): alarm → resistance → exhaustion
  • Vagus nerve (CN X): primary parasympathetic nerve; targeted in vagus nerve stimulation for depression
  • Anticholinergic side effects (dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention) result from parasympathetic blockade
  • Ekman's 6 basic emotions: happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, surprise — universally recognized across cultures; later added contempt
  • FACS (Facial Action Coding System): objective coding of facial expressions using anatomically defined action units (AUs); Duchenne smile = AU 6 + AU 12
  • Microexpressions: brief involuntary facial expressions (1/25th to 1/5th second) that reveal concealed emotions
  • Display rules: culturally learned norms governing emotional expression (intensification, de-intensification, masking, neutralization); emotion itself is universal, expression is culturally modulated
  • Categorical vs. dimensional models: Ekman = discrete basic emotions; Russell's circumplex model = valence (pleasant-unpleasant) x arousal (high-low) dimensions
