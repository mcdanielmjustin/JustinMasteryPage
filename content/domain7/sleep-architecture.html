Sleep Architecture, Dreaming & the Lifespan

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read

Sleep Stages & EEG Patterns

Sleep is organized into a cyclic architecture of NREM (non-rapid eye movement) and REM (rapid eye movement) stages, each with characteristic electroencephalographic (EEG) signatures. A complete sleep cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes, with 4-6 cycles occurring per night.

Sleep Spindles & K-Complexes:

Sleep spindles are brief bursts of 12-14 Hz activity generated by the thalamus during NREM Stage 2. They are believed to play a role in memory consolidation and protecting sleep from external disturbances. K-complexes are large, sharp waveforms that also appear in Stage 2; they are thought to represent cortical responses to external stimuli and may function in sleep maintenance.

As the night progresses, NREM Stage 3 (slow-wave sleep) decreases while REM periods become progressively longer. Most slow-wave sleep occurs in the first third of the night, while most REM sleep occurs in the last third. This distribution has important implications: disruptions to early-night sleep primarily affect slow-wave sleep (and declarative memory consolidation), while disruptions to late-night sleep primarily affect REM sleep (and procedural/emotional memory processing).

Circadian Rhythms & the Suprachiasmatic Nucleus

Circadian rhythms are endogenous biological cycles with a period of approximately 24 hours that regulate sleep-wake cycles, hormone secretion, body temperature, and other physiological processes. The master circadian clock is the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), located in the anterior hypothalamus directly above the optic chiasm.

The SCN receives light information directly from the retina via the retinohypothalamic tract (using specialized melanopsin-containing retinal ganglion cells), allowing it to synchronize the internal clock with the external light-dark cycle. The SCN projects to the pineal gland, which secretes melatonin during darkness. Melatonin promotes sleep onset and is suppressed by light exposure, particularly blue-wavelength light.

Clinical Application:

Exogenous melatonin is used therapeutically for circadian rhythm disorders, jet lag, and as an adjunct for insomnia. The timing of administration is critical: melatonin given in the evening advances the circadian phase (useful for delayed sleep-wake phase disorder), while melatonin given in the morning delays the phase. Blue-light-blocking glasses in the evening can also advance sleep onset by preventing melatonin suppression.

Sleep Disorders

Insomnia

Insomnia disorder involves difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep, or early morning awakening, at least three nights per week for at least three months, causing significant distress or functional impairment. Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line treatment and includes stimulus control, sleep restriction, sleep hygiene education, cognitive restructuring, and relaxation training.

Narcolepsy

Narcolepsy is characterized by excessive daytime sleepiness and may include cataplexy (sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by strong emotions), hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations, and sleep paralysis. Type 1 narcolepsy involves deficiency of hypocretin (orexin), a neuropeptide produced by the lateral hypothalamus that promotes wakefulness. The loss of hypocretin-producing neurons is believed to result from autoimmune destruction.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) involves repeated episodes of upper airway collapse during sleep, producing apneas (cessation of breathing for 10+ seconds) or hypopneas (partial airway obstruction). OSA disrupts sleep architecture, reduces oxygen saturation, and is associated with cardiovascular disease, cognitive impairment, and excessive daytime sleepiness. Treatment includes CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure).

Parasomnias

NREM Parasomnias (Disorders of Arousal):

Sleepwalking (somnambulism) and night terrors (sleep terrors) occur during NREM Stage 3 (slow-wave sleep), typically in the first third of the night. During night terrors, the individual may sit up screaming with autonomic arousal (tachycardia, sweating) but has no memory of the event. These parasomnias are most common in children and usually resolve with maturation. They should be distinguished from nightmares, which occur during REM sleep.

REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD):

REM sleep behavior disorder involves loss of the normal muscle atonia during REM sleep, allowing individuals to physically act out their dreams. RBD is a strong prodromal marker for synucleinopathies — a significant percentage of patients with RBD eventually develop Parkinson's disease or Lewy body dementia. This association is high-yield for the EPPP.

Theories of Dreaming

Three major theories of dreaming are tested on the EPPP:

  • Freud's wish-fulfillment theory: Dreams represent disguised expressions of unconscious desires. The manifest content (the dream as remembered) conceals the latent content (the underlying unconscious meaning) through dream work processes including condensation, displacement, and symbolization.
  • Activation-synthesis theory (Hobson & McCarley, 1977): Dreams result from the brain's attempt to make sense of random neural activation originating in the pons during REM sleep. The cortex synthesizes a narrative (the dream) from this essentially random brainstem activity. Dreams have no inherent meaning; they are the brain's "best guess" at interpreting noise.
  • Information-processing theory: Dreams serve a functional role in consolidating memories, processing emotional experiences, and integrating new information with existing knowledge. This theory is supported by evidence that REM sleep facilitates procedural learning and emotional memory processing.
Hobson, J. A., & McCarley, R. W. (1977). The brain as a dream state generator: An activation-synthesis hypothesis of the dream process. American Journal of Psychiatry, 134(12), 1335-1348.

Sleep Across the Lifespan & Deprivation Effects

Sleep architecture changes dramatically across development:

  • Newborns: Sleep 16-18 hours/day; approximately 50% REM sleep (called "active sleep"); polyphasic sleep pattern
  • Children: REM proportion decreases to ~25%; high amounts of slow-wave sleep; NREM parasomnias peak
  • Adolescents: Circadian phase delay (natural tendency toward later bedtime and waking); need ~8-10 hours
  • Older adults: Decreased total sleep time; reduced slow-wave sleep and sleep efficiency; increased nighttime awakenings; advanced circadian phase (earlier bedtime and wake time)
Sleep Deprivation Effects:

Chronic sleep deprivation impairs attention, working memory, decision-making, emotional regulation, and immune function. Sleep-deprived individuals show increased activity in the amygdala and decreased prefrontal cortex regulation, leading to heightened emotional reactivity. Severe prolonged sleep deprivation can produce perceptual disturbances, paranoia, and psychotic-like symptoms. Growth hormone, which is primarily secreted during slow-wave sleep, is also affected — relevant for pediatric growth and recovery from illness.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Sleep cycles last ~90 minutes; NREM 3 (slow-wave) dominates early night; REM dominates late night
  • EEG patterns: alpha (relaxed waking), theta (N1), sleep spindles + K-complexes (N2), delta (N3), beta-like desynchronized (REM)
  • SCN in the hypothalamus = master circadian clock; synchronized by light via retinohypothalamic tract
  • Melatonin: secreted by pineal gland during darkness; promotes sleep onset
  • Narcolepsy Type 1: deficiency of hypocretin/orexin (lateral hypothalamus); features cataplexy, sleep paralysis, hypnagogic hallucinations
  • Night terrors = NREM Stage 3 (first third of night); Nightmares = REM sleep (last third of night)
  • REM sleep behavior disorder: loss of muscle atonia during REM; strong predictor of Parkinson's and Lewy body dementia
  • CBT-I is the first-line treatment for insomnia disorder (not pharmacotherapy)
  • Freud: manifest vs. latent content; Activation-synthesis: random brainstem activation interpreted by cortex; Information-processing: memory consolidation function
  • Newborns: ~50% REM sleep; proportion decreases with age; Older adults: decreased slow-wave sleep and sleep efficiency
