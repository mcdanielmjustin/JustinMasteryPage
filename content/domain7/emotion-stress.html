Overview of Emotion and Stress

Emotion and stress are among the most clinically relevant topics in biological psychology. Understanding the biological mechanisms underlying emotional experience and the stress response is essential for EPPP preparation, as these processes are implicated in numerous psychological disorders, health conditions, and therapeutic interventions.

Emotion: A complex psychological state involving three distinct components: a subjective experience (feeling), a physiological response (autonomic arousal), and a behavioral or expressive response (facial expression, action tendency). Theories differ on the causal ordering of these components.

Theories of Emotion

Several major theories attempt to explain the relationship between physiological arousal, cognitive appraisal, and subjective emotional experience. These theories are frequently tested on the EPPP.

James-Lange Theory (1884/1885)

William James and Carl Lange independently proposed that emotions result from the perception of bodily changes. According to this theory, a stimulus triggers a physiological response, and the conscious experience of emotion follows from the perception of that response.

Sequence: Stimulus --> Physiological arousal/bodily response --> Perception of arousal --> Emotional experience. "We feel sorry because we cry, angry because we strike, afraid because we tremble" (James, 1884).

  • Implication: Different emotions should produce distinct patterns of physiological arousal
  • Supporting evidence: Some research shows that different emotions do involve partially distinct autonomic patterns (Levenson, Ekman, & Friesen, 1990)
  • Criticism: Cannon argued that visceral changes are too slow and too undifferentiated to account for the rapid and varied nature of emotional experience

Cannon-Bard Theory (1927)

Walter Cannon and Philip Bard proposed that emotional experience and physiological arousal occur simultaneously and independently, both triggered by the thalamus in response to an emotion-provoking stimulus.

Sequence: Stimulus --> Thalamus sends signals simultaneously to the cortex (emotional experience) and the body (physiological arousal). The two processes are parallel and independent.

  • Key claim: Physiological arousal does not cause emotion; both are products of the same neural event
  • Supporting evidence: Animals with severed connections between the brain and viscera still show emotional behavior; people with spinal cord injuries still experience emotions

Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory (1962)

Stanley Schachter and Jerome Singer proposed that emotion requires both physiological arousal AND cognitive interpretation (labeling) of that arousal. Arousal provides the intensity of the emotion, while cognition determines which emotion is experienced.

Sequence: Stimulus --> Physiological arousal --> Cognitive interpretation of the arousal based on context --> Emotional experience.

  • Classic experiment: Participants injected with epinephrine (causing arousal) who were not informed of the drug's effects labeled their arousal based on social context -- reporting happiness when with a euphoric confederate and anger when with an angry confederate
  • Misattribution of arousal: People may misattribute physiological arousal from one source to another (e.g., Dutton & Aron's [1974] bridge study, where men crossing a fear-inducing suspension bridge were more attracted to a female interviewer)

Other Important Emotion Theories

Lazarus's Cognitive Appraisal Theory (1991)

Richard Lazarus argued that cognitive appraisal is a necessary precondition for emotion. We first evaluate whether a stimulus is relevant and whether we have the resources to cope, and this appraisal determines both the emotion and the physiological response.

Zajonc's Affective Primacy Hypothesis (1984)

Robert Zajonc countered Lazarus by arguing that emotional reactions can occur before and independent of cognition. Simple emotional preferences (e.g., the mere exposure effect) can occur without conscious cognitive processing.

LeDoux's Dual-Pathway Model (1996)

Joseph LeDoux demonstrated that fear processing involves two neural pathways from the thalamus to the amygdala:

  • "Low road" (thalamo-amygdala pathway): Fast, direct, and crude processing. Enables rapid fear responses before conscious awareness.
  • "High road" (thalamo-cortical-amygdala pathway): Slower but more detailed cortical processing. Allows for more nuanced evaluation of threats.

The Autonomic Nervous System and Emotion

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) is the division of the peripheral nervous system that controls involuntary bodily functions and mediates the physiological component of emotional responses.

Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS)

The "fight-or-flight" system, activated during stress, danger, or emotional arousal:

  • Increases heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration
  • Dilates pupils and bronchioles
  • Redirects blood from digestive organs to skeletal muscles
  • Stimulates the adrenal medulla to release epinephrine and norepinephrine
  • Inhibits digestion and salivation
  • Uses norepinephrine as the primary postganglionic neurotransmitter

Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS)

The "rest-and-digest" system, promoting calm, recovery, and energy conservation:

  • Decreases heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration
  • Constricts pupils and bronchioles
  • Stimulates digestion, salivation, and sexual arousal
  • Promotes energy storage and tissue repair
  • Uses acetylcholine as the primary postganglionic neurotransmitter
  • The vagus nerve (cranial nerve X) is the primary parasympathetic pathway

Clinical Significance: Polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011) proposes that the vagus nerve has two branches: the ventral vagal complex (promotes social engagement and calm states) and the dorsal vagal complex (triggers immobilization/shutdown during overwhelming threat). This framework is increasingly used in trauma therapy.

The Stress Response: HPA Axis

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the primary neuroendocrine system that mediates the body's response to stress.

HPA Axis Activation Sequence

  1. The hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) in response to perceived stress
  2. CRH stimulates the anterior pituitary to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)
  3. ACTH travels through the bloodstream to the adrenal cortex
  4. The adrenal cortex releases cortisol (the primary stress hormone, a glucocorticoid)
  5. Cortisol acts on multiple organ systems and provides negative feedback to the hypothalamus and pituitary to terminate the stress response

Effects of Cortisol

  • Metabolic: Increases blood glucose (gluconeogenesis), mobilizes energy stores
  • Immune: Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects at sustained high levels
  • Cognitive: Moderate levels enhance memory consolidation; chronic high levels impair hippocampal function and memory
  • Neural: Chronic elevation causes hippocampal atrophy and dendritic remodeling in the prefrontal cortex

Cortisol: The primary glucocorticoid hormone released by the adrenal cortex in response to ACTH. It follows a diurnal rhythm (highest in the morning, lowest at night) and is a key biomarker of HPA axis function. Chronically elevated cortisol is associated with depression, PTSD, metabolic syndrome, and hippocampal damage.

Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

Hans Selye (1936, 1956) described the General Adaptation Syndrome, a three-stage model of the body's response to prolonged stress:

Stage 1: Alarm Reaction

The body's initial fight-or-flight response to a stressor. The sympathetic nervous system is activated, releasing catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine). Cortisol levels begin to rise. Resistance to stress temporarily drops below normal.

Stage 2: Resistance (Adaptation)

The body attempts to adapt to the continued presence of the stressor. Cortisol levels remain elevated but the body achieves a new homeostatic balance. Parasympathetic activity partially counterbalances sympathetic arousal. The individual appears to be coping but physiological resources are being depleted.

Stage 3: Exhaustion

Prolonged stress depletes the body's adaptive resources. The immune system becomes compromised, vulnerability to illness increases dramatically, and organ systems may begin to fail. This stage can lead to "diseases of adaptation" including cardiovascular disease, gastrointestinal disorders, and immune dysfunction.

EPPP Application: Selye distinguished between eustress (positive, motivating stress) and distress (negative, harmful stress). He also noted that the GAS response is nonspecific -- the body responds similarly regardless of the type of stressor.

Allostatic Load

Allostatic load (McEwen, 1998) refers to the cumulative physiological wear and tear on the body resulting from chronic exposure to stress hormones and repeated activation of the stress response. Unlike acute stress (which is adaptive), chronic stress overloads the system.

  • Allostasis: The process by which the body achieves stability through physiological change (as opposed to homeostasis, which maintains a fixed set point)
  • Allostatic overload: When cumulative stress exceeds the body's ability to adapt, leading to pathology
  • Contributors include chronic psychological stress, sleep deprivation, poor diet, lack of exercise, and social isolation
  • Measurable biomarkers include cortisol levels, blood pressure, waist-to-hip ratio, glycated hemoglobin, and inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein, IL-6)

Clinical Significance: Allostatic load provides a framework for understanding health disparities. Chronic exposure to discrimination, poverty, and adversity produces elevated allostatic load, contributing to poorer health outcomes among marginalized populations. This concept bridges biological psychology and health psychology on the EPPP.

Psychoneuroimmunology (PNI)

Psychoneuroimmunology is the interdisciplinary field studying the interactions among psychological processes, the nervous system, and the immune system.

Key Findings

  • Stress and immunity: Acute stress can temporarily enhance immune function (mobilizing natural killer cells), while chronic stress suppresses immune function through sustained cortisol elevation
  • Kiecolt-Glaser studies: Medical student exams, caregiver stress, and marital conflict all demonstrated measurable immune suppression (reduced natural killer cell activity, slower wound healing, reduced antibody response to vaccines)
  • Conditioning of immune responses: Ader and Cohen (1975) demonstrated that immune responses can be classically conditioned, providing direct evidence that the nervous system can regulate immune function
  • Cytokines and behavior: Pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., interleukin-1, interleukin-6, TNF-alpha) produced by the immune system can induce "sickness behavior" (fatigue, social withdrawal, anhedonia) that overlaps with depressive symptoms

Pathways of Communication

  • Neural pathway: The autonomic nervous system innervates lymphoid organs (spleen, thymus, lymph nodes) and can directly modulate immune cell activity
  • Endocrine pathway: Cortisol and other stress hormones regulate immune cell function through glucocorticoid receptors on immune cells
  • Immune-to-brain signaling: Cytokines produced by immune cells signal the brain via the vagus nerve and circumventricular organs, influencing mood and behavior

Biological Bases of Specific Emotions

Fear

The amygdala is the central structure for fear processing. Fear conditioning involves pairing a neutral stimulus (CS) with an aversive stimulus (US), resulting in the CS eliciting a fear response. The amygdala mediates both the acquisition and expression of conditioned fear, while the prefrontal cortex (particularly the ventromedial PFC) is involved in fear extinction.

Anger and Aggression

The neural circuitry of aggression involves the amygdala, hypothalamus (particularly the ventromedial hypothalamus), and the orbitofrontal cortex (which provides top-down inhibitory control). Low serotonin levels are consistently associated with increased impulsive aggression.

Reward and Pleasure

The mesolimbic dopamine pathway (VTA to nucleus accumbens) is the brain's primary reward circuit. Dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens is associated with reward anticipation and motivation rather than pleasure itself. The opioid system is more directly linked to hedonic "liking."
