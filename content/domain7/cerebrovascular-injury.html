Cerebrovascular Disease, Brain Injury & Seizures

Domain 7: Biopsychology~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Stroke: Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic

A stroke (cerebrovascular accident, CVA) occurs when blood flow to the brain is disrupted, causing neuronal death. Stroke is a leading cause of long-term disability and the fifth leading cause of death in the United States.

TypeMechanismPrevalenceKey Features
Ischemic strokeBlockage of a cerebral artery (thrombus or embolus) cutting off blood supply~87% of all strokesThrombotic (clot forms in situ) or embolic (clot travels from elsewhere); treated with tPA within 4.5 hours
Hemorrhagic strokeRupture of a cerebral blood vessel causing bleeding into or around the brain~13% of all strokesIntracerebral hemorrhage or subarachnoid hemorrhage; higher mortality; often associated with hypertension or aneurysm
Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Syndrome:

The MCA is the most commonly affected artery in ischemic stroke. Left MCA stroke produces: contralateral (right-sided) hemiparesis and sensory loss (face and arm > leg), Broca's or Wernicke's aphasia (depending on branch affected), and potentially global aphasia. Right MCA stroke produces: contralateral hemiparesis, left hemispatial neglect, anosognosia (unawareness of deficit), and constructional apraxia. The EPPP frequently tests the lateralized deficits associated with MCA stroke.

A transient ischemic attack (TIA) produces temporary stroke-like symptoms that resolve completely within 24 hours (typically within minutes). TIAs are a major warning sign — they significantly increase the risk of subsequent stroke and require urgent evaluation.

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Traumatic brain injury results from an external mechanical force applied to the head. TBIs are classified as mild (concussion), moderate, or severe, based on the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score, duration of loss of consciousness, and duration of post-traumatic amnesia.

Coup-Contrecoup Injury:

A coup injury occurs at the site of impact, while a contrecoup injury occurs at the opposite side of the brain (due to the brain rebounding against the skull). In falls, the frontal and temporal lobes are particularly vulnerable because they contact the rough bony surfaces of the anterior and middle cranial fossae. This explains why personality changes and memory impairment are common after TBI even when the primary impact is elsewhere.

Diffuse axonal injury (DAI) results from rotational acceleration-deceleration forces that shear axons throughout the brain. DAI is the most common cause of prolonged coma and persistent vegetative state following TBI. The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) assesses eye opening (1-4), verbal response (1-5), and motor response (1-6), yielding scores from 3 (deep coma) to 15 (fully alert). GCS scores classify TBI as mild (13-15), moderate (9-12), or severe (3-8).

Post-Concussion Syndrome & CTE:

Post-concussion syndrome involves persistent symptoms following mild TBI: headache, dizziness, fatigue, irritability, difficulty concentrating, and sleep disturbance, typically resolving within weeks to months but sometimes persisting longer. Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease associated with repeated head impacts (common in contact sports and military personnel). CTE is characterized by accumulation of tau protein, producing mood disturbance, impulsivity, cognitive decline, and eventually dementia. Definitive diagnosis currently requires post-mortem examination.

Epilepsy & Seizure Disorders

Epilepsy is a neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures resulting from abnormal, excessive, or synchronous neuronal activity. Seizures are classified as generalized (involving both hemispheres from onset) or focal/partial (beginning in a specific brain region).

Seizure TypeFeaturesKey Details
Generalized tonic-clonic (grand mal)Loss of consciousness, tonic phase (stiffening), clonic phase (rhythmic jerking), postictal confusionMost dramatic seizure type; postictal period may last minutes to hours
Absence (petit mal)Brief lapses in consciousness (5-30 seconds), staring, subtle automatismsCommon in children; characteristic 3 Hz spike-and-wave on EEG; often mistaken for daydreaming
Focal (partial) with awarenessSeizure activity in one brain region; consciousness preservedSymptoms depend on focus location (motor, sensory, autonomic, psychic)
Focal with impaired awarenessSeizure beginning focally with altered consciousness; automatismsMost commonly temporal lobe origin; may include deja vu, fear, lip smacking
Status epilepticusContinuous seizure activity lasting >5 minutes or repeated seizures without recoveryMedical emergency; can cause brain damage and death; requires IV benzodiazepines

Hemispatial Neglect:

Hemispatial neglect (typically left-sided) results from right parietal lobe damage and involves a failure to attend to, report, or orient toward stimuli in the contralesional (left) space. Patients may eat food from only the right side of their plate, dress only the right side of their body, or draw only the right half of a clock. This is not a sensory deficit but an attentional one — the visual system can detect the stimuli, but the attentional system fails to process them.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Ischemic stroke (~87%): blockage of artery; treat with tPA within 4.5 hours. Hemorrhagic stroke (~13%): vessel rupture; higher mortality
  • MCA stroke: contralateral hemiparesis (face/arm > leg); left MCA = aphasia; right MCA = neglect + anosognosia
  • TIA: temporary stroke symptoms resolving within 24 hours; major stroke warning sign
  • Coup-contrecoup: injury at impact site AND opposite side; frontal/temporal lobes most vulnerable
  • Glasgow Coma Scale: eye + verbal + motor (3-15); mild TBI = 13-15; severe = 3-8
  • CTE: progressive tauopathy from repeated head impacts; mood/cognitive changes; definitive diagnosis is post-mortem
  • Absence seizures: brief consciousness lapses; 3 Hz spike-and-wave EEG; common in children
  • Status epilepticus: seizure >5 min; medical emergency; treat with IV benzodiazepines
  • Broca's aphasia: nonfluent speech, intact comprehension; Wernicke's: fluent word salad, impaired comprehension
  • Conduction aphasia: fluent, intact comprehension, severely impaired repetition (arcuate fasciculus)
  • Left hemispatial neglect: right parietal lesion; attentional (not sensory) deficit
