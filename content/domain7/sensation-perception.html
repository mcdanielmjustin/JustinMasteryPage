Psychophysics: Thresholds & Signal Detection

Psychophysics is the study of the relationship between physical stimuli and the psychological sensations they produce. Several foundational concepts from psychophysics are heavily tested on the EPPP.

Absolute Threshold:

The absolute threshold is the minimum intensity of a stimulus that can be detected 50% of the time. For example, the absolute threshold for vision is roughly the equivalent of a candle flame visible from 30 miles on a clear dark night. Note that the 50% criterion is key — detection is probabilistic, not all-or-none.

Difference Threshold (JND):

The difference threshold, or just noticeable difference (JND), is the minimum difference between two stimuli that can be detected 50% of the time. Weber's Law states that the JND is a constant proportion of the original stimulus intensity (delta I / I = k). For example, if you can just barely notice the difference between a 100g weight and a 102g weight (2% difference), you would need a 4g difference to detect a change from 200g. The Weber fraction (k) is smaller for more sensitive modalities.

Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

Signal detection theory provides a framework for understanding how decisions are made under conditions of uncertainty, going beyond simple threshold concepts by acknowledging that detection depends on both the sensitivity of the observer and their response criterion (decision bias).

Signal PresentSignal Absent
Respond "Yes"Hit (correct detection)False Alarm (Type I error)
Respond "No"Miss (Type II error)Correct Rejection

The observer's sensitivity (d-prime) reflects their ability to discriminate signal from noise, independent of response bias. The response criterion (beta) reflects the observer's willingness to say "yes" — a liberal criterion produces more hits but also more false alarms, while a conservative criterion produces fewer false alarms but also more misses. Response criterion shifts depending on the payoff matrix (e.g., a radiologist screening for cancer should adopt a liberal criterion because missing a tumor [miss] is more costly than a false alarm).

Clinical Application:

Signal detection theory applies directly to clinical decision-making. A psychologist conducting a risk assessment must balance the cost of false positives (incorrectly identifying someone as dangerous, leading to unnecessary restriction) against the cost of false negatives (failing to identify a truly dangerous individual). The base rate of the behavior, the severity of consequences, and the clinician's threshold for action all influence the decision criterion.

Sensory Adaptation

Sensory adaptation is the diminished sensitivity to an unchanging stimulus over time. When you first enter a room with a strong odor, the smell is noticeable, but after several minutes, you barely detect it. This occurs because sensory receptors reduce their firing rate in response to constant stimulation. Importantly, pain receptors show minimal adaptation — a biologically adaptive feature that ensures continued attention to potentially harmful stimuli.

Vision: From Retina to Cortex

Light enters the eye through the cornea, passes through the pupil (regulated by the iris), is focused by the lens, and strikes the retina — a thin layer of neural tissue at the back of the eye containing the photoreceptors.

Rods and Cones

  • Rods (~120 million): Sensitive to low light levels (scotopic vision); located primarily in the periphery; provide black-and-white vision; no color sensitivity; high convergence onto ganglion cells (many rods feed one ganglion cell), producing high sensitivity but low acuity
  • Cones (~6 million): Function in bright light (photopic vision); concentrated in the fovea (center of the retina); provide color vision and high visual acuity; low convergence (one-to-one mapping in fovea), producing low sensitivity but high acuity

Color Vision Theories

Trichromatic Theory (Young-Helmholtz):

The retina contains three types of cones, each maximally sensitive to a different wavelength: short (blue), medium (green), and long (red). All perceived colors result from the combined activation of these three cone types. This theory explains color mixing and certain forms of color blindness (e.g., dichromacy from the absence of one cone type).

Opponent-Process Theory (Hering):

Color perception is organized into three opponent channels: red-green, blue-yellow, and black-white. Each channel involves antagonistic neural processing — stimulation of one color inhibits its opponent. This theory explains afterimages (staring at red produces a green afterimage), simultaneous contrast effects, and why certain color combinations (e.g., "reddish-green") are impossible to perceive. Modern understanding holds that trichromatic processing occurs at the retinal level, while opponent processing occurs at the level of retinal ganglion cells and the lateral geniculate nucleus.

Feature Detection

Hubel and Wiesel's Nobel Prize-winning research identified feature detectors in the primary visual cortex (V1): simple cells respond to lines and edges at specific orientations and locations; complex cells respond to moving lines at specific orientations regardless of exact position; and hypercomplex cells respond to lines of specific length or endpoints. This hierarchical processing demonstrates how the visual cortex builds complex representations from simple features.

Hubel, D. H., & Wiesel, T. N. (1962). Receptive fields, binocular interaction and functional architecture in the cat's visual cortex. Journal of Physiology, 160(1), 106-154.

Audition: Hearing Theories

Sound waves are converted to neural signals through the mechanical structures of the ear (outer ear, tympanic membrane, ossicles) and the cochlea, where hair cells on the basilar membrane transduce mechanical vibration into electrical signals.

TheoryMechanismFrequency Range
Place Theory (Helmholtz)Different frequencies cause peak vibration at different locations along the basilar membrane (base = high frequency, apex = low frequency)Best for high frequencies (above ~1,000 Hz)
Frequency Theory (Rutherford)The basilar membrane vibrates as a whole at the same frequency as the sound wave; firing rate matches frequencyBest for low frequencies (below ~500 Hz)
Volley PrincipleGroups of neurons fire in alternating volleys, with the combined firing rate matching the stimulus frequencyExtends frequency theory to ~5,000 Hz

Modern understanding: place theory and frequency/volley theory are complementary. Low frequencies are coded primarily by firing rate (frequency theory), high frequencies are coded by the location of maximal basilar membrane displacement (place theory), and intermediate frequencies use both mechanisms.

Gestalt Principles & Perceptual Organization

The Gestalt principles describe how the visual system organizes individual elements into coherent wholes ("the whole is different from the sum of its parts"):

  • Proximity: Elements that are close together are perceived as belonging to the same group
  • Similarity: Elements that share features (color, shape, size) are grouped together
  • Closure: Incomplete figures are perceived as complete; the brain fills in gaps
  • Continuity (good continuation): Elements arranged along a line or curve are perceived as related
  • Figure-ground: The visual field is automatically organized into a figure (the object of focus) and a ground (the background)
  • Common fate: Elements moving in the same direction are perceived as a group

Depth Perception

Binocular cues (requiring both eyes): retinal disparity (each eye receives a slightly different image; greater disparity = closer object) and convergence (the inward turning of the eyes for near objects). Monocular cues (available from one eye): relative size, interposition (overlap), linear perspective, texture gradient, aerial perspective (haziness), and motion parallax.

Gate-Control Theory of Pain

Melzack and Wall's (1965) gate-control theory proposes that pain signals can be modulated at the spinal cord level by a "gate" in the substantia gelatinosa. Large-diameter (A-beta) fibers carrying touch information can close the gate (inhibiting pain transmission), while small-diameter (C and A-delta) fibers open the gate (facilitating pain). Descending signals from the brain can also close the gate, explaining why attention, expectation, and emotional state influence pain perception. This theory accounts for why rubbing an injury reduces pain and why psychological interventions can modulate pain experience.

Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing:

Bottom-up processing (data-driven) begins with sensory input and builds up to perception without influence from prior knowledge. Top-down processing (concept-driven) uses prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret sensory input. Most perception involves an interaction of both. Top-down processing explains why context allows you to read words with missing letters, why ambiguous stimuli are perceived differently depending on expectations, and why cultural background influences perception.

Attention Models & Selective Processing

Attention determines which of the vast amount of incoming sensory information reaches conscious awareness and receives further processing. A central debate in cognitive psychology concerns where in the processing stream selection occurs — early (before meaning is extracted) or late (after full semantic analysis). The major models of attention are frequently tested on the EPPP.

Broadbent's Filter Model (Early Selection)

Broadbent's Filter Model (1958):

Donald Broadbent proposed the first formal model of selective attention. In this early selection model, all sensory information enters a sensory buffer where it is briefly held. A selective filter then allows only one channel of information (selected based on physical characteristics such as location, pitch, or loudness) to pass through to higher-level semantic processing. Unattended information is completely blocked at the filter and never reaches awareness or meaning extraction. Broadbent's model was supported by dichotic listening experiments in which participants could report the content of an attended ear but almost nothing from the unattended ear.

Treisman's Attenuation Model

Treisman's Attenuation Model (1964):

Anne Treisman modified Broadbent's model to account for findings that some unattended information does get through. In Treisman's attenuation model, the filter does not completely block unattended information but instead attenuates (turns down) it — like lowering the volume rather than switching it off. Highly relevant stimuli (e.g., one's own name, danger signals) have permanently lowered activation thresholds and can break through even when attenuated. This explains the cocktail party effect — the ability to detect your own name spoken in an unattended conversation at a noisy party, even though you were not consciously monitoring that conversation.

Deutsch-Norman Late Selection Model

Deutsch and Deutsch (1963), later refined by Norman (1968), proposed a late selection model in which all incoming information is fully processed for meaning (semantically analyzed), and selection occurs only at the response/awareness stage. According to this model, the bottleneck is not in perception but in the decision about what to act on or remember. This explains how people can be influenced by the meaning of unattended messages without being consciously aware of them.

Lavie's Perceptual Load Theory

Lavie's Perceptual Load Theory (1995):

Nilli Lavie proposed an integrative framework that resolves the early-vs.-late selection debate. According to perceptual load theory, whether selection occurs early or late depends on the perceptual load (processing demands) of the attended task. Under high perceptual load (complex, demanding tasks that consume all available processing capacity), selection occurs early — there is no spare capacity to process distractors. Under low perceptual load (simple tasks with spare capacity), unattended information is processed automatically, and selection occurs late. This theory is supported by neuroimaging studies showing reduced neural responses to distractors during high-load tasks.

Posner's Spotlight Model

Michael Posner (1980) proposed that spatial attention operates like a spotlight that can be directed to different regions of the visual field. Attention can be shifted overtly (with eye movements) or covertly (without moving the eyes). Posner identified three component operations of spatial attention: disengaging from the current focus, shifting to the new location, and engaging at the new location. Different brain regions mediate each operation: the posterior parietal cortex is involved in disengaging, the superior colliculus in shifting, and the pulvinar (thalamus) in engaging. Damage to the right parietal lobe produces hemispatial neglect, a dramatic failure to disengage attention from ipsilesional space and attend to contralateral stimuli.

Feature Integration Theory

Feature Integration Theory (Treisman & Gelade, 1980):

This influential theory proposes two stages of visual processing. In the preattentive stage, basic visual features (color, shape, orientation, size, motion) are processed automatically, in parallel, and without conscious effort across the entire visual field. In the focused attention stage, attention is required to bind these individual features together into coherent object representations. Without focused attention, features from different objects can be incorrectly combined, producing illusory conjunctions — for example, incorrectly perceiving a red circle and a blue square as a "red square." This theory explains why a unique feature (e.g., a red item among green items) "pops out" effortlessly in visual search, but searching for a conjunction of features (e.g., a red circle among red squares and green circles) requires slow, serial, attention-demanding search.

Change Blindness & Inattentional Blindness

Two striking phenomena demonstrate the limitations of attention and conscious awareness:

  • Inattentional blindness (Mack & Rock, 1998; Simons & Chabris, 1999): The failure to perceive a clearly visible stimulus when attention is directed elsewhere. In the famous "invisible gorilla" experiment, approximately 50% of observers failed to notice a person in a gorilla suit walking through a basketball game when they were counting passes. This demonstrates that attention, not just visual acuity, is necessary for conscious perception.
  • Change blindness (Rensink, O'Regan, & Clark, 1997): The failure to detect large changes in a visual scene when the change coincides with a brief visual disruption (e.g., a flicker, a saccade, or a cut in a film). Change blindness reveals that we do not maintain a detailed, moment-to-moment representation of the entire visual scene — our sense of a complete visual world is, in part, an illusion constructed by the brain.
Clinical Relevance:

Attentional deficits are a core feature of many psychological disorders tested on the EPPP. ADHD involves impairments in sustained attention and executive control of attention. Hemispatial neglect following right parietal stroke represents a dramatic failure of spatial attention (Posner's disengage operation). Anxiety disorders involve attentional bias toward threat-related stimuli, which can be measured with the dot-probe task. Understanding the theoretical models of attention provides the conceptual framework for understanding these clinical presentations.

EPPP Application:

A question might describe a dichotic listening task where a participant shadows (repeats) a message in one ear but still notices their own name in the unattended ear. The best explanation is Treisman's attenuation model (not Broadbent's filter model, which predicts the name would be completely blocked). If the question describes a participant who fails to notice a large, clearly visible stimulus because they were focused on a demanding task, the answer involves inattentional blindness. If the question asks why a simple visual search is fast but a conjunction search is slow, the answer is Feature Integration Theory.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Absolute threshold: minimum stimulus detected 50% of the time; JND: minimum detectable difference (50% of the time)
  • Weber's Law: JND is a constant proportion of the original stimulus (delta I / I = k)
  • Signal detection theory: performance depends on sensitivity (d-prime) AND response criterion (beta); know the 2x2 matrix (hit, miss, false alarm, correct rejection)
  • Sensory adaptation: decreased sensitivity to constant stimuli; pain receptors show minimal adaptation
  • Rods: periphery, low-light, black-and-white, high sensitivity; Cones: fovea, bright light, color, high acuity
  • Trichromatic theory: three cone types (retinal level); Opponent-process theory: antagonistic color channels (post-retinal); both are correct at different levels
  • Feature detectors (Hubel & Wiesel): simple, complex, and hypercomplex cells in V1 respond to edges, orientation, and movement
  • Place theory: high-frequency sounds; Frequency theory: low-frequency sounds; Volley principle: extends frequency theory
  • Gestalt principles: proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, figure-ground, common fate
  • Gate-control theory (Melzack & Wall): pain signals modulated at spinal cord; large fibers close the gate (reduce pain), small fibers open it
  • Bottom-up processing = data-driven (stimulus to perception); Top-down processing = concept-driven (expectations guide perception)
  • Broadbent's filter model (early selection): unattended info blocked before semantic processing; Treisman's attenuation model: unattended info turned down, not blocked — explains cocktail party effect
  • Deutsch-Norman late selection: all info processed semantically, selection at response stage; Lavie's perceptual load theory: high load = early selection, low load = late selection
  • Feature Integration Theory (Treisman & Gelade): preattentive parallel feature detection vs. focused attention for feature binding; illusory conjunctions occur without attention
  • Inattentional blindness: failure to see visible stimulus when attention is elsewhere (invisible gorilla); Change blindness: failure to detect large visual changes during disruption
  • Posner's spotlight model: spatial attention involves disengage (parietal), shift (superior colliculus), engage (pulvinar); right parietal damage produces hemispatial neglect
