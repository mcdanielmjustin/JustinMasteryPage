Learning Processes & Encoding Strategies

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Levels of Processing Framework

The levels of processing framework, proposed by Craik and Lockhart (1972), challenged the prevailing multi-store model of memory by arguing that memory durability depends not on which "store" information enters, but on the depth at which it is processed during encoding.

Depth of Processing:

Shallow processing involves attending to surface-level features of stimuli, such as the physical appearance of a word (structural encoding) or its sound (phonemic encoding). Deep processing involves attending to meaning and making semantic connections (semantic encoding). Deep processing produces significantly stronger and more durable memory traces than shallow processing.

Elaborative vs. Maintenance Rehearsal

Maintenance rehearsal involves simple repetition of information without engaging with its meaning, as when repeating a phone number to keep it in short-term memory. This form of rehearsal produces minimal long-term retention. In contrast, elaborative rehearsal involves connecting new information to existing knowledge, generating examples, and thinking about meaning. Elaborative rehearsal promotes deep processing and robust long-term encoding.

EPPP Example:

A student who reads the definition of "proactive interference" and then generates her own example of how previously learned Spanish vocabulary interferes with learning French vocabulary is engaging in elaborative rehearsal. A student who simply rereads the definition ten times is engaging in maintenance rehearsal. The first strategy produces far superior retention.

Craik, F. I. M., & Lockhart, R. S. (1972). Levels of processing: A framework for memory research. Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 11(6), 671-684.

Encoding Specificity & Context Effects

The encoding specificity principle, articulated by Tulving and Thomson (1973), states that memory is most effective when the conditions at retrieval match the conditions present during encoding. This principle has two major applications tested on the EPPP.

Context-Dependent Memory

Context-dependent memory refers to the finding that recall is improved when the external environment at retrieval matches the environment present during encoding. Godden and Baddeley (1975) famously demonstrated this with scuba divers who learned word lists either underwater or on land — recall was significantly better when the learning and testing environments matched.

State-Dependent Memory

State-dependent memory refers to the finding that recall is enhanced when an individual's internal physiological or psychological state at retrieval matches the state during encoding. This has been demonstrated with mood states (mood-dependent memory), pharmacological states, and arousal levels.

Clinical Relevance:

State-dependent memory has important implications for psychotherapy. Traumatic memories encoded during high-arousal states may be difficult to access during the calm conditions of a therapy session. Conversely, therapeutic insights gained in session may be harder to retrieve when the patient is in a distressed state outside of therapy. This principle also explains why substance-related memories may be more accessible under the influence of the same substance.

Tulving, E., & Thomson, D. M. (1973). Encoding specificity and retrieval processes in episodic memory. Psychological Review, 80(5), 352-373.

Self-Reference & Generation Effects

The self-reference effect is the finding that information encoded in relation to the self is remembered better than information encoded in other ways, including semantic processing. When individuals are asked "Does this word describe you?" they show superior recall compared to when asked "Does this word mean the same as [synonym]?"

Generation Effect:

The generation effect demonstrates that information that is actively generated by the learner is better remembered than information that is passively read. For example, completing "Proactive inter_____" produces better memory than simply reading "Proactive interference." This effect supports active learning strategies and the testing effect.

The Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)

The testing effect (also called retrieval practice) is one of the most robust findings in the learning sciences. Practicing retrieval of information — through self-testing, flashcards, or practice questions — produces substantially better long-term retention than restudying the same material for an equivalent amount of time. Roediger and Karpicke (2006) demonstrated that students who were tested on material retained it significantly better one week later than students who spent the same time restudying.

Study Strategy Application:

For EPPP preparation, the testing effect implies that repeatedly taking practice tests is more effective for long-term learning than repeatedly rereading study materials. Each act of retrieval strengthens the memory trace and makes the information more accessible for future recall, including on exam day.

Spacing Effect & Distributed Practice

The spacing effect (Ebbinghaus, 1885; Cepeda et al., 2006) is the finding that distributing study sessions over time produces superior long-term retention compared to massing the same amount of study into a single session (cramming). The spacing effect is one of the most replicable findings in all of experimental psychology.

The optimal inter-study interval depends on the retention interval: for a test one week away, spacing sessions 1-2 days apart is optimal; for a test one month away, spacing sessions one week apart is more effective. This principle interacts with the testing effect — spaced retrieval practice produces the strongest retention of all.

Dual Coding Theory & Mnemonic Strategies

Dual coding theory (Paivio, 1971) proposes that information is stored in two distinct but interconnected coding systems: a verbal system (for linguistic information) and an imaginal system (for visual-spatial information). Information encoded in both systems — such as a word paired with a vivid mental image — creates two retrieval routes and is therefore more memorable than information encoded in only one system.

Mnemonic Strategies

Mnemonic devices leverage dual coding and elaborative processing to enhance memory. Key techniques tested on the EPPP include:

  • Method of loci: Associating items to be remembered with specific locations along a familiar mental route. This ancient technique creates vivid spatial-visual associations and is highly effective for ordered recall.
  • Keyword method: Used for vocabulary learning; the learner creates a concrete image linking the sound of the unfamiliar word to the meaning through an intermediary keyword. Particularly effective for foreign language acquisition.
  • Pegword method: Items are associated with a pre-memorized sequence of rhyming cue words (e.g., "one is a bun, two is a shoe"). Useful for learning numbered lists.
Transfer-Appropriate Processing:

The transfer-appropriate processing principle (Morris, Bransford, & Franks, 1977) states that memory performance depends on the match between the type of processing at encoding and the type of processing required at retrieval. Semantic encoding produces superior performance on meaning-based tests, while phonemic encoding may actually produce better performance on rhyme-recognition tests. This principle refines the levels of processing framework by emphasizing that "deeper" is not always "better" — what matters is the match between encoding and retrieval demands.

Paivio, A. (1971). Imagery and verbal processes. New York: Holt, Rinehart, and Winston. — Paivio's dual coding theory remains influential in understanding how visual imagery enhances verbal learning.

Encoding Failure as a Cause of Forgetting

Not all forgetting reflects the loss of stored information. Encoding failure occurs when information never enters long-term memory in the first place, typically because it was not attended to or processed at a sufficient depth. The classic demonstration involves the penny experiment: most people cannot accurately draw the features of a penny despite having seen thousands of them, because they never encoded the details deeply.

Clinical Application:

Encoding failure is particularly relevant to understanding memory complaints in older adults and individuals with attentional disorders. Many reported "memory problems" actually reflect attentional or encoding deficits rather than retrieval failures. For example, an individual with ADHD who reports being unable to remember where they placed their keys may never have encoded the location in the first place due to divided attention at the time of placement. Distinguishing encoding failures from retrieval failures has important diagnostic implications.

Understanding encoding processes is essential for the EPPP not only as direct test content, but also for optimizing your own study strategies. The research consistently supports the superiority of active, elaborative, spaced, and retrieval-based approaches over passive rereading and massed practice.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Levels of processing (Craik & Lockhart): deep/semantic processing produces better memory than shallow/structural processing
  • Elaborative rehearsal (connecting to meaning) produces better retention than maintenance rehearsal (simple repetition)
  • Encoding specificity principle (Tulving & Thomson): retrieval is best when encoding and retrieval conditions match
  • Context-dependent memory: external environment match enhances recall; State-dependent memory: internal state match enhances recall
  • Self-reference effect: relating information to oneself enhances encoding
  • Generation effect: actively producing information is better than passively reading it
  • Testing effect (retrieval practice): self-testing is more effective than restudying for long-term retention
  • Spacing effect: distributed practice produces superior retention compared to massed practice
  • Dual coding theory (Paivio): encoding in both verbal and visual systems creates two retrieval routes
  • Method of loci: mental walk through familiar locations; Keyword method: linking sounds to images; Pegword: rhyming cue words
  • Transfer-appropriate processing: memory depends on the match between encoding type and retrieval demands, not just "depth"
  • Encoding failure: forgetting because information was never adequately encoded in the first place
