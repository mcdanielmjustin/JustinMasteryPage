Retrieval, Forgetting & Memory Distortion

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Recall, Recognition & the Serial Position Effect

Retrieval processes vary in the amount of environmental support they provide. Recall requires generating information from memory with minimal cues (e.g., "Name the four lobes of the cortex"), while recognition requires identifying previously encountered information from a set of alternatives (e.g., multiple-choice questions). Recognition is generally easier because the correct answer serves as its own retrieval cue.

Serial Position Effect:

The serial position effect describes the tendency to recall items at the beginning and end of a list better than items in the middle. The primacy effect (superior recall of early items) is attributed to greater rehearsal and transfer to long-term memory. The recency effect (superior recall of recent items) is attributed to the items still being active in short-term/working memory. A distractor task between learning and recall eliminates the recency effect but not the primacy effect, supporting the distinction between STM and LTM stores.

EPPP Application:

If an EPPP question describes a participant who learns a word list, performs 30 seconds of mental arithmetic, and then recalls the list, the expected finding is that the primacy effect is preserved (early items still recalled well) but the recency effect is eliminated (recent items are no longer in STM). This classic finding supports the Atkinson-Shiffrin dual-store model.

The Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve & Interference Theory

Hermann Ebbinghaus (1885) conducted the first systematic study of forgetting using nonsense syllables. His forgetting curve demonstrated that forgetting is most rapid immediately after learning, with the rate of forgetting decelerating over time. Approximately 50% of newly learned material is forgotten within the first hour, and about 70% within 24 hours, but the remaining material is retained relatively stably over longer periods.

Proactive vs. Retroactive Interference

Interference theory proposes that forgetting occurs because memories compete with one another during retrieval. Two forms of interference are critical for the EPPP:

TypeDefinitionExampleMnemonic
Proactive InterferencePreviously learned information interferes with the learning or recall of new informationYour old phone number interferes with remembering your new one"Pro" = forward; old disrupts new
Retroactive InterferenceNewly learned information interferes with the recall of previously learned informationLearning your new phone number makes it harder to remember your old one"Retro" = backward; new disrupts old
Clinical Relevance:

Proactive interference increases with age and is a significant contributor to age-related memory complaints. Older adults are particularly susceptible to proactive interference because they have accumulated more competing memories and may have reduced inhibitory control. In neuropsychological testing, proactive interference is assessed through paradigms such as the release-from-PI procedure and is a sensitive indicator of frontal lobe dysfunction.

Cue-Dependent Forgetting

Cue-dependent forgetting occurs when information is stored in long-term memory but cannot be accessed because the appropriate retrieval cues are absent. This is distinct from trace decay — the memory exists but is temporarily inaccessible. The tip-of-the-tongue (TOT) phenomenon is a vivid example: the person feels certain they know the word, can often recall partial information (first letter, number of syllables), but cannot produce the complete word until the right cue appears.

False Memories & the Misinformation Effect

Elizabeth Loftus's research on the misinformation effect demonstrates that post-event information can alter memory for the original event. In her classic studies, participants who watched a video of a car accident and were later asked "How fast were the cars going when they smashed into each other?" (vs. "contacted" or "hit") reported higher speed estimates and were more likely to falsely remember seeing broken glass.

Misinformation Effect:

The misinformation effect occurs when misleading post-event information is incorporated into an individual's memory of the original event. This can take the form of misleading questions, exposure to inaccurate accounts from other witnesses, or suggestive therapeutic techniques. The misinformation effect is more powerful when the original memory is weak, when the misleading information is presented with authority, and when there is a long delay between the event and the misinformation.

The DRM Paradigm

The Deese-Roediger-McDermott (DRM) paradigm demonstrates spontaneous false memory formation. Participants study a list of words all related to a non-presented critical lure (e.g., "bed," "rest," "awake," "tired," "dream" — the critical lure being "sleep"). At test, participants frequently recall or recognize the critical lure with high confidence, sometimes claiming to remember specific details of its presentation. This demonstrates that semantic activation during encoding can create false memories that feel phenomenologically real.

Loftus, E. F. (2005). Planting misinformation in the human mind: A 30-year investigation of the malleability of memory. Learning & Memory, 12(4), 361-366.

Source Monitoring Errors

Source monitoring refers to the process of attributing a memory to its correct source. Source monitoring errors occur when individuals correctly remember information but misattribute its origin — confusing something they imagined with something they actually experienced, something they read with something someone told them, or something one person said with something another person said. These errors are particularly common in children, older adults, and individuals with frontal lobe dysfunction.

Recovered Memories & the Repression Debate

The recovered memory debate centers on whether traumatic memories can be repressed and later accurately recovered, or whether "recovered" memories are typically false memories created through suggestive therapeutic techniques.

APA Position:

The American Psychological Association acknowledges that (1) both genuine recovered memories and false memories exist, (2) it is impossible to distinguish recovered memories from false memories based on their vividness, confidence, or emotional intensity alone, (3) suggestive therapeutic techniques (e.g., hypnosis, guided imagery, dream interpretation for memory recovery) can create false memories, and (4) therapists should avoid using such techniques specifically to "recover" memories. The EPPP may test the position that strong emotions and high confidence do NOT guarantee memory accuracy.

Flashbulb Memories

Flashbulb memories are vivid, apparently detailed memories for the circumstances surrounding the learning of a surprising, consequential, and emotionally arousing event (e.g., "Where were you when you heard about 9/11?"). Brown and Kulik (1977) proposed a special neural mechanism for their formation. However, subsequent research has demonstrated that flashbulb memories, despite their vividness and the high confidence with which they are held, are not as accurate as they feel. They are subject to the same distortions and reconstructive errors as ordinary memories, though people are more confident in their accuracy.

Motivated Forgetting & Repression

Motivated forgetting refers to the concept that individuals may forget information because it is unpleasant, threatening, or anxiety-provoking. Freud's concept of repression posits that traumatic memories are actively pushed out of conscious awareness as a defense mechanism. While there is evidence for intentional suppression of unwanted memories (Anderson & Green's "Think/No-Think" paradigm), the scientific evidence for Freudian repression of entire traumatic events is weak and contested.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Recall (generate from memory) is harder than recognition (identify from alternatives); both are easier than relearning (savings method)
  • Serial position effect: primacy (LTM, rehearsal) + recency (STM, still active); distractor task eliminates recency but not primacy
  • Ebbinghaus forgetting curve: forgetting is most rapid immediately after learning, then decelerates
  • Proactive interference: old material disrupts learning of new (forward in time); Retroactive interference: new material disrupts recall of old (backward in time)
  • Cue-dependent forgetting: information is stored but inaccessible without proper cues; TOT phenomenon is a classic example
  • Misinformation effect (Loftus): post-event information alters memory for the original event; leading questions can implant false details
  • DRM paradigm: demonstrates spontaneous false memory for semantically related non-presented words
  • Source monitoring errors: correctly remembering information but misattributing its source
  • Recovered memory debate: both genuine recovered memories and false memories can exist; suggestive techniques create risk of false memories
  • Flashbulb memories: vivid and confidently held, but NOT necessarily more accurate than ordinary memories
  • High confidence does NOT equal high accuracy — this is a critical EPPP principle for eyewitness testimony and recovered memories
