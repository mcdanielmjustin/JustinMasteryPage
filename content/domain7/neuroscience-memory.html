Neuroscience of Memory & Amnesia

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

The Hippocampus & Memory Consolidation

The hippocampus, a seahorse-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe, plays a critical role in the consolidation of new declarative (explicit) memories. It serves as a temporary indexing system that binds together distributed cortical representations of an experience, enabling the gradual transfer of memories to neocortical storage over time.

Memory Consolidation:

Consolidation is the process by which newly encoded, fragile memory traces are stabilized and integrated into long-term storage. Synaptic consolidation occurs within hours at the level of individual synapses (involving protein synthesis and structural changes). Systems consolidation occurs over weeks to years, involving the gradual transfer of memory representations from the hippocampus to distributed neocortical networks. Once fully consolidated, memories can be retrieved independently of the hippocampus.

The hippocampus is essential for encoding new episodic memories and spatial navigation (containing "place cells" that fire when an organism occupies a specific location). It is also part of the Papez circuit, which links the hippocampus to the mammillary bodies, anterior thalamus, and cingulate cortex in a circuit important for memory formation.

Patient H.M. & the Amnesia Syndromes

The case of H.M. (Henry Molaison, 1926-2008) is the most important single case in the history of memory neuroscience. In 1953, neurosurgeon William Beecher Scoville performed a bilateral medial temporal lobectomy (removing the hippocampus, amygdala, and surrounding cortex bilaterally) to treat H.M.'s severe epilepsy. The surgery controlled his seizures but produced profound amnesia.

H.M.'s Memory Profile:

H.M. demonstrated severe anterograde amnesia (inability to form new declarative memories after the surgery) and moderate retrograde amnesia (loss of memories for events from approximately 1-3 years before surgery, with more remote memories relatively preserved — a temporal gradient). Critically, his intelligence, personality, working memory, procedural learning, and priming were all intact. He could learn new motor skills (mirror drawing) despite having no memory of ever having practiced the task. This dissociation provided the first clear evidence that explicit and implicit memory depend on different neural systems.

Scoville, W. B., & Milner, B. (1957). Loss of recent memory after bilateral hippocampal lesions. Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery, and Psychiatry, 20(1), 11-21.

Korsakoff's Syndrome

Korsakoff's syndrome is a chronic neurological condition caused by severe thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, most commonly associated with chronic alcohol use disorder. The thiamine deficiency produces damage to the mammillary bodies, dorsomedial thalamus, and periventricular regions — all structures critical to memory circuits.

Clinical Features of Korsakoff's Syndrome:

The hallmark features include severe anterograde amnesia, variable retrograde amnesia, and confabulation — the production of fabricated, distorted, or misinterpreted memories without the conscious intention to deceive. Confabulation occurs because the patient fills memory gaps with plausible-sounding but inaccurate information. Patients may also show apathy, lack of insight (anosognosia), and difficulty with temporal ordering of events. Procedural memory and priming are typically preserved.

EPPP Distinction:

Wernicke's encephalopathy (acute phase: confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia) and Korsakoff's syndrome (chronic phase: amnesia, confabulation) are often considered a continuum — Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome. Wernicke's is treated emergently with IV thiamine; if untreated, approximately 80% of cases progress to Korsakoff's, which is largely irreversible. The EPPP tests the connection between chronic alcohol use, thiamine deficiency, and the specific memory symptoms of Korsakoff's.

Long-Term Potentiation & Hebb's Rule

Long-term potentiation (LTP) is the persistent strengthening of synaptic connections following high-frequency stimulation. First described in the hippocampus by Bliss and Lomo (1973), LTP is widely considered the primary cellular mechanism underlying learning and memory.

LTP involves several key molecular steps: (1) high-frequency stimulation of the presynaptic neuron releases glutamate, (2) this activates AMPA and NMDA receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, (3) NMDA receptor activation (which requires both glutamate binding and sufficient postsynaptic depolarization) allows calcium influx, (4) calcium triggers intracellular signaling cascades that strengthen the synapse through insertion of additional AMPA receptors and, eventually, structural changes (new dendritic spines).

Hebb's Rule:

Donald Hebb (1949) proposed that "cells that fire together wire together" — when a presynaptic neuron repeatedly contributes to firing a postsynaptic neuron, the synaptic connection between them is strengthened. This Hebbian learning rule anticipated the discovery of LTP by over two decades and remains a foundational principle of neural plasticity and memory formation. Hebb also proposed the concept of the "cell assembly" — a network of interconnected neurons that represents a memory and becomes increasingly coordinated through repeated co-activation.

Hebb, D. O. (1949). The Organization of Behavior. New York: Wiley. — Hebb's theoretical framework provided the conceptual foundation for modern theories of synaptic plasticity and learning.

Neural Substrates of Different Memory Systems

Amygdala & Emotional Memory

The amygdala modulates the encoding and consolidation of emotionally arousing memories. It does not store the declarative content of emotional memories but enhances hippocampal consolidation through stress hormone and norepinephrine signaling. This explains why emotionally charged events are generally better remembered — the amygdala amplifies the hippocampal encoding process. Amygdala damage impairs the emotional enhancement of memory without affecting the formation of non-emotional declarative memories.

Cerebellum & Procedural Memory

The cerebellum is essential for motor learning, classical conditioning of motor responses (e.g., eyeblink conditioning), and the fine-tuning of skilled movements. Cerebellar damage impairs the acquisition of conditioned motor responses and the performance of skilled procedural tasks.

Basal Ganglia & Habit Learning

The basal ganglia (particularly the striatum) are critical for habit learning, stimulus-response associations, and the automatization of behaviors through reinforcement. Patients with Huntington's disease (caudate nucleus atrophy) or Parkinson's disease (substantia nigra degeneration) show impaired procedural and habit learning despite relatively preserved declarative memory in early stages.

Reconsolidation Theory

Reconsolidation theory proposes that when a consolidated memory is reactivated (retrieved), it temporarily returns to a labile state and must be reconsolidated to persist. During this reconsolidation window, the memory is vulnerable to modification, disruption, or updating. This has clinical implications for treating PTSD — reactivating a traumatic memory during a therapeutic window (e.g., with propranolol administration or extinction training) may allow the emotional component to be weakened during reconsolidation.

Clinical Application — Memory Reconsolidation in Therapy:

Reconsolidation theory has informed trauma therapies that aim to reactivate traumatic memories in safe therapeutic conditions, then introduce new, corrective information during the reconsolidation window. This may update the original memory trace rather than simply creating a competing inhibitory memory (as in standard extinction). The approach is still being researched but represents a promising integration of neuroscience and psychotherapy.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Hippocampus: essential for consolidation of new declarative (explicit) memories; not the permanent storage site
  • H.M.: bilateral hippocampal removal caused severe anterograde amnesia with intact procedural learning, priming, and working memory — proved explicit and implicit memory are separate systems
  • Anterograde amnesia = cannot form new memories; Retrograde amnesia = cannot retrieve old memories; temporal gradient = recent memories lost more than remote
  • Korsakoff's syndrome: thiamine deficiency (chronic alcohol use) damages mammillary bodies and thalamus; produces amnesia + confabulation
  • Confabulation: fabricating memories to fill gaps, without intent to deceive — hallmark of Korsakoff's
  • Long-term potentiation (LTP): persistent synaptic strengthening; the primary cellular mechanism of learning; involves NMDA receptors and calcium signaling
  • Hebb's rule: "cells that fire together wire together" — co-activation strengthens synaptic connections
  • Amygdala: modulates emotional memory enhancement (does not store the memory itself)
  • Cerebellum: procedural memory and conditioned motor responses (eyeblink conditioning)
  • Basal ganglia (striatum): habit learning and stimulus-response associations
  • Reconsolidation: retrieved memories become temporarily labile and can be modified — potential mechanism for updating traumatic memories in therapy
