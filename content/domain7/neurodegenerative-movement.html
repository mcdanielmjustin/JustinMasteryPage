Neurodegenerative & Movement Disorders

Domain 7: Biopsychology~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by the loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, which projects to the striatum (caudate nucleus and putamen) via the nigrostriatal pathway. The result is a dramatic reduction in striatal dopamine, producing the characteristic motor symptoms.

Cardinal Motor Features (TRAP):

Tremor (resting tremor, "pill-rolling," 4-6 Hz, decreases with voluntary movement); Rigidity (cogwheel or lead-pipe resistance to passive movement); Akinesia/Bradykinesia (slowness of movement, difficulty initiating movement, reduced facial expression [masked facies]); Postural instability (impaired balance, tendency to fall). The tremor is typically asymmetric at onset and worsens with stress.

Non-motor features include depression (affects ~40% of PD patients), cognitive impairment progressing to dementia in ~30-40% of cases, REM sleep behavior disorder, anosmia (loss of smell), autonomic dysfunction (orthostatic hypotension, constipation), and psychosis (visual hallucinations, often medication-related).

Treatment:

Levodopa (L-DOPA) is the gold-standard treatment — it is a dopamine precursor that crosses the blood-brain barrier and is converted to dopamine. It is typically combined with carbidopa (which prevents peripheral conversion) to reduce side effects. Long-term levodopa use can produce motor fluctuations ("wearing off," "on-off" phenomena) and dyskinesias (involuntary choreiform movements). Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole), MAO-B inhibitors, and deep brain stimulation of the subthalamic nucleus are additional treatment options.

Huntington's Disease

Huntington's disease (HD) is an autosomal dominant neurodegenerative disorder caused by an expanded CAG trinucleotide repeat on chromosome 4, producing an abnormal huntingtin protein. The disease is 100% penetrant — anyone who inherits the gene will develop the disease if they live long enough. Typical onset is between ages 30 and 50.

Pathology & Clinical Features:

HD primarily affects the caudate nucleus and putamen (striatum), producing progressive atrophy. The characteristic movement disorder is chorea — involuntary, irregular, dance-like movements affecting the limbs, trunk, and face. The clinical triad includes: (1) choreiform movements, (2) progressive cognitive decline (subcortical dementia pattern with executive dysfunction, slowed processing), and (3) psychiatric disturbances (depression, irritability, personality changes, psychosis). Psychiatric symptoms often precede motor symptoms by years.

EPPP Distinction — Parkinson's vs. Huntington's:

Parkinson's involves too little dopamine (substantia nigra degeneration) and produces hypokinetic movements (slow, reduced). Huntington's involves loss of inhibitory neurons in the striatum and produces hyperkinetic movements (excessive, involuntary chorea). Parkinson's is typically sporadic; Huntington's is autosomal dominant. Both can produce dementia, but the cognitive profiles differ: PD dementia is more posterior-cortical; HD dementia is subcortical-frontal.

Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune demyelinating disease of the central nervous system in which the immune system attacks oligodendrocytes and the myelin sheath. This produces plaques (lesions) of demyelination scattered throughout the brain and spinal cord, disrupting saltatory conduction and producing a wide range of neurological symptoms.

MS SubtypeCoursePrevalence
Relapsing-Remitting (RRMS)Discrete attacks (relapses) followed by partial or complete recovery; most common initial form~85% at onset
Secondary Progressive (SPMS)Initially RRMS, then gradual worsening with or without relapses~50% of RRMS convert within 10-15 years
Primary Progressive (PPMS)Gradual worsening from onset without distinct relapses~10-15%

Common symptoms include optic neuritis (inflammation of the optic nerve causing vision loss), sensory disturbances (numbness, tingling, Lhermitte's sign), motor weakness, spasticity, cerebellar ataxia, bladder dysfunction, fatigue, and cognitive impairment (particularly in processing speed, working memory, and executive function). Symptoms often worsen with heat (Uhthoff's phenomenon).

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis & Other Conditions

ALS (Lou Gehrig's Disease)

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a progressive motor neuron disease affecting both upper and lower motor neurons. It produces progressive muscle weakness, atrophy, fasciculations (muscle twitching), spasticity, and ultimately respiratory failure. Critically, ALS typically spares cognitive function, sensation, and eye movements until very late stages. Approximately 5-10% of cases are familial (genetic); the remainder are sporadic. Average survival is 3-5 years from symptom onset.

Wilson's Disease

Wilson's disease is an autosomal recessive disorder of copper metabolism causing toxic copper accumulation in the liver, brain (particularly the basal ganglia), and cornea. Neurological symptoms include tremor, dystonia, dysarthria, and psychiatric disturbances (personality changes, depression, psychosis). The pathognomonic sign is Kayser-Fleischer rings — golden-brown copper deposits visible in the cornea. It is treatable with copper chelation therapy (penicillamine) and zinc supplementation if diagnosed early.

Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder in which antibodies attack acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, causing progressive muscle weakness that worsens with activity and improves with rest. Characteristic features include ptosis (drooping eyelids), diplopia (double vision), difficulty swallowing, and limb weakness. Treatment includes cholinesterase inhibitors (pyridostigmine), immunosuppressants, and thymectomy.

Cerebellar Disorders:

The cerebellum coordinates voluntary movement, balance, and motor learning. Cerebellar damage produces a characteristic syndrome: ataxia (uncoordinated, staggering gait), dysmetria (inability to accurately reach for objects — overshooting or undershooting), intention tremor (worsens as the hand approaches the target — opposite of Parkinson's resting tremor), dysarthria (slurred speech), and nystagmus (involuntary eye movements). Cerebellar signs are ipsilateral to the lesion (unlike cerebral cortex, which is contralateral).

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Parkinson's disease: substantia nigra dopamine depletion; TRAP symptoms (tremor, rigidity, akinesia/bradykinesia, postural instability); resting tremor
  • Levodopa: gold-standard PD treatment; dopamine precursor; long-term use causes dyskinesias and motor fluctuations
  • Huntington's disease: autosomal dominant; CAG trinucleotide repeat on chromosome 4; caudate nucleus atrophy; chorea; 100% penetrance
  • Parkinson's = hypokinetic (too little movement); Huntington's = hyperkinetic (too much movement/chorea)
  • Multiple sclerosis: autoimmune CNS demyelination; relapsing-remitting is most common form; Uhthoff's phenomenon (heat worsens symptoms)
  • ALS: upper and lower motor neuron degeneration; progressive weakness; cognition typically preserved; fatal within 3-5 years
  • Wilson's disease: copper accumulation; Kayser-Fleischer rings; basal ganglia involvement; treatable
  • Myasthenia gravis: ACh receptor antibodies at neuromuscular junction; weakness worsens with activity; ptosis and diplopia
  • Cerebellar signs: ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor, dysarthria; signs are IPSILATERAL to the lesion
  • Intention tremor (cerebellar) worsens on reaching; resting tremor (Parkinson's) diminishes with movement
