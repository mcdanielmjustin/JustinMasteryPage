Neurons, Neurotransmitters & Neural Signaling

Domain 7: Biopsychology ~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Neuron Structure & Types

The neuron is the fundamental signaling unit of the nervous system. Although neurons vary widely in shape and size, they share a common structural plan consisting of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon.

  • Soma (cell body): Contains the nucleus and organelles; integrates incoming signals from dendrites
  • Dendrites: Branching extensions that receive signals from other neurons; the primary input structures
  • Axon: A single long fiber that conducts action potentials away from the soma toward the terminal buttons
  • Myelin sheath: A fatty insulating layer produced by oligodendrocytes (CNS) or Schwann cells (PNS) that dramatically increases conduction speed
  • Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps in the myelin sheath where ion channels are concentrated, enabling saltatory conduction
  • Terminal buttons (axon terminals): End points of the axon that release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft
Glial Cells:

Glial cells outnumber neurons and provide essential support functions. Astrocytes regulate the blood-brain barrier, maintain the extracellular environment, and modulate synaptic transmission. Oligodendrocytes produce myelin in the CNS; Schwann cells produce myelin in the PNS (important distinction for the EPPP). Microglia serve as the brain's immune cells, removing cellular debris and responding to infection or injury.

The Action Potential

The resting potential of a neuron is approximately -70 mV, maintained by the sodium-potassium pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in) and selective ion channel permeability. When excitatory inputs depolarize the membrane to the threshold (approximately -55 mV), an action potential is triggered.

Phases of the Action Potential

  1. Depolarization: Voltage-gated sodium (Na+) channels open, allowing rapid Na+ influx. The membrane potential rises toward +30 to +40 mV.
  2. Repolarization: Na+ channels inactivate and voltage-gated potassium (K+) channels open, allowing K+ efflux. The membrane potential returns toward resting level.
  3. Hyperpolarization: K+ channels remain open briefly, causing the membrane to overshoot below resting potential (~-80 mV) before returning to -70 mV.
All-or-None Law:

The all-or-none law states that an action potential either fires at full amplitude or does not fire at all — there is no partial action potential. The intensity of a stimulus is coded not by the amplitude of individual action potentials but by the frequency of firing (rate coding) and the number of neurons activated.

Refractory Periods

The absolute refractory period occurs during depolarization and early repolarization, when no stimulus (regardless of intensity) can trigger another action potential. The relative refractory period follows, during which a stronger-than-normal stimulus can trigger an action potential. These periods limit the maximum firing rate and ensure unidirectional propagation of the signal along the axon.

Saltatory Conduction:

Saltatory conduction refers to the "jumping" of action potentials from one node of Ranvier to the next along myelinated axons. This dramatically increases conduction velocity (up to 120 m/s in large myelinated fibers) compared to continuous conduction in unmyelinated fibers (~1-2 m/s). Demyelinating diseases such as multiple sclerosis disrupt saltatory conduction, slowing or blocking neural transmission.

Synaptic Transmission

Communication between neurons occurs at the synapse, the junction between a presynaptic terminal button and a postsynaptic dendrite (or cell body). The synaptic cleft — a gap of approximately 20-40 nanometers — separates the pre- and postsynaptic membranes. The sequence of synaptic transmission is as follows:

  1. The action potential arrives at the terminal button and triggers calcium (Ca2+) influx through voltage-gated calcium channels
  2. Ca2+ causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane and release neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft (exocytosis)
  3. Neurotransmitter molecules bind to postsynaptic receptors, producing excitatory or inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs or IPSPs)
  4. Neurotransmitter action is terminated by reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion away from the synapse
Agonists & Antagonists:

An agonist is a substance that mimics or enhances the action of a neurotransmitter (e.g., nicotine is an acetylcholine agonist; morphine is an endorphin agonist). An antagonist blocks or reduces the action of a neurotransmitter (e.g., curare blocks acetylcholine receptors; naloxone blocks opioid receptors). Reuptake inhibitors (e.g., SSRIs for serotonin) block the transporter that removes the neurotransmitter from the synaptic cleft, effectively increasing its availability and prolonging its action.

Dopamine Pathways (High-Yield for EPPP):

Four major dopamine pathways are clinically important: (1) Mesolimbic pathway: ventral tegmental area (VTA) to nucleus accumbens — reward, motivation; hyperactivity linked to positive symptoms of schizophrenia. (2) Mesocortical pathway: VTA to prefrontal cortex — executive function, motivation; hypoactivity linked to negative symptoms and cognitive deficits. (3) Nigrostriatal pathway: substantia nigra to striatum — motor control; degeneration causes Parkinson's; blockade by antipsychotics causes extrapyramidal symptoms. (4) Tuberoinfundibular pathway: hypothalamus to pituitary — inhibits prolactin; blockade causes hyperprolactinemia.

Behavioral Genetics

Behavioral genetics investigates the relative contributions of genetic and environmental factors to individual differences in behavior, cognition, and psychopathology. This field provides essential evidence for understanding the biological bases of psychological traits and disorders, and its concepts are heavily tested on the EPPP.

Heritability

Heritability Coefficient (h²):

The heritability coefficient (h²) estimates the proportion of variance in a trait within a population that is attributable to genetic differences among individuals. Critical EPPP points: (1) heritability applies to populations, not individuals — you cannot say "80% of this person's IQ is genetic"; (2) heritability does not indicate the degree to which a trait is "determined" by genes — a highly heritable trait can still be strongly influenced by environment; (3) heritability estimates are specific to the population and environment studied — they can change if environmental conditions change; (4) high heritability does not mean the trait is immutable or untreatable.

Twin Studies

Twin studies are the cornerstone method of behavioral genetics. They compare concordance rates (or correlations) between monozygotic (MZ, identical) twins, who share 100% of their DNA, and dizygotic (DZ, fraternal) twins, who share approximately 50% of their segregating genes.

  • If MZ concordance is substantially higher than DZ concordance, genetic factors are implicated
  • If MZ concordance is similar to DZ concordance, shared environmental factors are more important
  • If MZ concordance is less than 100%, environmental factors play a role (since MZ twins are genetically identical)
  • MZ twins reared apart provide the strongest evidence for genetic influence, as any similarity cannot be attributed to shared rearing environment (e.g., the Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart; Bouchard et al., 1990)

Adoption Studies

Adoption studies separate genetic from environmental contributions by comparing adopted children to both their biological parents (who share genes but not environment) and their adoptive parents (who share environment but not genes). If adopted children more closely resemble their biological parents on a trait, genetic factors are implicated. If they more closely resemble their adoptive parents, environmental factors are implicated. Adoption studies of schizophrenia (Heston, 1966; Kety et al., 1968) provided pivotal evidence for a genetic contribution to the disorder.

Shared vs. Nonshared Environment

Environmental Influences:

Shared environment (also called common environment) refers to environmental factors that make siblings growing up in the same family similar to one another — including family socioeconomic status, parenting style, neighborhood, and family diet. Nonshared environment refers to environmental factors that make siblings growing up in the same family different from one another — including differential parental treatment, different peer groups, unique life events, illness or injury, and birth order effects. A landmark finding in behavioral genetics is that for most psychological traits (personality, psychopathology), nonshared environment accounts for more variance than shared environment (Plomin & Daniels, 1987). This means siblings growing up in the same household often turn out quite different, and the family-wide environment is less important than sibling-specific experiences.

Gene-Environment Interaction vs. Gene-Environment Correlation

Two distinct processes describe how genes and environments work together:

  • Gene-environment interaction (GxE): The effect of a gene on a phenotype depends on the environment, and vice versa. The same genotype produces different outcomes in different environments. Classic example: Caspi et al. (2003) found that individuals with the short allele of the serotonin transporter gene (5-HTTLPR) were more likely to develop depression following stressful life events than those with the long allele — the genetic vulnerability was only expressed under environmental stress.
  • Gene-environment correlation (rGE): Genetic factors influence the probability of exposure to certain environments. Three types:
    • Passive rGE: Children passively inherit both genes and environments from their parents (e.g., musically gifted parents both pass on music-related genes and provide a musical home environment)
    • Evocative (reactive) rGE: A person's genetically influenced characteristics evoke particular responses from others (e.g., a genetically temperamental child elicits harsh parenting)
    • Active rGE (niche-picking): Individuals actively select environments that match their genetic predispositions (e.g., an extraverted adolescent seeks out social activities). Active rGE becomes increasingly important with age as individuals gain more autonomy over their environment.

Epigenetics

Epigenetics:

Epigenetics refers to changes in gene expression that occur without alterations to the underlying DNA sequence. Environmental factors — including stress, nutrition, toxin exposure, early caregiving, and trauma — can add or remove chemical tags (e.g., DNA methylation, histone modification) that turn genes "on" or "off." Critically, some epigenetic changes can be transmitted across generations (transgenerational epigenetic inheritance). Research by Meaney and colleagues demonstrated that rat pups who received more maternal licking and grooming showed epigenetic changes in glucocorticoid receptor genes, resulting in reduced stress reactivity throughout life — providing a biological mechanism by which early experience programs later stress vulnerability.

EPPP-Relevant Heritability Estimates:

The following approximate heritability estimates are commonly cited on the EPPP and in board-preparation materials. Remember that these are population-level estimates that vary by study and population:

IQ (general intelligence): h² ~ .50-.80 (heritability increases across development — higher in adults than children, as active rGE becomes stronger)

Schizophrenia: h² ~ .80 (MZ concordance ~48%; DZ concordance ~17%; general population risk ~1%)

Bipolar disorder: h² ~ .85 (among the most heritable psychiatric disorders)

Major depressive disorder: h² ~ .37 (moderate heritability; substantial environmental contribution)

Personality traits (Big Five): h² ~ .40-.60 (remarkably consistent across cultures and measurement methods)

Autism spectrum disorder: h² ~ .80-.90 (very high heritability; MZ concordance far exceeds DZ concordance)

Alcohol use disorder: h² ~ .50-.60 (moderate-to-high genetic contribution)

EPPP Application:

An exam question might state: "The heritability of intelligence is .70. This means that: (A) 70% of an individual's intelligence is determined by genes, (B) 70% of the variance in intelligence in the population studied is attributable to genetic differences, (C) genes contribute 70% and environment contributes 30% to intelligence, (D) if environment improves, intelligence cannot increase." The correct answer is (B). Choice (A) is the most common error — heritability describes population variance, not individual determination. Choice (D) is incorrect because even highly heritable traits can respond to environmental intervention (e.g., IQ gains from adoption into enriched environments).

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Neuron structure: dendrites (receive signals) → soma (integrates) → axon (conducts) → terminal buttons (release neurotransmitter)
  • Resting potential = -70 mV; threshold = ~-55 mV; action potential peaks at ~+30 to +40 mV
  • All-or-none law: action potentials fire at full amplitude or not at all; intensity coded by firing rate
  • Saltatory conduction: action potentials jump between nodes of Ranvier on myelinated axons; much faster than continuous conduction
  • Oligodendrocytes = myelin in CNS; Schwann cells = myelin in PNS
  • Reuptake: primary mechanism for terminating neurotransmitter action at many synapses; SSRIs block serotonin reuptake
  • Agonist = mimics/enhances neurotransmitter; Antagonist = blocks/reduces neurotransmitter action
  • GABA = main inhibitory NT; Glutamate = main excitatory NT; know their clinical associations
  • Dopamine pathways: mesolimbic (reward/positive symptoms), mesocortical (executive function/negative symptoms), nigrostriatal (motor/Parkinson's), tuberoinfundibular (prolactin)
  • Acetylcholine deficit in Alzheimer's; treated with cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine)
  • Excitotoxicity: excessive glutamate kills neurons; occurs in stroke, TBI, and neurodegenerative disease
  • Heritability (h²): proportion of trait variance attributable to genetic differences IN A POPULATION — NOT how much of an individual's trait is "genetic"; high heritability does not mean immutable
  • Twin studies: MZ concordance >> DZ concordance = genetic contribution; MZ concordance < 100% = environmental contribution; MZ twins reared apart = strongest design
  • Shared vs. nonshared environment: nonshared environment explains more variance in personality and psychopathology than shared (family-wide) environment
  • GxE interaction: gene effect depends on environment (5-HTTLPR x stress = depression); rGE correlation: passive (inherit genes + environment), evocative (genes elicit responses), active/niche-picking (select matching environments)
  • Key heritability estimates: schizophrenia ~.80, bipolar ~.85, MDD ~.37, IQ ~.50-.80 (increases with age), personality ~.40-.60; Epigenetics: environment alters gene expression without changing DNA sequence (methylation, histone modification)
