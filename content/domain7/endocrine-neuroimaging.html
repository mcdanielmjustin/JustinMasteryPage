Domain 7: Biopsychology~30 min read

The Endocrine System: Overview & the Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

The endocrine system communicates via hormones secreted into the bloodstream by glands. Unlike the rapid electrochemical signaling of the nervous system, endocrine signaling is slower but produces longer-lasting and more widespread effects. For the EPPP, the critical concept is the tight integration between the nervous and endocrine systems, particularly through the hypothalamic-pituitary axis.

Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis:

The hypothalamus serves as the master integrator, linking the nervous system to the endocrine system through the pituitary gland. The anterior pituitary (adenohypophysis) releases hormones in response to hypothalamic releasing and inhibiting factors delivered via the hypophyseal portal system. Key anterior pituitary hormones include ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone), TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone), GH (growth hormone), FSH, LH, and prolactin. The posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis) stores and releases oxytocin and vasopressin (ADH), which are actually synthesized in the hypothalamus and transported down axons to the posterior pituitary.

Endocrine regulation relies on negative feedback loops: when circulating hormone levels rise above a set point, the hypothalamus and pituitary reduce stimulatory signals. This is analogous to a thermostat. Disruptions to feedback loops produce the clinical conditions frequently tested on the EPPP.

HPA Axis & Stress:

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the primary neuroendocrine stress response system. Stress activates the hypothalamus to release CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone), which stimulates ACTH release from the anterior pituitary, which stimulates cortisol release from the adrenal cortex. Chronically elevated cortisol is associated with hippocampal atrophy, immune suppression, insulin resistance, and psychiatric conditions including depression and PTSD. Dexamethasone suppression test abnormalities (failure to suppress cortisol) have been associated with melancholic depression.

Thyroid Disorders & Psychiatric Manifestations

The thyroid gland produces thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3), hormones that regulate metabolism, energy, and brain development. The hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis controls thyroid function: the hypothalamus releases TRH, which stimulates TSH from the anterior pituitary, which stimulates T3/T4 release from the thyroid.

Clinical Pearl — Thyroid Screening:

Thyroid dysfunction is one of the most common medical mimics of psychiatric disorders. The EPPP tests knowledge that thyroid function should be evaluated (via TSH and free T4 levels) whenever a patient presents with new-onset depression, anxiety, or cognitive complaints, particularly in women over 40, postpartum women, and elderly patients. Congenital hypothyroidism (cretinism), if untreated, causes severe intellectual disability, emphasizing the critical role of thyroid hormone in brain development.

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is the most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-sufficient areas and is an autoimmune condition. Graves' disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism and is also autoimmune, caused by thyroid-stimulating antibodies that mimic TSH. Lithium, a first-line mood stabilizer for bipolar disorder, can cause hypothyroidism in 20-30% of patients, requiring regular thyroid monitoring.

Adrenal Gland Disorders

The adrenal glands sit atop each kidney and contain two functionally distinct regions. The adrenal medulla (inner portion) is part of the sympathetic nervous system and releases epinephrine and norepinephrine during the fight-or-flight response. The adrenal cortex (outer portion) produces corticosteroids: glucocorticoids (cortisol), mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), and androgens.

Cushing's Syndrome vs. Addison's Disease:

Cushing's syndrome results from chronic cortisol excess, caused by exogenous corticosteroid use (most common), pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease), or adrenal tumor. Psychiatric features include depression (most common, ~50-80%), cognitive impairment, psychosis, and emotional lability. Physical signs: central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, purple striae, easy bruising, osteoporosis, and hypertension. Addison's disease results from adrenal insufficiency (insufficient cortisol and aldosterone production). Psychiatric features include fatigue, depression, irritability, social withdrawal, and cognitive slowing. Physical signs: hyperpigmentation, weight loss, hypotension, salt craving, and potentially life-threatening adrenal crisis. The classic EPPP distinction is that Cushing's produces cortisol excess effects while Addison's produces cortisol deficiency effects.

Sex Hormones & Behavioral Effects

Sex hormones (estrogen, progesterone, testosterone) are produced primarily by the gonads and also by the adrenal cortex. These hormones have significant effects on brain function and behavior throughout the lifespan.

  • Testosterone: Associated with aggression, dominance behavior, spatial cognition, and libido. Declining testosterone in older males (andropause) can produce fatigue, depressed mood, and cognitive changes. Anabolic steroid abuse can cause mood instability, aggression ("roid rage"), and psychotic symptoms.
  • Estrogen: Has neuroprotective effects, modulates serotonin receptors, and influences mood regulation. Estrogen withdrawal contributes to postpartum depression, perimenopausal mood changes, and premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD). Estrogen decline after menopause is associated with increased risk for Alzheimer's disease.
  • Oxytocin: Released during childbirth, breastfeeding, and social bonding. Promotes trust, pair bonding, and social recognition. Sometimes called the "bonding hormone," though its effects are context-dependent and more nuanced than popular accounts suggest.
Nemeroff, C. B. (2007). The Neurobiology of Depression. Scientific American, 296(6), 42-49. The HPA axis dysregulation model of depression remains one of the most replicated findings in biological psychiatry.

Structural Neuroimaging: CT & MRI

Neuroimaging techniques are categorized as structural (visualizing brain anatomy) or functional (measuring brain activity). The EPPP tests knowledge of when each modality is indicated and what it reveals.

ModalityMechanismStrengthsLimitationsPrimary Uses
CT (computed tomography)X-ray beams rotated around the head; computer reconstruction of cross-sectional imagesFast (minutes), widely available, excellent for bone and acute hemorrhageIonizing radiation, lower soft tissue resolution than MRIEmergency evaluation of acute stroke (rule out hemorrhage), head trauma, skull fractures
MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)Strong magnetic field aligns hydrogen atoms; radiofrequency pulses cause signal emission detected by receiver coilsSuperior soft tissue contrast, no ionizing radiation, multiplanar imagingSlower, more expensive, contraindicated with certain metal implants and pacemakers, claustrophobiaDetailed brain anatomy, white matter lesions (MS plaques), tumor characterization, hippocampal atrophy
Key MRI Variants:

Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) detects early ischemic stroke within minutes (before CT shows changes), making it the gold standard for acute stroke diagnosis. Diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) maps white matter tracts by tracking water molecule diffusion along axon bundles (tractography), used to assess traumatic axonal injury and white matter connectivity. FLAIR (Fluid-Attenuated Inversion Recovery) suppresses cerebrospinal fluid signal, making periventricular white matter lesions (as in MS) more visible.

Functional Neuroimaging: fMRI, PET & SPECT

Functional neuroimaging captures brain activity in real time (or near-real time), measuring metabolic or hemodynamic changes associated with neural activity.

EPPP Application — Choosing the Right Imaging:

For the EPPP, know these clinical decision rules: (1) Acute hemorrhagic stroke = CT first (fast, detects blood immediately); (2) Acute ischemic stroke = MRI with DWI (most sensitive early); (3) MS white matter plaques = MRI with FLAIR; (4) Alzheimer's disease = PET (temporoparietal hypometabolism) or MRI (hippocampal atrophy); (5) Epileptic focus localization = ictal SPECT or interictal PET; (6) Presurgical language mapping = fMRI or Wada test; (7) Research on real-time cognition = fMRI (best combination of spatial and temporal resolution among non-invasive methods).

Electrophysiological Methods: EEG & Evoked Potentials

The electroencephalogram (EEG) records electrical activity from the scalp using surface electrodes. It has excellent temporal resolution (milliseconds) but poor spatial resolution compared to neuroimaging. EEG is the primary diagnostic tool for epilepsy and is essential for sleep staging.

Evoked Potentials (EPs):

Evoked potentials are EEG recordings time-locked to specific sensory stimuli, averaged over many trials to extract signal from noise. Types include visual evoked potentials (VEPs), brainstem auditory evoked responses (BAERs), and somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs). The P300 is a cognitive evoked potential (event-related potential, or ERP) appearing ~300 ms after a rare target stimulus. The P300 amplitude is reduced in schizophrenia and Alzheimer's disease and is used in research on attention and working memory. Evoked potentials are particularly useful for detecting subclinical demyelination in multiple sclerosis.

Other Diagnostic Procedures

Lumbar Puncture (Spinal Tap)

A lumbar puncture involves inserting a needle into the subarachnoid space (typically L3-L4 or L4-L5 level) to collect cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) for analysis. Indications include suspected meningitis or encephalitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage (when CT is negative), normal pressure hydrocephalus (therapeutic and diagnostic), and certain dementia evaluations (CSF biomarkers for Alzheimer's: decreased amyloid-beta-42, increased tau and phospho-tau).

The Wada Test (Intracarotid Amobarbital Procedure)

Wada Test:

The Wada test involves injecting a barbiturate (sodium amobarbital) into one internal carotid artery at a time, temporarily anesthetizing one hemisphere. While one hemisphere is "offline," the patient is tested for language and memory functions to determine hemispheric dominance. Historically used before epilepsy surgery to identify the language-dominant hemisphere and assess whether the contralateral hemisphere can support memory after temporal lobectomy. Increasingly being replaced by fMRI for language lateralization, though Wada remains important for memory adequacy testing.

Neuropsychological Assessment in Diagnostic Context

Neuropsychological testing complements neuroimaging by providing sensitive measures of cognitive function. Key assessment batteries and their applications include:

  • Halstead-Reitan Neuropsychological Battery: Comprehensive fixed battery measuring attention, motor function, sensory-perceptual abilities, language, and abstract reasoning. Includes the Category Test, Tactual Performance Test, Trail Making Tests (A and B), and Finger Oscillation Test.
  • Luria-Nebraska Neuropsychological Battery: Based on Luria's theory of functional systems; includes scales for motor function, rhythm, tactile function, visual function, receptive and expressive speech, writing, reading, arithmetic, memory, and intellectual processes.
  • Boston Diagnostic Aphasia Examination (BDAE): Comprehensive assessment of language function used to classify aphasia type and severity.
  • Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST): Measures executive function, cognitive flexibility, and abstract reasoning; sensitive to frontal lobe dysfunction. Perseverative errors suggest prefrontal pathology.
Lezak, M. D., Howieson, D. B., Bigler, E. D., & Tranel, D. (2012). Neuropsychological Assessment (5th ed.). Oxford University Press. The standard reference for clinical neuropsychological assessment methodology.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Hypothalamus links nervous and endocrine systems through the pituitary; anterior pituitary releases ACTH, TSH, GH, FSH, LH, prolactin; posterior pituitary releases oxytocin and vasopressin
  • HPA axis: hypothalamus (CRH) → anterior pituitary (ACTH) → adrenal cortex (cortisol); chronic elevation = hippocampal atrophy, immune suppression, depression
  • Hypothyroidism mimics depression; hyperthyroidism mimics anxiety/mania. Always screen thyroid when psychiatric symptoms present
  • Lithium causes hypothyroidism in 20-30% of patients; requires thyroid monitoring
  • Cushing's = cortisol excess (moon face, central obesity, depression); Addison's = cortisol deficiency (hyperpigmentation, fatigue, hypotension)
  • CT: fast, best for acute hemorrhage and bone; MRI: best soft tissue resolution, no radiation
  • DWI-MRI: earliest detection of ischemic stroke; FLAIR-MRI: best for MS plaques
  • fMRI measures BOLD signal; best spatial resolution among functional methods; used for presurgical mapping
  • PET: detects Alzheimer's hypometabolism (temporoparietal), dopamine system (Parkinson's); uses radioactive tracers
  • EEG: best temporal resolution (ms); primary tool for epilepsy diagnosis; alpha = relaxed wakefulness, delta = deep sleep
  • P300 ERP: reduced amplitude in schizophrenia and Alzheimer's; ~300 ms after rare target stimulus
  • Wada test: anesthetizes one hemisphere to lateralize language; being replaced by fMRI
  • WCST: measures executive function/cognitive flexibility; perseverative errors = frontal lobe dysfunction
  • Halstead-Reitan: fixed comprehensive battery; Luria-Nebraska: based on functional systems theory
