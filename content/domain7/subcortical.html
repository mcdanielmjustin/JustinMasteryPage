Overview of Subcortical Structures

Subcortical structures are brain regions located beneath the cerebral cortex that perform vital functions including relaying sensory information, regulating homeostasis, coordinating movement, and mediating emotion and memory. For the EPPP, it is essential to know the key subcortical structures, their locations, their functions, and the clinical consequences of their damage.

Subcortical: Refers to structures situated below the cerebral cortex, including the thalamus, hypothalamus, basal ganglia, limbic system structures, brainstem, and cerebellum. These regions are phylogenetically older and more critical for survival-related functions than cortical structures.

The Thalamus

The thalamus is a large, egg-shaped structure sitting atop the brainstem at the center of the brain. It serves as the brain's primary sensory relay station, routing incoming sensory information to the appropriate cortical areas.

  • Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN): Relays visual information from the optic tract to the primary visual cortex (V1)
  • Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN): Relays auditory information from the inferior colliculus to the primary auditory cortex
  • Ventral posterolateral (VPL) nucleus: Relays somatosensory information from the body to the primary somatosensory cortex
  • Ventral posteromedial (VPM) nucleus: Relays somatosensory information from the face
  • Dorsomedial nucleus: Connects to the prefrontal cortex; involved in memory and emotion. Damage is implicated in Korsakoff's syndrome.
  • Pulvinar: Involved in visual attention and filtering of visual information

Clinical Significance: Olfaction (smell) is the only sensory modality that does not relay through the thalamus. Olfactory information travels directly from the olfactory bulb to the piriform cortex and amygdala. This is a frequently tested fact on the EPPP.

The Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus is a small structure (approximately the size of an almond) located below the thalamus. Despite its small size, it is critical for maintaining homeostasis and regulating the endocrine system through its connection with the pituitary gland.

The "Four F's" of the Hypothalamus

A classic mnemonic for the hypothalamus's primary regulatory functions:

  • Fighting (aggression): The ventromedial hypothalamus is involved in aggressive behavior
  • Fleeing (fear responses): Coordinates autonomic fear responses through connections with the amygdala and brainstem
  • Feeding: The lateral hypothalamus (LH) is the "hunger center" -- stimulation induces eating, and lesions cause aphagia (refusal to eat). The ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) is the "satiety center" -- stimulation inhibits eating, and lesions cause hyperphagia (overeating) and obesity.
  • Mating (sexual behavior): Regulates sexual behavior and reproductive functions; the sexually dimorphic nucleus differs in size between males and females

Other Hypothalamic Functions

  • Thermoregulation: Maintains body temperature through sweating, shivering, and vasoconstriction/vasodilation
  • Circadian rhythms: The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) serves as the master biological clock, entrained by light input from the retina
  • Endocrine control: Regulates the pituitary gland ("master gland") through releasing and inhibiting hormones. Controls the HPA axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis) for stress responses.
  • Thirst and water balance: Osmoreceptors detect blood osmolarity changes and trigger thirst and ADH release

EPPP Application: Lesion studies are frequently tested. Remember: lateral hypothalamus lesions produce "lateral leanness" (aphagia/weight loss), while ventromedial hypothalamus lesions produce "ventromedial voluminosity" (hyperphagia/obesity). These mnemonics appear on many EPPP study resources.

The Limbic System

The limbic system is a collection of interconnected structures surrounding the thalamus that mediate emotion, motivation, learning, and memory. While the exact boundaries of the limbic system are debated, the following structures are consistently included:

Hippocampus

A seahorse-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe that is essential for the consolidation of new declarative (explicit) memories -- the transfer of information from short-term to long-term storage.

  • Bilateral hippocampal damage produces severe anterograde amnesia for declarative information (the case of H.M.)
  • Contains place cells (O'Keefe, Nobel Prize 2014) that fire when an animal occupies a specific location, forming cognitive maps for spatial navigation
  • One of only two brain regions where adult neurogenesis has been confirmed (the other is the olfactory bulb/subventricular zone)
  • Particularly vulnerable to damage from chronic stress (cortisol is neurotoxic to hippocampal neurons), hypoxia, and Alzheimer's disease

Amygdala

An almond-shaped structure anterior to the hippocampus, the amygdala is the brain's "fear center" and is critical for emotional processing, particularly fear conditioning and threat detection.

  • Receives rapid, coarse sensory input via a direct pathway from the thalamus (LeDoux's "low road"), enabling fast fear responses before cortical processing is complete
  • Also receives slower, detailed input from the cortex ("high road") for more nuanced evaluation
  • Modulates memory consolidation: emotionally arousing events are remembered better because amygdala activation enhances hippocampal encoding
  • Bilateral damage (Kluver-Bucy syndrome) produces "psychic blindness" -- inability to recognize emotional significance of stimuli, hypersexuality, hyperorality, and flattened affect

Cingulate Cortex

The cingulate gyrus wraps around the corpus callosum and is divided into functionally distinct regions:

  • Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC): Involved in error detection, conflict monitoring, pain perception, and emotional regulation. Active when there is a mismatch between expected and actual outcomes. Plays a role in the emotional experience of pain.
  • Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC): Part of the default mode network; involved in self-referential processing, autobiographical memory retrieval, and internal mentation. One of the first regions affected in Alzheimer's disease.

Other Limbic Structures

  • Mammillary bodies: Part of the Papez circuit; relay information from the hippocampus to the thalamus. Degeneration occurs in Korsakoff's syndrome (thiamine deficiency) and contributes to anterograde amnesia and confabulation.
  • Fornix: Major white matter tract connecting the hippocampus to the mammillary bodies and septal nuclei
  • Septal nuclei: Involved in pleasure, reward, and the inhibition of aggression

Basal Ganglia

The basal ganglia are a group of subcortical nuclei involved in the initiation, execution, and modulation of voluntary movement, as well as procedural learning and habit formation.

Components

  • Caudate nucleus: Involved in cognitive control of movement, goal-directed behavior, and procedural memory
  • Putamen: Primary motor component; involved in the initiation and execution of movement. Together with the caudate, forms the striatum (so named for its striped appearance).
  • Globus pallidus: Output nucleus of the basal ganglia; sends inhibitory signals to the thalamus to regulate motor activity
  • Subthalamic nucleus: Regulates the globus pallidus; lesions or deep brain stimulation here can reduce Parkinsonian symptoms
  • Substantia nigra: Located in the midbrain; its pars compacta produces dopamine that is sent to the striatum via the nigrostriatal pathway. Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons here is the primary pathology of Parkinson's disease.

Clinical Significance of Basal Ganglia Dysfunction

DisorderPathologyMotor Symptoms
Parkinson's diseaseLoss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigraBradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity, postural instability (hypokinetic)

Clinical Significance: The direct pathway through the basal ganglia facilitates movement (excitatory net effect), while the indirect pathway inhibits movement (inhibitory net effect). Parkinson's disease (too little dopamine) results in difficulty initiating movement. Huntington's disease (loss of inhibitory neurons) results in excessive, uncontrolled movement.

The Brainstem

The brainstem connects the cerebral hemispheres to the spinal cord and controls vital autonomic functions. It consists of three major divisions:

Midbrain (Mesencephalon)

  • Superior colliculi: Visual reflexes and orienting responses (e.g., turning the head toward a visual stimulus)
  • Inferior colliculi: Auditory reflexes and relay of auditory information to the thalamus
  • Substantia nigra: Dopamine production for motor control (part of basal ganglia circuitry)
  • Ventral tegmental area (VTA): Dopamine production for the mesolimbic reward pathway (projects to nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex)
  • Periaqueductal gray (PAG): Involved in pain modulation and defensive behaviors

Pons

  • Relays information between the cerebellum and cerebral cortex
  • Contains nuclei for cranial nerves V (trigeminal), VI (abducens), VII (facial), and VIII (vestibulocochlear)
  • Contains the locus coeruleus, the brain's primary source of norepinephrine, involved in arousal, attention, and the stress response
  • Involved in regulating sleep, particularly REM sleep transitions

Medulla Oblongata

  • Controls vital autonomic functions: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and digestive reflexes
  • Contains the raphe nuclei, the brain's primary source of serotonin
  • Site of the pyramidal decussation, where motor fibers cross from one side to the other (explaining contralateral motor control)
  • Damage is often life-threatening due to disruption of vital functions

The Cerebellum

The cerebellum ("little brain") is located posterior to the brainstem and contains more neurons than the rest of the brain combined. Although comprising only 10% of brain volume, it contains approximately 50% of the brain's neurons.

  • Motor coordination: Fine-tunes motor output for smooth, coordinated, accurate movements
  • Balance and posture: Integrates vestibular input to maintain equilibrium
  • Motor learning: Essential for acquiring skilled movements and classical conditioning of motor responses (e.g., eyeblink conditioning)
  • Timing: Internal clock for the precise timing of movements and cognitive operations
  • Cognitive functions: Increasingly recognized role in language, attention, and executive function

Cerebellar Damage: Produces ataxia (loss of coordination), dysarthria (slurred speech), intention tremor (tremor that worsens as the hand approaches a target, in contrast to Parkinson's resting tremor), nystagmus (involuntary eye movements), and gait instability. Cerebellar damage does NOT cause paralysis.

Reticular Activating System (RAS)

The reticular activating system is a network of neurons extending through the brainstem core from the medulla to the midbrain. It plays a critical role in regulating consciousness, arousal, and the sleep-wake cycle.

  • Filters incoming sensory information, determining what reaches conscious awareness
  • Maintains cortical alertness through diffuse projections to the thalamus and cortex
  • Damage to the RAS can produce coma or persistent vegetative state
  • Anesthetic agents exert their effects partly by suppressing RAS activity

Clinical Significance: The RAS is clinically important for understanding disorders of consciousness. Coma results from bilateral cortical damage or damage to the RAS. Damage to specific brainstem regions can produce "locked-in syndrome," where patients are conscious but unable to move or communicate except through eye movements.
