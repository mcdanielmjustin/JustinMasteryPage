Predicting Outcomes: Criterion-Related Validity

Predictive validity, sensitivity, specificity, and clinical utility
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Introduction to Criterion-Related Validity

Criterion-related validity refers to the degree to which test scores correlate with an external criterion—a measure of the outcome the test is designed to predict or relate to. This form of validity evidence is essential when tests are used for selection, classification, or prediction purposes (AERA et al., 2014). The strength of the relationship between test scores and criterion performance is quantified by a validity coefficient, typically a Pearson correlation.

Key Terminology

  • Criterion: An external measure of the outcome a test is designed to predict
  • Validity Coefficient: Correlation between predictor test scores and criterion scores
  • Predictor: The test or assessment used to forecast criterion performance
  • Hit Rate: Proportion of correct decisions (predictions) made using the test

Criterion-related validity is subdivided into two types based on when the criterion is measured: predictive validity involves a time interval between testing and criterion measurement, while concurrent validity involves simultaneous measurement of predictor and criterion.

Predictive and Concurrent Validity

Predictive Validity

Predictive validity is demonstrated when test scores predict future performance on a criterion measured at a later time. This is the most important type of criterion-related validity for selection decisions, as it addresses the practical question: "How well does this test predict future outcomes?"

Predictive Validity Example

An admissions test administered to graduate school applicants is used to predict first-year GPA. A correlation of .45 between admission test scores and subsequent GPA demonstrates predictive validity. The test provides useful information about likely academic performance, though the moderate correlation indicates other factors also influence success.

Concurrent Validity

Concurrent validity is demonstrated when test scores correlate with a criterion measured at approximately the same time. This approach is often used when developing a shorter or more efficient assessment to replace a longer criterion measure, or when establishing that a new test measures the same construct as an established instrument.

Predictive vs. Concurrent Validity

Feature Predictive Concurrent
Timing Criterion measured later Criterion measured simultaneously
Purpose Forecast future outcomes Substitute for existing measure
Sample Applicants/candidates Current group members
Limitation Time-consuming, attrition May not generalize to prediction

When to Use Each Approach

  • Predictive validity is preferred for selection/admissions decisions where forecasting future performance is the goal
  • Concurrent validity is appropriate when substituting one measure for another, screening, or diagnosis where the criterion is currently observable
  • Concurrent validity evidence should not be assumed to generalize to predictive contexts without additional investigation

Factors Affecting Validity Coefficients

Several factors can attenuate or inflate validity coefficients, making it important to interpret them in context.

Restriction of Range

Restriction of range occurs when the variability of scores in the validation sample is less than in the applicant population. This commonly happens in predictive validity studies when only those who score above a cutoff are selected, reducing the range of predictor scores available for validation. Range restriction attenuates the observed validity coefficient.

Range Restriction Example

A company uses a cognitive ability test to select employees, hiring only those who score above the 70th percentile. When validating the test against job performance, only selected employees (those with high scores) are available. The restricted range of test scores reduces the observed validity coefficient. Statistical corrections can estimate what the validity would be in the full applicant population.

Criterion Contamination

Criterion contamination occurs when knowledge of predictor scores influences criterion ratings. If supervisors know employees' test scores, their performance ratings may be biased by this knowledge, artificially inflating the validity coefficient.

Criterion Deficiency

Criterion deficiency occurs when the criterion measure fails to capture all relevant aspects of the outcome domain. A deficient criterion underestimates true validity because the test may validly predict aspects of performance not captured by the criterion.

Unreliability

Unreliability in either the predictor or criterion attenuates the validity coefficient. The correction for attenuation formula estimates what validity would be if both measures were perfectly reliable:

rxy(corrected) = rxy / √(rxx × ryy)

Base Rates and Selection Ratios

The practical utility of a test depends not only on its validity but also on the base rate and selection ratio.

Base Rate

The base rate is the proportion of people in the population who would succeed on the criterion without selection (or who have the condition being assessed). Base rate affects how much a test can improve upon chance prediction.

Base Rate Effects

  • Very high base rate (e.g., 90%): Most people succeed; test has little room to improve prediction
  • Very low base rate (e.g., 10%): Most people fail/don't have condition; predicting "no" is often correct
  • Moderate base rate (around 50%): Maximum opportunity for test to improve prediction

Selection Ratio

The selection ratio is the proportion of applicants who are selected. A lower selection ratio (selecting fewer applicants) allows for selecting only those with the highest scores, potentially improving outcomes but reducing the number selected.

Taylor-Russell Tables

Taylor and Russell (1939) developed tables showing the expected success rate given different combinations of validity coefficient, base rate, and selection ratio. These tables demonstrate that even tests with modest validity can substantially improve selection outcomes when base rates are moderate and selection ratios are low.

Sensitivity and Specificity

When tests are used for classification or diagnosis, four outcomes are possible. Sensitivity and specificity describe the accuracy of the test in identifying true positives and true negatives.

Classification Outcomes

Condition Present Condition Absent
Test Positive True Positive (TP) False Positive (FP)
Test Negative False Negative (FN) True Negative (TN)

Sensitivity

Sensitivity (also called true positive rate or recall) is the proportion of people with the condition who test positive. It indicates how well the test detects the condition when it is present.

Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)

Specificity

Specificity (also called true negative rate) is the proportion of people without the condition who test negative. It indicates how well the test correctly identifies the absence of the condition.

Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)

Sensitivity and Specificity Example

A depression screening test is administered to 1,000 patients. Of 200 who actually have depression, 180 test positive (sensitivity = 180/200 = 90%). Of 800 without depression, 720 test negative (specificity = 720/800 = 90%). The test correctly identifies 90% of depressed patients and 90% of non-depressed patients.

Positive and Negative Predictive Value

While sensitivity and specificity describe test performance, clinicians often need to know: "Given a positive (or negative) test result, what is the probability the person has (or doesn't have) the condition?"

Positive Predictive Value (PPV) is the proportion of positive test results that are true positives:

PPV = TP / (TP + FP)

Negative Predictive Value (NPV) is the proportion of negative test results that are true negatives:

NPV = TN / (TN + FN)

Base Rate and Predictive Values

Predictive values are strongly influenced by base rate:

  • When base rate is low, PPV is low (most positives are false positives) even with good sensitivity/specificity
  • When base rate is high, NPV is low (most negatives are false negatives)
  • This is why screening tests in low-prevalence populations often require confirmation testing

Incremental Validity

Incremental validity refers to the degree to which a test improves prediction beyond what is already achievable with existing information or measures. A test may have adequate validity on its own but add little when combined with other predictors that measure similar constructs (Hunsley & Meyer, 2003).

Assessing Incremental Validity

Incremental validity is typically assessed using hierarchical multiple regression:

  • Step 1: Enter existing predictors and note R²
  • Step 2: Add the new test and note the change in R² (ΔR²)
  • If ΔR² is statistically significant and practically meaningful, the new test has incremental validity

Incremental Validity Example

A clinician wants to know if a new personality measure adds predictive value for therapy outcome beyond established predictors (severity, chronicity, social support). Existing predictors account for 25% of outcome variance. Adding the personality measure increases this to 32%. The 7% increase (ΔR² = .07) represents the incremental validity of the new measure.

Clinical Decision-Making

When selecting assessment instruments, consider:

  • Does this test provide information beyond what I already have?
  • Does the incremental validity justify the time and cost of administration?
  • Would the additional information change my clinical decisions?

Clinical Utility

Clinical utility refers to the practical value of a test in clinical settings—whether using the test improves patient outcomes or clinical decision-making. A test may have adequate validity evidence but limited clinical utility if it doesn't lead to better decisions or outcomes.

Components of Clinical Utility

  • Treatment selection: Does the test help match patients to effective treatments?
  • Outcome prediction: Does the test predict who will benefit from intervention?
  • Progress monitoring: Is the test sensitive to changes during treatment?
  • Cost-effectiveness: Do the benefits of testing justify the costs?

Evaluating Clinical Utility

Questions to ask about a test's clinical utility:

  • Does use of this test lead to different clinical decisions than would be made without it?
  • Do those different decisions result in better patient outcomes?
  • Is the test feasible to administer in typical clinical settings?
  • Is the test acceptable to patients and clinicians?

Decision Theory and Cutoff Scores

When tests are used to make categorical decisions (select/reject, diagnose/not diagnose), a cutoff score must be established. The choice of cutoff involves trade-offs between different types of errors.

Setting Cutoff Scores

Lowering the cutoff score increases sensitivity but decreases specificity (more true positives but also more false positives). Raising the cutoff increases specificity but decreases sensitivity (fewer false positives but also more false negatives).

Trade-offs in Cutoff Selection

Lower Cutoff Higher Cutoff
↑ Sensitivity (more true positives) ↑ Specificity (more true negatives)
↓ Specificity (more false positives) ↓ Sensitivity (more false negatives)
Best when missing a case is costly Best when false alarms are costly

Cutoff Selection Example

For a suicide risk screening, a lower cutoff is appropriate because the cost of missing someone at risk (false negative) is much greater than the cost of additional evaluation for someone not at risk (false positive). For a highly invasive or stigmatizing diagnosis, a higher cutoff might be preferred to minimize false positives.

ROC Curves

Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves plot sensitivity against (1 - specificity) across all possible cutoff scores. The area under the curve (AUC) provides an overall index of diagnostic accuracy independent of any particular cutoff. AUC of .50 indicates chance performance; AUC of 1.0 indicates perfect discrimination.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Predictive validity: Criterion measured after testing (forecasting future performance)
  • Concurrent validity: Criterion measured simultaneously (substituting measures)
  • Restriction of range attenuates validity coefficients; occurs when only selected individuals are available for validation
  • Criterion contamination: Knowledge of predictor scores influences criterion ratings (inflates validity)
  • Base rate: Proportion with condition/who succeed without selection; affects test utility
  • Selection ratio: Proportion selected; lower ratio allows selecting only top scorers
  • Sensitivity = TP/(TP+FN): Ability to detect true positives (people with condition)
  • Specificity = TN/(TN+FP): Ability to detect true negatives (people without condition)
  • PPV and NPV are influenced by base rate—low base rate = low PPV even with good sensitivity/specificity
  • Incremental validity: Improvement in prediction beyond existing measures (assessed with ΔR²)
  • Lowering cutoff ↑ sensitivity ↓ specificity; raising cutoff ↓ sensitivity ↑ specificity
