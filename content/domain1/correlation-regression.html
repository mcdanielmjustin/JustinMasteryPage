Relationships in Data: Correlation & Regression

Introduction to Correlation

Correlation is a statistical technique used to describe and measure the relationship between two variables. Understanding correlation is essential for psychological research and practice, as it underlies many assessment concepts including test reliability and validity. The correlation coefficient quantifies both the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables (Cohen et al., 2003).

It is critical to understand that correlation does not imply causation. Even a strong correlation between two variables does not establish that one variable causes changes in the other. The relationship could be due to a third variable influencing both, reverse causation, or coincidence.

Key Terminology

Correlation Coefficient: A numerical index ranging from -1.00 to +1.00 that indicates the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables.

Positive Correlation: As one variable increases, the other tends to increase (e.g., height and weight).

Negative Correlation: As one variable increases, the other tends to decrease (e.g., stress and immune function).

Zero Correlation: No systematic linear relationship exists between the variables.

Types of Correlation Coefficients

Pearson Product-Moment Correlation (r)

The Pearson r is the most commonly used correlation coefficient and is appropriate when both variables are measured on interval or ratio scales and the relationship is linear. The Pearson r measures the degree to which data points fall on a straight line.

Assumptions for Pearson r include:

  • Both variables are continuous (interval or ratio scale)
  • The relationship between variables is linear
  • Both variables are normally distributed (for significance testing)
  • Homoscedasticity: variance is similar across the range of scores

Spearman Rank-Order Correlation (rho)

Spearman's rho (ρ) is used when one or both variables are measured on an ordinal scale, or when the relationship is monotonic but not linear. This coefficient correlates the ranks of the data rather than the raw scores. Spearman's rho is also more robust to outliers than Pearson's r (Field, 2018).

Point-Biserial Correlation

The point-biserial correlation is used when one variable is continuous and the other is a true dichotomy (e.g., male/female, pass/fail). It is mathematically equivalent to the Pearson r but is appropriate for this specific combination of variable types.

Phi Coefficient

The phi coefficient (φ) is used when both variables are true dichotomies. It is commonly used in 2×2 contingency tables and is related to chi-square statistics.

Choosing the Right Correlation Coefficient

Both continuous: Pearson r (if linear) or Spearman rho (if non-linear or ordinal)

One continuous, one dichotomous: Point-biserial correlation

Both dichotomous: Phi coefficient

Both ordinal: Spearman rho

Interpreting Correlation Coefficients

Strength of Correlation

The absolute value of the correlation coefficient indicates the strength of the relationship. Cohen (1988) provided widely used guidelines for interpreting effect sizes in behavioral sciences:

Absolute Value of r Interpretation
.10 to .29 Small (weak)
.30 to .49 Medium (moderate)
.50 and above Large (strong)

However, these guidelines should be interpreted in context. In some research areas (e.g., personality psychology), correlations of .30 are considered substantial, while in others (e.g., test-retest reliability), correlations below .80 might be considered inadequate.

Coefficient of Determination (r²)

The coefficient of determination (r²) is obtained by squaring the correlation coefficient. It represents the proportion of variance in one variable that is explained by (or shared with) the other variable. This is also referred to as shared variance or common variance.

Example: Interpreting r²

If the correlation between SAT scores and college GPA is r = .50, then r² = .25. This means that 25% of the variance in college GPA can be explained by SAT scores. Conversely, 75% of the variance in GPA is due to other factors not captured by SAT scores. This interpretation helps put correlation coefficients in practical perspective.

Coefficient of Alienation

The coefficient of alienation represents the proportion of variance that is NOT shared between two variables. It is calculated as 1 - r², or equivalently, it indicates the proportion of unexplained variance.

Factors Affecting Correlation

Restriction of Range

Restriction of range occurs when the variability in one or both variables is limited compared to the full population. This artificial restriction attenuates (reduces) the observed correlation coefficient. When range is restricted, correlations appear weaker than they would be in the unrestricted population (Sackett & Yang, 2000).

Example: Range Restriction in Selection

A company uses a cognitive ability test to select employees, hiring only those who score above the 75th percentile. When later examining the correlation between test scores and job performance among hired employees, they find a modest correlation of r = .20. However, if they could have studied all applicants (unrestricted range), the correlation might be r = .50. The selection process restricted the range of test scores, attenuating the observed validity coefficient.

Outliers

Extreme scores (outliers) can dramatically affect correlation coefficients, either inflating or deflating them depending on their position. A single outlier can change a strong correlation to a weak one or vice versa. Researchers should always examine scatterplots and consider the influence of outliers on their findings.

Non-linearity

Pearson's r only captures linear relationships. If the relationship between variables is curvilinear (e.g., the Yerkes-Dodson law relating arousal and performance), Pearson's r will underestimate the strength of the actual relationship. Examining scatterplots is essential for detecting non-linear patterns.

Heteroscedasticity

Heteroscedasticity occurs when the variability of one variable differs across levels of the other variable. For example, the variance in job performance might be larger for people with low test scores than for people with high scores. This violates an assumption of Pearson's r and can affect the accuracy of the correlation coefficient.

Clinical Implications

Understanding factors that affect correlation is critical for interpreting validity studies. When a test shows lower validity in clinical samples than in research samples, restriction of range may be responsible. Clinicians should consider whether the validation sample is representative of the population they serve.

Simple Linear Regression

Regression extends correlation by allowing prediction of one variable from another. While correlation describes the relationship between variables, regression provides a mathematical equation for predicting scores on a criterion variable (Y) from scores on a predictor variable (X).

The Regression Equation

The simple linear regression equation is: Y' = a + bX

  • Y' (Y-prime): The predicted score on the criterion variable
  • a: The Y-intercept (the value of Y when X = 0)
  • b: The slope (regression coefficient) indicating the change in Y for each one-unit change in X
  • X: The score on the predictor variable

The regression line is the "line of best fit" that minimizes the sum of squared errors (differences between actual and predicted Y values). This is called the least squares criterion.

Standard Error of Estimate

The standard error of estimate (SEE) quantifies the average amount of error in predictions made from the regression equation. It represents the standard deviation of the residuals (prediction errors). A smaller SEE indicates more accurate predictions.

The relationship between SEE and correlation is: as r increases, SEE decreases. When r = 1.00 (perfect correlation), SEE = 0 (no prediction error). When r = 0, the SEE equals the standard deviation of the Y variable (knowing X provides no improvement over just guessing the mean of Y).

Key Regression Concepts

Predictor Variable (X): The variable used to make predictions; also called the independent variable in regression.

Criterion Variable (Y): The variable being predicted; also called the dependent variable in regression.

Residual: The difference between the actual Y score and the predicted Y score (Y - Y').

SEE: The standard deviation of the residuals; indicates prediction accuracy.

Multiple Regression

Multiple regression extends simple regression by using two or more predictor variables to predict a single criterion variable. The multiple regression equation is: Y' = a + b₁X₁ + b₂X₂ + ... + bₖXₖ

Each predictor variable has its own regression coefficient (b) indicating its unique contribution to predicting Y while controlling for other predictors.

Multiple Correlation Coefficient (R)

The multiple correlation coefficient (R) indicates the correlation between the actual criterion scores and the predicted scores from the multiple regression equation. R ranges from 0 to 1.00 (it is always positive because it represents the correlation between actual and predicted values).

R² indicates the proportion of variance in the criterion variable explained by the combination of all predictor variables. R² always increases (or stays the same) when additional predictors are added, even if those predictors have no true relationship with the criterion. For this reason, adjusted R² is often reported, which accounts for the number of predictors and sample size.

Incremental Validity

Incremental validity refers to the additional predictive power provided by adding a new predictor to an existing set of predictors. A predictor has incremental validity if it significantly increases R² beyond what existing predictors already explain. This concept is important in personnel selection and clinical assessment when evaluating whether adding a new test improves prediction.

Example: Incremental Validity in Clinical Assessment

A researcher examines whether a new anxiety questionnaire adds predictive value for treatment outcomes beyond existing measures. A regression using only the BDI-II yields R² = .30. Adding the new anxiety measure increases R² to .38. The increment of .08 (8% additional variance explained) represents the incremental validity of the new measure. Statistical tests can determine whether this increment is significant.

Regression to the Mean

Regression to the mean is a statistical phenomenon wherein extreme scores on one measurement tend to be followed by less extreme scores on subsequent measurements. This occurs whenever the correlation between two variables is less than perfect (r < 1.00). The term "regression" was coined by Francis Galton based on this observation (Galton, 1886).

Example: Regression to the Mean

Students who score extremely high on a midterm exam will, on average, score somewhat lower on the final exam. Similarly, students who score extremely low on the midterm will, on average, score somewhat higher on the final. This is not because high scorers "got worse" or low scorers "improved" - it's a statistical artifact of imperfect correlation between the two measurements.

Regression to the mean has important implications for interpreting treatment effects. If participants are selected for treatment based on extreme scores (e.g., high depression scores), some improvement would be expected on subsequent measurement due to regression alone, even without any treatment effect.

Clinical Implications: Threats to Internal Validity

Regression to the mean is a threat to internal validity in clinical research. When clients seek treatment because they're at their worst, some apparent improvement may simply reflect regression rather than treatment effects. Proper research designs (e.g., control groups, randomization) are necessary to separate regression effects from true treatment effects.

Galton, F. (1886). Regression towards mediocrity in hereditary stature. Journal of the Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland, 15, 246-263. - Galton's observations on the heights of parents and children led to the development of regression analysis.

Applications in Psychological Assessment

Test Reliability

Correlation coefficients are fundamental to assessing test reliability. Test-retest reliability, alternate forms reliability, and internal consistency (via coefficient alpha) all rely on correlational methods. Higher correlations indicate greater reliability.

Test Validity

Correlation is central to evaluating criterion-related validity (both predictive and concurrent). The correlation between test scores and criterion measures indicates how well the test predicts the criterion. Validity coefficients are interpreted considering factors like range restriction and criterion reliability.

Meta-Analysis

Meta-analysis is a quantitative method for systematically combining results from multiple studies to arrive at an overall estimate of an effect. Unlike a narrative literature review, meta-analysis uses statistical techniques to integrate findings, weight studies by quality or sample size, and assess variability across studies (Borenstein et al., 2009). Correlation coefficients can be converted to other effect size metrics and combined to estimate population-level relationships while accounting for sampling error and study artifacts (Hunter & Schmidt, 2004).

Effect Size Calculation

The foundation of meta-analysis is the effect size—a standardized measure of the magnitude of an effect that can be compared across studies with different scales and designs. Common effect size metrics include Cohen's d (standardized mean difference), Pearson's r (correlation), and odds ratios (for binary outcomes). Each study's effect size is calculated and then combined into a weighted average, with larger studies typically receiving more weight.

Fixed vs. Random Effects Models

Two Approaches to Combining Studies

  • Fixed effects model: Assumes all studies estimate the same underlying true effect size. Differences between studies are attributed solely to sampling error. Appropriate when studies are very similar in design, population, and intervention. Inferences are limited to the specific studies included.
  • Random effects model: Assumes that true effect sizes vary across studies due to differences in populations, methods, and contexts. Accounts for both within-study sampling error and between-study variability. Generally preferred in psychological research because studies are rarely identical in all respects. Produces wider confidence intervals than fixed effects models.

Forest Plots

A forest plot is the standard graphical display of meta-analytic results. Each study is represented by a horizontal line (confidence interval) with a square at the point estimate (the square's size reflects the study's weight). The overall combined estimate is displayed as a diamond at the bottom. Forest plots allow readers to visually assess the consistency and direction of effects across studies.

Heterogeneity

Heterogeneity refers to variability in effect sizes across studies beyond what would be expected from sampling error alone. Key measures include:

  • Q statistic: Tests whether observed variability exceeds chance; a significant Q indicates heterogeneity is present
  • I² statistic: Quantifies the percentage of variability due to true differences between studies rather than sampling error. Values of 25%, 50%, and 75% are considered low, moderate, and high heterogeneity, respectively (Higgins et al., 2003)

When substantial heterogeneity exists, researchers conduct moderator analysis (also called subgroup analysis or meta-regression) to identify variables that explain why effect sizes differ across studies (e.g., type of intervention, participant age, study quality).

Publication Bias

Publication bias (also called the "file drawer problem") occurs when studies with statistically significant results are more likely to be published than studies with null findings. This can inflate meta-analytic effect size estimates because non-significant studies are missing from the analysis.

  • Funnel plot: A scatter plot of effect sizes (x-axis) against study precision or sample size (y-axis). In the absence of bias, the plot should be symmetrical around the mean effect. Asymmetry (missing studies in the lower-left corner) suggests publication bias.
  • File drawer problem (Rosenthal): Refers to the unknown number of unpublished studies with null findings "sitting in file drawers." Rosenthal's fail-safe N estimates how many null studies would need to exist to reduce the combined effect to non-significance.
  • Trim and fill method: Statistically estimates and corrects for missing studies in an asymmetric funnel plot

Meta-Analysis in Evidence-Based Practice

Meta-analyses are considered among the highest levels of evidence in evidence-based practice hierarchies. They inform clinical practice guidelines (e.g., APA treatment guidelines), resolve conflicting findings across individual studies, and provide more precise effect size estimates than any single study. However, the quality of a meta-analysis depends on the quality and representativeness of the included studies—"garbage in, garbage out."

EPPP Exam Focus Points

Commonly Tested Topics for the EPPP

  • Correlation ≠ Causation: Correlation describes relationships but cannot establish cause and effect
  • Range of r: Correlation coefficients range from -1.00 to +1.00
  • r²: The coefficient of determination indicates shared/explained variance
  • Restriction of Range: Attenuates (reduces) correlation coefficients
  • Pearson r: Requires continuous, normally distributed variables with linear relationship
  • Spearman rho: Used for ordinal data or non-linear monotonic relationships
  • Regression Equation: Y' = a + bX for simple regression
  • Standard Error of Estimate: Measures prediction accuracy; decreases as r increases
  • Multiple R: Always positive; R² indicates total explained variance
  • Incremental Validity: Additional predictive power from adding a predictor
  • Regression to the Mean: Extreme scores tend toward the mean on retest; threat to internal validity
  • Cohen's Guidelines: Small (r = .10), Medium (r = .30), Large (r = .50)
  • Meta-analysis: Quantitative synthesis of effect sizes across studies; forest plots display results
  • Fixed vs. random effects: Fixed assumes one true effect; random accounts for variability across studies (preferred in psychology)
  • Heterogeneity: I² quantifies between-study variability (25% = low, 50% = moderate, 75% = high)
  • Publication bias: Assessed via funnel plots; the file drawer problem refers to unpublished null results
