Measuring What Matters: Content & Construct Validity

Content, construct, and convergent/divergent validity
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Introduction to Validity

Validity is the most fundamental consideration in test development and use. It refers to the degree to which evidence and theory support the interpretations of test scores for proposed uses. Unlike reliability, which concerns consistency of measurement, validity addresses whether a test actually measures what it claims to measure and whether inferences drawn from scores are appropriate (AERA et al., 2014).

Key Terminology

  • Validity: The degree to which evidence supports the intended interpretation of test scores for a specific purpose
  • Validation: The ongoing process of accumulating evidence to support score interpretations
  • Validity Coefficient: A correlation between test scores and a criterion measure
  • Construct: A theoretical concept or attribute that a test is designed to measure

Contemporary validity theory emphasizes that validity is not a property of the test itself, but rather of the interpretations and uses of test scores. A test may be valid for one purpose (e.g., screening for depression) but not for another (e.g., diagnosing specific depressive disorders). Messick (1989) articulated an influential unitary concept of validity, arguing that all validity evidence relates to construct validity.

Sources of Validity Evidence

The Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing (AERA et al., 2014) identifies five major sources of validity evidence. Each provides different information about the appropriateness of score interpretations.

Evidence Based on Test Content

Content validity refers to the degree to which test items adequately sample the domain of content the test is intended to measure. It is established through logical analysis rather than statistical procedures and is particularly important for achievement tests and job performance assessments.

Establishing Content Validity

  • Domain definition: Clearly specify the content domain to be measured
  • Test blueprint: Create a table of specifications linking items to content areas
  • Expert review: Have subject matter experts evaluate item relevance and representativeness
  • Content validity ratio (CVR): Quantify agreement among experts about item essentiality

Content Validity Example

A licensing exam for clinical psychologists should sample knowledge across relevant domains: assessment, diagnosis, treatment, ethics, and research methods. If the exam heavily emphasizes one area while neglecting others, it would have poor content validity for measuring overall competence in clinical psychology.

Face Validity

Face validity refers to whether a test appears to measure what it claims to measure, from the perspective of examinees or untrained observers. While not technically a form of validity evidence, face validity can affect examinee motivation, public acceptance, and perceived fairness. A test can have good construct validity but poor face validity, or vice versa.

Evidence Based on Response Processes

This type of evidence examines whether examinees actually engage in the cognitive processes the test intends to measure. Methods include think-aloud protocols, eye-tracking studies, and analysis of response patterns.

Response Process Investigation

If a reading comprehension test is designed to measure inference-making, validity evidence should demonstrate that examinees actually make inferences to answer questions, rather than relying on prior knowledge or test-taking strategies that bypass the intended processes.

Evidence Based on Internal Structure

Internal structure evidence examines whether the relationships among test items and components conform to the theoretical structure of the construct. Factor analysis is the primary statistical technique for evaluating internal structure.

Factor Analysis

Exploratory factor analysis (EFA) identifies the underlying factor structure without prior hypotheses about the number or nature of factors. Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) tests whether data fit a hypothesized factor structure based on theory.

Factor Analysis Example

An anxiety measure theorized to assess three components (cognitive, somatic, behavioral) should show three distinct factors in factor analysis. If items from different subscales load on the same factor, or if a single general factor emerges, the theoretical structure is not supported.

Evidence Based on Relations to Other Variables

This evidence demonstrates that test scores relate to external variables in ways consistent with the theoretical understanding of the construct. This category includes convergent and discriminant validity evidence, as well as criterion-related validity.

Convergent Validity

Convergent validity is demonstrated when a test correlates strongly with other measures of the same or similar constructs. High correlations with conceptually related measures support the interpretation that the test measures the intended construct.

Discriminant (Divergent) Validity

Discriminant validity is demonstrated when a test shows low correlations with measures of theoretically unrelated constructs. This evidence supports the distinctiveness of the measured construct.

Multitrait-Multimethod Matrix

Campbell and Fiske (1959) introduced the multitrait-multimethod (MTMM) matrix to evaluate both convergent and discriminant validity simultaneously. The matrix examines correlations among multiple traits measured by multiple methods.

  • Convergent validity: High correlations between the same trait measured by different methods (monotrait-heteromethod)
  • Discriminant validity: Lower correlations between different traits, even when measured by the same method (heterotrait-monomethod)

Evidence Based on Consequences of Testing

This evidence considers the intended and unintended consequences of test use, including potential benefits and harms. It addresses whether test use achieves its intended outcomes and whether any negative consequences are related to construct-irrelevant factors such as bias.

Construct Validity

Construct validity is the overarching concept that encompasses all validity evidence. It addresses the fundamental question: Does the test measure the theoretical construct it purports to measure? All other forms of validity evidence contribute to construct validity (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955).

The Nomological Network

Cronbach and Meehl (1955) introduced the concept of a nomological network—the theoretical framework specifying the relationships between a construct and other constructs, as well as observable manifestations. Validity evidence supports or challenges elements of this network.

Nomological Network for Intelligence

The nomological network for intelligence includes theoretical relationships (intelligence should correlate positively with academic achievement, problem-solving ability, and processing speed) and observable indicators (IQ scores should predict school grades, job performance in complex occupations, and performance on cognitive tasks). Evidence supporting these relationships strengthens construct validity.

Threats to Construct Validity

Two major threats can undermine construct validity:

Construct Underrepresentation

Construct underrepresentation occurs when a test fails to capture important aspects of the construct. The test is too narrow, measuring only part of what it should measure.

Construct-Irrelevant Variance

Construct-irrelevant variance occurs when test scores are influenced by factors unrelated to the construct. These irrelevant factors can include test anxiety, reading ability on non-reading tests, or cultural bias.

Addressing Construct Validity Threats

  • Underrepresentation: Ensure comprehensive sampling of the construct domain; use multiple item formats
  • Irrelevant variance: Minimize irrelevant difficulty sources; provide accommodations when appropriate; evaluate differential item functioning across groups

Validity Generalization

Validity generalization refers to the extent to which validity evidence obtained in one context applies to other contexts, populations, or situations. Schmidt and Hunter (1977) demonstrated that much apparent variability in validity coefficients across studies reflects statistical artifacts (sampling error, range restriction, measurement error) rather than true differences.

Meta-Analysis of Validity Coefficients

Validity generalization studies use meta-analytic techniques to:

  • Estimate the mean true validity across studies
  • Determine how much variability remains after correcting for artifacts
  • Identify moderators that explain remaining variability

Validity Generalization Example

Meta-analyses of cognitive ability tests show that their validity for predicting job performance generalizes across jobs and settings, with corrected validity coefficients typically ranging from .50 to .60 for overall job performance. This suggests that cognitive ability is a valid predictor across many employment contexts.

Test Bias and Fairness

Test bias is a psychometric concept referring to systematic errors in test scores or predictions for members of particular groups. A test is considered biased if it systematically over- or underpredicts criterion performance for certain groups.

Types of Bias

Predictive Bias

Predictive bias exists when a test's regression line for predicting a criterion differs across groups. If the same test score predicts different criterion levels for different groups, selection decisions based on the test may be unfair.

Differential Item Functioning (DIF)

Differential item functioning occurs when examinees of equal ability from different groups have different probabilities of answering an item correctly. DIF analysis identifies potentially biased items that may function differently across demographic groups.

Bias vs. Fairness

Bias is a statistical/psychometric concept referring to systematic measurement error. Fairness is a broader concept encompassing social values and judgments about appropriate test use. A test can be psychometrically unbiased yet still raise fairness concerns if its use produces adverse impact on protected groups.

Evaluating Test Bias

  • Compare regression equations across groups (intercepts and slopes)
  • Conduct DIF analyses to identify problematic items
  • Examine whether content represents all groups fairly
  • Consider the consequences of test use for different groups

Relationship Between Reliability and Validity

Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity. A test cannot measure a construct accurately (be valid) if it does not measure consistently (be reliable). However, a test can be highly reliable while measuring the wrong construct.

Key Relationships

  • Validity cannot exceed the square root of reliability: rxy ≤ √rxx
  • High reliability does not guarantee validity
  • Low reliability limits the maximum possible validity
  • Both reliability and validity are essential for meaningful measurement

Reliability-Validity Relationship

A weight scale might reliably measure weight (high reliability) but would have no validity for measuring intelligence. Conversely, a test with reliability of .64 can have maximum validity of only √.64 = .80, regardless of how well the content represents the intended construct.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Validity refers to the appropriateness of interpretations of test scores for specific purposes—not a property of the test itself
  • Content validity is established through logical analysis and expert judgment, not statistical methods
  • Face validity is not technically a form of validity evidence, but affects examinee acceptance
  • Construct validity is the overarching concept; all validity evidence contributes to it
  • Convergent validity: High correlations with measures of similar constructs
  • Discriminant validity: Low correlations with measures of unrelated constructs
  • The MTMM matrix evaluates both convergent and discriminant validity simultaneously
  • Construct underrepresentation: Test is too narrow; Construct-irrelevant variance: Test measures unintended factors
  • Validity generalization shows validity evidence can apply across settings when corrected for artifacts
  • Test bias is systematic measurement error for specific groups; fairness involves value judgments about test use
