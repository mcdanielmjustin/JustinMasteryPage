Variables, Scales & the Language of Data

Measurement scales, distributions, and descriptive statistics

Introduction to Variables

Variables are characteristics or attributes that can take on different values across individuals, time, or conditions. Understanding the types of variables and how they are measured is fundamental to psychological research and assessment. The nature of a variable determines which statistical analyses are appropriate and what conclusions can be drawn from data (Gravetter & Wallnau, 2017).

Key Terminology

  • Variable: Any characteristic that can vary or take on different values
  • Constant: A characteristic that does not change across observations
  • Operationalization: The process of defining a variable in terms of specific, measurable procedures
  • Measurement: The assignment of numbers to objects or events according to rules

Types of Variables in Research

Independent and Dependent Variables

The independent variable (IV) is the variable that is manipulated or selected by the researcher to determine its effect on behavior. The dependent variable (DV) is the measured outcome that is expected to change as a function of the independent variable (Kerlinger & Lee, 2000).

Research Example

In a study examining the effects of sleep deprivation on memory, the researcher manipulates hours of sleep (IV: 8 hours vs. 4 hours) and measures performance on a memory test (DV). The dependent variable "depends on" the level of the independent variable.

Extraneous and Confounding Variables

Extraneous variables are variables other than the IV that could potentially influence the DV. When an extraneous variable systematically varies with the IV, it becomes a confounding variable, making it impossible to determine whether the IV or the confound caused changes in the DV.

Control Techniques

  • Random assignment: Distributes individual differences across conditions
  • Matching: Ensures groups are equivalent on specific variables
  • Holding constant: Keeps a variable at one level across all conditions
  • Counterbalancing: Varies order of conditions to control sequence effects
  • Statistical control: Uses ANCOVA or partial correlation to remove effects

Mediating and Moderating Variables

A mediating variable explains the mechanism through which an IV affects a DV. It represents the pathway or process by which the effect occurs. A moderating variable affects the strength or direction of the relationship between an IV and DV—it identifies when or for whom an effect occurs (Baron & Kenny, 1986).

Mediator vs. Moderator

Mediator example: Therapy → Increased self-efficacy → Decreased depression. Self-efficacy mediates the relationship between therapy and depression by explaining how therapy works.

Moderator example: The effect of therapy on depression is stronger for patients with high motivation (motivation moderates the therapy-depression relationship by explaining when therapy works best).

Scales of Measurement

Stevens (1946) identified four scales of measurement, often remembered by the mnemonic NOIR: Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, and Ratio. The scale of measurement determines which mathematical operations and statistical procedures are appropriate for the data.

Nominal Scale

The nominal scale uses numbers only as labels or names for categories. Numbers cannot be meaningfully added, subtracted, or compared in terms of magnitude. Examples include gender (1 = male, 2 = female), diagnostic categories, or political party affiliation.

Appropriate statistics for nominal data include frequency counts, percentages, mode, and chi-square tests.

Ordinal Scale

The ordinal scale ranks observations from lowest to highest but does not specify the distance between ranks. The difference between ranks 1 and 2 may not equal the difference between ranks 2 and 3. Examples include class rank, Likert-type scales, and socioeconomic status categories.

Appropriate statistics include median, percentile ranks, and nonparametric tests (Spearman correlation, Mann-Whitney U).

Interval Scale

The interval scale has equal intervals between values but no true zero point. The zero is arbitrary, and ratios are not meaningful. The classic example is temperature in Fahrenheit or Celsius—the difference between 20° and 30° equals the difference between 70° and 80°, but 40° is not "twice as hot" as 20°.

Most psychological tests are treated as interval scales. Appropriate statistics include mean, standard deviation, Pearson correlation, t-tests, and ANOVA.

Ratio Scale

The ratio scale has all the properties of the interval scale plus a true zero point representing the absence of the measured attribute. Ratios are meaningful—10 seconds is twice as long as 5 seconds. Examples include reaction time, number of errors, height, and weight.

All statistical procedures are appropriate for ratio data.

Summary: Scales of Measurement (NOIR)

Scale Properties Example Central Tendency
Nominal Categories only Diagnosis, gender Mode
Ordinal Rank order Class rank, severity ratings Median
Interval Equal intervals, no true zero Temperature, IQ scores Mean
Ratio Equal intervals, true zero Reaction time, height Mean

Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics summarize and organize data to make patterns more interpretable. They fall into three main categories: measures of central tendency, measures of variability, and measures of relative position (Howell, 2013).

Measures of Central Tendency

Central tendency statistics identify the typical or representative score in a distribution.

Mean

The mean (M or X̄) is the arithmetic average, calculated by summing all scores and dividing by the number of scores. It uses information from every score but is sensitive to extreme values (outliers). The mean is appropriate for interval and ratio data.

Median

The median (Mdn) is the middle score when data are arranged in order. It is less affected by outliers than the mean and is appropriate for ordinal, interval, and ratio data. In a skewed distribution, the median better represents the typical score.

Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring score. It is the only measure of central tendency appropriate for nominal data. A distribution can be bimodal (two modes) or multimodal (multiple modes).

Choosing the Right Measure

Use the mean when the distribution is relatively normal and outliers are absent. Use the median when the distribution is skewed or contains outliers (e.g., income data). Use the mode for nominal data or to identify the most common category.

Measures of Variability

Variability statistics describe how spread out scores are in a distribution.

Range

The range is the difference between the highest and lowest scores. It is simple but unstable because it is based on only two scores and is highly sensitive to outliers.

Variance

The variance (s² or σ²) is the average of the squared deviations from the mean. Squaring the deviations ensures that positive and negative deviations do not cancel out. Sample variance uses n-1 in the denominator to provide an unbiased estimate of population variance.

Standard Deviation

The standard deviation (SD or s) is the square root of the variance, returning the measure to the original unit of measurement. It represents the average distance of scores from the mean and is the most commonly used measure of variability.

Computational Formulas

Variance (sample): s² = Σ(X - M)² / (n - 1)

Standard Deviation: s = √s²

The divisor (n - 1) is called degrees of freedom and provides an unbiased estimate of the population parameter.

The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution (also called the Gaussian distribution or bell curve) is a theoretical probability distribution that is symmetric, unimodal, and asymptotic. Many psychological variables approximate normal distributions in large samples, and the normal distribution underlies many statistical procedures (Cohen et al., 2003).

Properties of the Normal Distribution

  • Symmetric around the mean (mean = median = mode)
  • Defined by two parameters: mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ)
  • Approximately 68% of scores fall within ±1 SD of the mean
  • Approximately 95% of scores fall within ±2 SD of the mean
  • Approximately 99.7% of scores fall within ±3 SD of the mean

The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7)

In a normal distribution:

  • 68% of observations fall between M - 1SD and M + 1SD
  • 95% of observations fall between M - 2SD and M + 2SD
  • 99.7% of observations fall between M - 3SD and M + 3SD

Z-Scores and Standard Scores

A z-score (standard score) expresses a raw score in terms of how many standard deviations it falls above or below the mean. Z-scores allow comparison of scores from different distributions by converting them to a common scale.

Z-Score Formula

z = (X - M) / SD

Where X is the raw score, M is the mean, and SD is the standard deviation.

The z-score distribution always has a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1.

Z-Score Calculation Example

On a test with M = 100 and SD = 15, a score of 130 has a z-score of:

z = (130 - 100) / 15 = 30 / 15 = 2.0

This score is 2 standard deviations above the mean, falling at approximately the 98th percentile in a normal distribution.

Other Standard Score Transformations

Z-scores can be linearly transformed to other standard score scales that eliminate negative values and decimals:

Scale Mean SD Formula
T-score 50 10 T = 10z + 50
IQ (deviation) 100 15 IQ = 15z + 100
SAT/GRE subtest 500 100 Score = 100z + 500
Stanine 5 2 Stanine = 2z + 5

Skewness and Kurtosis

Real data often deviate from the normal distribution. Two important characteristics describe the shape of distributions: skewness and kurtosis.

Skewness

Skewness refers to the asymmetry of a distribution. In a positively skewed distribution, the tail extends toward higher values (right), and the mean is greater than the median. In a negatively skewed distribution, the tail extends toward lower values (left), and the mean is less than the median.

Skewness Examples

Positive skew: Income distribution (many low-to-moderate incomes, few very high incomes pulling the mean up)

Negative skew: Age at death in developed countries (most people live to old age, with some early deaths pulling the mean down)

Kurtosis

Kurtosis describes the "peakedness" of a distribution and the heaviness of its tails. Leptokurtic distributions are more peaked with heavier tails than normal (positive kurtosis). Platykurtic distributions are flatter with lighter tails (negative kurtosis). Mesokurtic distributions have the same kurtosis as the normal distribution.

Practical Implications

Severe skewness or kurtosis can violate assumptions of parametric statistical tests. Options include:

  • Using nonparametric alternatives
  • Transforming data (log, square root)
  • Using robust statistical methods
  • Reporting median instead of mean for skewed data

Percentiles and Percentile Ranks

Percentiles describe scores in terms of their relative position in a distribution.

A percentile rank indicates the percentage of scores in a distribution that fall at or below a given score. A score at the 75th percentile (P75) exceeds 75% of scores in the distribution.

A percentile point (or percentile) is the score at or below which a specified percentage of scores falls. The 75th percentile point is the score that 75% of examinees scored at or below.

Special Percentiles: Quartiles

  • Q1 (25th percentile): First quartile—25% of scores fall below
  • Q2 (50th percentile): Second quartile—the median
  • Q3 (75th percentile): Third quartile—75% of scores fall below
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): Q3 - Q1, contains middle 50% of scores

Interpreting Test Scores

When interpreting psychological test scores, percentile ranks are often more intuitive for clients than standard scores. However, percentile ranks have important limitations:

  • They are not equal-interval—a difference of 10 percentile points near the mean represents fewer raw score points than the same difference at the extremes
  • They cannot be meaningfully averaged or subjected to arithmetic operations
  • They should not be compared across different normative samples

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Know NOIR: Nominal (categories), Ordinal (rank), Interval (equal intervals, no true zero), Ratio (equal intervals, true zero)
  • Mediators explain how an effect occurs; moderators explain when/for whom the effect occurs
  • In a positively skewed distribution, mean > median > mode (tail points right)
  • In a negatively skewed distribution, mode > median > mean (tail points left)
  • Z-score formula: z = (X - M) / SD; z-scores have M = 0 and SD = 1
  • The 68-95-99.7 rule describes the percentage of scores within 1, 2, and 3 SDs of the mean in a normal distribution
  • Standard deviation is the most commonly reported measure of variability; it represents the average distance from the mean
  • Confounding variables vary systematically with the IV, making causal inference impossible
  • The median is the preferred measure of central tendency for skewed distributions
  • T-scores have M = 50 and SD = 10; deviation IQ scores have M = 100 and SD = 15
