Introduction to Interpersonal Attraction

Interpersonal attraction refers to the positive attitudes and feelings one person has toward another. Social psychologists have identified several key factors that determine who we are drawn to and why. These factors operate across friendships and romantic relationships alike, though their relative importance shifts depending on the type of relationship.

Understanding attraction is clinically relevant because relationship difficulties are among the most common presenting concerns in therapy, and the mechanisms underlying attraction inform couples therapy, social skills training, and interventions for loneliness and social isolation.

Determinants of Attraction

Proximity (Propinquity)

Proximity Effect

Physical and functional proximity is one of the strongest predictors of attraction. People are most likely to form relationships with those they encounter frequently. Festinger, Schachter, and Back's (1950) classic study of MIT housing found that residents were most likely to become friends with their next-door neighbors and those they passed frequently in hallways and stairwells.

The mere exposure effect (Zajonc, 1968) provides a partial explanation: repeated exposure to a stimulus (including a person) tends to increase liking for that stimulus, even in the absence of any interaction. This effect operates below conscious awareness and is one of the most robust findings in social psychology.

Similarity

The similarity-attraction effect demonstrates that we are drawn to people who are similar to us in attitudes, values, interests, personality, and demographic characteristics. Byrne's (1971) "bogus stranger" paradigm consistently showed that perceived attitude similarity predicts interpersonal attraction in a linear fashion -- the greater the proportion of similar attitudes, the greater the attraction.

Similarity vs. Complementarity

The popular belief that "opposites attract" (complementarity hypothesis) has received minimal empirical support. Research overwhelmingly favors the similarity hypothesis. The one exception involves dominance-submissiveness: some research suggests that dominant and submissive individuals may prefer each other in certain contexts, but even this finding is inconsistent.

Reciprocity

Reciprocity of liking refers to our tendency to like those who like us. Learning that someone is attracted to us increases our attraction to them. This effect is particularly strong when the liking is perceived as genuine and selective (not indiscriminate). Aronson's (1969) gain-loss theory extends this by proposing that we are most attracted to those whose liking for us increases over time (gain condition) and least attracted to those whose liking decreases (loss condition).

Physical Attractiveness

Physical attractiveness is a powerful predictor of initial attraction, particularly in romantic contexts. The halo effect associated with attractiveness leads people to attribute other positive qualities (intelligence, competence, social skills) to physically attractive individuals -- a phenomenon Dion, Berscheid, and Walster (1972) described as the "what is beautiful is good" stereotype.

  • Matching hypothesis: People tend to form relationships with partners who are similar to themselves in physical attractiveness (Walster et al., 1966). While people may prefer highly attractive partners, they ultimately select partners at their own level of attractiveness.
  • Cultural variation: Standards of attractiveness vary across cultures, though some features (e.g., facial symmetry, averageness) show cross-cultural consistency.
  • Gender differences: Research has consistently found that men place greater emphasis on physical attractiveness when selecting a partner, while women place relatively greater emphasis on status and resources, though both sexes value attractiveness.
Berscheid, E., & Walster, E. (1974). Physical attractiveness. Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 7, 157-215.

Sternberg's Triangular Theory of Love

Robert Sternberg's (1986) triangular theory proposes that love consists of three components that combine in different ways to produce different types of love.

Three Components

Intimacy

The emotional component -- feelings of closeness, connectedness, and bondedness. It includes warmth, sharing, communication, and emotional support. Intimacy tends to increase gradually and may be difficult to detect because it develops slowly.

Passion

The motivational component -- physical attraction, sexual desire, and intense emotional arousal. Passion develops quickly but also tends to habituate over time. It is strongly related to physiological arousal.

Commitment

The cognitive/decision component -- the short-term decision that one loves another person and the long-term decision to maintain that love. Commitment tends to increase gradually and may remain stable even as passion declines.

EPPP Exam Tip

Distinguish companionate love (intimacy + commitment, typical of long-term marriages) from romantic love (intimacy + passion, the "honeymoon" phase). Fatuous love (passion + commitment without intimacy) characterizes whirlwind courtships. Consummate love -- all three components -- is the ideal but hardest to maintain.

Sternberg, R. J. (1986). A triangular theory of love. Psychological Review, 93(2), 119-135.

Attachment Styles in Adult Relationships

Building on Bowlby's attachment theory and Ainsworth's infant attachment classifications, Hazan and Shaver (1987) proposed that romantic love can be understood as an attachment process. The attachment style developed in infancy influences adult romantic relationships.

Four-Category Model (Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991)

Bartholomew and Horowitz expanded the three-category model into four categories based on two dimensions: model of self (positive vs. negative) and model of others (positive vs. negative).

Clinical Applications

Attachment theory has become central to couples therapy approaches such as Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT; Johnson, 2004). Understanding a client's attachment style helps explain patterns of conflict, withdrawal, and emotional reactivity in relationships. In individual therapy, the therapeutic relationship itself can serve as a corrective attachment experience, particularly for clients with insecure attachment styles.

Key Research Findings

  • Approximately 60% of adults are classified as securely attached in most Western samples.
  • Attachment style shows moderate stability from infancy to adulthood but can change in response to significant relationship experiences.
  • Secure attachment is associated with greater relationship satisfaction, better conflict resolution, and more effective emotion regulation.
  • Insecure attachment is a risk factor for depression, anxiety, and relationship dysfunction, but it is not destiny -- attachment can be "earned" through positive relationship experiences and therapy.
Hazan, C., & Shaver, P. (1987). Romantic love conceptualized as an attachment process. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 52(3), 511-524.

Social Exchange and Equity Theories

Social exchange theory (Thibaut & Kelley, 1959) applies an economic framework to relationships. According to this model, people evaluate relationships in terms of costs and rewards. Satisfaction depends on two standards of comparison:

  • Comparison Level (CL): The standard against which a person evaluates the rewards and costs of a given relationship. This is based on past experiences and expectations. If outcomes exceed the CL, the person is satisfied.
  • Comparison Level for Alternatives (CLalt): The lowest level of outcomes a person will accept given available alternatives. Even if outcomes are below the CL (unsatisfied), a person may stay if outcomes are above the CLalt (no better alternatives).

Equity Theory

Equity theory (Walster, Walster, & Berscheid, 1978) proposes that people are most satisfied when they perceive the ratio of their contributions and benefits to be roughly equal to their partner's ratio. Both over-benefiting and under-benefiting create distress, though under-benefiting produces more dissatisfaction. People in inequitable relationships are motivated to restore equity through behavioral changes (adjusting contributions) or cognitive changes (reappraising contributions).

Clinical Application

Social exchange and equity theories are directly relevant to couples therapy. Many relationship conflicts involve perceived inequity -- one partner feels they contribute more or receive less. Gottman's research on marriage emphasizes that stable relationships maintain a ratio of approximately 5:1 positive to negative interactions. Understanding these transactional dynamics helps therapists identify specific patterns that erode relationship satisfaction.

Relationship Dissolution

Research on relationship dissolution has identified several predictors of breakup and models of the ending process.

Gottman's Four Horsemen

John Gottman identified four communication patterns that predict relationship dissolution with high accuracy:

  1. Criticism: Attacking the partner's character rather than addressing a specific behavior. ("You always forget things -- you're so selfish.")
  2. Contempt: Expressions of superiority, disgust, or disrespect (eye-rolling, name-calling, sarcasm). Contempt is the single strongest predictor of divorce.
  3. Defensiveness: Self-protection through counter-complaints or playing the victim rather than accepting responsibility.
  4. Stonewalling: Withdrawing from interaction, shutting down, or refusing to engage. Often a response to emotional flooding.

Duck's Phase Model of Relationship Dissolution

Steve Duck (1982) described four phases of relationship breakdown:

  1. Intrapsychic phase: One partner privately broods about dissatisfaction but does not discuss it.
  2. Dyadic phase: The dissatisfied partner confronts the other. Discussion, negotiation, or argument about the relationship occurs.
  3. Social phase: The couple goes public with their problems. Friends and family become involved, often taking sides.
  4. Grave-dressing phase: Each partner constructs a narrative account of the relationship's rise and fall that preserves their self-image.

EPPP Exam Tip

For attraction and relationships topics, the EPPP often tests: (1) the four determinants of attraction (proximity, similarity, reciprocity, attractiveness), (2) Sternberg's components and types of love, (3) the four attachment styles and their underlying dimensions, and (4) Gottman's "Four Horsemen." Be prepared to apply these concepts to clinical vignettes.

Gottman, J. M. (1994). What predicts divorce? The relationship between marital processes and marital outcomes. Lawrence Erlbaum.

Evolutionary and Sociocultural Perspectives on Mate Selection

Two major theoretical frameworks offer complementary explanations for mate preferences and attraction patterns.

Evolutionary Psychology Perspective

David Buss (1989) conducted a landmark cross-cultural study of mate preferences across 37 cultures and found consistent patterns:

  • Men across cultures placed greater value on physical attractiveness and youth (indicators of fertility and reproductive value).
  • Women across cultures placed greater value on financial resources, ambition, and social status (indicators of ability to provide for offspring).
  • Both sexes valued kindness, intelligence, and mutual attraction -- these were consistently the highest-rated traits across all cultures.

Parental investment theory (Trivers, 1972) provides the theoretical framework: the sex that invests more in offspring (typically females in mammals) is more selective in mate choice, while the sex that invests less (typically males) competes more intensely for mating opportunities.

Social Role Theory

Alice Eagly's (1987) social role theory provides an alternative explanation for gender differences in mate preferences. This perspective argues that these differences arise not from evolved psychological mechanisms but from the division of labor between men and women in society. As gender roles become more egalitarian, gender differences in mate preferences are expected to diminish -- a prediction that has received some empirical support in more egalitarian societies.

Misattribution of Arousal (Dutton & Aron, 1974)

In the famous "bridge study," men who crossed a fear-arousing suspension bridge were more likely to call an attractive female experimenter afterward than men who crossed a sturdy bridge. This demonstrates misattribution of arousal (also known as the excitation transfer effect): physiological arousal from one source (fear) can be misattributed to another source (attraction). This finding connects attraction research to Schachter and Singer's two-factor theory of emotion.

Summary: Key Concepts for the EPPP

Attraction and relationships questions on the EPPP draw from multiple theoretical perspectives. Be prepared to identify: the four major determinants of attraction, distinguish between Sternberg's seven types of love, map attachment styles onto the two-dimensional model, recognize Gottman's four predictive communication patterns, and understand the theoretical debate between evolutionary and sociocultural explanations for mate preferences. Clinically, attachment theory is the most directly applied framework in therapeutic practice, particularly in Emotionally Focused Therapy.

Buss, D. M. (1989). Sex differences in human mate preferences: Evolutionary hypotheses tested in 37 cultures. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 12(1), 1-49.

Self-Disclosure and Relationship Development

The development of close relationships involves a process of escalating self-disclosure -- the voluntary sharing of personal information that the other person would not otherwise know.

Social Penetration Theory (Altman & Taylor, 1973)

This theory describes relationship development as a process of increasing breadth (range of topics discussed) and depth (level of intimacy of disclosures). Early interactions involve broad but shallow disclosures. As trust develops, disclosures become deeper and more personal. Relationship deterioration involves a reversal of this process -- "depenetration."

Reciprocity of Disclosure

Self-disclosure follows a norm of reciprocity, particularly in early relationship stages. When one person discloses personal information, the other tends to match the level of disclosure. This reciprocal escalation builds trust and intimacy progressively. In therapy, appropriate therapist self-disclosure can model vulnerability and strengthen the therapeutic alliance, though it must be used judiciously and in the client's interest.

Clinical Note on Disclosure

Jourard's (1971) research showed that therapist self-disclosure tends to increase client self-disclosure. However, cultural factors significantly moderate the appropriateness and impact of disclosure. Clients from cultures that value emotional restraint may find therapist self-disclosure inappropriate or confusing, while clients from cultures that value relational warmth may view it as a sign of genuine caring and trust.

Loneliness and Social Isolation

Loneliness is the subjective experience of perceived social isolation -- a discrepancy between desired and actual social connections. It is distinct from objective social isolation (physically being alone). Research shows that loneliness is associated with increased risk for depression, cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and mortality. Cacioppo's (2009) evolutionary model of loneliness proposes that loneliness functions as a biological signal (analogous to hunger or thirst) that motivates reconnection with others. However, chronic loneliness creates a self-reinforcing cycle: lonely individuals become hypervigilant to social threats, interpret ambiguous social cues negatively, and withdraw further from social contact.

Weiss's Types of Loneliness (1973)

Robert Weiss distinguished between social loneliness (absence of a social network or sense of community belonging) and emotional loneliness (absence of a close, intimate attachment figure). Different interventions are needed for each type: social loneliness responds to group-based interventions and community integration, while emotional loneliness requires the development of close, confiding relationships. This distinction is clinically important because a person can experience one type without the other.

Interventions for loneliness are most effective when they address maladaptive social cognition (the negative interpretive biases that perpetuate loneliness) rather than simply providing social opportunities. Cognitive-behavioral approaches that target distorted social perceptions have shown the largest effect sizes in meta-analyses (Masi et al., 2011).

Cacioppo, J. T., & Patrick, W. (2008). Loneliness: Human nature and the need for social connection. W. W. Norton.
