Why We Connect — Attraction, Altruism & Helping

Interpersonal attraction, love, prosocial behavior, bystander effect

Interpersonal Attraction

Why are we drawn to some people and not others? Research has identified several robust predictors of interpersonal attraction that apply to both friendships and romantic relationships.

Determinants of Attraction

  • Proximity (propinquity): Physical closeness is one of the strongest predictors of attraction. We tend to like people we encounter frequently. The mere exposure effect (Zajonc) contributes: repeated contact increases familiarity and liking.
  • Similarity: We are attracted to people who share our attitudes, values, interests, and backgrounds. The matching hypothesis states that people tend to form relationships with others of similar physical attractiveness.
  • Reciprocity: We like people who like us. Learning that someone is attracted to us increases our attraction to them.
  • Physical attractiveness: Attractive individuals are rated more positively on many dimensions (the halo effect). There is some cross-cultural consistency in what is considered attractive (e.g., facial symmetry, youthfulness cues).
Matching Hypothesis

People tend to form long-term partnerships with others who are similar in physical attractiveness. While people may initially prefer the most attractive partner, they ultimately choose partners whose attractiveness level matches their own, due to the balance of fear of rejection and desire for the best available partner.

Misattribution of Arousal (Dutton & Aron, 1974)

In the famous "bridge study," male participants who crossed a fear-arousing suspension bridge were more likely to call an attractive female experimenter afterward than those who crossed a low, stable bridge. Physiological arousal from fear was misattributed to attraction, demonstrating the two-factor theory of emotion (Schachter & Singer): we experience arousal and then label it based on context.

Dutton, D. G., & Aron, A. P. (1974). Some evidence for heightened sexual attraction under conditions of high anxiety. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 30(4), 510–517.

Adult Attachment Styles

Building on Bowlby and Ainsworth's attachment theory, Hazan and Shaver (1987) proposed that adult romantic relationships follow attachment patterns similar to infant-caregiver bonds. Bartholomew and Horowitz (1991) expanded this into a four-category model:

SecurePositivePositiveComfortable with intimacy and autonomy

Clinical Application

Attachment style significantly impacts the therapeutic relationship. Preoccupied clients may become overly dependent on the therapist, while dismissing clients may devalue the relationship. Fearful-avoidant clients may alternate between seeking and rejecting help. Understanding attachment styles helps therapists anticipate transference patterns and tailor interventions accordingly.

Prosocial Behavior & Altruism

Prosocial behavior encompasses any action intended to benefit another person. Altruism is prosocial behavior motivated purely by concern for the other, with no expectation of personal reward.

The Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis

Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis (Batson, 1991)

When we feel empathic concern for a person in need, we are motivated to help for purely altruistic reasons — to reduce their suffering, not our own discomfort. If we feel personal distress instead of empathy, we help only if it is the easiest way to reduce our own negative feelings (egoistic motivation).

The Bystander Effect

Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané, 1968)

The probability of helping decreases as the number of bystanders increases. Two key mechanisms:
Diffusion of responsibility: Each individual feels less personally responsible when others are present.
Pluralistic ignorance: Each bystander looks to others for cues; when everyone appears calm, each concludes the situation is not an emergency.

Latané & Darley's Five Steps to Helping

  1. Notice the event (distraction can prevent this)
  2. Interpret as emergency (pluralistic ignorance can block this)
  3. Assume personal responsibility (diffusion of responsibility can block this)
  4. Know how to help (lack of competence can block this)
  5. Decide to help (audience inhibition and cost-benefit analysis can block this)
Example

A person collapses in a crowded subway station. Each bystander sees others not reacting and concludes the situation is not serious (pluralistic ignorance). Each assumes someone else will call 911 (diffusion of responsibility). The same person collapsing in front of a single bystander is much more likely to receive immediate help.

Social Exchange & Equity Theory

Social exchange theory views relationships as economic transactions: people seek to maximize rewards and minimize costs. Equity theory adds that satisfaction requires perceived fairness — each partner's ratio of inputs to outcomes should be roughly equal. Both over-benefiting and under-benefiting cause distress.

Clinical Application

Couples therapy often involves restoring equity and reciprocity. Behavioral couples therapy uses behavioral exchange techniques where partners agree to increase rewarding behaviors. Understanding social exchange frameworks helps clinicians identify reward-cost imbalances driving relational distress.

Batson, C. D. (1991). The altruism question: Toward a social-psychological answer. Erlbaum.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Proximity is the strongest predictor of initial attraction; similarity sustains relationships over time.
  • The matching hypothesis: people pair with partners of similar attractiveness.
  • Misattribution of arousal (Dutton & Aron): physiological arousal from any source can be misinterpreted as attraction.
  • Sternberg's triangular theory: Know the three components and the seven types of love, especially consummate love (all three).
  • Know the four adult attachment styles and their corresponding self/other models.
  • Empathy-altruism hypothesis (Batson): Empathic concern leads to true altruism; personal distress leads to egoistic helping.
  • Bystander effect: More bystanders = less helping. Know diffusion of responsibility and pluralistic ignorance.
  • Latané & Darley's five steps to helping — each step represents a potential barrier.
  • Social exchange theory: Relationships as reward/cost equations. Equity theory: Satisfaction requires perceived fairness.
