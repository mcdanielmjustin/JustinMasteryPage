How We're Moved — Persuasion, Conformity & Resistance

ELM, Cialdini's principles, Asch, Milgram, compliance techniques
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Introduction: Social Influence

Social influence — the myriad ways in which people change each other's attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors — lies at the heart of social psychology. This chapter covers three major categories: persuasion (deliberate attitude change), conformity (adjusting to group norms), and obedience (complying with authority). These are among the most heavily tested topics on the EPPP.

The Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)

Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo, 1986)

The ELM proposes two distinct routes to persuasion:
Central route: The audience carefully evaluates the quality and logic of the arguments. Requires high motivation and ability to process the message. Produces durable, resistant attitude change.
Peripheral route: The audience relies on superficial cues (source attractiveness, number of arguments, emotional appeals) rather than argument quality. Used when motivation or ability to process is low. Produces temporary, less resistant attitude change.

Processing depthDeep, systematicShallow, heuristic

Example

A consumer deeply researches car safety ratings, fuel efficiency data, and engineering reviews before purchasing (central route). Another consumer buys the same car because a celebrity endorses it in a commercial (peripheral route). The first consumer's attitude toward the car will be more persistent and predictive of future behavior.

Petty, R. E., & Cacioppo, J. T. (1986). The elaboration likelihood model of persuasion. Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 19, 123–205.

Hovland's Communication Model

Carl Hovland and the Yale Communication Research Program (1950s) identified four key variables that affect persuasion effectiveness:

Source Variables

  • Credibility: Expert, trustworthy sources are more persuasive
  • Attractiveness: Physically attractive and likable sources are more persuasive (especially via the peripheral route)
  • Sleeper effect: Over time, the persuasive impact of a low-credibility source may increase as the message is dissociated from its source

Message Variables

  • Fear appeals: Moderate fear is most effective; too little does not motivate, too much causes defensive avoidance. Most effective when paired with clear, actionable recommendations
  • One-sided vs. two-sided arguments: Two-sided messages (acknowledging opposing views) are more effective with educated or initially skeptical audiences
  • Primacy vs. recency: When two opposing messages are presented back-to-back, the first message has more impact (primacy). When there is a delay between messages, the second has more impact (recency)

Audience Variables

  • Need for cognition: People high in need for cognition are more persuaded by strong arguments (central route)
  • Self-monitoring: High self-monitors are more responsive to image-based appeals; low self-monitors to quality-based appeals
Sleeper Effect

A delayed increase in the persuasive impact of a message from a non-credible source. Initially, the message is discounted because of its source. Over time, the message content is remembered but the source is forgotten, allowing the message to exert delayed influence.

Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence

Robert Cialdini (1984) identified six principles that underlie most influence and compliance strategies. These are essential EPPP material:

  1. Reciprocity: People feel obligated to return favors. "I did something for you, now you owe me."
  2. Commitment and Consistency: Once people commit to a position, they feel pressure to behave consistently with it. This underlies the foot-in-the-door technique.
  3. Social Proof: People look to others to determine correct behavior, especially in ambiguous situations. "Everyone else is doing it."
  4. Authority: People tend to obey legitimate authority figures. This relates directly to Milgram's research.
  5. Liking: We are more easily persuaded by people we like. Factors: physical attractiveness, similarity, compliments, familiarity, association.
  6. Scarcity: People value things more when they are rare or dwindling. "Limited time offer" and "Only 3 left!" exploit this principle.

Cialdini, R. B. (2001). Influence: Science and practice (4th ed.). Allyn & Bacon.

Conformity

Asch's Line Studies (1951)

Solomon Asch demonstrated that people will conform to a clearly incorrect majority. Participants judged which comparison line matched a standard line — an objectively easy task. When confederates unanimously gave the wrong answer, approximately 75% of participants conformed at least once, and the overall conformity rate across trials was about 37%.

Two Types of Social Influence

Normative influence: Conforming to be liked and accepted by the group (going along to get along). Driven by the desire for social approval. Produces public compliance without private acceptance.
Informational influence: Conforming because we believe the group has better information than we do. Driven by the desire to be correct. Produces private acceptance (true attitude change).

Key findings from Asch's research on factors that affect conformity:

  • Conformity increases with group size — but only up to about 3–4 members; beyond that, additional members add little
  • Conformity drops sharply when there is even one dissenter (ally), even if the dissenter gives a different wrong answer
  • Conformity increases when the task is more difficult or ambiguous
  • Conformity increases when responses are public (vs. private)
  • Conformity varies by culture — higher in collectivist cultures

Obedience: Milgram's Studies (1963)

Milgram's Obedience Paradigm

Stanley Milgram studied obedience to authority by instructing participants ("teachers") to administer increasingly severe electric shocks to a confederate ("learner") for wrong answers. In the baseline condition, 65% of participants administered the maximum 450-volt shock, despite hearing screams and pleas to stop. The shocks were not real, but participants believed they were.

Factors That Reduced Obedience in Milgram's Variations

Obedience decreased when: the learner was in the same room (proximity), another "teacher" refused to continue (social support for defiance), the experimenter gave orders by telephone (reduced authority presence), or the study was conducted in a less prestigious setting. Obedience increased when the participant only had an indirect role (e.g., reading the word pairs while another person delivered the shock).

Clinical & Ethical Note

Milgram's study is a landmark in both social psychology and research ethics. Participants experienced significant distress, which contributed to the development of stronger ethical guidelines, including the requirement for informed consent and debriefing. The APA's current Ethics Code (Standard 8.07) requires that deception be justified and that participants be debriefed as early as feasible.

Milgram, S. (1963). Behavioral study of obedience. Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology, 67(4), 371–378.

Reactance Theory

Psychological Reactance (Brehm, 1966)

When people perceive that their freedom to choose is being threatened or eliminated, they experience reactance — a motivational state directed toward restoring the threatened freedom. This often results in doing the opposite of what is demanded. The "forbidden fruit" effect and the "Romeo and Juliet effect" (parental opposition intensifying romantic attraction) are examples of reactance.

Clinical Application

Reactance is clinically relevant in working with resistant clients. Paradoxical interventions (prescribing the symptom) and motivational interviewing (rolling with resistance) are both strategies that leverage or reduce reactance. When a therapist says "You probably aren't ready to change yet," the client's reactance may motivate them to prove the therapist wrong.

Minority Influence

Serge Moscovici demonstrated that consistent minorities can influence the majority, particularly through informational influence. A minority position that is maintained consistently over time leads the majority to re-examine the issue more deeply (central processing), which can produce genuine private attitude change — even when the majority does not publicly agree.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • ELM: Central route = deep processing, durable change; Peripheral route = superficial cues, temporary change. Know what determines which route is used (motivation and ability).
  • Sleeper effect: Low-credibility source gains persuasive power over time as source is forgotten.
  • Cialdini's 6 principles: Reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity. Be able to match techniques to principles.
  • Asch conformity: ~75% conformed at least once; one dissenter dramatically reduces conformity.
  • Normative vs. informational influence: Normative = acceptance by group; informational = accuracy.
  • Milgram obedience: 65% obeyed to maximum shock. Know factors that increase and decrease obedience.
  • Compliance techniques: Know foot-in-the-door (small then large), door-in-the-face (large then small), and lowball.
  • Reactance theory: Threatened freedom motivates opposing behavior. Relevant to paradoxical interventions in therapy.
  • Minority influence: Consistency is the key to a minority changing the majority's views.
