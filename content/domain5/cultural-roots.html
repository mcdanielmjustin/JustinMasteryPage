Introduction: Culture & Psychology

Culture is the invisible architecture of human experience. It shapes how we perceive ourselves, relate to others, interpret emotions, and define what counts as normal or pathological. For the EPPP, understanding cultural dimensions is essential not only for the Social & Cultural Psychology domain but also for ethical practice, assessment, and treatment planning across all domains.

Culture

A shared system of meanings, values, beliefs, norms, and practices that is transmitted across generations and shapes the behavior, cognition, and emotional experience of its members. Culture includes but extends beyond race and ethnicity to encompass religion, socioeconomic status, gender, sexual orientation, disability, and other dimensions of identity.

Cross-cultural psychology examines how culture influences psychological processes, while cultural psychology argues that mind and culture are inseparable — each constitutes the other. Both perspectives are relevant to the EPPP.

Triandis, H. C. (1996). The psychological measurement of cultural syndromes. American Psychologist, 51(4), 407–415.

Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

Geert Hofstede conducted one of the largest cross-cultural studies in history, surveying over 100,000 IBM employees in more than 70 countries. His framework identifies key dimensions along which national cultures differ. These dimensions are continua, not categories.

Individualism vs. Collectivism

This is the most frequently tested cultural dimension on the EPPP. Individualist cultures (e.g., United States, United Kingdom, Australia) emphasize personal autonomy, individual achievement, and self-reliance. Collectivist cultures (e.g., China, Japan, many Latin American and African nations) prioritize group harmony, family obligation, and interdependence.

Example

A student from an individualist culture might describe success as "getting into my top-choice graduate program," while a student from a collectivist culture might say "bringing honor to my family by earning a degree." Neither is more adaptive — each reflects a coherent cultural value system.

Power Distance

Power distance refers to the degree to which less powerful members of a society accept and expect that power is distributed unequally. High power-distance cultures (e.g., Malaysia, Philippines, Mexico) show greater deference to authority figures and accept hierarchical structures. Low power-distance cultures (e.g., Denmark, Austria, Israel) expect more egalitarian relationships.

Uncertainty Avoidance

Uncertainty avoidance describes a society's tolerance for ambiguity and the unknown. Cultures high in uncertainty avoidance (e.g., Greece, Japan, France) tend to create elaborate rules, rituals, and institutions to minimize unpredictability. Low uncertainty-avoidance cultures (e.g., Singapore, Jamaica, Denmark) are more comfortable with ambiguity and change.

Masculinity vs. Femininity

Masculine cultures (e.g., Japan, Hungary, Austria) emphasize competition, achievement, and assertiveness. Feminine cultures (e.g., Sweden, Norway, Netherlands) prioritize quality of life, cooperation, nurturing, and caring for the vulnerable.

Additional Dimensions

Hofstede later added long-term vs. short-term orientation (thrift and perseverance vs. tradition and meeting social obligations) and indulgence vs. restraint (allowing gratification of basic human desires vs. suppressing it through strict social norms).

Acculturation Models

Acculturation is the process of cultural and psychological change that results from continuous contact between two distinct cultural groups. It is bidirectional — both the immigrant group and the host culture may change — though typically the minority group changes more.

Berry's Acculturation Model

John Berry proposed the most widely cited acculturation framework, based on two independent questions: (1) Is it valuable to maintain one's heritage culture? (2) Is it valuable to develop relationships with the larger society? The answers to these two questions produce four acculturation strategies.

StrategyHeritage Culture?Host Culture?Description
IntegrationYesYesMaintain heritage culture while also engaging with host culture; generally associated with best psychological outcomes
AssimilationNoYesAdopt host culture at the expense of heritage culture
SeparationYesNoMaintain heritage culture while avoiding engagement with host culture
MarginalizationNoNoReject both heritage and host cultures; associated with poorest psychological outcomes
Clinical Application

Research consistently shows that integration (biculturalism) is associated with the best mental health outcomes, including lower depression, higher self-esteem, and better social functioning. Marginalization is associated with the worst outcomes. When assessing clients from immigrant backgrounds, understanding their acculturation strategy provides critical clinical information.

Acculturative stress refers to the psychological strain arising from the acculturation process. It can manifest as anxiety, depression, identity confusion, somatic complaints, and substance use. Risk factors include language barriers, discrimination, loss of social support, and involuntary migration.

Berry, J. W. (2005). Acculturation: Living successfully in two cultures. International Journal of Intercultural Relations, 29(6), 697–712.

Ethnic Identity Development

Ethnic identity refers to a person's subjective sense of belonging to an ethnic group, including the feelings, attitudes, and knowledge associated with that membership. It is a developmental process, not a fixed trait.

Phinney's Model of Ethnic Identity Development

Jean Phinney proposed a three-stage model of ethnic identity development that applies across ethnic groups. It draws on Erikson's identity theory and Marcia's identity statuses.

Phinney's Stages

1. Unexamined Ethnic Identity (Diffusion/Foreclosure): The individual has not explored their ethnic identity. They may hold internalized stereotypes or show little interest in ethnicity.
2. Ethnic Identity Search (Moratorium): Triggered by a significant experience (e.g., encountering discrimination), the individual actively explores the meaning and implications of their ethnic group membership.
3. Achieved Ethnic Identity: The individual has a secure, committed sense of their ethnicity. This does not require in-group bias — it reflects a confident, internalized understanding of what ethnic membership means to them.

Example

A biracial adolescent in high school (Stage 1) may have never questioned their identity. After experiencing a microaggression in which a peer asks "But what are you really?" they enter Stage 2, reading about their cultural history and connecting with cultural organizations. Over time, they arrive at Stage 3 with a secure, self-defined sense of identity.

Research indicates that a strong, achieved ethnic identity is associated with higher self-esteem, better academic outcomes, and psychological well-being, particularly for members of minority groups.

Phinney, J. S. (1992). The Multigroup Ethnic Identity Measure: A new scale for use with diverse groups. Journal of Adolescent Research, 7(2), 156–176.

The Self Across Cultures

One of the most influential frameworks in cultural psychology distinguishes between independent and interdependent self-construals, proposed by Hazel Markus and Shinobu Kitayama.

Independent vs. Interdependent Self-Construal

The independent self views the person as a bounded, autonomous entity defined by internal attributes (traits, abilities, preferences). Prevalent in Western, individualist cultures. The interdependent self views the person as fundamentally connected to others, defined by social roles and relationships. Prevalent in East Asian, Latin American, and African collectivist cultures.

Example

The Twenty Statements Test ("I am ___") reveals cross-cultural differences: American respondents tend to list personal attributes ("I am creative," "I am ambitious"), while Japanese respondents tend to list social roles and relationships ("I am a daughter," "I am a member of the swim team").

Markus, H. R., & Kitayama, S. (1991). Culture and the self: Implications for cognition, emotion, and motivation. Psychological Review, 98(2), 224–253.

Cultural Values & Worldviews

Beyond Hofstede's dimensions, several other frameworks describe how cultural values shape psychological experience.

Schwartz's Value Theory

Shalom Schwartz identified 10 basic human values organized along two bipolar dimensions: self-enhancement vs. self-transcendence and openness to change vs. conservation. These values are universal in their existence but vary in their relative importance across cultures.

Emic vs. Etic Approaches

Emic & Etic

Etic approaches study culture from the outside, seeking universal laws that apply across cultures (e.g., Hofstede's dimensions). Emic approaches study culture from the inside, seeking to understand phenomena as they are experienced within a particular cultural context. Effective cross-cultural research typically combines both.

Intersectionality

Intersectionality, a term coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw, recognizes that individuals hold multiple, overlapping social identities (e.g., race, gender, class, sexual orientation, disability) that interact to produce unique experiences of privilege and oppression. A Black woman's experience of discrimination, for example, cannot be understood as the simple sum of racism plus sexism — the intersection creates qualitatively distinct experiences.

Clinical Application

Culturally competent assessment requires understanding clients at the intersection of their identities, not reducing them to a single cultural variable. A clinician should explore how multiple identity dimensions interact to shape the client's presenting problems, coping strategies, and treatment expectations.

Crenshaw, K. (1991). Mapping the margins: Intersectionality, identity politics, and violence against women of color. Stanford Law Review, 43(6), 1241–1299.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Individualism vs. collectivism is the most heavily tested cultural dimension — know examples and psychological implications of each.
  • Berry's acculturation model: Know all four strategies and that integration is associated with the best outcomes.
  • Phinney's ethnic identity model parallels Marcia's identity statuses — the EPPP may present scenarios requiring you to identify the stage.
  • Independent vs. interdependent self-construal (Markus & Kitayama) affects attribution, emotion, motivation, and self-concept.
  • Know the distinction between emic (culture-specific, inside perspective) and etic (universal, outside perspective) approaches.
  • Acculturative stress is a common clinical presentation — be prepared to identify risk factors and protective factors.
  • Intersectionality is increasingly tested: understand that cultural identity is multidimensional and interacting.
  • Do not confuse acculturation (cultural change from contact) with enculturation (socialization into one's own heritage culture).
