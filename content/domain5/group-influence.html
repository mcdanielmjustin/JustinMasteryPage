Group Influence

Conformity, obedience, social facilitation, groupthink

Introduction to Group Influence

Groups exert powerful influence on individual behavior, thought, and emotion. Social psychology has demonstrated that the presence of others -- whether real, imagined, or implied -- fundamentally alters how we think, feel, and act. This chapter covers the major phenomena of group influence that are frequently tested on the EPPP.

The key themes across this chapter are: (1) people are strongly motivated to fit in with groups, (2) group settings can amplify individual tendencies (for better or worse), and (3) awareness of group dynamics is essential for effective clinical and organizational work.

Conformity

Conformity is a change in behavior or belief as a result of real or imagined group pressure. Two types of social influence produce conformity:

  • Informational social influence: Conforming because we believe the group has accurate information (especially in ambiguous situations). Leads to private acceptance -- genuine belief change.
  • Normative social influence: Conforming to gain approval or avoid rejection. Leads to public compliance without private acceptance.

Asch's Conformity Experiments (1951, 1956)

Procedure and Findings

Participants judged which of three comparison lines matched a standard line -- a task with an obvious correct answer. They responded in a group where all other members (confederates) unanimously gave the same wrong answer on critical trials. Results: approximately 75% of participants conformed at least once, and the overall conformity rate was about 37% across all critical trials. Notably, in a control condition without group pressure, errors were less than 1%.

Factors Affecting Conformity

Sherif's Autokinetic Effect (1935)

Sherif used the autokinetic effect (a stationary point of light appearing to move in a dark room) to study conformity in ambiguous situations. Individual judgments converged toward a group norm when participants were tested together. This norm persisted even when participants were later tested alone, demonstrating informational social influence producing genuine private acceptance.

Asch, S. E. (1956). Studies of independence and conformity: A minority of one against a unanimous majority. Psychological Monographs, 70(9), 1-70.

Obedience

Obedience is compliance with a direct command, usually from an authority figure. Stanley Milgram's (1963, 1974) obedience studies are among the most famous and controversial experiments in psychology.

Milgram's Obedience Studies

Procedure and Key Findings

Participants (the "teacher") were instructed by an experimenter to deliver increasingly intense electric shocks (15V to 450V) to a confederate (the "learner") for wrong answers on a memory task. No real shocks were given, but participants believed they were real. Results: 65% of participants obeyed to the maximum 450V level in the baseline condition. All participants obeyed to at least 300V.

Explanations for Obedience

  • Agentic state: Milgram proposed that people shift from an autonomous state to an agentic state in which they see themselves as agents carrying out another person's wishes and therefore feel less personally responsible.
  • Gradual commitment: The incremental increase in shock levels (15V at a time) made it difficult to identify a clear point at which to disobey -- similar to the foot-in-the-door technique.
  • Binding factors: Social politeness, desire not to disrupt the experiment, and commitment to the agreed-upon role made disobedience psychologically difficult.

Ethical Legacy

Milgram's studies were instrumental in developing modern research ethics standards. They contributed to the establishment of Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) and the ethical principles of informed consent, right to withdraw, and debriefing. These studies could not be conducted in their original form today due to the psychological distress experienced by participants.

Milgram, S. (1974). Obedience to authority: An experimental view. Harper & Row.

Social Facilitation and Social Loafing

Social Facilitation

Definition

Social facilitation refers to the tendency for the presence of others to improve performance on simple or well-learned tasks and impair performance on complex or novel tasks. Zajonc (1965) proposed the drive theory explanation: the presence of others increases physiological arousal, which enhances the dominant response. For well-practiced tasks, the dominant response is correct; for novel tasks, the dominant response is often incorrect.

Social Loafing

Definition

Social loafing is the tendency for individuals to exert less effort when working in a group than when working alone (Latane, Williams, & Harkins, 1979). Unlike social facilitation (which involves performing alongside others), social loafing occurs when individual contributions are pooled and individual effort is not identifiable.

Reducing Social Loafing

  • Making individual contributions identifiable
  • Increasing task importance or personal relevance
  • Keeping groups small
  • Increasing group cohesion
  • Setting clear individual goals and accountability

Key Distinction for the EPPP

Social facilitation: Others are present as audience or co-actors; individual performance is evaluable; the presence of others increases arousal. Social loafing: Working in a group with pooled output; individual effort is not identifiable; effort decreases. These are opposite effects that depend on whether individual performance can be evaluated.

Groupthink

Irving Janis (1972, 1982) introduced the concept of groupthink to describe a mode of thinking in highly cohesive groups where the desire for harmony and conformity overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. Janis analyzed several foreign policy disasters (Bay of Pigs, Pearl Harbor) as examples of groupthink.

Antecedent Conditions

  • High group cohesiveness
  • Insulation from outside experts
  • Directive leadership (leader states preference early)
  • Lack of systematic procedures for evaluating alternatives
  • High stress with perceived low likelihood of finding a better solution than the leader's

Prevention Strategies

  • Leaders should refrain from stating preferences at the outset
  • Assign a "devil's advocate" role
  • Invite outside experts to challenge the group
  • Encourage critical evaluation of all alternatives
  • Hold a "second-chance" meeting to reconsider decisions
Janis, I. L. (1982). Groupthink: Psychological studies of policy decisions and fiascoes (2nd ed.). Houghton Mifflin.

Group Polarization

Definition

Group polarization is the tendency for group discussion to strengthen the average initial tendency of group members (Moscovici & Zavalloni, 1969). If members initially lean toward risk, the group decision will be riskier than the average individual decision. If members initially lean toward caution, the group will be more cautious. The group does not move toward a moderate position -- it moves toward a more extreme version of its initial inclination.

Explanations

  • Persuasive arguments theory: During discussion, members hear novel arguments supporting the prevailing position. This pool of predominantly one-sided arguments shifts individuals further in that direction.
  • Social comparison theory: People discover during discussion that others hold positions more extreme than their own and shift to be at least as extreme as others to gain social approval or maintain a positive self-image.

EPPP Exam Tip

Group polarization is distinct from groupthink. Group polarization describes the direction of the shift (toward the extreme of the initial tendency), while groupthink describes a process of defective decision-making in cohesive groups. A group can polarize without experiencing groupthink, and groupthink involves more than just polarization.

Deindividuation

Definition

Deindividuation is a psychological state of reduced self-awareness and diminished sense of personal identity that can occur in group settings, particularly when individuals feel anonymous. In this state, people are more likely to engage in impulsive, antisocial, or norm-violating behavior because the normal restraints of self-monitoring and concern about evaluation are weakened.

Conditions That Promote Deindividuation

  • Anonymity: Wearing uniforms, masks, or being in darkness reduces identifiability.
  • Large group size: Being one of many reduces the feeling of individual accountability.
  • Arousal: High emotional arousal shifts attention away from self-regulation.
  • Reduced self-awareness: Conditions that focus attention outward (rather than inward) promote deindividuation.

Key Research

Zimbardo (1969) demonstrated that participants wearing hoods and lab coats (anonymous condition) delivered more intense shocks than identifiable participants. Diener et al. (1976) found that trick-or-treating children in costumes (anonymous) stole more candy when in groups than when alone or identifiable.

Clinical and Practical Relevance

Deindividuation helps explain mob behavior, online trolling and cyberbullying (where anonymity is high), and the behavior of individuals in institutional settings (e.g., the Stanford Prison Experiment). Understanding deindividuation is relevant to workplace consultation, school psychology, and forensic evaluations. Interventions that increase self-awareness and accountability (mirrors, cameras, name tags) can counteract deindividuation effects.

Minority Influence

While most group influence research focuses on how majorities influence individuals, Serge Moscovici's (1976) work on minority influence examines how a consistent minority can change majority opinions over time.

Conditions for Effective Minority Influence

  • Consistency: The minority must maintain a consistent position over time and across situations. This is the single most important factor.
  • Flexibility: While consistent in their core position, effective minorities demonstrate willingness to negotiate on peripheral issues.
  • Confidence: The minority must appear confident and committed to their position.
  • Identification: Minority influence is stronger when the minority is perceived as part of the ingroup rather than an outgroup.

Conversion Theory (Moscovici, 1980)

Moscovici proposed that majority and minority influence operate through different processes. Majority influence produces compliance (public conformity without private change) through comparison. Minority influence produces conversion (genuine private attitude change) through validation -- the minority's consistent position triggers deeper processing of the issue. Importantly, minority influence often manifests as indirect influence -- the person changes attitudes on related (but not the exact) issues raised by the minority.

Clinical and Organizational Applications

Understanding group influence processes is essential for psychologists working in organizational consulting, group therapy, and community settings. Group therapists must manage conformity pressure, encourage productive dissent, guard against groupthink in treatment teams, and use group processes therapeutically. In organizational settings, understanding social facilitation, loafing, polarization, and minority influence helps design more effective teams and decision-making procedures.
