Where We Break — Prejudice, Discrimination & Conflict

Stereotypes, social identity theory, stereotype threat, reducing prejudice
FlashcardsQuick Quiz: FundamentalAdvanced Learning

The Anatomy of Prejudice

Prejudice, stereotyping, and discrimination form a triad of interrelated but distinct phenomena. Understanding the distinctions is critical for the EPPP.

The Three Components

Stereotypes (cognitive): Generalized beliefs about the characteristics of a group and its members.
Prejudice (affective): A negative (or positive) emotional attitude toward a group and its members.
Discrimination (behavioral): Unjustified negative behavior directed toward members of a group based on their group membership.

A person can hold stereotypes without being prejudiced and can be prejudiced without discriminating. However, all three frequently co-occur and reinforce one another.

Theories of Prejudice

Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)

Social Identity Theory

Henri Tajfel proposed that people derive part of their self-concept from the groups to which they belong (in-groups). To maintain positive self-esteem, people are motivated to view their in-groups favorably, leading to in-group favoritism and out-group derogation. Social categorization alone is sufficient to trigger bias.

Minimal Group Paradigm

Tajfel demonstrated that even when people are assigned to groups based on trivial criteria (e.g., preference for Klee vs. Kandinsky paintings), they show in-group bias — allocating more resources to in-group members. Mere categorization, not conflict, is sufficient for bias.

Realistic Conflict Theory

Realistic Conflict Theory & Robbers Cave (Sherif, 1961)

Muzafer Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment demonstrated that intergroup hostility arises from competition over limited resources. Two groups of boys ("Eagles" and "Rattlers") developed intense animosity when placed in competition. Hostility was reduced only when the groups had to cooperate on superordinate goals — tasks neither group could accomplish alone.

Example: Superordinate Goals

Simply bringing the groups together for pleasant activities (movies, meals) did not reduce hostility. Only when they had to work together — fixing the camp water supply, pulling a stalled truck — did prejudice decrease. This finding has been applied in schools, workplaces, and international diplomacy.

Allport's Contact Hypothesis

Gordon Allport (1954) proposed that intergroup contact can reduce prejudice only under specific conditions:

  • Equal status: Groups must interact as equals
  • Common goals: Groups must work toward shared objectives
  • Intergroup cooperation: Achievement of goals must require cooperation
  • Support of authorities: Contact must be sanctioned by institutional authority

Allport, G. W. (1954). The nature of prejudice. Addison-Wesley.

Modern Forms of Prejudice

Aversive Racism

Aversive racism (Gaertner & Dovidio) describes people who sincerely endorse egalitarian values but harbor unconscious negative feelings toward out-groups. Bias emerges in ambiguous situations where discrimination can be rationalized on non-racial grounds.

Modern (Symbolic) Racism

Modern racism expresses prejudice indirectly through opposition to policies perceived as benefiting minority groups while denying that discrimination still exists.

Microaggressions

Microaggressions (Sue et al., 2007)

Brief, commonplace exchanges that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative slights toward marginalized groups. Three types: microassaults (deliberate), microinsults (unintentionally demeaning), and microinvalidations (dismissing experiential reality). Examples: "Where are you really from?" or "You're so articulate" (expressed with surprise to a person of color).

Clinical Relevance

Cumulative exposure to microaggressions is associated with depression, anxiety, decreased self-esteem, and racial battle fatigue. Clinicians must recognize microaggressions both as experiences reported by clients and as behaviors they themselves might inadvertently commit in the therapeutic relationship.

Stereotype Threat

Stereotype Threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995)

Stereotype threat is the apprehension experienced when a person is at risk of confirming a negative stereotype about their group. This anxiety consumes working memory and impairs performance. The person does not need to believe the stereotype — mere awareness that others might apply it is sufficient.

Classic Findings

When Black students were told a test measured intellectual ability, they performed worse than White students. When the same test was described as non-diagnostic, the gap disappeared. Similarly, women performed worse on math tests when gender stereotypes were salient, and White men performed worse on athletic tasks when racial athletic stereotypes were activated.

Factors that reduce stereotype threat: reframing the task as non-diagnostic, emphasizing growth mindset, providing in-group role models, and self-affirmation exercises.

Clinical Application

Stereotype threat is directly relevant to psychological testing. When administering cognitive or achievement tests to members of stereotyped groups, clinicians should create non-threatening testing environments and frame tests carefully to obtain more valid results.

Steele, C. M., & Aronson, J. (1995). Stereotype threat and the intellectual test performance of African Americans. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 69(5), 797–811.

Reducing Prejudice

The Jigsaw Classroom

Developed by Elliot Aronson, the jigsaw classroom gives each student a unique piece of information necessary for the group to complete a task. This creates interdependence, equal status, and shared goals — Allport's conditions for prejudice reduction.

Common In-Group Identity Model

Gaertner and Dovidio proposed that prejudice can be reduced by encouraging members of different groups to recategorize themselves as members of a single, inclusive group — transforming "us vs. them" into "we."

Example

A company reorganizes competitive departments into cross-functional teams. Employees who identified as "Marketing" or "Engineering" begin identifying as "Team Alpha," reducing interdepartmental bias and increasing cooperation.

Aggression

Aggression Defined

Aggression is behavior intended to harm another person who is motivated to avoid that harm. The key element is intent — accidental harm is not aggression, and the target must be unwilling. Aggression can be physical, verbal, or relational.

Types of Aggression

Researchers distinguish two fundamental types of aggression:

  • Hostile (affective/reactive) aggression: Driven by anger and the desire to harm the target. The harm itself is the goal. It is impulsive, emotional, and typically a response to perceived provocation or frustration.
  • Instrumental (proactive) aggression: Aggression used as a means to an end — the goal is not to harm per se, but to obtain something (resources, power, status). It is calculated and goal-directed rather than emotional.
Example

A person who punches someone in a rage after being insulted is displaying hostile aggression. A hired enforcer who threatens a shop owner to collect a debt is displaying instrumental aggression — the harm serves a strategic purpose.

Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis

Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis (Dollard et al., 1939)

The original hypothesis proposed that frustration always leads to some form of aggression, and aggression is always the result of frustration. Frustration was defined as the blocking of a goal-directed behavior.

This strong formulation was later revised by Leonard Berkowitz, who argued that frustration produces a readiness for aggression (negative affect and arousal), but whether aggression actually occurs depends on the presence of aggressive cues in the environment. Frustration creates the emotional groundwork; situational cues trigger the aggressive response.

Dollard, J., Doob, L. W., Miller, N. E., Mowrer, O. H., & Sears, R. R. (1939). Frustration and aggression. Yale University Press.

Social Learning Theory of Aggression (Bandura)

Bandura's Social Learning Theory & the Bobo Doll Experiment

Albert Bandura demonstrated that aggression can be learned through observational learning (modeling). In his famous Bobo doll experiments (1961, 1963), children who observed an adult model behaving aggressively toward an inflatable Bobo doll were significantly more aggressive when later placed in a room with the doll, compared to children who observed a non-aggressive model or no model. Critically, children imitated specific novel aggressive acts they had observed (e.g., hitting the doll with a mallet, sitting on it, punching its nose).

Key principles from Bandura's social learning theory of aggression:

  • Observational learning: Aggression is acquired by watching others behave aggressively — children do not need to be directly reinforced to learn aggressive behavior
  • Vicarious reinforcement: When observers see a model rewarded for aggression, they are more likely to imitate it; when the model is punished, imitation decreases (though the behavior is still learned)
  • Modeling: The model's status, similarity to the observer, and the perceived consequences of aggression all influence whether the observed behavior is performed
  • Distinction between acquisition and performance: People may learn aggressive behaviors through observation but only perform them when conditions are favorable

Bandura, A., Ross, D., & Ross, S. A. (1961). Transmission of aggression through imitation of aggressive models. Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology, 63, 575–582.

Excitation Transfer Theory

Excitation Transfer Theory (Zillmann)

Dolf Zillmann proposed that residual physiological arousal from one situation can transfer to a subsequent situation and intensify an emotional response, including aggression. If a person is still physiologically aroused from exercise, an exciting movie, or a prior confrontation, that residual arousal can be misattributed to a new provocation, making an aggressive response more intense than it otherwise would be.

Example

A person finishes an intense workout and is still physiologically aroused (elevated heart rate, adrenaline). Shortly after, another driver honks at them. The residual arousal from the workout transfers to the new situation, amplifying their anger response beyond what the provocation alone would produce.

The Weapons Effect

Weapons Effect (Berkowitz & LePage, 1967)

The mere presence of weapons in the environment increases aggressive behavior. In the original study, participants who had been angered delivered more electric shocks when a gun was visible in the room than when a neutral object (such as a badminton racquet) was present. Berkowitz summarized this as: "The finger pulls the trigger, but the trigger may also be pulling the finger." Weapons serve as aggressive cues that prime aggressive cognitions and behaviors.

The Catharsis Hypothesis

The catharsis hypothesis suggests that expressing aggression — or engaging in aggressive activities like watching violent media, punching a pillow, or venting anger — reduces the urge to aggress by releasing pent-up aggressive energy. This idea has roots in Freudian hydraulic models of emotion.

Research Verdict: Largely Debunked

Extensive research has shown that catharsis does not reduce aggression and often increases it. Venting anger, hitting objects, or engaging in aggressive fantasies tends to maintain or escalate arousal and aggressive feelings rather than dissipate them. Bushman (2002) found that participants who hit a punching bag while thinking about someone who angered them were more aggressive afterward, not less. This is an important counterintuitive finding for the EPPP.

Biological Factors in Aggression

Multiple biological systems contribute to aggressive behavior:

  • Testosterone: Higher levels are correlated with increased aggression, dominance-seeking, and status competition, though the relationship is bidirectional (winning also raises testosterone)
  • Serotonin: Low serotonin levels are associated with impulsive aggression (hostile/reactive type). The serotonin deficiency hypothesis suggests that low serotonergic activity reduces inhibition of aggressive impulses
  • Amygdala: Central to threat detection and emotional arousal; stimulation of the amygdala can trigger aggressive responses. Lesions may reduce aggressive behavior
  • Prefrontal cortex: Responsible for impulse control and behavioral regulation. Damage or reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex (particularly the orbitofrontal region) is associated with disinhibited aggression

Cultural Factors in Aggression

Culture of Honor (Nisbett & Cohen, 1996)

Research by Nisbett and Cohen documented a culture of honor in the U.S. South, where insults — particularly those threatening a man's reputation — provoke stronger physiological and behavioral aggressive responses than in Northern U.S. samples. Southern participants who were insulted showed greater increases in cortisol and testosterone and were more likely to respond aggressively. This cultural pattern is linked historically to herding economies where protecting one's reputation and property was essential for survival.

Media violence effects: Meta-analyses consistently show that exposure to media violence is associated with increased aggressive cognitions, affect, and behavior, and with decreased empathy and prosocial behavior. Effects are present in both short-term (priming) and long-term (desensitization, script development) contexts, though the magnitude of the effect is debated.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Know the cognitive-affective-behavioral triad: stereotypes, prejudice, discrimination.
  • Social identity theory (Tajfel): In-group favoritism from need for positive self-esteem. Minimal group paradigm shows mere categorization suffices for bias.
  • Realistic conflict theory (Sherif): Competition generates hostility; superordinate goals reduce it.
  • Allport's contact hypothesis: Equal status, common goals, cooperation, institutional support.
  • Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson): Awareness of stereotypes impairs performance via working memory drain. Relevant to testing validity.
  • Aversive racism: Unconscious bias in egalitarian people; emerges in ambiguous situations.
  • Microaggressions: Three types (microassaults, microinsults, microinvalidations) with cumulative psychological impact.
  • Jigsaw classroom (Aronson): Cooperative learning that creates interdependence.
  • Common In-Group Identity Model: Recategorization into shared group reduces bias.
  • Hostile vs. instrumental aggression: Hostile is emotional/reactive (harm is the goal); instrumental is calculated/proactive (harm is a means).
  • Frustration-aggression hypothesis (Dollard et al.): Frustration leads to aggression; Berkowitz's revision adds that frustration creates readiness but aggressive cues trigger the behavior.
  • Bandura's Bobo doll experiment: Aggression is learned through observation and modeling. Children imitated specific novel aggressive acts. Vicarious reinforcement modulates performance, not acquisition.
  • Excitation transfer (Zillmann): Residual arousal from one source intensifies aggression in a new situation.
  • Weapons effect (Berkowitz & LePage): Presence of weapons increases aggressive behavior — weapons serve as aggressive cues.
  • Catharsis hypothesis is debunked: Venting anger increases rather than decreases aggression.
  • Biological factors: Low serotonin = impulsive aggression; high testosterone = dominance; amygdala = threat response; prefrontal cortex = inhibition.
  • Culture of honor (Nisbett & Cohen): Insults provoke stronger aggressive responses in honor cultures.
