Personality Disorders

Cluster A, B, C; BPD, ASPD, differential diagnosis
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

General Diagnostic Criteria for Personality Disorders

A personality disorder is an enduring pattern of inner experience and behavior that deviates markedly from the expectations of the individual's culture, is pervasive and inflexible, has an onset in adolescence or early adulthood, is stable over time, and leads to distress or impairment. The general criteria require the pattern to be manifested in two or more of the following areas:

  1. Cognition -- ways of perceiving and interpreting self, others, and events
  2. Affectivity -- range, intensity, lability, and appropriateness of emotional response
  3. Interpersonal functioning
  4. Impulse control

Key Concept: Ego-Syntonic Nature

Personality disorder traits are typically ego-syntonic -- the individual views them as consistent with their self-image and may not recognize them as problematic. This contrasts with most Axis I disorders, where symptoms are ego-dystonic. This ego-syntonic quality often leads to poor insight and resistance to treatment, as individuals may not perceive a need for change.

The DSM-5 retains the categorical model of 10 personality disorders organized into three clusters, while also including an Alternative DSM-5 Model for Personality Disorders (AMPD) in Section III for further study.

Cluster A: Odd or Eccentric

Cluster A personality disorders are characterized by odd, eccentric thinking and behavior. There is a genetic association with schizophrenia spectrum disorders.

Paranoid Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of distrust and suspiciousness of others such that their motives are interpreted as malevolent. Features include suspecting exploitation or harm, doubting loyalty of friends, reluctance to confide, reading hidden meanings into benign remarks, bearing grudges, perceiving attacks on character, and recurring suspicions of partner infidelity.

Differential Diagnosis

Paranoid PD vs. delusional disorder (persecutory type): In paranoid PD, the suspicions are not of delusional intensity -- they are exaggerated interpretations but not fixed, false beliefs. Paranoid PD vs. paranoid schizophrenia: Paranoid PD lacks hallucinations, disorganized speech, and other psychotic features.

Schizoid Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of detachment from social relationships and a restricted range of emotional expression. Features include neither desiring nor enjoying close relationships, choosing solitary activities, little interest in sexual experiences, taking pleasure in few activities, lacking close friends, appearing indifferent to praise or criticism, and emotional coldness or flattened affect.

Schizotypal Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of social and interpersonal deficits marked by acute discomfort with close relationships, cognitive or perceptual distortions, and eccentricities of behavior. Features include ideas of reference, odd beliefs or magical thinking, unusual perceptual experiences, odd thinking and speech, suspiciousness, inappropriate or constricted affect, odd behavior or appearance, lack of close friends, and excessive social anxiety that does not diminish with familiarity.

Clinical Pearl

Schizotypal PD has the strongest genetic link to schizophrenia and is considered part of the schizophrenia spectrum. It involves quasi-psychotic symptoms (ideas of reference, magical thinking, perceptual distortions) that fall short of true psychosis. In the ICD-10, schizotypal disorder is classified as a psychotic disorder rather than a personality disorder.

Cluster B: Dramatic, Emotional, or Erratic

Cluster B personality disorders involve dramatic, emotional, or erratic behavior. These are among the most frequently tested personality disorders on the EPPP.

Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD)

A pervasive pattern of disregard for and violation of the rights of others, occurring since age 15. The individual must be at least 18 years old, and there must be evidence of conduct disorder before age 15.

  • Failure to conform to social norms with respect to lawful behaviors
  • Deceitfulness (lying, using aliases, conning for profit/pleasure)
  • Impulsivity or failure to plan ahead
  • Irritability and aggressiveness (repeated fights/assaults)
  • Reckless disregard for safety of self or others
  • Consistent irresponsibility (work, financial obligations)
  • Lack of remorse

ASPD vs. Psychopathy

Psychopathy (as measured by Hare's PCL-R) is a related but distinct construct. The PCL-R captures two factors: Factor 1 (interpersonal/affective traits: glibness, grandiosity, lack of empathy, shallow affect) and Factor 2 (antisocial lifestyle: impulsivity, irresponsibility, criminal versatility). Most individuals with psychopathy meet criteria for ASPD, but most individuals with ASPD do not meet criteria for psychopathy (approximately 50-80% of inmates meet ASPD criteria, but only about 15-25% score high on the PCL-R).

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

A pervasive pattern of instability in interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affects, and marked impulsivity. Five or more of nine criteria are required:

  1. Frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment
  2. Pattern of unstable and intense interpersonal relationships (idealization/devaluation, "splitting")
  3. Identity disturbance: markedly unstable self-image or sense of self
  4. Impulsivity in at least two areas that are potentially self-damaging
  5. Recurrent suicidal behavior, gestures, threats, or self-mutilating behavior
  6. Affective instability due to marked reactivity of mood
  7. Chronic feelings of emptiness
  8. Inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling anger
  9. Transient, stress-related paranoid ideation or severe dissociative symptoms

Clinical Pearl

Linehan's (1993) biosocial theory proposes that BPD arises from the transaction between biological vulnerability to emotional dysregulation and an invalidating environment (one that erratically, inappropriately, or negligently responds to private experiences). Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is the best-supported treatment for BPD and targets emotion regulation, distress tolerance, interpersonal effectiveness, and mindfulness. Kernberg's object relations approach emphasizes splitting as the primary defense mechanism in BPD.

Histrionic Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of excessive emotionality and attention seeking. Features include discomfort when not the center of attention, inappropriately seductive or provocative behavior, shallow and rapidly shifting emotions, use of physical appearance to draw attention, impressionistic speech lacking detail, self-dramatization, suggestibility, and considering relationships more intimate than they are.

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy. Features include grandiose sense of self-importance, preoccupation with fantasies of success/power/beauty, belief in being special, requiring excessive admiration, sense of entitlement, interpersonal exploitation, lack of empathy, envy of others or belief that others are envious, and arrogant behaviors/attitudes.

EPPP Example

Kohut's self psychology conceptualizes narcissistic pathology as resulting from empathic failures by early caregivers (selfobjects), leading to a fragmented self. Treatment involves providing empathic mirroring and idealizing transferences. Kernberg, in contrast, views narcissistic personality as a pathological grandiose self that defends against underlying aggression and envy, requiring confrontation of defenses in treatment.

Cluster C: Anxious or Fearful

Cluster C personality disorders are characterized by anxious and fearful behavior. They share features with anxiety disorders but are distinguished by their pervasiveness and ego-syntonic quality.

Avoidant Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation. Features include avoidance of occupational activities involving interpersonal contact due to fear of criticism, unwillingness to get involved unless certain of being liked, restraint in intimate relationships due to shame, preoccupation with rejection in social situations, inhibited in new interpersonal situations, views self as inept/unappealing/inferior, and reluctance to take personal risks or engage in new activities.

Avoidant PD and social anxiety disorder share significant overlap. The key distinction is that avoidant PD involves a more pervasive pattern of avoidance and a more deeply ingrained sense of personal inadequacy that extends beyond specific social performance situations (Lampe & Malhi, 2018).

Dependent Personality Disorder

A pervasive and excessive need to be taken care of that leads to submissive and clinging behavior and fears of separation. Features include difficulty making everyday decisions without advice, needing others to assume responsibility for major life areas, difficulty expressing disagreement, difficulty initiating projects independently, going to excessive lengths to obtain nurturance, feeling uncomfortable or helpless when alone, urgently seeking another relationship when one ends, and unrealistic preoccupation with fears of being left to care for oneself.

Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD)

A pervasive pattern of preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and mental and interpersonal control, at the expense of flexibility, openness, and efficiency. Features include preoccupation with details/rules/order, perfectionism interfering with task completion, excessive devotion to work, over-conscientiousness about morality, inability to discard worn-out objects, reluctance to delegate, miserly spending style, and rigidity/stubbornness.

OCPD vs. OCD

OCPD involves ego-syntonic traits -- the person's perfectionism and control feel consistent with who they are. OCD involves ego-dystonic obsessions and compulsions -- the person recognizes them as excessive and distressing. OCPD does not involve true obsessions or compulsions. Despite the name similarity, only about 23-32% of individuals with OCD also have OCPD. OCPD is the most prevalent personality disorder in the general population (approximately 3-8%).

Alternative DSM-5 Model for Personality Disorders (AMPD)

Section III of the DSM-5 presents an alternative dimensional-categorical hybrid model for personality disorder diagnosis. This model proposes two core criteria:

Criterion A: Level of Personality Functioning

Assessed using the Level of Personality Functioning Scale (LPFS), which evaluates impairments across four elements:

  • Identity: Experience of oneself as unique; self-esteem stability; emotional range
  • Self-direction: Pursuit of goals; self-reflective functioning; internal standards
  • Empathy: Comprehension of others' experiences; tolerance of differing perspectives
  • Intimacy: Depth and duration of connection; desire and capacity for closeness

Criterion B: Pathological Personality Traits

Organized into five broad trait domains (with 25 trait facets):

  1. Negative Affectivity (vs. Emotional Stability)
  2. Detachment (vs. Extraversion)
  3. Antagonism (vs. Agreeableness)
  4. Disinhibition (vs. Conscientiousness)
  5. Psychoticism (vs. Lucidity)

The AMPD retains six specific personality disorders: antisocial, avoidant, borderline, narcissistic, obsessive-compulsive, and schizotypal. The remaining four (paranoid, schizoid, histrionic, dependent) can be described using Criterion A and B but are not retained as named types.

Epidemiology, Course, and Treatment

Prevalence

The overall prevalence of any personality disorder in the general population is estimated at approximately 9-15% (Trull et al., 2010). By cluster:

  • Cluster A: Approximately 5.7% total (paranoid 2.3-4.4%, schizoid 3.1-4.9%, schizotypal 3.3-3.9%)
  • Cluster B: Approximately 1.5-6% (antisocial 1-4% with higher rates in males, borderline 1.6-5.9%, histrionic 1.8%, narcissistic 0-6.2%)
  • Cluster C: Approximately 2.4-6% (avoidant 2.4%, dependent 0.5-0.6%, OCPD 2.1-7.9%)

Course

Longitudinal research has challenged the assumption that personality disorders are stable and unchanging. The Collaborative Longitudinal Personality Disorders Study (CLPS) and other longitudinal studies have shown that many personality disorders improve over time, particularly BPD. Remission rates for BPD are approximately 85% over 10 years, though functional impairment tends to persist even after symptomatic remission (Zanarini et al., 2012).

DisorderTreatment Approaches

EPPP Example

Mentalization-Based Treatment (MBT), developed by Bateman and Fonagy, targets the capacity to understand behavior in terms of mental states (thoughts, feelings, intentions). Individuals with BPD show impaired mentalization, particularly under emotional stress, which contributes to interpersonal difficulties. MBT has demonstrated efficacy in reducing self-harm, suicide attempts, and hospitalization in BPD. The concept of mentalization overlaps with but is distinct from theory of mind, empathy, and psychological mindedness.

EPPP Focus Points

  • Personality disorders are ego-syntonic; most Axis I disorders are ego-dystonic
  • Cluster A (odd/eccentric): Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal -- genetically linked to schizophrenia
  • Cluster B (dramatic/erratic): Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic
  • Cluster C (anxious/fearful): Avoidant, Dependent, OCPD
  • ASPD requires age 18+ with evidence of CD before age 15
  • Psychopathy (PCL-R) is narrower than ASPD -- most ASPD do not meet PCL-R psychopathy criteria
  • BPD's core features: splitting, abandonment fears, identity disturbance, affective instability
  • DBT (Linehan) is the gold-standard treatment for BPD
  • OCPD is ego-syntonic orderliness; OCD is ego-dystonic obsessions/compulsions
  • Schizotypal PD has the strongest genetic link to schizophrenia
  • The AMPD uses Level of Personality Functioning (Criterion A) and pathological traits (Criterion B)
  • Kohut (empathic mirroring) vs. Kernberg (confrontation of defenses) for narcissistic pathology
