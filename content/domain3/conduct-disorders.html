Disruptive & Conduct Disorders

ODD, conduct disorder, IED, antisocial trajectories
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of Disruptive, Impulse-Control, and Conduct Disorders

This DSM-5 chapter groups disorders characterized by problems in the self-regulation of emotions and behaviors. These conditions manifest as behaviors that violate the rights of others (e.g., aggression, destruction of property) and/or bring the individual into significant conflict with societal norms or authority figures. These disorders are more common in males and typically have onset in childhood or adolescence.

Key Concept: Externalizing vs. Internalizing

Disruptive and conduct disorders are classified as externalizing disorders, meaning the core symptoms are directed outward toward the environment (aggression, defiance, rule violation). This contrasts with internalizing disorders (e.g., depression, anxiety), where distress is directed inward. However, significant comorbidity between externalizing and internalizing disorders is common.

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Oppositional Defiant Disorder is a pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, or vindictiveness lasting at least 6 months, evidenced by at least four symptoms from three categories:

Symptom Categories

Angry/Irritable Mood

  • Often loses temper
  • Is often touchy or easily annoyed
  • Is often angry and resentful

Argumentative/Defiant Behavior

  • Often argues with authority figures or adults
  • Often actively defies or refuses to comply with requests or rules
  • Often deliberately annoys others
  • Often blames others for his or her mistakes or misbehavior

Vindictiveness

  • Has been spiteful or vindictive at least twice within the past 6 months

Clinical Pearl

DSM-5 added severity specifiers for ODD: mild (symptoms confined to one setting), moderate (some symptoms in at least two settings), and severe (symptoms in three or more settings). Research suggests the angry/irritable mood dimension of ODD is a stronger predictor of later internalizing disorders (depression, anxiety), while the behavioral dimensions predict later conduct disorder and ASPD (Stringaris & Goodman, 2009).

ODD prevalence is approximately 3.3%, with a male predominance in childhood that equalizes by adolescence. It typically emerges in the preschool years and is usually evident before age 8. ODD often precedes the development of conduct disorder, but the majority of children with ODD do not progress to conduct disorder.

Conduct Disorder (CD)

Conduct Disorder involves a repetitive and persistent pattern of behavior in which the basic rights of others or major age-appropriate societal norms are violated. The DSM-5 requires at least 3 of 15 criteria in the past 12 months, with at least 1 criterion present in the past 6 months, across four categories:

Symptom Categories

CategoryExamples
Aggression to people and animalsBullying, initiating fights, using weapons, physical cruelty to people or animals, forcing sexual activity
Destruction of propertyDeliberately setting fires, deliberately destroying property
Deceitfulness or theftBreaking into someone's house/car, lying to obtain goods, shoplifting
Serious violations of rulesStaying out late (before age 13), running away, truancy (before age 13)

Onset Specifiers

Childhood-Onset vs. Adolescent-Onset

Childhood-onset type: At least one criterion characteristic of CD prior to age 10. Associated with more persistent aggression, worse prognosis, and higher likelihood of developing ASPD in adulthood. Adolescent-onset type: No criteria present prior to age 10. Associated with less aggression, more influence of deviant peers, and a better prognosis. Unspecified onset: Criteria met but insufficient information to determine onset.

The Callous-Unemotional (CU) Specifier

The DSM-5 added the specifier "with limited prosocial emotions" (the callous-unemotional traits specifier). To qualify, the individual must display at least two of the following persistently over at least 12 months and in multiple settings:

  • Lack of remorse or guilt -- does not feel bad about misbehavior
  • Callous -- lack of empathy -- disregards others' feelings
  • Unconcerned about performance -- does not show concern about poor performance at school, work, or other activities
  • Shallow or deficient affect -- does not express emotions or appears insincere

EPPP Example

Children with CD and CU traits show distinct neurobiological features: reduced amygdala reactivity to fearful facial expressions, lower cortisol reactivity to stress, and diminished autonomic arousal (Frick et al., 2014). They tend to show proactive (instrumental) aggression rather than reactive aggression, and they respond poorly to punishment-based interventions but may respond to reward-based approaches.

Intermittent Explosive Disorder (IED)

Intermittent Explosive Disorder is characterized by recurrent behavioral outbursts representing a failure to control aggressive impulses. The outbursts are grossly out of proportion to the provocation and are not premeditated.

DSM-5 Criteria

  • Frequency: Verbal aggression (tantrums, tirades) or physical aggression toward property, animals, or others occurring twice weekly on average for 3 months (need not cause damage), OR three behavioral outbursts involving damage/destruction or physical assault within a 12-month period
  • The magnitude of aggression is grossly out of proportion to the provocation
  • Not premeditated or committed to achieve a tangible objective
  • Causes marked distress or impairment, or financial/legal consequences
  • Chronological age of at least 6 years (or equivalent developmental level)
  • Not better explained by another mental disorder, medical condition, or substance
Research suggests that IED involves dysregulation of serotonergic systems, particularly reduced serotonin activity in the prefrontal cortex, leading to impaired top-down regulation of aggression mediated by the amygdala (Coccaro et al., 2015).

The Developmental Trajectory to Antisocial Personality Disorder

One of the most important concepts for the EPPP is the developmental progression from childhood disruptive behavior to adult Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD). This trajectory is well-established but not inevitable.

The Typical Pathway

  1. Temperamental risk factors in infancy (difficult temperament, irritability)
  2. ODD emerging in preschool/early childhood (ages 3-8)
  3. Conduct Disorder, childhood-onset type (before age 10)
  4. ASPD diagnosis at age 18 or older (requires evidence of CD before age 15)

Clinical Pearl

ASPD in DSM-5 requires evidence of conduct disorder with onset before age 15. Approximately 40% of children with childhood-onset CD develop ASPD. Moffitt's (1993) developmental taxonomy distinguishes life-course persistent antisocial behavior (beginning in childhood, neuropsychological deficits, poor prognosis) from adolescence-limited antisocial behavior (emerging in teen years, peer-influenced, typically resolves by adulthood).

Etiology and Theoretical Models

Biological Factors

  • Genetics: Heritability of antisocial behavior is estimated at 40-60%. CU traits show particularly strong heritability (Viding et al., 2005).
  • Autonomic underarousal: Low resting heart rate is one of the most replicated biological correlates of antisocial behavior in children (Ortiz & Raine, 2004).
  • Neurotransmitters: Low serotonin levels associated with impulsive aggression; abnormal dopamine functioning linked to reward sensitivity.
  • Neuroanatomy: Reduced prefrontal cortex volume and function, reduced amygdala reactivity (particularly in those with CU traits).

Psychological and Social Factors

  • Patterson's coercion theory (1982): Maladaptive parent-child interaction cycles in which the child learns that escalating aversive behavior is reinforced by parental capitulation, creating a coercive cycle.
  • Social information processing: Dodge and Crick's (1994) model posits that aggressive children show biases in processing social cues, particularly a hostile attribution bias -- the tendency to attribute hostile intent to ambiguous social situations.
  • Attachment: Disorganized attachment in infancy is associated with increased risk for externalizing behavior problems.

Evidence-Based Treatments

Parent-Focused Interventions

  • Parent Management Training (PMT): Based on Patterson's coercion theory; teaches parents to use positive reinforcement, effective commands, consistent consequences, and to avoid coercive cycles. PMT has the strongest evidence base for ODD and early-onset CD (Kazdin, 2005).
  • Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT): For children ages 2-7; uses live coaching (via earpiece) to improve the parent-child relationship and teach effective discipline strategies in real time.
  • The Incredible Years: Group-based parent training program with additional child and teacher components; reduces conduct problems and increases positive parenting.

Child/Adolescent-Focused Interventions

  • Problem-Solving Skills Training (PSST): Teaches cognitive problem-solving steps to reduce aggressive responses to social situations; addresses hostile attribution bias.
  • Multisystemic Therapy (MST): Intensive, family- and community-based treatment for serious juvenile offenders; addresses multiple systems (family, peer, school, community); reduces recidivism and out-of-home placements (Henggeler et al., 1998).
  • Functional Family Therapy (FFT): Addresses family communication patterns and relational dynamics; evidence-based for adolescents with conduct problems.

EPPP Example

For children with CU traits, standard punishment-based approaches are less effective because these children show reduced sensitivity to punishment cues. Reward-based approaches emphasizing positive reinforcement are recommended instead. Dadds et al. (2012) demonstrated that targeting emotion recognition and empathy through parent-mediated interventions can improve outcomes for children with CU traits.

EPPP Focus Points

  • ODD requires 4 symptoms for 6 months across three categories: angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, vindictiveness
  • Conduct disorder requires 3 of 15 criteria in 12 months (at least 1 in past 6 months)
  • Childhood-onset CD (before age 10) has a worse prognosis than adolescent-onset
  • The "with limited prosocial emotions" specifier (CU traits) is new in DSM-5 and associated with proactive aggression, reduced empathy, and lower treatment response
  • ASPD requires evidence of CD before age 15 and cannot be diagnosed before age 18
  • Moffitt's taxonomy: life-course persistent vs. adolescence-limited antisocial behavior
  • Low resting heart rate is a robust biological correlate of antisocial behavior in youth
  • Patterson's coercion theory: parent-child coercive cycles maintain oppositional behavior
  • Dodge's hostile attribution bias: aggressive children attribute hostile intent to ambiguous situations
  • IED requires outbursts grossly out of proportion to the provocation and is linked to serotonergic dysfunction
