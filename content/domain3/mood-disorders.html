Bipolar & Depressive Disorders

MDD, bipolar I/II, cyclothymia, suicide risk

Overview of Depressive and Bipolar Disorders

The DSM-5 separates mood disorders into two distinct chapters: Depressive Disorders and Bipolar and Related Disorders. This reorganization reflects growing evidence that bipolar disorders are more closely related to schizophrenia spectrum disorders in terms of genetics, symptomatology, and neurobiology. Mood disorders collectively represent one of the leading causes of disability worldwide (WHO, 2017).

Mood Episode Types

A major depressive episode involves at least 5 of 9 symptoms lasting 2 or more weeks, with depressed mood or anhedonia required. A manic episode involves abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with increased energy lasting at least 7 days (or any duration if hospitalization is required). A hypomanic episode meets the same symptom criteria as mania but lasts at least 4 consecutive days and does not cause marked impairment or psychotic features.

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

Five or more of the following symptoms present during the same 2-week period, representing a change from previous functioning. At least one must be (1) depressed mood or (2) loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia):

  1. Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day (in children/adolescents, can be irritable mood)
  2. Anhedonia -- markedly diminished interest or pleasure in all or almost all activities
  3. Significant weight change (more than 5% in a month) or appetite change
  4. Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day
  5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation observable by others
  6. Fatigue or loss of energy
  7. Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt
  8. Diminished ability to think or concentrate, or indecisiveness
  9. Recurrent thoughts of death, suicidal ideation, or a suicide attempt

The mnemonic SIG E CAPS (Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor changes, Suicidality) is commonly used to remember these criteria.

Clinical Pearl

DSM-5 removed the bereavement exclusion that was present in DSM-IV. Previously, depressive symptoms within 2 months of losing a loved one were excluded from an MDD diagnosis. The DSM-5 now allows MDD to be diagnosed even in the context of bereavement, recognizing that bereavement can trigger a full depressive episode.

Neurotransmitter Theories

  • Monoamine hypothesis: Depression results from deficits in serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine (NE), and/or dopamine (DA). Supported by the mechanism of action of SSRIs, SNRIs, and MAOIs.
  • HPA axis dysregulation: Chronic stress leads to elevated cortisol via the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. Non-suppression on the dexamethasone suppression test is associated with depression, though it lacks diagnostic specificity.
  • Neuroplasticity model: Reduced brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and hippocampal volume reductions are observed in chronic depression (Duman & Monteggia, 2006).

Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Persistent depressive disorder (PDD) consolidates what DSM-IV called dysthymic disorder and chronic major depressive disorder. It requires depressed mood for most of the day, for more days than not, for at least 2 years (1 year in children and adolescents), plus two or more of the following:

  • Poor appetite or overeating
  • Insomnia or hypersomnia
  • Low energy or fatigue
  • Low self-esteem
  • Poor concentration or difficulty making decisions
  • Feelings of hopelessness

EPPP Example

When a patient has PDD and also meets criteria for a major depressive episode, this is termed "double depression." The prognosis for double depression is worse than for MDD alone, with higher relapse rates and greater functional impairment (Klein et al., 2006).

Bipolar I and Bipolar II Disorders

Bipolar I Disorder

Bipolar I disorder requires the occurrence of at least one manic episode. The manic episode may have been preceded by or followed by hypomanic or major depressive episodes, but these are not required for diagnosis. During a manic episode, at least three of the following symptoms are present (four if mood is only irritable):

  1. Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity
  2. Decreased need for sleep (e.g., feels rested after 3 hours)
  3. More talkative than usual or pressure to keep talking
  4. Flight of ideas or subjective experience of racing thoughts
  5. Distractibility
  6. Increase in goal-directed activity or psychomotor agitation
  7. Excessive involvement in activities with high potential for painful consequences

The mnemonic DIG FAST (Distractibility, Indiscretion/Impulsivity, Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity increase, Sleep deficit, Talkativeness) helps recall mania criteria.

Bipolar II Disorder

Bipolar II disorder requires at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode. Bipolar II is not a "milder" form of Bipolar I -- the depressive episodes are often more chronic and debilitating. If a full manic episode ever occurs, the diagnosis changes to Bipolar I.

Clinical Pearl

Bipolar II is frequently misdiagnosed as MDD because patients typically present during depressive episodes and may not recognize hypomania as pathological. Screening for a history of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, and increased activity is essential. Antidepressant monotherapy in unrecognized Bipolar II can precipitate a manic switch.

Cyclothymic Disorder

Cyclothymic disorder involves chronic fluctuating mood disturbance with numerous periods of hypomanic symptoms and periods of depressive symptoms that do not meet full criteria for either a hypomanic or major depressive episode. Symptoms must be present for at least 2 years (1 year in children/adolescents), and the individual must not have been symptom-free for more than 2 months at a time. There is a 15-50% risk of eventually developing Bipolar I or II disorder.

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD) is classified as a depressive disorder in the DSM-5. It involves the presence of 5 or more symptoms in the final week before the onset of menses, with symptoms beginning to improve within a few days after the onset of menses and becoming minimal or absent in the week post-menses.

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

At least one of the following core affective symptoms must be present:

  1. Marked affective lability (e.g., mood swings, feeling suddenly sad or tearful, increased sensitivity to rejection)
  2. Marked irritability or anger or increased interpersonal conflicts
  3. Marked depressed mood, feelings of hopelessness, or self-deprecating thoughts
  4. Marked anxiety, tension, and/or feelings of being keyed up or on edge

Plus one or more of the following additional symptoms (to reach a total of at least 5):

  • Decreased interest in usual activities
  • Subjective difficulty in concentration
  • Lethargy, easy fatigability, or marked lack of energy
  • Marked change in appetite, overeating, or specific food cravings
  • Hypersomnia or insomnia
  • A sense of being overwhelmed or out of control
  • Physical symptoms such as breast tenderness or swelling, joint or muscle pain, a sensation of bloating, or weight gain

Clinical Pearl

PMDD diagnosis must be confirmed by prospective daily ratings for at least 2 consecutive symptomatic menstrual cycles. Retrospective self-report alone is insufficient because women often overestimate premenstrual symptom severity when recalling past cycles. This prospective confirmation requirement is unique among DSM-5 diagnoses.

Distinction from PMS

Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS) is a common, less severe condition that affects up to 75% of menstruating women. PMDD is distinct from PMS in that PMDD causes significant functional impairment in social, occupational, or interpersonal domains, whereas PMS involves milder symptoms that do not substantially interfere with daily functioning. PMDD prevalence is estimated at 1.8-5.8% of menstruating women.

Treatment

  • SSRIs are the first-line pharmacological treatment for PMDD. Uniquely, SSRIs can be used either continuously (daily throughout the cycle) or only during the luteal phase (the approximately 2 weeks between ovulation and menses onset), with both approaches demonstrating efficacy.
  • Hormonal treatments (oral contraceptives, GnRH agonists) may be used to suppress ovulation and reduce symptom cycling, particularly when SSRIs are insufficient.

EPPP Example

PMDD is a new addition to the depressive disorders chapter in DSM-5. The key features to remember are: (1) symptoms must occur in the final week before menses and remit after onset; (2) at least one of four core affective symptoms is required; (3) prospective daily ratings over 2 cycles are required for confirmation; (4) SSRIs are effective even when used only during the luteal phase.

Beck's Cognitive Triad and Cognitive Theory

Aaron Beck's (1967) cognitive theory of depression is one of the most influential models and is heavily tested on the EPPP. The theory proposes that depression results from negative cognitive patterns organized around three domains:

The Cognitive Triad

1. Negative view of the self: "I am worthless, inadequate, or defective."
2. Negative view of the world/experience: "The world is unfair, and nothing works out for me."
3. Negative view of the future: "Things will never get better; there is no hope."

These negative views are maintained by cognitive distortions (systematic errors in thinking) such as arbitrary inference, selective abstraction, overgeneralization, magnification/minimization, personalization, and all-or-nothing thinking. The distortions arise from underlying depressogenic schemas -- stable cognitive structures formed through early experience that become activated by stressful life events.

Seligman's (1975) learned helplessness model proposes that depression results from perceived inability to control outcomes. The reformulated model (Abramson et al., 1978) added attributional style: internal, stable, and global attributions for negative events are depressogenic.

Treatment of Mood Disorders

Pharmacotherapy for Depression

  • SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine, citalopram, escitalopram): First-line treatment; selectively block serotonin reuptake; generally well tolerated with side effects including sexual dysfunction, GI symptoms, and initial anxiety
  • SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine): Block both serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake; useful for comorbid pain conditions
  • TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine): Older antidepressants; effective but more side effects (anticholinergic effects, cardiac conduction delays); lethal in overdose
  • MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine): Require dietary restrictions (tyramine-free diet to avoid hypertensive crisis); generally reserved for treatment-resistant depression or atypical depression
  • Atypicals: Bupropion (norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor; no sexual side effects; lowers seizure threshold); mirtazapine (increases appetite, causes sedation)

Clinical Pearl

The black box warning on antidepressants warns of increased suicidal thinking and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (ages 18-24) during initial treatment. This does not mean antidepressants cause suicide -- the risk is that as energy and motivation return before mood improves, the individual may have increased capacity to act on suicidal thoughts. The net benefit of antidepressant treatment for depression far outweighs this risk.

Pharmacotherapy for Bipolar Disorder

  • Lithium: The gold standard mood stabilizer; effective for acute mania and maintenance; requires blood level monitoring (therapeutic range 0.6-1.2 mEq/L); risks include thyroid dysfunction, renal impairment, and teratogenicity (Ebstein's anomaly)
  • Anticonvulsants: Valproate (effective for acute mania and mixed states; teratogenic -- neural tube defects); carbamazepine; lamotrigine (particularly effective for bipolar depression prevention)
  • Atypical antipsychotics: Quetiapine, olanzapine, aripiprazole, lurasidone are used for acute mania and/or bipolar depression

Psychotherapy for Depression

Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) have the strongest evidence base for treating depression. CBT targets cognitive distortions and behavioral activation. IPT focuses on interpersonal disputes, role transitions, grief, and interpersonal deficits. Behavioral Activation (BA) -- increasing engagement in positively reinforcing activities -- has emerged as an effective standalone treatment, with efficacy comparable to full CBT in some studies (Dimidjian et al., 2006).

Suicide Risk Assessment

Suicide is a critical topic for the EPPP. Key risk and protective factors include:

Risk Factors

  • Previous suicide attempt -- the single strongest predictor of completed suicide
  • Psychiatric diagnosis -- over 90% of completers had a diagnosable disorder, with depression and substance use being the most common
  • Hopelessness -- a stronger predictor of suicidal intent than depression severity (Beck et al., 1985)
  • Demographic factors: Males complete suicide at approximately 4 times the rate of females; females attempt suicide approximately 3 times as often as males
  • Access to lethal means, family history of suicide, recent losses, social isolation
  • Age: Highest rates in adults 45-64 and those 85+

Protective Factors

  • Social connectedness and reasons for living
  • Effective clinical care for mental and substance use disorders
  • Restricted access to lethal means
  • Cultural and religious beliefs that discourage suicide

Clinical Pearl

The Joiner interpersonal theory of suicide (2005) identifies three factors necessary for a lethal suicide attempt: thwarted belongingness (feeling disconnected), perceived burdensomeness (feeling like a burden to others), and acquired capability (reduced fear of death through habituation to pain and self-harm).

EPPP Focus Points

  • MDD requires 5 of 9 symptoms for 2 weeks; must include depressed mood or anhedonia
  • DSM-5 removed the bereavement exclusion for MDD
  • PDD duration: 2 years in adults, 1 year in children/adolescents
  • Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode; depressive episodes are not required
  • Bipolar II requires at least one hypomanic + one major depressive episode; if mania ever occurs, diagnosis changes to Bipolar I
  • Mania lasts at least 7 days; hypomania at least 4 days
  • Beck's cognitive triad: negative views of self, world, and future
  • Hopelessness is a stronger predictor of suicide than depression severity
  • Previous suicide attempt is the strongest predictor of future completed suicide
  • Males complete suicide more often; females attempt more often
  • Atypical depression features: mood reactivity, hypersomnia, leaden paralysis, rejection sensitivity
  • PMDD requires 5+ symptoms in the final week before menses; must include at least one core affective symptom
  • PMDD requires prospective daily ratings for 2 consecutive cycles for confirmation
  • PMDD prevalence: 1.8-5.8% of menstruating women; distinct from PMS by severity and functional impairment
  • SSRIs for PMDD can be used continuously or during the luteal phase only
