ADHD, autism spectrum, learning disorders, tic disorders

Overview of Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Neurodevelopmental disorders are a group of conditions with onset in the developmental period, typically manifesting early in development and producing impairments in personal, social, academic, or occupational functioning. These disorders frequently co-occur with one another. The DSM-5 neurodevelopmental chapter includes intellectual disabilities, communication disorders, autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, specific learning disorder, and motor disorders (including tic disorders).

Key Concept: Dimensional Approach

DSM-5 moved toward a more dimensional approach for neurodevelopmental disorders, using severity levels and specifiers rather than rigid categorical subtypes. This is most evident in ASD (which consolidated previously separate diagnoses) and intellectual disability (which now emphasizes adaptive functioning over IQ scores for severity classification).

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

ADHD is characterized by a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. DSM-5 made several important changes to ADHD criteria compared to DSM-IV.

DSM-5 Criteria Changes

  • Age of onset changed from before age 7 to before age 12
  • For adults (age 17+), only 5 of 9 symptoms required (instead of 6)
  • ADHD can now be diagnosed comorbidly with ASD (previously excluded)
  • Examples of symptom manifestation in older adolescents and adults were added
  • Subtypes were relabeled as "presentations" to reflect their potential instability over time

PresentationRequirementKey Features
Predominantly Inattentive6+ inattention symptoms (5+ for age 17+)Difficulty sustaining attention, does not follow through, loses things, easily distracted, forgetful

Clinical Pearl

Symptoms must be present in two or more settings (home, school, work), must be present for at least 6 months, and must interfere with or reduce the quality of functioning. ADHD persists into adulthood in approximately 50-65% of cases, though hyperactive-impulsive symptoms tend to decline more than inattentive symptoms (Faraone et al., 2006). Executive function deficits (working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility) are central to Barkley's (1997) model of ADHD.

Etiology

  • Genetics: ADHD is highly heritable (approximately 74%). It is polygenic, with dopamine-related genes (DRD4, DAT1) implicated.
  • Neurobiology: Dysregulation of dopamine and norepinephrine in prefrontal-striatal circuits. Structural imaging shows reduced volume in prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia, and cerebellum.
  • Environmental risk factors: Prenatal tobacco and alcohol exposure, low birth weight, prematurity, and lead exposure.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Autism Spectrum Disorder represents one of the most significant diagnostic changes in DSM-5. The previous DSM-IV categories of autistic disorder, Asperger's disorder, childhood disintegrative disorder, and PDD-NOS were consolidated into the single diagnosis of ASD.

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

ASD requires deficits in both of the following domains:

Domain A: Social Communication and Social Interaction (all 3 required)

  1. Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity (e.g., abnormal social approach, failure of back-and-forth conversation, reduced sharing of interests/emotions)
  2. Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors (e.g., poor eye contact, abnormalities in body language, deficits in understanding/use of gestures)
  3. Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships (e.g., difficulty adjusting behavior to social contexts, difficulty sharing imaginative play, absence of interest in peers)

Domain B: Restricted, Repetitive Behaviors (at least 2 of 4 required)

  1. Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech (echolalia, idiosyncratic phrases)
  2. Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines, ritualized patterns
  3. Highly restricted, fixated interests that are abnormal in intensity or focus
  4. Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment

DSM-5 Changes from DSM-IV

DSM-IV used a triad (social interaction, communication, restricted behaviors). DSM-5 collapsed this into a dyad: (1) social communication/interaction and (2) restricted/repetitive behaviors. The sensory reactivity criterion (B4) is new. Language delay is no longer part of the ASD criteria but can be noted as a specifier. Importantly, DSM-5 requires specifying severity levels for each domain.

EPPP Example

Individuals previously diagnosed with Asperger's disorder under DSM-IV would now typically receive a diagnosis of ASD Level 1 ("requiring support") without accompanying intellectual or language impairment. The DSM-5 includes a note that individuals with well-established DSM-IV diagnoses should be given the ASD diagnosis, and those with marked deficits in social communication but not meeting RRB criteria may be evaluated for Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder, a new DSM-5 diagnosis.

Treatment of ADHD

Pharmacotherapy

Stimulant medications are the first-line pharmacological treatment for ADHD and are effective in approximately 70-80% of cases:

  • Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta): Blocks dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake
  • Amphetamine salts (Adderall, Vyvanse): Block reuptake and increase release of dopamine and norepinephrine

Non-stimulant options include atomoxetine (Strattera, a norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor), guanfacine and clonidine (alpha-2 agonists), and bupropion (off-label). The MTA Study (Multimodal Treatment Study of ADHD, 1999) found that medication management was superior to behavioral treatment alone for core ADHD symptoms, while combined treatment was superior for functional outcomes and comorbid conditions.

Behavioral and Psychosocial Interventions

  • Behavioral parent training: Teaching parents contingency management strategies (positive reinforcement, token economies, time-out)
  • Classroom-based behavioral interventions: Daily report cards, token systems, classroom accommodations (preferential seating, breaks, extended time)
  • Organizational skills training: Particularly important for the predominantly inattentive presentation
  • Social skills training: Effectiveness is debated; best when integrated into naturalistic settings

Clinical Pearl

Common misconceptions about ADHD stimulant medications: (1) Stimulants do not cause a "paradoxical" calming effect unique to ADHD -- they enhance focus in most individuals; the difference is that individuals with ADHD show greater behavioral improvement. (2) The "medication holiday" debate: some families choose to stop medication on weekends/summers, but this may affect social functioning and self-esteem. (3) Long-term stimulant use may temporarily slow growth velocity by approximately 1-3 cm, but adult height appears largely unaffected.

Specific Learning Disorder

Specific Learning Disorder (SLD) is a unified diagnosis in DSM-5 that replaced the separate DSM-IV categories of reading disorder, mathematics disorder, and disorder of written expression. It requires persistent difficulties learning keystone academic skills, with onset during school-age years.

Specifiers

  • With impairment in reading (encompasses what was formerly called dyslexia): word reading accuracy, reading rate/fluency, reading comprehension
  • With impairment in written expression (encompasses dysgraphia): spelling accuracy, grammar/punctuation, clarity/organization
  • With impairment in mathematics (encompasses dyscalculia): number sense, math facts, calculation, math reasoning

SLD affects approximately 5-15% of school-age children. It requires academic skills substantially below age expectations, confirmed by both clinical assessment and standardized testing. The DSM-5 does not require an IQ-achievement discrepancy, moving away from the discrepancy model toward a response to intervention (RTI) approach.

Intellectual Disability (Intellectual Developmental Disorder)

Intellectual disability involves deficits in intellectual functions (reasoning, problem-solving, abstract thinking, academic learning) and adaptive functioning (conceptual, social, and practical domains) with onset during the developmental period.

Clinical Pearl

A major DSM-5 change: severity levels (mild, moderate, severe, profound) are now determined by adaptive functioning rather than IQ score alone. While IQ testing is still used (approximately 2 standard deviations below the mean, or roughly IQ 65-75), the DSM-5 emphasizes that it is the level of adaptive support needed that determines severity. IQ scores are less valid at lower ranges and can be influenced by the Flynn effect and other measurement issues.

Tic Disorders and Tourette's Disorder

Tic disorders involve sudden, rapid, recurrent, nonrhythmic motor movements or vocalizations. Tics can be simple (eye blinking, throat clearing) or complex (facial gestures, echolalia, coprolalia).

DSM-5 Tic Disorder Hierarchy

  • Tourette's disorder: Both multiple motor and one or more vocal tics present at some time (not necessarily concurrently). Onset before age 18. Duration more than 1 year. Tics wax and wane.
  • Persistent (chronic) motor or vocal tic disorder: Single or multiple motor OR vocal tics (not both). Onset before 18. Duration more than 1 year.
  • Provisional tic disorder: Single or multiple motor and/or vocal tics. Duration less than 1 year since first onset. Onset before 18.

EPPP Example

Coprolalia (involuntary utterance of obscene words) is often associated with Tourette's in popular culture, but it actually occurs in only about 10-15% of individuals with Tourette's disorder. The most common initial tics are facial (eye blinking, grimacing). Tourette's is highly comorbid with ADHD (approximately 50%) and OCD (approximately 30%). The CSTC circuit implicated in OCD is also involved in tic disorders.

EPPP Focus Points

  • ADHD age of onset changed to before age 12 in DSM-5 (from age 7 in DSM-IV)
  • ADHD now uses "presentations" instead of "subtypes"; can be comorbid with ASD
  • ASD consolidated autistic disorder, Asperger's, CDD, and PDD-NOS into one diagnosis
  • ASD uses a dyad (social communication + RRBs) instead of DSM-IV's triad
  • ASD severity: Level 1 (requiring support), Level 2 (substantial support), Level 3 (very substantial support)
  • Former Asperger's diagnosis maps to ASD Level 1 without intellectual/language impairment
  • SLD is a unified diagnosis with specifiers for reading, written expression, and mathematics
  • Intellectual disability severity now based on adaptive functioning, not IQ alone
  • Tourette's requires both motor AND vocal tics for more than 1 year, onset before age 18
  • Coprolalia occurs in only 10-15% of Tourette's cases
  • ADHD heritability is approximately 74%; dopamine and norepinephrine are the key neurotransmitters
