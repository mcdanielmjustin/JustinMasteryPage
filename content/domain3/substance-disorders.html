Substance Use Disorders

Alcohol, opioids, cannabis, withdrawal syndromes
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders

The DSM-5 made significant changes to the diagnosis of substance-related disorders. The most notable change was the elimination of the abuse/dependence distinction from DSM-IV, replacing it with a single substance use disorder (SUD) diagnosis on a continuum of severity. The DSM-5 also added gambling disorder as the first non-substance behavioral addiction and removed the legal problems criterion while adding craving.

DSM-5 Substance Use Disorder Criteria

A problematic pattern of substance use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress, manifested by at least 2 of 11 criteria within a 12-month period. Severity: Mild (2-3 criteria), Moderate (4-5 criteria), Severe (6+ criteria). The 11 criteria cluster into four groups: impaired control, social impairment, risky use, and pharmacological criteria.

The 11 SUD Diagnostic Criteria

Impaired Control (Criteria 1-4)

  1. Taking the substance in larger amounts or over a longer period than intended
  2. Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down or control use
  3. Great deal of time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from the substance
  4. Craving or a strong desire to use the substance (new in DSM-5)

Social Impairment (Criteria 5-7)

  1. Recurrent use resulting in failure to fulfill major role obligations
  2. Continued use despite persistent social or interpersonal problems
  3. Important activities given up or reduced because of use

Risky Use (Criteria 8-9)

  1. Recurrent use in situations in which it is physically hazardous
  2. Continued use despite knowledge of having a physical or psychological problem caused or exacerbated by the substance

Pharmacological Criteria (Criteria 10-11)

  1. Tolerance: Need for markedly increased amounts to achieve desired effect, or markedly diminished effect with continued use of the same amount
  2. Withdrawal: The characteristic withdrawal syndrome for the substance, or the substance is taken to relieve or avoid withdrawal symptoms

Clinical Pearl

Tolerance and withdrawal are neither necessary nor sufficient for an SUD diagnosis. A person taking prescribed opioids after surgery may develop tolerance and withdrawal but would not necessarily have an SUD. Conversely, a person can meet criteria for SUD (e.g., 2-3 social/behavioral criteria) without exhibiting tolerance or withdrawal. The DSM-5 notes that tolerance and withdrawal criteria should not be counted when substances are taken solely under appropriate medical supervision.

Alcohol Use Disorder

Alcohol use disorder (AUD) is the most commonly diagnosed SUD. Lifetime prevalence in the United States is approximately 29% (Grant et al., 2015). Genetic factors account for approximately 50-60% of the variance in risk for AUD.

Alcohol Withdrawal

Alcohol withdrawal occurs after cessation or reduction of heavy, prolonged use. Symptoms begin within 6-24 hours after the last drink and can include:

  • Autonomic hyperactivity: sweating, tachycardia, elevated blood pressure
  • Hand tremor
  • Insomnia, nausea/vomiting
  • Psychomotor agitation, anxiety
  • Transient visual, tactile, or auditory hallucinations or illusions
  • Generalized tonic-clonic seizures (typically 12-48 hours post-cessation)

Delirium Tremens (DTs)

Delirium tremens is the most severe form of alcohol withdrawal, occurring in approximately 5% of individuals undergoing withdrawal, typically 48-72 hours after the last drink. Features include profound confusion, vivid hallucinations (often visual -- classically insects or small animals), autonomic instability, agitation, and fever. DTs carry a mortality rate of up to 5% without treatment. Treatment involves benzodiazepines (especially long-acting agents like chlordiazepoxide or diazepam).

Alcohol-Related Medical Conditions

  • Wernicke's encephalopathy: Acute condition caused by thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency. Classic triad: confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia (eye movement abnormalities). A medical emergency requiring IV thiamine.
  • Korsakoff's syndrome: Chronic condition following untreated Wernicke's. Characterized by severe anterograde amnesia (inability to form new memories) and confabulation. Often irreversible.
  • Fetal alcohol spectrum disorders: Leading preventable cause of intellectual disability. Features include facial dysmorphology, growth deficiency, and CNS dysfunction.

Opioid Use Disorder

Opioid use disorder involves problematic use of opioids (heroin, prescription painkillers such as oxycodone, hydrocodone, fentanyl, or morphine). Opioids act on mu, delta, and kappa receptors, with mu receptor activation producing analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, and physical dependence.

Opioid Withdrawal

Opioid withdrawal is intensely uncomfortable but generally not life-threatening (unlike alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal). Symptoms emerge within hours of last use and include:

  • Dysphoric mood, nausea/vomiting, muscle aches
  • Lacrimation (tearing), rhinorrhea (runny nose)
  • Pupillary dilation, piloerection ("goosebumps"), sweating
  • Diarrhea, yawning, fever, insomnia

EPPP Example

Pharmacological treatments for opioid use disorder: Methadone (full mu-opioid agonist; reduces cravings and withdrawal; dispensed through certified clinics). Buprenorphine (partial mu-opioid agonist; lower abuse potential than methadone; can be prescribed in office settings; often combined with naloxone as Suboxone). Naltrexone (opioid antagonist; blocks euphoric effects; available as daily oral or monthly injectable [Vivitrol]; requires full detoxification before initiation to avoid precipitated withdrawal).

Opioid Overdose Management

Opioid overdose is a life-threatening medical emergency. Recognition and rapid intervention are critical topics for clinical practice and the EPPP.

The Opioid Overdose Triad

The classic presentation of opioid overdose involves a characteristic triad of three signs:

  1. Respiratory depression (slow, shallow, or absent breathing) -- the primary cause of death in opioid overdose
  2. Miosis (pinpoint pupils) -- highly characteristic of opioid toxicity
  3. Decreased consciousness (ranging from sedation to coma)

Naloxone (Narcan)

Naloxone (brand name Narcan) is a pure opioid antagonist that rapidly reverses the effects of opioid overdose by competitively binding to mu-opioid receptors, displacing the opioid. Key clinical features include:

  • Onset of action within 2-5 minutes when administered intravenously (slightly longer intranasally or intramuscularly)
  • Duration of action is 30-90 minutes, which is often shorter than the duration of action of the opioid itself -- therefore, repeated doses may be necessary to prevent recurrence of respiratory depression
  • Administration can precipitate acute opioid withdrawal in dependent individuals, but this is not life-threatening
  • Available in intranasal, intramuscular, intravenous, and subcutaneous formulations; widely available without a prescription in many states

Clinical Pearl

Good Samaritan laws have been enacted in most U.S. states to encourage bystanders to call emergency services and administer naloxone during an overdose without fear of legal prosecution for drug-related offenses. These laws are an important public health tool in the opioid epidemic. Psychologists should be familiar with naloxone access and Good Samaritan protections in their jurisdiction.

Cannabis, Stimulants, and Other Substances

Cannabis Use Disorder

Cannabis use disorder is recognized in DSM-5 with the same 11-criterion framework. Cannabis withdrawal is now an official diagnosis in DSM-5, occurring within approximately 1 week of cessation of heavy use. Symptoms include irritability/anger, nervousness/anxiety, sleep difficulty, decreased appetite/weight loss, restlessness, depressed mood, and at least one physical symptom (abdominal pain, shakiness, sweating, fever, chills, headache).

Stimulant Use Disorder

Includes cocaine, amphetamines, and other stimulant-type substances. Stimulants increase dopamine in the mesolimbic reward pathway. Stimulant withdrawal ("crash") is characterized by dysphoric mood, fatigue, vivid unpleasant dreams, insomnia or hypersomnia, increased appetite, and psychomotor retardation or agitation.

Tolerance, Dependence, and Key Concepts

Tolerance vs. Dependence vs. Addiction

Tolerance: Needing more of a substance to achieve the same effect, or diminished effect with the same amount. Physical dependence: A physiological state in which withdrawal symptoms occur upon cessation; can occur with appropriate medical use and does not necessarily indicate a SUD. Addiction (SUD): A behavioral pattern of compulsive substance use despite adverse consequences, involving impaired control, social impairment, and risky use. The DSM-5 avoids the term "addiction" in diagnostic labels to reduce stigma.

Cross-Tolerance and Cross-Dependence

Cross-tolerance occurs when tolerance to one substance extends to another substance within the same pharmacological class (e.g., alcohol and benzodiazepines, both of which act on GABA-A receptors). Cross-dependence is when one substance can suppress withdrawal symptoms from another substance in the same class, which is the basis for using benzodiazepines to manage alcohol withdrawal.

Neurobiology of Addiction

Understanding the neurobiological basis of addiction is essential for the EPPP. The brain's reward system is central to addiction:

The Mesolimbic Dopamine Pathway

The mesolimbic dopamine pathway (ventral tegmental area to nucleus accumbens) is the primary neural circuit involved in reward and reinforcement. All substances of abuse, despite their different mechanisms of action, ultimately increase dopamine transmission in the nucleus accumbens. This "final common pathway" explains why diverse substances can all produce addiction (Koob & Volkow, 2016).

  • Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines): Directly increase dopamine by blocking reuptake or increasing release
  • Opioids: Disinhibit dopamine neurons by inhibiting GABAergic interneurons in the VTA
  • Alcohol: Enhances GABA and inhibits glutamate; also indirectly increases dopamine release
  • Nicotine: Stimulates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors on dopamine neurons
  • Cannabis: Activates CB1 receptors, which indirectly modulate dopamine release

Neuroadaptation and Allostasis

With chronic substance use, the brain undergoes neuroadaptation: the reward system becomes downregulated (fewer dopamine receptors, reduced dopamine release), leading to tolerance and a new hedonic set point. Koob's allostatic model proposes that addiction involves a shift from positive reinforcement (seeking pleasure) to negative reinforcement (avoiding withdrawal and dysphoria). The extended amygdala and stress systems (CRF, norepinephrine) become hyperactivated, driving the compulsive drug-seeking characteristic of addiction.

EPPP Example

The opponent-process theory (Solomon & Corbit, 1974) explains drug tolerance and withdrawal through opposing emotional processes. The initial positive effect (a-process) triggers a compensatory negative effect (b-process). With repeated use, the b-process strengthens, requiring more substance to achieve the same positive effect (tolerance) and producing a powerful negative state when the substance is absent (withdrawal). This model applies beyond substance use to understanding any habituation to emotional stimuli.

Stages of Change Model

Prochaska and DiClemente's (1983) Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change) is widely used in substance use treatment and is a commonly tested EPPP topic.

Clinical Pearl

Motivational interviewing (MI) (Miller & Rollnick, 2013) is the therapeutic approach most closely aligned with the stages of change model. MI uses a collaborative, empathic, non-confrontational approach to strengthen a person's own motivation for change. Core principles include expressing empathy, developing discrepancy, rolling with resistance, and supporting self-efficacy. The OARS technique (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries) is fundamental to MI practice.

Gambling Disorder

Gambling disorder is the first and only non-substance behavioral addiction included in the DSM-5, placed in the Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders chapter. This placement reflects growing evidence that gambling disorder shares significant neurobiological, genetic, and clinical features with substance use disorders.

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

Persistent and recurrent problematic gambling behavior leading to clinically significant impairment or distress, as indicated by 4 or more of 9 criteria within a 12-month period:

  1. Needs to gamble with increasing amounts of money to achieve the desired excitement (tolerance)
  2. Is restless or irritable when attempting to cut down or stop gambling (withdrawal)
  3. Has made repeated unsuccessful efforts to control, cut back, or stop gambling (loss of control)
  4. Is often preoccupied with gambling (e.g., reliving past gambling experiences, planning the next venture, thinking of ways to obtain money)
  5. Often gambles when feeling distressed (e.g., helpless, guilty, anxious, depressed)
  6. After losing money gambling, often returns another day to get even ("chasing" losses)
  7. Lies to conceal the extent of involvement with gambling
  8. Has jeopardized or lost a significant relationship, job, or educational/career opportunity because of gambling
  9. Relies on others to provide money to relieve desperate financial situations caused by gambling

Parallels with Substance Use Disorders

The diagnostic criteria for gambling disorder closely parallel the 11 SUD criteria, including tolerance (needing more to achieve excitement), withdrawal (restlessness when stopping), loss of control, and continued engagement despite negative consequences. This parallel is deliberate and reflects the shared underlying pathology.

Neurobiology

Neuroimaging and neuropharmacological studies have demonstrated that gambling disorder shares key neurobiological features with substance addiction. The dopamine reward system -- particularly the mesolimbic pathway from the ventral tegmental area to the nucleus accumbens -- is activated during gambling in ways that closely parallel activation seen with substance use. Individuals with gambling disorder show reduced striatal dopamine receptor availability, impaired prefrontal cortex function (contributing to poor decision-making), and heightened responsivity to gambling-related cues.

Prevalence and Clinical Features

Prevalence of gambling disorder is estimated at 0.4-1% of adults. Males are affected more frequently than females, though the gender gap is narrowing. Gambling disorder has high comorbidity with substance use disorders, mood disorders, and personality disorders (particularly antisocial personality disorder). Severity specifiers follow the SUD model: mild (4-5 criteria), moderate (6-7 criteria), severe (8-9 criteria).

EPPP Example

Internet gaming disorder was not included as an official DSM-5 diagnosis but was placed in Section III ("Conditions for Further Study"). Gambling disorder remains the only recognized behavioral addiction in the DSM-5. The decision to include gambling disorder was based on decades of evidence showing that it parallels substance addiction in clinical presentation, comorbidity, neurobiology, physiology, and treatment response.
