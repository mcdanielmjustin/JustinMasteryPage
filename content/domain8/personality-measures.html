Overview of Personality Measures

This chapter covers personality assessment instruments beyond the MMPI family. These include objective (self-report) personality inventories and projective (performance-based) techniques. For the EPPP, you must know the theoretical basis, structure, scoring, and appropriate uses of each major instrument, as well as the ongoing debate about the reliability and validity of projective methods.

Objective vs. Projective: Objective personality tests use structured, standardized items (typically true/false or Likert-scale) with empirically derived scoring. Projective tests present ambiguous stimuli and assume that responses reveal underlying personality dynamics, unconscious conflicts, and thought organization. Modern terminology increasingly refers to projective tests as "performance-based" measures to reduce theoretical assumptions embedded in the name.

NEO Personality Inventory-Revised (NEO-PI-R) and NEO-FFI-3

The NEO-PI-R (Costa & McCrae, 1992) is the standard measure of the Five-Factor Model (FFM) of personality, also known as the "Big Five." The NEO-FFI-3 is the abbreviated 60-item version.

The Five Factors (OCEAN/CANOE)

FactorDescriptionFacets (6 per domain)
Neuroticism (N)Emotional instability, proneness to negative affect, vulnerability to stressAnxiety, Angry Hostility, Depression, Self-Consciousness, Impulsiveness, Vulnerability
Extraversion (E)Sociability, assertiveness, positive emotionality, energyWarmth, Gregariousness, Assertiveness, Activity, Excitement-Seeking, Positive Emotions
Openness (O)Intellectual curiosity, imagination, aesthetic sensitivity, openness to new experiencesFantasy, Aesthetics, Feelings, Actions, Ideas, Values
Agreeableness (A)Cooperative, trusting, altruistic, compliant, tender-mindedTrust, Straightforwardness, Altruism, Compliance, Modesty, Tender-Mindedness
Conscientiousness (C)Organized, dependable, self-disciplined, achievement-striving, deliberateCompetence, Order, Dutifulness, Achievement Striving, Self-Discipline, Deliberation

Test Characteristics

  • NEO-PI-R: 240 items on a 5-point Likert scale; 5 domain scores and 30 facet scores
  • NEO-FFI-3: 60 items (12 per domain); domain scores only, no facets
  • Both self-report (Form S) and observer-report (Form R) versions available
  • Scores reported as T-scores (M = 50, SD = 10)
  • Includes three validity indices: Acquiescence, Nay-Saying, and Random Responding
EPPP Note: The NEO-PI-R is used for normal-range personality assessment, not psychopathology. It measures personality traits on continua, not diagnostic categories. Very high Neuroticism and very low Agreeableness or Conscientiousness may correlate with personality pathology, but the NEO was not designed as a clinical diagnostic tool.

Sixteen Personality Factor Questionnaire (16PF)

The 16PF (Cattell, Cattell, & Cattell, 1993, 5th ed.) was developed by Raymond Cattell using factor analysis to identify the fundamental dimensions (source traits) of personality.

Structure

  • 185 items measuring 16 primary factor scales and 5 global (second-order) factors
  • The 16 primary factors include: Warmth, Reasoning, Emotional Stability, Dominance, Liveliness, Rule-Consciousness, Social Boldness, Sensitivity, Vigilance, Abstractedness, Privateness, Apprehension, Openness to Change, Self-Reliance, Perfectionism, Tension
  • The 5 global factors approximate the Big Five: Extraversion, Anxiety, Tough-Mindedness, Independence, Self-Control
  • Scores are reported as sten scores (standard ten scores, M = 5.5, SD = 2, range 1-10)

Uses

  • Personnel selection and industrial-organizational psychology
  • Clinical assessment of personality style (not primarily a clinical/pathology instrument)
  • Career counseling and vocational guidance
  • Research on personality structure
EPPP Tip: The 16PF uses sten scores (1-10), not T-scores. Scores of 1-3 are low, 4-7 are average, and 8-10 are high. Cattell used factor analysis to derive the scales, making the 16PF a prime example of the factor-analytic approach to test construction.

Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventory-IV (MCMI-IV)

The MCMI-IV (Millon, Grossman, & Millon, 2015) is a self-report inventory specifically designed to assess personality disorders and clinical syndromes. It is directly tied to Theodore Millon's theory of personality and to the DSM diagnostic system.

Structure

  • 195 true/false items
  • Three types of scales:
    • Clinical Personality Pattern scales (15): Correspond to DSM personality disorders (e.g., Schizoid, Avoidant, Dependent, Histrionic, Narcissistic, Antisocial, Compulsive, Negativistic, Masochistic, Sadistic, Schizotypal, Borderline, Paranoid)
    • Clinical Syndrome scales (7): Generalized Anxiety, Somatic Symptom, Bipolar Spectrum, Persistent Depression, Alcohol Use, Drug Use, Post-Traumatic Stress
    • Severe Clinical Syndrome scales (3): Schizophrenic Spectrum, Major Depression, Delusional
  • Validity indices: Invalidity, Inconsistency, Disclosure, Desirability, Debasement

Scoring

  • Uses Base Rate (BR) scores rather than T-scores
  • BR scores are anchored to the prevalence (base rate) of each disorder in clinical populations
  • BR score of 75 = presence of the trait/syndrome; BR score of 85 = prominence (clinically significant)
  • This approach accounts for the fact that different disorders have different base rates in clinical settings
BR Scores vs. T-Scores: T-scores assume a normal distribution and compare an individual to a normative mean. BR scores are calibrated to the actual prevalence of disorders in the target population. Because personality disorders vary in prevalence, using BR scores helps avoid overdiagnosis of rare disorders and underdiagnosis of common ones.

Important Limitations

  • Normed only on clinical (psychiatric) populations -- should not be used with non-clinical samples
  • Tied specifically to Millon's theory; may not map perfectly onto all DSM conceptualizations
  • Scales overlap substantially (items shared across scales), which inflates inter-scale correlations
EPPP Commonly Tested: The MCMI is designed for use with clinical populations only. Using it with non-clinical populations (e.g., personnel selection) is inappropriate and may lead to over-pathologizing. Also remember: BR scores, not T-scores; cutoffs of 75 (present) and 85 (prominent).

Rorschach Inkblot Method

The Rorschach (Rorschach, 1921) consists of 10 symmetrical inkblots (5 achromatic, 2 red-and-black, 3 multicolored) presented one at a time. The examinee describes what they see in each blot. Two major scoring systems are used today.

Exner's Comprehensive System (CS)

The Comprehensive System (Exner, 2003) standardized Rorschach administration, scoring, and interpretation by integrating elements from earlier systems (Beck, Klopfer, Hertz, Piotrowski, Rapaport).

  • Administration: Two phases -- Response Phase (free association to each card) and Inquiry Phase (examiner clarifies what and where the examinee saw each percept)
  • Scoring categories: Location (W = whole, D = common detail, Dd = unusual detail), Determinants (form, color, shading, movement), Content (human, animal, anatomy, etc.), Popular responses, Organizational Activity (Z-scores)
  • Structural Summary: A quantitative compilation of all coded scores, including ratios, percentages, and derived indices

Key CS Indices

  • Erlebnistypus (EB): Ratio of human movement (M) to weighted color responses (WSumC) -- reflects whether the individual is introverted (M > WSumC), extraverted (WSumC > M), or ambitent
  • Lambda (L): Ratio of pure form responses to all other responses -- high Lambda suggests avoidant, simplistic approach to complexity
  • X-% and X+%: Measures of perceptual accuracy (reality testing). X+% = conventional form quality; X-% = distorted form quality. Elevated X-% suggests impaired reality testing.
  • SCZI / PTI (Perceptual-Thinking Index): Identifies thought disorder and perceptual distortion associated with psychotic processes
  • DEPI (Depression Index): Identifies features associated with affective disturbance
  • S-CON (Suicide Constellation): Risk index for suicidal behavior (one of the best-validated indices)

Rorschach Performance Assessment System (R-PAS)

The R-PAS (Meyer, Viglione, Mihura, Erard, & Erdberg, 2011) was developed to address psychometric criticisms of the CS. Key improvements include:

  • Standardized response process: Prompts examinees to give 2-3 responses per card (reduces variability in number of responses)
  • International normative reference data (more diverse than CS norms)
  • Evidence-based variable selection (retained variables with the strongest empirical support; dropped poorly validated ones)
  • Standard score profiles (similar to MMPI profiles) for easier interpretation
  • Updated scoring guidelines and reduced complexity
EPPP Note: The Rorschach has been highly controversial. Meta-analyses (Mihura et al., 2013) found that some Rorschach variables have validity comparable to MMPI scales (e.g., Thought Disorder Index, M- for disordered thinking) while others lack sufficient support. The R-PAS specifically retained only empirically supported variables.

Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)

The TAT (Murray, 1943) consists of 31 cards (20 administered per session) depicting ambiguous interpersonal scenes. The examinee tells a story about each card, including what is happening, what led up to it, what the characters are thinking and feeling, and what the outcome will be.

Theoretical Basis

The TAT is based on Murray's need-press theory. Responses are interpreted in terms of:

  • Needs: Internal drives and motives of the protagonist (who is assumed to represent the examinee)
  • Press: Environmental forces acting on the protagonist (reflecting the examinee's perceived environment)
  • Thema: The interaction between needs and press -- the story's central conflict and resolution

Scoring and Interpretation

  • The TAT lacks a universally accepted scoring system, which is a major psychometric limitation
  • Notable systems include: Murray's need-press analysis, Bellak's system, McClelland's need for achievement (nAch) scoring, Westen's SCORS (Social Cognition and Object Relations Scale)
  • SCORS (Westen): The best-validated TAT scoring system; assesses complexity of representations of people, affective quality of relationships, emotional investment in relationships, and understanding of social causality
EPPP Key Point: The TAT is primarily used to assess interpersonal themes, motivations, and object relations rather than for psychodiagnosis. McClelland's nAch scoring system is the most empirically supported specific scoring method. Westen's SCORS has the best support for clinical use.

Sentence Completion Tests

Sentence completion tests present the examinee with sentence stems (e.g., "I wish...", "My mother...", "I feel guilty about...") to be completed. They are considered semi-projective because the stimuli provide more structure than inkblots or pictures.

Major Instruments

  • Rotter Incomplete Sentences Blank (RISB): 40 stems scored on a 7-point scale from positive adjustment (0-1) to maladjustment (4-6). Yields a total adjustment score. Has adequate psychometric support.
  • Washington University Sentence Completion Test (WUSCT): Developed by Loevinger to measure ego development. Responses are scored for developmental stage (Impulsive, Self-Protective, Conformist, Conscientious, Autonomous, Integrated).

Projective Drawings

Projective drawing techniques ask the examinee to draw specific subjects. Interpretation is based on features of the drawing (size, placement, detail, pressure, omissions). These techniques are among the most controversial in clinical assessment.

Draw-A-Person (DAP)

  • Developed by Karen Machover (1949) based on the assumption that drawings reflect self-concept and body image
  • Features such as size, detail, placement, and erasures are interpreted qualitatively
  • The DAP:SPED (Draw-A-Person: Screening Procedure for Emotional Disturbance) is a standardized scoring system with better psychometric properties than clinical interpretation

House-Tree-Person (HTP)

  • Developed by John Buck (1948)
  • The examinee draws a house, a tree, and a person; each drawing is then discussed in a post-drawing inquiry
  • The house is said to represent family/home life, the tree represents growth and development, and the person represents self-concept
  • Interpretation is primarily qualitative and lacks standardized scoring

Kinetic Family Drawing (KFD)

  • Developed by Burns and Kaufman (1972)
  • The examinee draws their family doing something together
  • Used primarily with children to assess family dynamics, roles, and relationships
EPPP Critical Point: Projective drawings have generally poor reliability and validity for personality or diagnostic assessment. However, they remain widely used in clinical practice, particularly with children. On the EPPP, you should recognize that drawing techniques have limited empirical support and are best used as clinical tools to generate hypotheses rather than as diagnostic instruments.

Commonly Tested EPPP Summary

NEO-PI-R: Big Five (OCEAN): Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness. 240 items, 30 facets. Normal-range personality, not pathology.
16PF: Cattell's factor-analytic test. 16 primary factors, 5 global factors. Uses sten scores (1-10). Primarily used in I/O and career settings.
MCMI-IV: Clinical populations only. Uses Base Rate scores (75 = present, 85 = prominent). Personality disorder + clinical syndrome scales. Tied to Millon's theory.
Rorschach: 10 inkblots. CS (Exner) and R-PAS scoring systems. Key variables: EB, Lambda, X-%, S-CON. R-PAS uses standardized response process and evidence-based variable selection.
TAT: Murray's need-press theory. 20 ambiguous scenes. No standard scoring system. McClelland's nAch and Westen's SCORS are best validated.
Projective drawings (DAP, HTP): Poor reliability and validity. Hypothesis-generating only. Should not be used for diagnosis in isolation.
