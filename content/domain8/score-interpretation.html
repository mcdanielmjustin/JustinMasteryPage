Overview of Score Interpretation

Understanding how to convert, compare, and interpret test scores is fundamental to psychological assessment. This chapter covers the major types of derived scores used in psychological testing, the normal distribution that underlies them, and key statistical concepts that affect score interpretation. For the EPPP, you must be able to convert between score types, understand confidence intervals, and apply concepts like regression to the mean and the reliable change index.

Raw Score vs. Derived Score: A raw score is the initial, untransformed score on a test (e.g., number correct). A derived score is obtained by transforming the raw score using a normative reference group, making it interpretable by comparison to a defined population. Almost all clinically meaningful interpretation uses derived scores.

The Normal Distribution

Most psychological test scores are assumed to follow a normal (Gaussian) distribution, which is symmetrical and bell-shaped. Understanding the normal curve is essential because nearly all derived scores are referenced to it.

Key Properties

  • The mean, median, and mode are equal and located at the center
  • The distribution is symmetrical around the mean
  • Approximately 68% of scores fall within +/- 1 standard deviation (SD) of the mean
  • Approximately 95% fall within +/- 2 SDs
  • Approximately 99.7% fall within +/- 3 SDs
  • The tails extend infinitely in both directions but approach (never reach) zero

Z-Scores

The z-score is the most fundamental derived score. It expresses how many standard deviations a raw score falls above or below the mean of its distribution.

Formula: z = (X - M) / SD, where X = individual's raw score, M = mean of the distribution, and SD = standard deviation. A z-score of 0 = the mean. A z-score of +1.0 = 1 SD above the mean. A z-score of -2.0 = 2 SDs below the mean.

Properties of Z-Scores

  • Mean = 0, SD = 1
  • Can be positive (above the mean) or negative (below the mean)
  • Unitless -- allows comparison across different tests and metrics
  • The z-score distribution has the same shape as the original raw score distribution (it is a linear transformation that does not normalize the distribution)
Example: An examinee scores 130 on a test with M = 100 and SD = 15. The z-score = (130 - 100) / 15 = +2.0. This means the score is 2 SDs above the mean, corresponding to approximately the 98th percentile.

T-Scores

A T-score is a standardized score with M = 50 and SD = 10. T-scores eliminate the negative numbers and decimals of z-scores, making them easier to communicate.

Formula: T = 10(z) + 50, or equivalently, T = 10[(X - M) / SD] + 50. A T-score of 50 = the mean. A T-score of 60 = 1 SD above the mean. A T-score of 30 = 2 SDs below the mean.

Common Uses

  • MMPI-2, MMPI-2-RF, MMPI-3: All scales reported as T-scores (clinical significance at T >= 65)
  • NEO-PI-R: Domain and facet scores reported as T-scores
  • Behavior Rating Inventory of Executive Function (BRIEF): T-scores with clinical cutoff at 65
  • Many behavioral checklists and symptom measures use T-scores

Linear vs. Normalized T-Scores

  • Linear T-scores: Direct transformation of raw scores (preserves original distribution shape). Used on MMPI-2 Scales 5 and 0.
  • Normalized T-scores: Raw scores are first transformed to percentile ranks, then converted to T-scores based on the normal distribution. This forces the distribution into a normal shape.
  • Uniform T-scores: Used on most MMPI-2 clinical scales. A method developed by Tellegen that ensures the percentile value of a given T-score is the same across all scales, even though the raw score distributions differ.

Standard Scores (IQ-type Scores)

Standard scores with M = 100 and SD = 15 are used by the Wechsler scales, Stanford-Binet 5, and most other intelligence and cognitive ability tests for composite/index scores.

Percentile Ranks

A percentile rank indicates the percentage of scores in the normative distribution that fall at or below a given score. It is one of the most intuitive and commonly communicated score types.

Key Properties

  • Range: 1 to 99 (the 0th and 100th percentiles are theoretical limits)
  • The 50th percentile = the median = the mean (in a normal distribution)
  • Percentile ranks are ordinal, not interval -- equal differences in percentiles do NOT represent equal differences in ability
  • Percentile ranks are compressed near the mean (where most scores cluster) and stretched at the extremes
Important Caution: The difference between the 50th and 60th percentiles in raw score points is much smaller than the difference between the 90th and 99th percentiles. This is because percentile ranks are not on an equal-interval scale. A child moving from the 50th to 55th percentile may have gained 2 raw score points, while a child moving from the 95th to 99th percentile may have gained 10 raw score points. Never average percentile ranks.

Normal Curve Equivalents (NCEs)

Normal Curve Equivalents are standard scores with M = 50 and SD = 21.06. They were developed for use in federally funded educational programs (e.g., Title I evaluations) to allow averaging and statistical comparison of scores across different tests.

  • Range: 1 to 99
  • NCEs of 1, 50, and 99 correspond to percentile ranks of 1, 50, and 99
  • Unlike percentile ranks, NCEs are on an equal-interval scale and can be meaningfully averaged
  • NCEs are rarely used in clinical practice but may appear on the EPPP

Standard Error of Measurement (SEM)

The Standard Error of Measurement (SEM) quantifies the amount of error in an individual's observed test score. It reflects the fact that no test is perfectly reliable -- an individual's observed score may differ from their "true score" due to measurement error.

Formula: SEM = SD * sqrt(1 - rxx), where SD = standard deviation of the test scores and rxx = reliability coefficient of the test. Higher reliability yields a smaller SEM (less measurement error). A perfectly reliable test (r = 1.0) would have SEM = 0.

SEM Properties

  • The SEM is in the same units as the test score
  • The SEM decreases as reliability increases
  • The SEM is assumed to be constant across all score levels (though in practice, it may vary)
  • The SEM is used to construct confidence intervals around an observed score
Example: A test has SD = 15 and reliability = .91. SEM = 15 * sqrt(1 - .91) = 15 * sqrt(.09) = 15 * 0.30 = 4.5. For the WAIS-IV FSIQ (reliability ~.98), SEM ~ 15 * sqrt(.02) ~ 2.1 points. This means the FSIQ is measured with very high precision.

Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval (CI) provides a range around the observed score within which the true score is likely to fall, given a specified level of confidence.

Construction

  • 68% CI: Observed score +/- 1 SEM
  • 90% CI: Observed score +/- 1.645 SEM
  • 95% CI: Observed score +/- 1.96 SEM (often rounded to 2 SEM)
  • 99% CI: Observed score +/- 2.576 SEM
Example: An examinee obtains an IQ of 112 on a test with SEM = 4.5. The 95% CI = 112 +/- (1.96 x 4.5) = 112 +/- 8.82, or approximately 103 to 121. We can state with 95% confidence that the individual's true IQ falls between 103 and 121.
EPPP Practice Point: Reporting confidence intervals rather than single scores is considered best practice because it communicates the inherent imprecision in any test score. APA guidelines recommend that psychologists report confidence intervals when communicating test results.

Reliable Change Index (RCI)

The Reliable Change Index (RCI) (Jacobson & Truax, 1991) determines whether the difference between two test scores (e.g., pre- and post-treatment) reflects true change rather than measurement error.

Formula: RCI = (X2 - X1) / Sdiff, where Sdiff = sqrt(2 * SEM2) = SEM * sqrt(2). If |RCI| > 1.96, the change is statistically significant at the .05 level (i.e., unlikely to be due to measurement error alone).

Clinical Application

  • Used to evaluate whether therapeutic interventions produced meaningful change
  • Used in neuropsychological re-evaluation to determine whether cognitive decline is beyond what measurement error would predict
  • Provides an objective criterion for "reliable" change rather than relying on arbitrary cutoffs
Example: A client scores 85 on a depression measure before treatment and 70 after treatment. The SEM = 5. Sdiff = 5 * sqrt(2) = 7.07. RCI = (70 - 85) / 7.07 = -2.12. Since |2.12| > 1.96, the change is statistically reliable -- the improvement is unlikely due to measurement error alone.

Regression to the Mean

Regression to the mean is the statistical phenomenon whereby extreme scores on a first measurement tend to be less extreme on subsequent measurements. This occurs because any observed score contains both true score variance and error variance, and the error component is random.

Key Implications

  • A student who scores very high on a test is likely to score somewhat lower on retest (and vice versa) -- not because their ability changed, but because the extreme score was partly due to random error in the favorable direction
  • Regression to the mean is more pronounced when reliability is lower (more error variance) and when the initial score is more extreme
  • This effect must be considered when evaluating treatment outcomes: improvement from an extremely low baseline may partly reflect regression to the mean rather than true treatment effects
  • The estimated true score = M + rxx(X - M), where M = population mean, rxx = reliability, and X = observed score. This formula shows that the estimated true score is always closer to the mean than the observed score (regressed toward the mean by a factor of the reliability coefficient)
Estimated True Score Formula: Estimated True Score = M + r(X - M). If a test has M = 100, r = .90, and an individual scores 130, the estimated true score = 100 + .90(130 - 100) = 100 + 27 = 127. The estimated true score (127) is regressed 3 points toward the mean from the observed score (130).
EPPP Application: When predicting retest scores, always expect regression to the mean. An individual who scored 75 (low) is predicted to score somewhat higher on retest; an individual who scored 130 (high) is predicted to score somewhat lower. The amount of regression depends on the test's reliability -- lower reliability produces more regression.

Additional Score Types

Age Equivalents and Grade Equivalents

  • Age equivalent: The age at which the individual's raw score is the average performance. A 7-year-old with an age equivalent of 9:6 performs like the average 9-year-6-month-old.
  • Grade equivalent: The grade level at which the individual's raw score is the average. A 4th grader with a grade equivalent of 6.2 performs like the average student in the 2nd month of 6th grade.
  • Both are ordinal scales and are widely criticized because they are often misinterpreted (e.g., a 2nd grader with a grade equivalent of 5.0 in math does not necessarily know 5th-grade math content)
  • Age and grade equivalents should not be used for comparison across different tests or subtests

Scaled Scores

Wechsler subtest scores use a scaled score metric with M = 10 and SD = 3. The range is 1-19. Scaled scores allow comparison of performance across subtests.

Sten Scores

Sten scores (standard ten) have M = 5.5 and SD = 2, with a range of 1-10. Used by the 16PF and some other personality measures. Scores of 4-7 are average; 1-3 are low; 8-10 are high.

Commonly Tested EPPP Summary

Score conversions: z = (X-M)/SD. T = 10z + 50. IQ = 15z + 100. Scaled = 3z + 10. Know how to convert between all types.
Normal curve: 34-14-2 rule for percentage of scores in each SD band. 1 SD below mean ~ 16th percentile. 2 SDs below ~ 2nd percentile.
SEM: SEM = SD * sqrt(1 - reliability). Used to build confidence intervals. 95% CI = Score +/- 1.96 SEM.
RCI: (Score2 - Score1) / (SEM * sqrt(2)). If |RCI| > 1.96, change is reliable at p < .05.
Regression to the mean: Extreme scores become less extreme on retest. Estimated true score = M + r(X - M). More regression when reliability is lower.
Percentile ranks: Ordinal, not interval. Cannot be averaged. Compressed near the mean, spread at extremes.
