Overview of the Wechsler Scales

The Wechsler intelligence scales are the most widely used individually administered intelligence tests in the world. Developed by David Wechsler beginning in 1939, the scales are based on Wechsler's definition of intelligence as "the aggregate or global capacity of the individual to act purposefully, think rationally, and deal effectively with his environment." The current editions include measures for adults, children, and preschoolers.

Wechsler's Definition: Intelligence is not a single ability but an aggregate of abilities. Wechsler also emphasized that non-intellective factors (motivation, persistence, emotional regulation) influence intelligent behavior. This holistic view influenced his decision to include both verbal and performance measures.

Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale - Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV)

The WAIS-IV (Wechsler, 2008) is the premier adult intelligence measure for ages 16-90. It yields a Full Scale IQ (FSIQ) and four index scores based on the Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) theoretical framework.

Four Index Scores

Subtest Descriptions

Verbal Comprehension Subtests

  • Similarities: How are two words alike? Measures verbal abstract reasoning and concept formation
  • Vocabulary: Define words. Measures word knowledge, verbal expression, and crystallized ability
  • Information: General knowledge questions. Measures breadth of acquired knowledge and long-term memory
  • Comprehension (supplemental): Social judgment questions. Measures practical reasoning and social knowledge

Perceptual Reasoning Subtests

  • Block Design: Replicate patterns using red-and-white blocks within time limits. Measures visual-spatial analysis, construction, and nonverbal problem-solving. Often considered the best single nonverbal measure of g.
  • Matrix Reasoning: Select the missing element from a visual pattern. Measures fluid reasoning with minimal motor demands
  • Visual Puzzles: Identify pieces that combine to form a target design. Measures visual-spatial reasoning
  • Figure Weights (supplemental): Quantitative fluid reasoning using visual balance scale

Working Memory Subtests

  • Digit Span: Three conditions -- Forward (attention, short-term memory), Backward (working memory, mental manipulation), and Sequencing (working memory, cognitive flexibility)
  • Arithmetic: Mental math problems. Measures working memory, attention, and numerical reasoning

Processing Speed Subtests

  • Symbol Search: Visual scanning task -- does the target symbol match any in the row? Measures processing speed and visual discrimination
  • Coding: Transcribe symbol-number pairs using a key. Measures processing speed, visual-motor coordination, and incidental learning
EPPP Commonly Tested Subtests: Block Design is considered the best single nonverbal measure of g. Vocabulary is considered the best single verbal measure of g (and the single best overall predictor of FSIQ). Digit Span is the most commonly used subtest for assessing attention and working memory across clinical settings.

WAIS-IV Scoring

  • Subtest scaled scores: M = 10, SD = 3 (range 1-19)
  • Index scores and FSIQ: M = 100, SD = 15
  • FSIQ is based on the 10 core subtests
  • Significant discrepancies between indices (e.g., VCI-PRI or WMI-PSI) may have clinical significance but must be interpreted in context

General Ability Index (GAI)

The GAI is composed of VCI + PRI subtests only, excluding WMI and PSI. It provides an estimate of general intellectual ability that is less influenced by processing speed and working memory. The GAI is useful when:

  • WMI or PSI scores are significantly lower than VCI/PRI (e.g., in ADHD, TBI, or processing disorders)
  • The clinician wants to estimate cognitive ability without the confound of processing speed deficits

Cognitive Proficiency Index (CPI)

The CPI combines WMI + PSI. A significant GAI-CPI discrepancy may indicate that processing efficiency is significantly lower than reasoning ability, which is clinically meaningful in conditions like ADHD, learning disabilities, and neurological impairment.

Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children - Fifth Edition (WISC-V)

The WISC-V (Wechsler, 2014) is the children's version for ages 6-16:11. It expanded from four to five index scores:

IndexCore SubtestsCHC Ability
Verbal Comprehension (VCI)Similarities, VocabularyGc (Crystallized)
Visual Spatial (VSI)Block Design, Visual PuzzlesGv (Visual Processing)
Fluid Reasoning (FRI)Matrix Reasoning, Figure WeightsGf (Fluid Reasoning)
Working Memory (WMI)Digit Span, Picture SpanGsm (Short-Term Memory)
Processing Speed (PSI)Coding, Symbol SearchGs (Processing Speed)

Key Change from WISC-IV to WISC-V

The major structural change was splitting the WISC-IV's Perceptual Reasoning Index into two separate indices: Visual Spatial (VSI) and Fluid Reasoning (FRI). This provides better alignment with the CHC model by distinguishing visual-spatial processing (Gv) from fluid reasoning (Gf).

EPPP Comparison: WISC-V has 5 primary indices vs. WAIS-IV's 4. The WISC-V separates visual-spatial and fluid reasoning, while the WAIS-IV combines them in the PRI. Also note: the WISC-V includes Picture Span (a visual working memory task) as a core WMI subtest, expanding working memory assessment beyond the auditory-verbal modality.

Wechsler Preschool and Primary Scale of Intelligence - Fourth Edition (WPPSI-IV)

The WPPSI-IV (Wechsler, 2012) is designed for children ages 2:6 to 7:7. It has two age bands:

Ages 2:6-3:11

  • Three index scores: VCI, VSI, and Working Memory
  • Core subtests: Receptive Vocabulary, Information, Block Design, Object Assembly, Picture Memory
  • Shorter and more engaging for very young children

Ages 4:0-7:7

  • Five index scores: VCI, VSI, FRI, WMI, PSI
  • Includes Fluid Reasoning subtests (Matrix Reasoning, Picture Concepts)
  • Processing Speed subtests added (Bug Search, Cancellation)

Special Considerations

  • Subtests use manipulatives, colorful stimuli, and game-like formats to maintain the attention of young children
  • Administration time is shorter than the WISC-V or WAIS-IV
  • Test-retest reliability is somewhat lower than for older-child and adult versions (due to developmental variability at young ages)

Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scales, Fifth Edition (SB5)

The SB5 (Roid, 2003) is the latest revision of the oldest intelligence test in continuous use. Originally developed by Binet and Simon in 1905 (adapted by Terman at Stanford in 1916), the SB5 measures five factors across both verbal and nonverbal domains.

Five Factor Structure

FactorNonverbal SubtestVerbal SubtestCHC Ability
Fluid ReasoningNonverbal Fluid ReasoningVerbal Fluid ReasoningGf
KnowledgeNonverbal KnowledgeVerbal KnowledgeGc
Quantitative ReasoningNonverbal Quantitative ReasoningVerbal Quantitative ReasoningGq
Visual-Spatial ProcessingNonverbal Visual-SpatialVerbal Visual-SpatialGv
Working MemoryNonverbal Working MemoryVerbal Working MemoryGsm

Composite Scores

  • Full Scale IQ (FSIQ): Based on all 10 subtests (M = 100, SD = 15)
  • Nonverbal IQ (NVIQ): Based on the 5 nonverbal subtests
  • Verbal IQ (VIQ): Based on the 5 verbal subtests
  • Five Factor Index scores (one for each factor)

Routing Procedure

The SB5 uses a unique routing procedure to establish the appropriate starting point for each subtest. Two routing subtests are administered first:

  • Nonverbal Fluid Reasoning (first nonverbal routing subtest)
  • Vocabulary (first verbal routing subtest)

These routing subtests determine the functional level (starting item set) for the remaining subtests, ensuring that the examinee is tested at an appropriate difficulty level and reducing floor/ceiling effects.

SB5 vs. Wechsler Scales

  • The SB5 has a broader age range (2-85+) than any single Wechsler scale
  • The SB5 measures each factor in both verbal and nonverbal domains (the Wechsler scales do not systematically do this)
  • The SB5 uses a routing procedure; the Wechsler scales use age-based starting points
  • The SB5 has a wider score range (extended IQ from 40 to 160), which is useful at the extremes
  • The Wechsler scales generally have more subtests and offer more detailed clinical information within each domain
Historical Note: The original Stanford-Binet introduced the concept of the Intelligence Quotient (IQ), originally computed as Mental Age / Chronological Age x 100 (the ratio IQ). Modern intelligence tests, including the SB5 and Wechsler scales, use deviation IQ scores (M = 100, SD = 15) rather than ratio IQs. The deviation IQ was introduced by Wechsler and later adopted by the Stanford-Binet.

EPPP Note: A diagnosis of intellectual disability requires: (1) significantly subaverage intellectual functioning (typically IQ ~70 or below), (2) concurrent deficits in adaptive functioning, and (3) onset during the developmental period (before age 18). An IQ score alone is never sufficient for the diagnosis.

The Flynn Effect

The Flynn Effect (Flynn, 1984, 1987) refers to the well-documented finding that IQ scores have been rising approximately 3 points per decade across populations over the past century. Key implications:

  • When a new edition of a test is normed, the normative sample performs better than the previous edition's sample, resulting in slightly lower scores on the new edition
  • Using an outdated test version can inflate IQ scores by approximately 3 points per decade since the norms were established
  • The Flynn Effect is larger for fluid intelligence measures (~5 points/decade) than for crystallized measures (~2 points/decade)
  • This has significant forensic implications, particularly in Atkins v. Virginia (2002) intellectual disability death penalty cases, where the version of the test used can affect whether an individual falls above or below the IQ 70 cutoff
EPPP Application: If a person was tested with the WAIS-III (normed in 1997) and obtained an FSIQ of 73, retesting with the WAIS-IV (normed in 2008) might yield an FSIQ of approximately 70 due to the Flynn Effect. This difference can have legal consequences in intellectual disability determinations.

Factor Structure and CHC Theory

Modern intelligence tests are organized around the Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) theory, which integrates Cattell and Horn's Gf-Gc theory with Carroll's three-stratum model:

  • Stratum III: General intelligence (g)
  • Stratum II: Broad abilities -- Gf (Fluid Reasoning), Gc (Crystallized Ability), Gv (Visual Processing), Gsm (Short-Term Memory), Gs (Processing Speed), Glr (Long-Term Retrieval), Ga (Auditory Processing), Gq (Quantitative Knowledge), and others
  • Stratum I: Narrow abilities (specific skills measured by individual subtests)

Gf vs. Gc

  • Fluid Intelligence (Gf): The ability to reason, solve novel problems, and identify patterns without relying on previously acquired knowledge. Largely biologically determined. Peaks in young adulthood and declines with age.
  • Crystallized Intelligence (Gc): Accumulated knowledge, vocabulary, and skills acquired through education and experience. Largely culturally and educationally influenced. Continues to grow throughout the lifespan (or at least remains stable into late life).
Aging Pattern: Gf declines with age (particularly after age 60-70), while Gc remains stable or increases through middle and later adulthood. This produces the "classic aging pattern" on the Wechsler scales: relatively preserved VCI with declining PSI and WMI scores. Processing speed (Gs) shows the earliest and most pronounced age-related decline.

Commonly Tested EPPP Summary

WAIS-IV: Ages 16-90. Four indices (VCI, PRI, WMI, PSI) + FSIQ. Subtest M = 10, SD = 3. Index/FSIQ M = 100, SD = 15. GAI = VCI + PRI (excludes working memory and speed).
WISC-V: Ages 6-16. Five indices (VCI, VSI, FRI, WMI, PSI). Separates Visual Spatial from Fluid Reasoning. Includes Picture Span for visual working memory.
SB5: Ages 2-85+. Five factors, each measured verbally and nonverbally. Uses routing procedure. Wider score range (40-160). Introduced the original IQ concept (ratio IQ, now replaced by deviation IQ).
Flynn Effect: ~3 IQ points per decade increase. Using outdated norms inflates scores. Forensically significant (Atkins v. Virginia). Larger for Gf than Gc.
Classic aging pattern: Gf and Gs decline with age; Gc remains stable or increases. On WAIS-IV: VCI preserved, PSI declines most.
