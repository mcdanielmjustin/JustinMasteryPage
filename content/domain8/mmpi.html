Validity scales, clinical scales, code types
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of the MMPI Family

The Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory is the most widely used and researched objective personality test in clinical psychology. Originally developed by Hathaway and McKinley in 1943, the instrument has undergone several major revisions. For the EPPP, you must understand the MMPI-2, MMPI-2-RF, and MMPI-3, including their scale structures, construction methods, and interpretation strategies.

Historical Development

  • Original MMPI (1943): Hathaway and McKinley developed the instrument at the University of Minnesota using empirical criterion keying -- items were selected based on their ability to discriminate between clinical groups and normals, not based on item content
  • MMPI-2 (1989): Butcher, Dahlstrom, Graham, Tellegen, and Kaemmer restandardized the instrument with a more representative normative sample (N = 2,600). Item content was updated but the clinical scales were preserved. Uniform T-scores were introduced.
  • MMPI-2-RF (2008): Ben-Porath and Tellegen restructured the instrument using factor analysis, creating 51 scales organized hierarchically. The 10 clinical scales were replaced by Restructured Clinical (RC) scales.
  • MMPI-3 (2020): Ben-Porath and Tellegen further revised the RF, with updated norms, revised items, and modernized content. 335 items, 52 scales.
Empirical Criterion Keying: Items are selected not based on their face validity (what they appear to measure) but based on their empirical ability to discriminate between criterion groups (e.g., patients diagnosed with depression vs. normals). This approach means that an item about headaches might appear on the Depression scale -- not because headaches are theoretically related to depression, but because depressed individuals endorsed that item significantly more often than non-depressed individuals.

MMPI-2: Structure and Scales

The MMPI-2 contains 567 true/false items, takes approximately 60-90 minutes to administer, and requires a sixth-grade reading level. It is normed for ages 18 and older.

Validity Scales

The validity scales assess the test-taking attitude and response style of the examinee. They must be evaluated before interpreting the clinical scales.

ScaleNameWhat It Measures
? (CNS)Cannot SayNumber of items left unanswered. More than 30 unanswered items invalidates the profile.
LLieNaive, unsophisticated attempts to present oneself in a favorable light. Denying common human faults. Elevated L = "faking good" or rigid/moralistic personality.
FInfrequencyItems endorsed by fewer than 10% of the normative sample. Elevated F = "faking bad," exaggeration, random responding, severe psychopathology, or a "cry for help."
KDefensivenessMore sophisticated defensiveness than L. Moderate K = healthy self-presentation; very high K = denial/defensiveness; very low K = excessive openness or self-criticism.
FbBack FInfrequency items from the second half of the test. Detects changes in response style partway through administration.
VRINVariable Response InconsistencyPairs of items with similar or opposite content; inconsistent responding suggests random or careless responding.
TRINTrue Response InconsistencyPairs of items where answering both True or both False is contradictory. Detects acquiescence (yea-saying) or nay-saying bias.
FpInfrequency-PsychopathologyItems infrequently endorsed even by psychiatric inpatients. Helps distinguish genuine severe psychopathology (F elevated, Fp normal) from overreporting (both elevated).
SSuperlative Self-PresentationItems reflecting an extremely positive self-view; supplement to L and K for detecting underreporting.
EPPP Commonly Tested: F-K Index (Gough Dissimulation Index): F minus K. A positive value (F > K) suggests overreporting/faking bad. A negative value (K > F) suggests underreporting/faking good. This index is commonly tested.

T-Score Interpretation

  • Clinical scales use Uniform T-scores (developed by Auke Tellegen) to ensure comparable percentile rankings across scales
  • T-score of 65 or above is considered clinically significant (93rd percentile)
  • T-scores between 60-64 are marginally elevated
  • Scales 5 (Mf) and 0 (Si) use linear T-scores rather than uniform T-scores because their distributions are close to normal

Code Types

A code type is determined by the two or three highest clinical scales in the profile. Code type interpretation is based on decades of research correlating specific code types with behavioral, personality, and diagnostic characteristics.

Common Two-Point Code Types

EPPP Commonly Tested: The "Conversion V" (1-3/3-1 code type with Scale 2 lower than both 1 and 3) is one of the most commonly tested code types. The visual pattern on the profile looks like a "V" because Scale 2 (Depression) dips below Scales 1 and 3. It classically indicates somatic complaints with denial of emotional distress.

Harris-Lingoes Subscales

The Harris-Lingoes subscales were developed by Harris and Lingoes in 1955 to clarify the content within the heterogeneous clinical scales. They are particularly useful when a clinical scale is elevated and the clinician wants to determine which content domain is driving the elevation. Subscales exist for Scales 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, and 9.

MMPI-2-RF (Restructured Form)

The MMPI-2-RF (Ben-Porath & Tellegen, 2008) contains 338 items and uses a hierarchically organized structure of 51 scales. The key innovation was removing the demoralization variance (a general distress factor) that permeated and inflated correlations among the original clinical scales.

Scale Hierarchy

  1. Validity Scales (9): CNS, VRIN-r, TRIN-r, F-r, Fp-r, Fs, FBS-r, L-r, K-r
  2. Higher-Order (H-O) Scales (3): Emotional/Internalizing Dysfunction (EID), Thought Dysfunction (THD), Behavioral/Externalizing Dysfunction (BXD)
  3. Restructured Clinical (RC) Scales (9): The core interpretive scales
    • RCd: Demoralization (general unhappiness/distress)
    • RC1: Somatic Complaints
    • RC2: Low Positive Emotions
    • RC3: Cynicism
    • RC4: Antisocial Behavior
    • RC6: Ideas of Persecution
    • RC7: Dysfunctional Negative Emotions
    • RC8: Aberrant Experiences
    • RC9: Hypomanic Activation
  4. Specific Problems (SP) Scales (23): Provide detailed information within domains (e.g., Head Pain Complaints, Stress/Worry, Juvenile Conduct Problems)
  5. Interest Scales (2): Aesthetic-Literary Interests, Mechanical-Physical Interests
  6. Personality Psychopathology Five (PSY-5) Scales (5): Aggressiveness-Revised, Psychoticism-Revised, Disconstraint-Revised, Negative Emotionality/Neuroticism-Revised, Introversion/Low Positive Emotionality-Revised
Key Distinction: The MMPI-2-RF RC scales differ from the original clinical scales primarily in the removal of the demoralization factor. In the original MMPI-2, many clinical scales intercorrelated highly because they all contained items tapping general distress. The RC scales isolate the distinctive core construct of each scale, improving discriminant validity.

MMPI-3

The MMPI-3 (Ben-Porath & Tellegen, 2020) is the latest version, containing 335 items and 52 scales. It retains the hierarchical structure of the MMPI-2-RF with several key updates:

  • Updated normative sample (N = 1,620) that is more representative of the U.S. population
  • Revised and modernized item content (removed outdated or offensive items)
  • New scales addressing interpersonal functioning and compulsive behaviors
  • Improved validity scales for detecting over- and underreporting
  • Updated PSY-5 scales

Key Differences from MMPI-2-RF

  • The MMPI-3 replaces some specific problems scales and adds new ones (e.g., Compulsivity, Self-Importance)
  • The normative sample is more diverse and contemporary
  • Item content was further refined for clarity and modern language
  • The MMPI-3 is not backward-compatible with the MMPI-2 or MMPI-2-RF

Administration and Scoring

Administration Requirements

  • Age: 18+ for all MMPI instruments (the MMPI-A and MMPI-A-RF are the adolescent versions for ages 14-18)
  • Reading level: Approximately sixth grade for the MMPI-2; similar for MMPI-2-RF and MMPI-3
  • Format: True/False items; can be administered via booklet, computer, or audio recording
  • Completion of first 370 items of the MMPI-2 allows scoring of all validity and clinical scales (the remaining items contribute to supplementary and content scales)

Scoring Considerations

  • Raw scores are converted to T-scores (M = 50, SD = 10)
  • The MMPI-2 uses Uniform T-scores for most scales (except Mf and Si)
  • The MMPI-2-RF and MMPI-3 use non-gendered norms (combined male/female reference group)
  • K-correction is applied to five clinical scales on the MMPI-2 (Scales 1, 4, 7, 8, 9) -- a proportion of the K score is added to compensate for defensiveness. K-correction is NOT used on the MMPI-2-RF or MMPI-3.
EPPP Tip: K-correction adds a fraction of K to specific clinical scales on the MMPI-2 to adjust for defensiveness. This was controversial because it could inflate scores for genuinely well-adjusted individuals. The MMPI-2-RF and MMPI-3 eliminated K-correction entirely, instead relying on validity scale interpretation to contextualize clinical scale scores.

Interpretation Strategies

Steps in MMPI-2 Interpretation

  1. Evaluate validity scales: Determine whether the profile is valid and interpretable. Check CNS, VRIN, TRIN, F, L, K, Fb, Fp, S
  2. Examine overall profile elevation: Look at how many scales are elevated above 65T
  3. Identify code type: Determine the two or three highest clinical scales and look up the corresponding code type interpretation
  4. Examine individual scale elevations: Consider the meaning of each elevated scale
  5. Review Harris-Lingoes subscales: Clarify what content is driving clinical scale elevations
  6. Examine content and supplementary scales: Use additional scales (e.g., Anxiety, Depression, Addiction Acknowledgment) to refine interpretation
  7. Integrate with clinical data: Combine MMPI results with interview, history, behavioral observation, and other test data
EPPP Reminder: Never interpret an MMPI profile in isolation. Results must be integrated with clinical interview, behavioral observations, collateral information, and the referral question. The MMPI is an aid to clinical decision-making, not a diagnostic algorithm.

Commonly Tested EPPP Topics

Construction method: Empirical criterion keying -- items selected based on ability to discriminate groups, not face validity or theory.
Validity scale summary: L = naive faking good. F = faking bad / random / severe pathology. K = sophisticated defensiveness. VRIN = random responding. TRIN = yea/nay-saying. Fp = distinguishes true pathology from overreporting.
Conversion V: 1-3/3-1 code type with Scale 2 depressed. Somatic complaints, denial of psychological distress, la belle indifference.
MMPI-2-RF key innovation: Removal of demoralization variance from clinical scales, creating purer RC scales with improved discriminant validity.
K-correction: Applied to MMPI-2 Scales 1, 4, 7, 8, 9. Eliminated in MMPI-2-RF and MMPI-3.
