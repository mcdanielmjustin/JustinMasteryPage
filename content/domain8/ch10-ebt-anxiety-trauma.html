EBT: Anxiety, Trauma & OCD

Domain 8: Clinical Assessment & Interpretation~27 min read

Exposure-Based Treatments for Anxiety

Exposure therapy is the gold-standard behavioral intervention for anxiety disorders. The core principle involves systematic confrontation with feared stimuli in the absence of the feared consequence, leading to habituation (within- and between-session reduction of fear) and inhibitory learning (formation of new, non-threat associations that compete with the original fear memory). Exposure is grounded in classical conditioning principles: the feared stimulus (CS) was paired with a threatening or aversive experience (US), producing a conditioned fear response (CR). Exposure allows for extinction of this learned association.

Systematic Desensitization vs. Flooding

Systematic desensitization (Wolpe, 1958) pairs graduated exposure with relaxation training based on the principle of reciprocal inhibition — anxiety and relaxation are mutually incompatible states. The client constructs a fear hierarchy (using SUDS ratings from 0-100) and progresses through it while maintaining a relaxed state. Treatment typically begins with training in progressive muscle relaxation before any exposure begins.

Flooding involves prolonged exposure to the most anxiety-provoking stimulus without the graduated hierarchy, relying on habituation through extended contact with the feared stimulus. Unlike systematic desensitization, flooding does not include relaxation training and begins at high anxiety levels. It is critical that flooding sessions are of sufficient duration to allow anxiety to decrease — premature termination of a flooding session while anxiety is still elevated can actually sensitize the client and increase fear (a process called incubation).

Implosive therapy (Stampfl) is a variant that uses imaginal flooding with psychodynamic content — the therapist describes the feared scenario in exaggerated detail, sometimes incorporating hypothesized unconscious themes. Implosive therapy is rarely used in contemporary practice.

Graduated vs. Intensive Exposure:

Research comparing graduated and intensive (massed) exposure formats generally shows equivalent outcomes, though intensive formats may produce faster results. The critical variable is that exposure must be of sufficient duration and intensity to promote new learning. Brief exposure sessions that are terminated prematurely (before habituation or expectancy violation occurs) may worsen anxiety. Session duration of at least 60-90 minutes is typically recommended for prolonged exposure exercises.

Inhibitory Learning Model:

The contemporary inhibitory learning model (Craske et al., 2014) proposes that exposure does not erase the original fear memory but rather creates a new, competing inhibitory association (CS-no US). This model has largely replaced the habituation model as the prevailing theoretical account of exposure therapy. Strategies to enhance inhibitory learning include:

  • Expectancy violation: Maximizing the mismatch between expected and actual outcomes during exposure
  • Deepened extinction: Combining multiple fear cues during a single exposure trial
  • Variability: Varying exposure parameters (context, duration, intensity) across sessions
  • Occasional reinforced extinction: Occasionally including the feared outcome during exposure to strengthen new learning
  • Affect labeling: Verbally labeling the emotional experience during exposure
  • Retrieval cues: Using cues from the exposure context in everyday life to trigger the inhibitory memory

CBT for Panic Disorder

CBT for panic disorder (Clark, 1986; Barlow & Craske) includes psychoeducation about the nature of panic (the "fear of fear" model), cognitive restructuring of catastrophic misinterpretations of bodily sensations (e.g., "my racing heart means I'm having a heart attack"), interoceptive exposure (deliberately inducing feared sensations), and in vivo exposure to avoided situations. The treatment targets the maintenance cycle in which normal bodily sensations are catastrophically misinterpreted, producing anxiety that intensifies the sensations.

Interoceptive Exposure Exercises:

Common exercises include: hyperventilation (30 seconds) to induce dizziness and tingling; breathing through a thin straw to create air hunger; spinning in a chair to produce dizziness; running in place to elevate heart rate; staring at a spot on the wall to induce derealization. Each exercise is designed to provoke the specific bodily sensations the client fears. The client rates the similarity of each exercise to their natural panic symptoms and begins exposure with exercises most closely matching their feared sensations.

CBT for Social Anxiety Disorder

Cognitive-behavioral treatment for social anxiety disorder typically includes cognitive restructuring targeting fears of negative evaluation, behavioral experiments to test catastrophic predictions, and graduated in vivo exposure to feared social situations. Heimberg's Cognitive Behavioral Group Therapy (CBGT) is a well-established protocol that combines cognitive restructuring with in-session simulated exposures conducted within the therapy group. Clark and Wells' individual cognitive therapy model emphasizes reducing self-focused attention and safety behaviors during social interactions.

Craske, M. G., Treanor, M., Conway, C. C., Zbozinek, T., & Vervliet, B. (2014). Maximizing exposure therapy: An inhibitory learning approach. Behaviour Research and Therapy, 58, 10-23.

Exposure and Response Prevention for OCD

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is the first-line psychotherapy for OCD. The treatment involves two components:

  • Exposure: Systematic confrontation with obsession-triggering stimuli (e.g., touching a "contaminated" object, writing a feared phrase, looking at an asymmetrical arrangement)
  • Response Prevention: Refraining from performing compulsive rituals that typically follow the obsession (e.g., not washing hands after touching the trigger, not checking the lock, not mentally reviewing for reassurance)

ERP works by breaking the reinforcement cycle in which compulsions temporarily reduce obsessional distress, thereby negatively reinforcing both the obsession and the compulsion. Without the ritual, habituation to the distress occurs, and the client learns that feared consequences do not materialize (or that the distress is tolerable). ERP produces response rates of 60-80% with significant symptom reduction. Treatment typically involves 12-20 sessions, and gains are well-maintained at follow-up.

ERP Application:

A client with contamination OCD constructs a hierarchy from low-anxiety triggers (touching a doorknob — SUDS 30) to high-anxiety triggers (touching a bathroom floor and then eating — SUDS 95). Sessions involve progressing through the hierarchy while completely refraining from hand washing, use of sanitizer, or other decontamination rituals. Between sessions, the client practices daily exposure exercises with response prevention.

OCD Subtypes and ERP Adaptations

ERP is adapted for different OCD presentations. For checking compulsions, exposure involves resisting the urge to check (locks, stove, alarms) after a single verification. For symmetry/ordering OCD, exposure involves deliberately creating asymmetry and tolerating the resulting distress. For "pure O" (primarily obsessional OCD), imaginal exposure scripts describing the feared scenario are used when behavioral exposure is not feasible. For harm-related obsessions, exposure may involve holding a knife near a loved one while resisting mental rituals. Response prevention targets not only behavioral compulsions but also mental rituals such as mental reviewing, counting, or praying.

ERP and Medication:

SSRIs (particularly fluvoxamine, fluoxetine, sertraline, and paroxetine) and clomipramine (a tricyclic antidepressant with strong serotonergic activity) are first-line pharmacological treatments for OCD. The combination of ERP plus an SSRI is often recommended for moderate-to-severe OCD. However, research suggests that ERP alone produces equivalent or superior long-term outcomes compared to medication alone, with lower relapse rates after treatment discontinuation.

Evidence-Based Treatments for PTSD

Prolonged Exposure (PE)

Prolonged Exposure (Foa et al., 2007) is based on Foa's emotional processing theory, which proposes that PTSD involves a pathological fear structure that is activated by trauma-related cues but maintained by avoidance. The fear structure contains stimulus representations, response representations, and meaning representations — successful processing requires activation of the entire fear structure followed by integration of corrective information.

PE consists of four components: (1) psychoeducation about trauma reactions, (2) breathing retraining, (3) imaginal exposure (repeated detailed revisiting of the traumatic memory in session, usually 45-60 minutes, recorded for homework listening), and (4) in vivo exposure to avoided but objectively safe trauma-related situations. PE is typically delivered in 8-15 weekly 90-minute sessions. The imaginal exposure component involves the client narrating the traumatic event in the present tense with eyes closed, repeatedly processing the memory until emotional distress decreases.

Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

CPT (Resick et al., 2007) targets maladaptive trauma-related cognitions — "stuck points" — that maintain PTSD symptoms. Common stuck points involve themes of safety, trust, power/control, esteem, and intimacy. Stuck points may be assimilated (the trauma is distorted to fit pre-existing beliefs, e.g., "it was my fault") or over-accommodated (pre-existing beliefs are distorted to fit the trauma, e.g., "no one can be trusted"). CPT uses Socratic questioning and cognitive worksheets (ABC worksheets, Challenging Beliefs Worksheets) to help clients identify and modify distorted beliefs. CPT can be delivered with or without a written trauma account (CPT vs. CPT-C, the cognitive-only version). Both 12-session individual and group formats are well-supported.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)

EMDR (Shapiro, 1989) proposes that traumatic memories are inadequately processed and stored in a dysfunctional manner. The Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) model holds that bilateral stimulation (typically eye movements, but also tapping or auditory tones) facilitates the processing and integration of traumatic memories.

While EMDR is listed as an effective treatment for PTSD by multiple guidelines (APA, VA/DoD, WHO), debate continues about whether the bilateral stimulation component provides benefit beyond standard imaginal exposure. Component analyses have yielded mixed results, and some researchers argue that the exposure and cognitive restructuring elements, rather than the bilateral stimulation, account for EMDR's efficacy.

Trauma-Focused CBT (TF-CBT)

TF-CBT (Cohen, Mannarino, & Deblinger) is the treatment of choice for children and adolescents (ages 3-18) with PTSD. The components are summarized by the acronym PRACTICE:

  • Psychoeducation — about trauma reactions and the treatment process
  • Parenting skills — teaching caregivers effective behavior management
  • Relaxation — progressive muscle relaxation, focused breathing, mindfulness
  • Affective modulation — identifying and regulating emotions
  • Cognitive coping and processing — connecting thoughts, feelings, and behaviors
  • Trauma narrative — gradual exposure through creating a narrative of the trauma experience
  • In vivo mastery — real-world exposure to trauma reminders that are objectively safe
  • Conjoint sessions — sharing the trauma narrative with the caregiver
  • Enhancing safety — safety planning and healthy relationship skills

TF-CBT includes parallel child and caregiver sessions throughout treatment and culminates in the creation and sharing of the trauma narrative. The caregiver component is considered essential — it addresses caregiver distress, teaches supportive responses, and prepares the caregiver for the conjoint narrative sharing session.

Treatment Selection:

PE, CPT, and EMDR are all recommended as first-line treatments for PTSD by the APA Clinical Practice Guideline (2017) and the VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline. PE is particularly strong for avoidance-dominated presentations, while CPT may be preferred when cognitive distortions (guilt, self-blame) are prominent. For children and adolescents, TF-CBT is the first-line recommendation. Written exposure therapy (WET) is an emerging brief intervention (5 sessions) showing promise as an efficient trauma-focused treatment.

Specific Phobia Treatment and Pharmacological Considerations

One-Session Treatment for Specific Phobias

One-session treatment (OST) (Ost, 1989) is a single prolonged session (typically 2-3 hours) of participant modeling and in vivo exposure for specific phobias. The therapist models approach behavior, provides graduated exposure, and uses guided participation. OST has demonstrated large effect sizes for various specific phobias including animal phobias, blood-injection-injury phobia, and claustrophobia. This intensive format achieves comparable outcomes to multi-session exposure therapy.

Blood-Injection-Injury (BII) Phobia:

BII phobia is unique among phobias because it involves a diphasic physiological response — an initial sympathetic activation (increased heart rate) followed by a strong parasympathetic response (vasovagal syncope — sudden drop in heart rate and blood pressure leading to fainting). For this reason, standard exposure alone may be insufficient; applied tension (Ost & Sterner) — tensing the large muscles of the body during exposure to maintain blood pressure — is the treatment of choice. This is the only phobia where relaxation training is contraindicated, as relaxation would lower blood pressure further.

Exposure Therapy and Benzodiazepines:

Concurrent benzodiazepine use during exposure therapy is generally discouraged because it may interfere with new learning. State-dependent learning theory suggests that fear extinction learned in a benzodiazepine-modulated state may not transfer to the drug-free state. Research suggests that D-cycloserine (DCS), an NMDA receptor partial agonist, may enhance extinction learning when administered shortly before exposure sessions, though results are mixed and the effect appears strongest in early sessions.

ACT and Third-Wave Approaches for Anxiety

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) (Hayes, Strosahl, & Wilson, 1999) approaches anxiety from a fundamentally different stance than traditional CBT. Rather than attempting to reduce or control anxiety, ACT aims to increase psychological flexibility — the ability to contact the present moment fully and engage in values-driven behavior even in the presence of difficult thoughts and emotions. ACT is rooted in Relational Frame Theory (RFT), a behavioral account of human language and cognition that explains how verbal processes create psychological suffering through cognitive fusion and experiential avoidance.

ACT distinguishes between "clean" pain (natural suffering inherent in life) and "dirty" pain (suffering caused by attempts to avoid or control clean pain). The treatment motto is sometimes summarized as: "The solution is the problem" — efforts to control internal experiences paradoxically increase suffering. Instead, ACT teaches clients to hold difficult experiences lightly while pursuing meaningful action.

The six core ACT processes — often depicted as the hexaflex model — are: acceptance, cognitive defusion, being present (contact with the present moment), self-as-context (the observing self), values clarification, and committed action. Together, these processes promote psychological flexibility, which is the central therapeutic target. ACT has growing empirical support for mixed anxiety disorders, social anxiety, GAD, and OCD, and is particularly useful when avoidance of internal experiences (experiential avoidance) is a central maintaining factor.

Key ACT Concepts:

Cognitive defusion involves changing the relationship to thoughts rather than changing thought content (e.g., "I'm having the thought that I'm going to fail" rather than "I'm going to fail"). Self-as-context refers to the transcendent sense of self that observes experience without being defined by it. Values in ACT are freely chosen life directions (not goals) that give meaning to behavior. ACT explicitly rejects symptom reduction as a treatment goal, instead focusing on living a valued life even in the presence of difficult internal experiences.

Applied Relaxation (Ost)

Applied relaxation (Ost, 1987) is a structured behavioral treatment primarily for GAD that progresses through several stages: progressive muscle relaxation, release-only relaxation, cue-controlled relaxation (pairing relaxation with a cue word), differential relaxation (relaxing in non-demanding situations), rapid relaxation (achieving relaxation in 20-30 seconds), and application training (using the skill in naturally anxiety-provoking situations). It is considered an empirically supported treatment for GAD and panic disorder.

CBT for GAD:

Treatment for Generalized Anxiety Disorder typically combines cognitive restructuring of worry-related beliefs (e.g., "worrying helps me prepare"), behavioral experiments to test catastrophic predictions, and worry exposure (deliberately imagining the worst-case scenario without engaging avoidance). Borkovec's model emphasizes that worry serves as cognitive avoidance of emotional processing — the verbal-linguistic nature of worry dampens emotional imagery and prevents full emotional processing of feared outcomes. Intolerance of uncertainty is a key cognitive target in Dugas's model of GAD treatment.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Exposure types: In vivo (real), imaginal (visualization), interoceptive (bodily sensations), VR (virtual). Know which is used for which disorder.
  • Systematic desensitization: Wolpe. Graduated exposure + relaxation. Based on reciprocal inhibition.
  • Flooding: Prolonged exposure to most feared stimulus. No relaxation component. Relies on habituation.
  • ERP for OCD: Exposure to obsessional triggers + prevention of compulsive rituals. First-line psychotherapy for OCD. 60-80% response rate.
  • Prolonged Exposure (PE): Foa. Emotional processing theory. Imaginal + in vivo exposure for PTSD.
  • CPT: Resick. Targets "stuck points" — maladaptive trauma cognitions. Themes: safety, trust, power, esteem, intimacy.
  • EMDR: Shapiro. Adaptive Information Processing model. 8-phase protocol with bilateral stimulation. Effective but mechanism debated.
  • TF-CBT: Treatment of choice for children/adolescents with PTSD. PRACTICE acronym. Includes caregiver component and trauma narrative.
  • Interoceptive exposure: Deliberately inducing feared bodily sensations. Key component of panic disorder CBT. Exercises include hyperventilation, spinning, straw breathing.
  • Inhibitory learning model: Craske. Exposure creates new competing memory rather than erasing old one. Emphasizes expectancy violation, variability, and deepened extinction.
  • Habituation vs. inhibitory learning: Older model = fear decreases through extinction/habituation. Newer model = original fear memory persists but a new inhibitory memory competes with it.
  • ACT: Hayes. Psychological flexibility (not symptom reduction) is the goal. Six core processes in hexaflex model. Third-wave CBT approach.
  • Cognitive defusion: ACT technique — changing the relationship to thoughts rather than changing thought content.
  • Implosive therapy: Stampfl. Imaginal flooding with psychodynamic content. Distinguished from standard flooding by inclusion of hypothesized unconscious themes.
  • CBT for social anxiety: Heimberg's CBGT uses cognitive restructuring + in-session exposures in group format. Clark and Wells model targets self-focused attention and safety behaviors.
  • GAD treatment: Borkovec — worry as cognitive avoidance. Dugas — intolerance of uncertainty. Applied relaxation (Ost) — progressive stages from PMR to application in real situations.
  • BII phobia: Unique diphasic response (sympathetic then parasympathetic/vasovagal). Treatment = applied tension (Ost). Relaxation training is CONTRAINDICATED.
  • One-session treatment: Ost. Single 2-3 hour session with participant modeling and in vivo exposure for specific phobias. Large effect sizes.
  • SSRIs for anxiety: First-line pharmacotherapy for most anxiety disorders (GAD, SAD, panic, OCD, PTSD). OCD requires higher doses than depression.
  • Benzodiazepines and exposure: Generally discouraged during exposure therapy due to state-dependent learning interference.
  • D-cycloserine (DCS): NMDA partial agonist that may enhance extinction learning when taken before exposure sessions.
  • Buspirone: 5-HT1A partial agonist for GAD. Non-sedating, no dependence potential, but slow onset (2-4 weeks). NOT effective for panic disorder.
  • CPT stuck points: Assimilated (distort trauma to fit beliefs) vs. over-accommodated (distort beliefs to fit trauma). Five themes: safety, trust, power, esteem, intimacy.
  • PE vs. CPT: PE emphasizes exposure to the trauma memory; CPT emphasizes cognitive restructuring of trauma-related beliefs. Both are first-line for PTSD.
  • EMDR mechanism debate: Bilateral stimulation may not be the active ingredient. Exposure and cognitive reprocessing elements may account for efficacy.
  • Incubation: Premature termination of flooding while anxiety is still high can worsen fear. Sessions must be long enough for habituation to occur.
  • Reciprocal inhibition: Wolpe's principle underlying systematic desensitization — anxiety and relaxation are mutually exclusive states. A relaxation response inhibits the anxiety response.
  • SUDS: Subjective Units of Distress Scale (0-100). Used to rate anxiety during exposure. Fear hierarchies are constructed using SUDS ratings.
  • RFT: Relational Frame Theory — ACT's theoretical foundation. A behavioral account of how language creates suffering through cognitive fusion.
  • Experiential avoidance: Central target in ACT. The attempt to avoid or control unwanted internal experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations) that paradoxically increases suffering.
  • Clark's cognitive model of panic: Catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations creates a vicious cycle — sensation leads to catastrophic thought leads to anxiety leads to more sensations.
  • Safety behaviors: Actions taken to prevent feared outcomes (e.g., always carrying medication, sitting near exits). Eliminating safety behaviors is essential for effective exposure because they prevent disconfirmation of feared consequences.
