Standards 9 & 10, informed consent, test security

Overview of Assessment Ethics

Ethical practice in psychological assessment is governed primarily by the APA Ethical Principles of Psychologists and Code of Conduct, particularly Standard 9 (Assessment) and Standard 10 (Therapy). These standards establish the obligations psychologists hold when selecting, administering, scoring, interpreting, and communicating test results. For the EPPP, you must understand both the specific ethical mandates and their practical applications in diverse clinical settings.

Core Principle: Psychologists provide opinions about the psychological characteristics of individuals only after conducting an examination adequate to support their statements or conclusions. When an individual examination is not practical, psychologists document their efforts to obtain one, clarify the impact of limited information on the reliability and validity of their opinions, and appropriately limit the nature and extent of their conclusions (Standard 9.01).

APA Standard 9: Assessment

9.01 Bases for Assessments

Psychologists must base their assessment opinions on information and techniques sufficient to substantiate their findings. This standard applies across forensic, clinical, educational, and organizational settings. When a personal examination is not possible (e.g., in record review or forensic consultation), the psychologist must clearly state the limitations of the resulting opinion.

  • Adequate examination means using procedures appropriate to the referral question, the individual being assessed, and the context of the evaluation
  • If a psychologist cannot conduct a direct examination, they must identify the sources of information used and qualify their conclusions accordingly
  • Opinions must not extend beyond what the data support

9.02 Use of Assessments

Psychologists must administer, adapt, score, interpret, and use assessment instruments in a manner consistent with the research on and evidence for the instrument's usefulness and proper application. Key provisions include:

  • Instruments must be used for purposes for which validity evidence exists
  • When validity or reliability has not been established for a specific population, the psychologist must describe the strengths and limitations of results and interpretation
  • Assessments must be administered in the standardized manner prescribed in the test manual

9.03 Informed Consent in Assessments

Psychologists must obtain informed consent for assessments, except when: (a) testing is mandated by law or regulation, (b) informed consent is implied because testing is a routine educational or organizational activity, or (c) the purpose of testing is to evaluate decisional capacity. Informed consent for assessment should include:

  • The nature and purpose of the assessment
  • Fees and billing arrangements
  • Involvement of third parties (e.g., referring attorneys, insurance companies)
  • Limits of confidentiality, including who will receive results
  • The examinee's right to receive an explanation of results (in language reasonably understandable to the person)
EPPP Exam Tip: When testing is court-ordered, informed consent is not required, but the psychologist is still obligated to inform the individual about the nature and purpose of the evaluation, including the limits of confidentiality and who will receive the report.

9.04 Release of Test Data

Standard 9.04 distinguishes between test data and test materials. Test data includes raw and scaled scores, client responses, and psychologist notes about client statements and behavior during an examination. Test materials include manuals, instruments, protocols, and test questions.

  • Psychologists must release test data to the client or other persons identified in a client release, unless substantial harm or misrepresentation is anticipated
  • In the absence of a client release, psychologists provide test data only when required by law or court order
  • Test materials are protected by copyright and are generally not released to non-qualified individuals
Key Distinction: Test data (the client's actual responses and scores) may be released with client consent. Test materials (the copyrighted items, stimuli, and manuals) are protected under Standard 9.11 and by copyright law. The EPPP frequently tests this distinction.

9.05 Test Construction

Psychologists who develop tests and other assessment techniques use appropriate psychometric procedures and current scientific or professional knowledge for test design, standardization, validation, reduction or elimination of bias, and recommendations for use.

9.06 Interpreting Assessment Results

When interpreting assessment results, psychologists must take into account the purpose of the assessment, various test factors, situational factors, and the test-taking abilities of the person being assessed. Factors that affect interpretation include:

  • The individual's linguistic and cultural background
  • Any situational factors (e.g., testing conditions, fatigue, medication)
  • Disabilities or impairments that may require accommodations
  • The appropriateness of the norms for the individual being assessed

9.07 Assessment by Unqualified Persons

Psychologists do not promote the use of assessment techniques by unqualified persons, except when the use of such instruments is conducted with proper supervision. This means psychologists may delegate scoring and administrative duties to technicians or trainees, but the psychologist retains responsibility for appropriate application, interpretation, and use of results.

9.08 Obsolete Tests and Outdated Test Results

Psychologists do not base their assessment or intervention decisions or recommendations on data or test results that are outdated for the current purpose. They also do not base decisions on measures or procedures that are obsolete and not useful for the current purpose.

Example: Using the original MMPI (published 1943) in current clinical practice would be problematic because the MMPI-2 and MMPI-3 represent substantially revised and re-normed instruments. The original norms are considered outdated and the item content in many cases obsolete.

9.09 Test Scoring and Interpretation Services

Psychologists who offer scoring or interpretation services retain responsibility for the accurate application of the procedure, whether the scoring is done by themselves, assistants, or automated services. They must select scoring and interpretation services based on evidence of their validity.

9.10 Explaining Assessment Results

Unless the nature of the relationship precludes it (e.g., organizational screening, forensic evaluation where results go to a third party), psychologists take reasonable steps to ensure that explanations of results are given to the individual or a designated representative. Results should be communicated in language the person can understand.

9.11 Maintaining Test Security

Psychologists make reasonable efforts to maintain the integrity and security of test materials and other assessment techniques consistent with law, contractual obligations, and the APA Ethics Code. This includes not reproducing or publishing copyrighted test items.

APA Standard 10: Therapy (Selected Assessment-Relevant Provisions)

10.01 Informed Consent to Therapy

Although primarily a therapy standard, 10.01 intersects with assessment when psychological testing is used as part of treatment planning. Clients must be informed about the nature of the therapeutic procedures, including any assessment components used to guide intervention.

10.10 Terminating Therapy

Assessment plays a role in determining when therapy goals have been met or when treatment is no longer beneficial. Outcome monitoring instruments help inform these decisions ethically.

Cultural Considerations in Assessment

Culturally competent assessment is an ethical obligation, not merely a best practice. Several standards and guidelines shape this domain:

Test Bias and Fairness

  • Construct bias occurs when the construct measured by the test differs across cultural groups
  • Method bias arises from differential familiarity with the testing format or medium
  • Item bias (differential item functioning, or DIF) occurs when members of different groups with the same ability level have different probabilities of endorsing an item

Use of Interpreters

When assessment involves individuals with limited English proficiency, psychologists should ideally use tests validated in the individual's native language. When interpreters are used, the psychologist must:

  • Ensure the interpreter is trained in psychological assessment contexts
  • Note the impact of using an interpreter on the standardization of the test
  • Document any limitations to the conclusions that result from the use of an interpreter

APA Multicultural Guidelines

The APA Multicultural Guidelines (2017) emphasize that psychologists should adopt an ecological approach to context, identity, and intersectionality when conducting assessments. This means recognizing how power, privilege, and oppression can affect test performance, help-seeking, and the meaning of assessment results.

Multicultural Assessment-Intervention Process (MAIP): A framework by Dana (1993, 2005) for integrating cultural considerations into assessment, including: (1) determining the cultural orientation of the client, (2) selecting culturally appropriate instruments and norms, (3) using culture-specific service delivery styles, and (4) incorporating emic (culture-specific) versus etic (universal) perspectives in interpretation.

Norm Appropriateness

A recurring EPPP theme is whether available norms are appropriate for the individual being tested. When normative samples do not include adequate representation of the examinee's demographic group, the psychologist must note this limitation and interpret results with caution.

Special Assessment Contexts

Forensic Assessment Ethics

The Specialty Guidelines for Forensic Psychology (APA, 2013) provide additional ethical guidance for assessment in legal contexts:

  • The psychologist's primary obligation is to the retaining party (court, attorney), not the examinee
  • Informed consent is replaced by notification of purpose when the evaluation is court-ordered
  • Results and reports go to the referring party; the examinee typically does not have unilateral access to the report
  • Psychologists must clearly identify their role (e.g., evaluator vs. treating clinician) and avoid dual relationships

Neuropsychological Assessment

The release of raw neuropsychological test data has been a significant ethical concern. The NAN (National Academy of Neuropsychology) Position Paper on test security emphasizes that releasing raw test data to non-qualified individuals can compromise the validity of the instruments for future use.

Assessment of Children and Adolescents

When assessing minors, informed consent is obtained from the parent or legal guardian, while assent is obtained from the child. Assent means the child agrees to participate in the evaluation with an age-appropriate understanding of what will occur. Special considerations include:

  • Results may be shared with schools, courts, and other agencies depending on the purpose of the evaluation
  • The child's developmental level affects which instruments and norms are appropriate
  • Cultural and linguistic factors may be more pronounced in pediatric populations

Competence in Assessment

Standard 2.01 requires psychologists to provide services within the boundaries of their competence, based on their education, training, supervised experience, consultation, study, or professional experience. In assessment, this means:

  • Psychologists should only administer and interpret tests for which they have received specific training
  • Competence in administering a test does not automatically confer competence in interpreting it (and vice versa)
  • Psychologists must stay current with revisions to instruments, new normative data, and evolving research on test validity
  • When using computerized or automated interpretive reports, the psychologist must be competent to evaluate the quality and clinical utility of the output
Practice Point: The EPPP frequently tests the principle that the psychologist, not the computer, is responsible for the interpretation. Even when using computerized scoring and narrative reports, the psychologist must integrate results with clinical observations, background data, and the specific referral question.

Record Keeping and Documentation

Standard 6.01 requires psychologists to create and maintain documentation of their professional and scientific work. In assessment contexts, this includes:

  • Complete records of all tests administered, including raw data and scored protocols
  • Documentation of the rationale for the selection of instruments
  • Notes on behavioral observations during testing
  • Records of all interpretive procedures used (including computerized reports)
  • Copies of the written report and any feedback provided to the client or referring party

Records must be maintained for the period required by applicable laws and institutional regulations, typically a minimum of seven years after the last date of service (or three years after a minor reaches the age of majority, whichever is later).

Emerging Issues in Assessment Ethics

Telepsychology and Remote Assessment

The growth of telepsychology has introduced new ethical considerations for assessment practice. The APA Guidelines for the Practice of Telepsychology (2013) require psychologists to consider whether remote administration alters standardized procedures, whether test security can be maintained in a remote environment, and whether the test's normative data are applicable to a remotely administered version.

  • Some tests have validated telehealth administration protocols; others do not
  • Test publishers have varied in their positions on remote administration
  • Psychologists must document any modifications to standardized procedures and note potential impacts on validity

Use of Psychological Tests in Immigration Proceedings

Assessment in immigration contexts presents unique ethical challenges, including the availability of appropriate norms, the use of interpreters, the potential for coerced evaluation, and the need to understand trauma-related presentations in culturally diverse populations. Psychologists must be especially careful about the cultural validity of instruments used in these settings.

Automated and AI-Assisted Interpretation

The increasing availability of computer-generated interpretive reports raises ethical questions about the role of the psychologist in the interpretive process. Standard 9.09 makes clear that the psychologist remains responsible for the accuracy and appropriateness of any automated interpretation, regardless of who or what generated it.

Commonly Tested EPPP Topics

Test data vs. test materials: Test data = client responses and scores (releasable with consent). Test materials = copyrighted items and manuals (protected). This is the most frequently tested distinction in assessment ethics.
Informed consent exceptions: Consent is not required when testing is (a) mandated by law, (b) a routine institutional activity, or (c) assessing decisional capacity. But notification of purpose is still required.
Outdated instruments: Using an obsolete version of a test (e.g., WAIS-R instead of WAIS-IV) violates Standard 9.08 unless there is a specific, defensible reason.
Cultural competence: The psychologist must consider norm appropriateness, language factors, and cultural context when interpreting results. Failure to do so violates Standard 9.06.
