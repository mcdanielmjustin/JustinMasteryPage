The Evidence-Based Practice Model

Evidence-based practice in psychology (EBPP) is defined by the APA (2006) as the integration of the best available research with clinical expertise in the context of patient characteristics, culture, and preferences. This three-legged stool model forms the conceptual foundation for treatment selection on the EPPP.

APA Definition (2006): "Evidence-based practice in psychology (EBPP) is the integration of the best available research with clinical expertise in the context of patient characteristics, culture, and preferences." This definition deliberately broadens the concept beyond simply applying manuals to include clinical judgment and individual factors.

The Three Components

  • Best available research evidence -- includes meta-analyses, RCTs, effectiveness studies, single-case designs, qualitative research, and clinical observation. The hierarchy typically places systematic reviews and RCTs at the top, but all levels of evidence contribute.
  • Clinical expertise -- refers to the competencies acquired through education, training, and experience, including assessment skill, diagnostic acumen, case formulation, treatment planning, therapeutic relationship management, and self-reflection.
  • Patient characteristics, culture, and preferences -- encompasses the client's values, goals, religious beliefs, worldview, cultural identity, socioeconomic context, readiness for change, social support, and treatment preferences.

EBPP vs. ESTs

Empirically Supported Treatments (ESTs) are specific psychological interventions that have demonstrated efficacy for specific disorders through controlled research. ESTs are one component of EBPP -- they represent the "best available research" leg of the stool. EBPP is the broader framework for clinical decision-making.

EPPP Distinction: ESTs = specific treatments validated by research. EBPP = the process of integrating research, clinical expertise, and patient factors. The EPPP expects you to know both concepts and recognize that EBPP is broader than simply applying ESTs.

Treatment Planning

Effective treatment planning integrates diagnostic assessment, case formulation, treatment selection, and ongoing monitoring. Key principles include:

Case Formulation

A case formulation is a hypothesis about the causes, precipitants, and maintaining factors of an individual's psychological problems. It goes beyond diagnosis to provide a conceptual framework for treatment. Components typically include:

  • Presenting problems and their functional impact
  • Predisposing factors (e.g., developmental history, genetics, early learning)
  • Precipitating factors (e.g., recent stressors, life transitions)
  • Perpetuating factors (e.g., avoidance patterns, cognitive distortions, environmental reinforcement)
  • Protective factors and strengths

Matching Treatment to Client

Beutler and colleagues developed the Systematic Treatment Selection (STS) model, which identifies patient dimensions that predict differential response to specific treatment characteristics:

  • Problem complexity / chronicity: More complex or chronic problems may require longer-term, insight-oriented approaches
  • Coping style: Internalizers (high self-blame) respond better to insight-oriented treatments; externalizers respond better to symptom-focused, skill-building approaches
  • Resistance level / reactance: Highly resistant clients respond better to nondirective approaches; low-resistance clients respond well to directive approaches
  • Subjective distress: Moderate distress levels optimize treatment engagement; very low or very high distress may impede progress

Stages of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente)

The Transtheoretical Model suggests that interventions should match the client's readiness for change:

  • Precontemplation: Consciousness-raising, motivational interviewing
  • Contemplation: Decisional balance, exploring ambivalence
  • Preparation: Goal setting, commitment enhancement
  • Action: Behavioral strategies, skill building, contingency management
  • Maintenance: Relapse prevention, stimulus control, social support

Common Factors and the Therapeutic Alliance

Research consistently shows that common factors account for a substantial proportion of therapy outcome variance -- often cited as more predictive than specific technique factors. Key common factors include:

The Therapeutic Alliance

Bordin's (1979) pantheoretical model defines the alliance as consisting of three components:

  1. Agreement on goals: Therapist and client share an understanding of the targets of treatment
  2. Agreement on tasks: Both parties agree on the activities and methods used in therapy
  3. The relational bond: Mutual trust, respect, liking, and attachment between therapist and client
Meta-analytic findings: The alliance-outcome correlation is approximately r = .28 (Horvath et al., 2011), accounting for roughly 7-8% of outcome variance. While this is a modest effect size, the alliance is one of the strongest and most consistent predictors of treatment outcome across orientations.

Other Common Factors

  • Empathy: The therapist's ability to understand the client's experience (r = .28 with outcome)
  • Positive regard / warmth: Unconditional acceptance of the client
  • Congruence / genuineness: The therapist's authenticity in the relationship
  • Client expectations (placebo/hope): The client's belief that therapy will help
  • Extratherapeutic factors: Client variables and life events outside of therapy -- Lambert (1992) estimated these account for approximately 40% of outcome variance

Lambert's Outcome Factors

Lambert (1992) proposed the following estimated contributions to therapeutic outcome:

EPPP Note: These percentages are frequently tested. Remember that Lambert's model suggests technique-specific factors account for only about 15% of outcome variance, while extratherapeutic factors contribute the most at 40%. This supports the "common factors" perspective, though it does not negate the importance of ESTs.

Outcome Monitoring

Routine outcome monitoring (ROM) refers to the systematic tracking of client progress during treatment using standardized measures. Research demonstrates that monitoring outcomes and providing feedback to clinicians improves results, particularly for clients who are not responding well (not-on-track clients).

Key Outcome Monitoring Instruments

  • Outcome Questionnaire-45 (OQ-45): Lambert's 45-item self-report measure assessing symptom distress, interpersonal relations, and social role functioning. Most extensively researched feedback tool.
  • Session Rating Scale (SRS) and Outcome Rating Scale (ORS): Miller and Duncan's ultra-brief (4-item each) measures of the therapeutic alliance and client functioning
  • Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9): 9-item depression severity measure commonly used in integrated care settings
  • GAD-7: 7-item measure of generalized anxiety severity
  • PCOMS (Partners for Change Outcome Management System): An integrated feedback system using the ORS and SRS

The Dose-Effect Model

Howard et al. (1986) proposed the dose-effect model, which suggests a log-linear relationship between the number of therapy sessions and improvement. Key findings:

  • Approximately 50% of clients show clinically significant improvement by session 8
  • Approximately 75% improve by session 26
  • There are diminishing returns with additional sessions, though some clients require longer treatment
  • The rate of improvement is fastest in the earliest sessions (negatively accelerating curve)

Feedback-Informed Treatment

Lambert and colleagues demonstrated that providing therapists with feedback about client progress (via the OQ-45) improved outcomes for not-on-track clients by 50-100% compared to treatment-as-usual. This approach has been termed feedback-informed treatment (FIT).

The Dodo Bird Verdict and Its Critics

The Dodo bird verdict (from Rosenzweig, 1936, and later Luborsky et al., 1975) holds that all bona fide psychotherapies produce roughly equivalent outcomes -- "everybody has won, and all must have prizes." Supporting and critiquing evidence includes:

  • Supporting evidence: Meta-analyses by Wampold (2001) found minimal differences between active treatments when researcher allegiance and other confounds are controlled
  • Opposing evidence: For specific disorders, some treatments show clear superiority (e.g., ERP for OCD, exposure therapy for specific phobias, behavioral treatments for autism spectrum disorder)
  • Middle ground: Most researchers now accept that common factors are powerful, but specific techniques add incremental value for certain conditions
EPPP Exam Tip: The exam may present scenarios asking you to evaluate whether one treatment is superior to another. Remember: across disorders, differences between bona fide treatments are often small, but for specific conditions (especially anxiety disorders), behavioral and exposure-based treatments have demonstrated superiority.

Cultural Considerations in Treatment Selection

EBPP requires that treatment selection account for patient cultural context. Key considerations include:

  • Cultural adaptation of ESTs: Research supports that culturally adapted treatments (those modified to account for the client's language, cultural values, and worldview) are more effective for ethnic minority clients than unadapted versions (Griner & Smith, 2006, d = 0.45)
  • Therapist cultural competence: The therapist's ability to understand and work within the client's cultural framework enhances the therapeutic alliance and improves outcomes
  • Client treatment preferences: Racial and ethnic minority clients may have preferences for certain treatment approaches or may be less likely to engage with treatments perceived as culturally incongruent
  • Access and retention: Structural barriers (language, transportation, insurance, stigma) disproportionately affect minority populations and must be considered in treatment planning
EPPP Note: The EBPP model explicitly includes patient characteristics, culture, and preferences as a co-equal component alongside research evidence. This means that the "best" treatment is not simply the most researched one -- it is the one that integrates the strongest evidence with the specific client's context and values.

Prescriptive Matching and Treatment Resistance

Research on prescriptive treatment matching has identified several principles for improving outcomes with treatment-resistant clients:

  • Motivational enhancement: For clients low in motivation, beginning with motivational interviewing (MI) before introducing structured treatments improves engagement and outcomes
  • Stepped care: Starting with less intensive interventions and stepping up to more intensive treatments as needed is both cost-effective and clinically sound
  • Alliance rupture repair: Safran and Muran (2000) demonstrated that identifying and repairing alliance ruptures (confrontation ruptures and withdrawal ruptures) is associated with improved outcomes, particularly with difficult-to-engage clients
  • Therapist effects: Some therapists consistently achieve better outcomes than others. Therapist effects account for approximately 5-8% of outcome variance, comparable to or exceeding the contribution of specific techniques
