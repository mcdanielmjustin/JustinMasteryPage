Overview of Clinical and Neuropsychological Tests

This chapter covers specialized clinical instruments and neuropsychological test batteries that are commonly tested on the EPPP. Unlike the broad cognitive measures (Wechsler, Stanford-Binet) or personality inventories (MMPI, NEO), these instruments target specific clinical functions such as visual-motor integration, cognitive screening, executive functioning, and comprehensive neuropsychological assessment.

EPPP Focus: For neuropsychological tests, the exam typically asks about what each test measures, the populations for which it is used, and key scoring or interpretation features. You are less likely to be asked about specific administration details.

Bender Visual Motor Gestalt Test

The Bender Visual Motor Gestalt Test (commonly called the Bender-Gestalt) was originally developed by Lauretta Bender in 1938 based on Gestalt psychology principles from Max Wertheimer. It assesses visual-motor integration by asking examinees to copy geometric designs.

Test Description

  • The original version presents 9 geometric designs one at a time; the examinee copies each design onto blank paper
  • The Bender-Gestalt II (Brannigan & Decker, 2003) updated the instrument with 16 designs, modern norms (ages 4-85+), and supplemental tests (motor and perception tests)
  • Administration takes approximately 5-10 minutes for the copy phase

Scoring Systems

  • Koppitz Developmental Scoring System: Used primarily with children ages 5-11; scores 30 possible errors across categories of distortion, rotation, integration, and perseveration
  • Lacks Scoring System: Designed for adults; identifies 12 essential discriminators of organic brain impairment
  • Bender-Gestalt II Global Scoring System: Uses a 0-4 rating for each design based on overall quality of reproduction

Clinical Uses

  • Screening for visual-motor integration deficits
  • Screening for possible neurological impairment (though sensitivity/specificity is limited)
  • Developmental assessment in children
  • A recall phase (redrawing from memory) can assess visual memory
EPPP Key Point: The Bender-Gestalt is a screening instrument, not a diagnostic tool. Errors such as rotation, fragmentation, perseveration, and collision of figures may suggest neurological impairment but require follow-up with comprehensive neuropsychological testing.

Cognitive Screening Instruments: MMSE and MoCA

Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE)

The MMSE was developed by Folstein, Folstein, and McHugh (1975) as a brief screening measure for cognitive impairment, particularly dementia. Key features:

  • Total score range: 0-30 points
  • Assesses orientation (time and place), registration, attention and calculation, recall, and language
  • Traditional cutoff: score of 23/24 suggests possible cognitive impairment
  • Administration time: approximately 5-10 minutes
MMSE Domains and Point Values: Orientation to time (5), orientation to place (5), registration (3), attention/calculation -- serial 7s or WORLD backwards (5), recall (3), naming (2), repetition (1), 3-stage command (3), reading (1), writing (1), copying (1).

Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA)

The MoCA (Nasreddine et al., 2005) was developed to address the MMSE's limitations in detecting mild cognitive impairment (MCI). It is more sensitive to early cognitive decline than the MMSE.

  • Total score range: 0-30 points (cutoff of 26 for normal cognition)
  • Assesses visuospatial/executive, naming, memory, attention, language, abstraction, delayed recall, and orientation
  • Includes executive function tasks (Trail Making B adaptation, clock drawing) that the MMSE lacks
  • Better ceiling effect performance: more sensitive to MCI because the MMSE shows ceiling effects in educated individuals
EPPP Comparison: The MoCA is more sensitive to mild cognitive impairment than the MMSE because it includes executive function tasks and has a higher ceiling. The MMSE has a well-known education bias (higher-educated individuals score artificially high).

Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST)

The WCST (Berg, 1948; Heaton et al., 1993) is the gold standard measure of executive functioning, specifically cognitive flexibility and set-shifting. It is highly sensitive to frontal lobe dysfunction.

Administration

  • The examinee sorts cards according to an unknown rule (color, form, or number)
  • After each sort, the examiner indicates "right" or "wrong" -- the examinee must infer the sorting rule from feedback
  • After 10 consecutive correct sorts, the rule changes without warning, and the examinee must shift to the new rule
  • The test continues until 6 categories are completed or all 128 cards are used

Key Scores

  • Categories completed: Number of sorting categories successfully identified (max = 6)
  • Perseverative errors: Continuing to sort by a previously correct but now incorrect rule -- the most clinically significant score, sensitive to frontal lobe dysfunction
  • Perseverative responses: Total responses matching a previously reinforced category
  • Failure to maintain set: Making an error after 5+ consecutive correct sorts within a category (suggests attentional lapses)
Primary Measure: Perseverative errors are the most clinically meaningful score on the WCST. Elevated perseverative errors are associated with dorsolateral prefrontal cortex dysfunction and indicate difficulty with cognitive flexibility and set-shifting.

Trail Making Test (TMT)

The Trail Making Test is one of the most widely used neuropsychological instruments, included in nearly all neuropsychological batteries. It consists of two parts:

Part A (Trails A)

  • The examinee draws a line connecting numbered circles (1-2-3-4...25) in sequential order as quickly as possible
  • Primarily measures processing speed, visual scanning, and simple sequencing
  • Score = time to completion in seconds

Part B (Trails B)

  • The examinee alternates between numbers and letters (1-A-2-B-3-C...13)
  • Measures cognitive flexibility, set-shifting, and divided attention in addition to processing speed
  • Score = time to completion in seconds
  • Much more sensitive to executive dysfunction than Part A
EPPP Interpretation: A large discrepancy between Trails A and B performance (with B being disproportionately slow) suggests executive dysfunction beyond simple slowing. If both A and B are slow proportionally, the difficulty is more likely due to processing speed or motor impairment rather than executive dysfunction specifically.

Halstead-Reitan Neuropsychological Battery (HRB)

The Halstead-Reitan Battery was developed by Ward Halstead and later refined by Ralph Reitan. It is one of the two major comprehensive fixed neuropsychological batteries (along with the Luria-Nebraska).

Core Tests

  • Category Test: Measures abstract reasoning and concept formation -- the examinee identifies a common principle underlying groups of stimuli (208 stimuli, 7 subtests). Most sensitive test in the battery to brain damage.
  • Tactual Performance Test (TPT): Blindfolded examinee places blocks into a form board using dominant hand, non-dominant hand, then both hands; followed by memory and localization drawings. Measures tactile perception, psychomotor speed, spatial memory.
  • Speech Sounds Perception Test: Examinee matches spoken nonsense syllables to printed options. Measures auditory-verbal perception and sustained attention.
  • Seashore Rhythm Test: Examinee identifies whether pairs of rhythmic patterns are the same or different. Measures nonverbal auditory perception and attention.
  • Finger Oscillation Test (Finger Tapping): Measures motor speed for each hand separately.
  • Trail Making Test (Parts A and B): Often considered part of the HRB battery.

The Halstead Impairment Index

The Impairment Index is the proportion of tests (out of 7 core measures) on which the examinee scores in the impaired range. A score above 0.4 (i.e., impaired on more than 40% of tests) suggests brain dysfunction.

Interpretive Approaches

  • Level of performance: Comparing scores to normative cutoffs
  • Pattern of performance: Examining the profile of strengths and weaknesses across tests
  • Right-left comparisons: Comparing performance between the two sides of the body (lateralization)
  • Pathognomonic signs: Specific indicators (e.g., dysnomia, sensory suppression) that are strongly associated with brain dysfunction
EPPP Note: The Halstead-Reitan uses a fixed battery approach (same tests for all patients). This contrasts with the flexible or hypothesis-testing approach favored by some neuropsychologists. The fixed approach ensures comprehensive coverage; the flexible approach tailors the battery to the referral question.

Luria-Nebraska Neuropsychological Battery (LNNB)

The LNNB was developed by Golden, Purisch, and Hammeke (1985) based on A. R. Luria's qualitative neuropsychological assessment procedures. It was designed to standardize and quantify Luria's methods.

Structure

  • 269 items organized into 11 clinical scales and 3 summary scales
  • Clinical scales: Motor, Rhythm, Tactile, Visual, Receptive Speech, Expressive Speech, Writing, Reading, Arithmetic, Memory, Intellectual Processes
  • Summary scales: Pathognomonic (items most sensitive to brain damage), Left Hemisphere, Right Hemisphere
  • Administration time: approximately 1.5-2.5 hours (shorter than the Halstead-Reitan)

Scoring

  • Items scored 0 (normal), 1 (borderline), or 2 (impaired)
  • The critical level is determined based on the examinee's age and education -- scale scores above this level suggest impairment
  • Localization scales (Left Hemisphere, Right Hemisphere) help identify lateralized dysfunction
HRB vs. LNNB: The HRB has a longer history and more extensive validation. The LNNB is shorter and based on Luria's qualitative tradition. Both are fixed batteries. The HRB is generally considered more thoroughly validated, but the LNNB may be preferred when time is limited.

Rey Complex Figure Test (RCFT)

The Rey Complex Figure Test (Rey, 1941; Osterrieth, 1944) assesses visuospatial constructional ability and visual memory. It involves copying a complex geometric figure and then reproducing it from memory.

Administration

  1. Copy trial: The examinee copies the complex figure while looking at it (no time limit)
  2. Immediate recall: After a brief delay (approximately 3 minutes), the examinee draws the figure from memory
  3. Delayed recall: After approximately 30 minutes, the examinee draws the figure again from memory
  4. Optional: a recognition trial may also be administered

Scoring

  • The figure is divided into 18 scorable elements
  • Each element is scored 0-2 based on accuracy and placement
  • Maximum score = 36 for each trial
  • Clinicians also assess the organizational strategy (e.g., does the examinee begin with the overall structure or with details?)

Clinical Significance

  • Copy performance: Impaired copy suggests visuospatial constructional deficits (right hemisphere, parietal lobe dysfunction)
  • Memory performance: Impaired recall with intact copy suggests visual memory deficits (right temporal or hippocampal dysfunction)
  • Organizational approach: A piecemeal or fragmented approach to copying (focusing on details rather than the global structure) is associated with executive dysfunction and may also predict poor recall
EPPP Tip: The key clinical distinction on the RCFT is between the copy and recall phases. Intact copy with poor recall = memory problem. Poor copy with poor recall = constructional deficit (memory cannot be fairly assessed). Good organizational strategy during copy predicts better recall.
