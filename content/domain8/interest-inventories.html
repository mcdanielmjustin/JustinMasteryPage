Interest Inventories

Strong, Kuder, Holland themes, career assessment
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of Interest Inventories

Interest inventories are assessment instruments designed to measure an individual's preferences for various activities, occupations, and work environments. They are primarily used in career counseling and vocational guidance. Unlike aptitude or ability tests, interest inventories do not measure what a person can do, but rather what they prefer to do. For the EPPP, you should know the major instruments, their theoretical foundations, and the specific scales and scores they produce.

Interests vs. Aptitudes: Interests refer to what activities a person finds enjoyable or appealing. Aptitudes refer to the capacity to learn or perform a skill. A person may have strong interest in an area without corresponding aptitude (and vice versa). Comprehensive career assessment typically evaluates both.

Holland's RIASEC Model

John Holland's theory of vocational personalities and work environments is the most widely used framework for understanding vocational interests. Holland proposed that both people and work environments can be classified into six types, forming the RIASEC hexagon:

TypeDescriptionExample Occupations
Realistic (R)Prefers hands-on, physical, mechanical, or outdoor activities; values practical solutionsEngineer, mechanic, carpenter, farmer
Investigative (I)Prefers analytical, intellectual, scientific activities; values research and inquiryScientist, physician, mathematician, researcher
Artistic (A)Prefers creative, unstructured, expressive activities; values aesthetics and imaginationArtist, musician, writer, actor, graphic designer
Social (S)Prefers helping, teaching, counseling, and cooperative activities; values interpersonal relationshipsTeacher, counselor, social worker, nurse
Enterprising (E)Prefers leading, persuading, managing, and selling activities; values influence and statusManager, salesperson, lawyer, politician
Conventional (C)Prefers structured, organized, data-management activities; values accuracy and orderAccountant, banker, clerk, office manager

Key Principles of Holland's Theory

  • Congruence: People are most satisfied and productive when their personality type matches their work environment (e.g., an Artistic person in an Artistic job)
  • Consistency: Some type combinations are more related to each other (adjacent on the hexagon, e.g., RI or SE) than others (opposite, e.g., RA or SC). Consistent profiles are more stable and predictable.
  • Differentiation: A person who strongly resembles one type and little resembles others is well-differentiated. A flat profile (similar scores across all types) is undifferentiated and harder to interpret.
  • Identity: The clarity and stability of a person's goals, interests, and talents
  • Calculus: The hexagonal arrangement reflects the relationships among the types -- adjacent types are most similar; opposite types are most different
EPPP Key Point: The hexagonal model arranges types so that adjacent types (e.g., R-I, I-A, A-S, S-E, E-C, C-R) are most similar, and opposite types (R-S, I-E, A-C) are most dissimilar. Congruence predicts job satisfaction, stability, and achievement.

Strong Interest Inventory (SII)

The Strong Interest Inventory is the most widely used and researched interest inventory. Originally developed by E. K. Strong Jr. in 1927, the current version was revised in 2012 and is published by CPP (now The Myers-Briggs Company). It uses Holland's RIASEC framework as its organizing structure.

Three Levels of Scales

1. General Occupational Themes (GOTs)

The six GOTs correspond directly to Holland's RIASEC types. They provide the broadest level of information about an individual's interests.

  • Scores are reported as standard scores with a mean of 50 and SD of 10
  • Scores are compared to a combined-gender or same-gender reference group
  • The GOT profile provides the Holland code (e.g., ISA, SEC) used to identify congruent occupations

2. Basic Interest Scales (BIS)

The 30 Basic Interest Scales provide more specific information within each GOT. For example, within the Investigative theme, separate BIS scores are provided for Science, Mathematics, and Medical Science.

  • BIS scores help differentiate within a broad interest area
  • Also reported as standard scores (M = 50, SD = 10)
  • Useful for identifying specific activity clusters within a general theme

3. Occupational Scales (OS)

The 260 Occupational Scales are the most specific level. Each scale was developed using the criterion keying (empirical method) approach -- comparing the item responses of individuals employed in a specific occupation with those of a general reference sample.

  • A high score on an occupational scale indicates that the examinee's pattern of interests is similar to that of satisfied workers in that occupation
  • Scores are reported as standard scores (M = 50, SD = 10) for each occupation
  • Separate male and female norms are available for each occupational scale

Personal Style Scales

The SII also includes five Personal Style Scales that assess preferences in work style:

  • Work Style (prefers working with people vs. ideas/data/things)
  • Learning Environment (prefers academic/practical learning)
  • Leadership Style (prefers leading by example vs. directing others)
  • Risk Taking (prefers adventure and risk vs. caution and safety)
  • Team Orientation (prefers working as part of a team vs. independently)
EPPP Note: The Strong is constructed primarily through empirical criterion keying -- the Occupational Scales were developed by comparing responses of satisfied workers to a general reference group. This is the same construction method used for the MMPI. The GOTs and BIS use homogeneous item clustering (items that intercorrelate).

Self-Directed Search (SDS)

The Self-Directed Search was developed by John Holland himself as a self-administered, self-scored, and self-interpreted career exploration tool. It is the most direct operationalization of Holland's RIASEC theory.

Structure

  • The SDS consists of four sections: Activities (liked/disliked), Competencies (can/cannot do), Occupations (appealing/not), and Self-Estimates (self-rated abilities)
  • Each section yields scores for all six RIASEC types
  • The three highest scores form the individual's three-letter Holland code (called the Summary Code)
  • The code is then used with the Occupations Finder or Dictionary of Holland Occupational Codes to identify matching occupations

Strengths and Limitations

  • Strengths: Self-administered and quick (20-30 minutes); cost-effective for group use; strong theoretical foundation; well-validated
  • Limitations: Less detailed than the Strong; self-scoring can lead to errors; relies heavily on self-estimates that may be biased; less useful for individuals with undifferentiated profiles

Kuder Career Search (KCS)

The Kuder Career Search (Zytowski, 2007) is the latest version of the Kuder interest inventories, which date back to Frederic Kuder's work in the 1930s. The Kuder approach differs from the Strong in its use of forced-choice triads.

Item Format

  • Each item presents three activities, and the examinee selects the most preferred and least preferred
  • This forced-choice format reduces the tendency toward acquiescence (saying "like" to everything) or social desirability
  • Scores are ipsative (within-person comparisons) rather than normative (compared to a reference group)

Activity Preference Scales

The KCS produces scores on six Activity Preference Scales that align with Holland's RIASEC themes, though Kuder uses different labels:

  • Nature (aligns with Realistic)
  • Science/Technical (aligns with Investigative)
  • Art/Communication (aligns with Artistic)
  • Social/Personal Services (aligns with Social)
  • Influence/Sales (aligns with Enterprising)
  • Organization/Management (aligns with Conventional)

Person Match

A unique feature of the KCS is the Person Match system, which compares the examinee's interest profile to those of satisfied individuals in various occupations. This is similar in concept to the Strong's Occupational Scales but uses a different matching algorithm.

Ipsative vs. Normative Scoring: Ipsative scores compare an individual's interests to their own other interests (within-person). Normative scores compare to a reference group (between-person). Ipsative scores from forced-choice instruments cannot be directly compared across individuals. This is a key limitation of the Kuder's forced-choice format.

Other Notable Interest Instruments

Career Assessment Inventory (CAI)

Developed by Charles Johansson, the CAI is structured similarly to the Strong but focuses on non-professional and skilled/technical occupations that typically require two years or less of postsecondary training. It fills a gap because the Strong focuses primarily on professional occupations requiring college education.

Jackson Vocational Interest Survey (JVIS)

Developed by Douglas Jackson using a construct validation approach (rather than empirical keying). It measures 34 basic interest scales organized into themes. It is notable for its strong psychometric properties and theoretical grounding.

Campbell Interest and Skill Survey (CISS)

Developed by David Campbell (who was also a key developer of the Strong), the CISS uniquely measures both interests and self-reported skills across the same domains. This allows for four quadrants of interpretation: Pursue (high interest, high skill), Develop (high interest, low skill), Explore (low interest, high skill), Avoid (low interest, low skill).

Psychometric Considerations

Validity of Interest Inventories

  • Concurrent validity: Individuals in a given occupation tend to score highest on the corresponding scale
  • Predictive validity: Interest scores measured in adolescence or young adulthood predict occupational choice years later at moderate levels (r ~ .30-.40)
  • Hit rates: The Strong correctly predicts occupational group membership in approximately 50% of cases over long follow-up periods

Stability of Interests

Interests are relatively stable over time, particularly after age 25. Test-retest reliabilities for the Strong GOTs range from .80-.92 over periods of 1-5 years. Interests are less stable in adolescence and early adulthood.

Gender Differences

Gender differences in vocational interests are among the largest in psychology. Meta-analyses show substantial differences on Realistic (males higher) and Social (females higher) dimensions, with smaller differences on other RIASEC types. The Strong addresses this by providing both same-sex and combined-sex norms.

EPPP Note: The use of same-sex vs. combined-sex norms on interest inventories is an important consideration. Same-sex norms compare the individual to their own gender group; combined norms compare to both genders. Using same-sex norms can expand the range of suggested occupations for an individual, particularly when gender-atypical interests are present.

Using Interest Inventories in Practice

Integrating Results in Career Counseling

Interest inventories are most useful when combined with other sources of information in a comprehensive career counseling process:

  • Interests + abilities: High interest without corresponding aptitude may lead to frustration; high aptitude without interest may lead to dissatisfaction. Comprehensive career assessment evaluates both.
  • Interests + values: Work values (e.g., autonomy, financial security, altruism) add a dimension beyond what interest inventories measure. Instruments like the Work Values Inventory can supplement interest results.
  • Interests + personality: Personality measures (e.g., NEO-PI-R, MBTI) can help explain why certain interests exist and how they might manifest in different work environments.
  • Interests + developmental context: Consider the client's age, life stage, career development stage (Super's Life-Span model), and prior work experiences when interpreting interest profiles.

Interests Across the Lifespan

Super's Life-Span, Life-Space theory recognizes that career development occurs across the lifespan and is influenced by changing roles and life circumstances. Interest inventories are most commonly used during the Exploration stage (ages 15-24) and at career transition points. However, interests themselves demonstrate remarkable stability -- among the most stable of all individual difference constructs after age 25.

Computer-Based and Online Inventories

Most major interest inventories are now available in computer-administered formats. The O*NET Interest Profiler is a free, government-developed interest inventory based on Holland's RIASEC model and linked to the extensive O*NET occupational database. It provides a convenient, no-cost alternative to commercial instruments, though it lacks the depth of the Strong or CISS.

Commonly Tested EPPP Summary

Holland's RIASEC: Realistic-Investigative-Artistic-Social-Enterprising-Conventional. Adjacent types most similar, opposite types most different. Congruence = person-environment match.
Strong Interest Inventory: Three scale levels -- GOTs (6 RIASEC themes), BIS (30 specific interest areas), and Occupational Scales (260 scales, criterion-keyed). Also includes Personal Style Scales.
SDS: Holland's self-administered instrument. Produces a three-letter Holland code. Self-scored. Most direct application of RIASEC theory.
Kuder: Forced-choice triads produce ipsative scores. Person Match feature. Ipsative scores cannot be compared across individuals.
Key distinction: The Strong uses empirical criterion keying (Occupational Scales) and homogeneous keying (GOTs, BIS). The SDS is based purely on Holland's theory. The Kuder uses forced-choice format producing ipsative scores.
