Overview of Alternative Cognitive Measures

While the Wechsler scales and Stanford-Binet are the most widely used individual intelligence tests, several alternative instruments serve important roles in cognitive assessment. These instruments are particularly valuable for populations where language, motor ability, or cultural factors may affect performance on traditional IQ tests. For the EPPP, you should know the structure, theoretical basis, and appropriate populations for each instrument.

When to Use Alternatives: Alternative cognitive measures are typically selected when the examinee has limited English proficiency, a motor or sensory disability, a cultural background that makes traditional verbally-loaded tests inappropriate, or when a brief screening (rather than comprehensive assessment) is needed.

Kaufman Assessment Battery for Children, Second Edition (KABC-II)

The KABC-II (Kaufman & Kaufman, 2004) is an individually administered intelligence test for children ages 3-18. It is unique in offering two different theoretical models for interpretation.

Dual Theoretical Model

The KABC-II can be interpreted using either of two frameworks:

  • Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) model: The default interpretive framework; yields a Fluid-Crystallized Index (FCI) as the global score. Includes five CHC broad abilities: Gc (Crystallized), Gf (Fluid), Gsm (Short-Term Memory), Gv (Visual Processing), Glr (Long-Term Storage and Retrieval).
  • Luria model: Based on Luria's neuropsychological theory; yields a Mental Processing Index (MPI) that deliberately excludes measures of acquired knowledge (crystallized ability). The Luria model includes Sequential Processing, Simultaneous Processing, Learning, and Planning.

When to Use the Luria Model

The Luria/MPI model is recommended when the examiner wants to minimize the influence of language and crystallized knowledge on the global score. This is particularly appropriate for:

  • Children from non-mainstream cultural or linguistic backgrounds
  • English Language Learners (ELLs)
  • Children whose educational opportunities have been limited
  • Any situation where acquired knowledge may not represent the child's cognitive potential
KABC-II Global Scores: FCI (CHC model) = Fluid-Crystallized Index, includes all 5 scales. MPI (Luria model) = Mental Processing Index, excludes the Knowledge/Crystallized scale. Both use M = 100, SD = 15.

Kaufman Brief Intelligence Test, Second Edition (KBIT-2)

The KBIT-2 (Kaufman & Kaufman, 2004) is a brief screening measure of verbal and nonverbal intelligence for ages 4-90. It yields three scores:

  • Verbal Score (Riddles and Verbal Knowledge subtests)
  • Nonverbal Score (Matrices subtest)
  • IQ Composite (M = 100, SD = 15)

The KBIT-2 takes approximately 15-30 minutes and is used for screening purposes. It is not a substitute for a comprehensive intelligence test.

Raven's Progressive Matrices

Raven's Progressive Matrices (Raven, 1938, with multiple subsequent editions) is one of the most widely used nonverbal measures of fluid intelligence (Gf) and abstract reasoning. It is considered the prototypical measure of Spearman's g factor.

Versions

  • Standard Progressive Matrices (SPM): 60 items in 5 sets (A through E) of increasing difficulty. For ages 6 to adult. Most widely used version.
  • Coloured Progressive Matrices (CPM): Easier version for children ages 5-11 and individuals with cognitive impairment. Uses colored backgrounds to maintain attention.
  • Advanced Progressive Matrices (APM): More difficult version for above-average adults and adolescents.

Test Description

Each item presents a visual pattern (matrix) with a missing piece. The examinee selects the correct piece from multiple options. Items progress from simple pattern completion to complex abstract reasoning involving multiple simultaneous rules.

Strengths and Limitations

  • Strengths: Minimal verbal demands; can be administered to non-English speakers; strong measure of Gf and g; widely researched cross-culturally; brief administration
  • Limitations: Measures only one narrow facet of intelligence (nonverbal reasoning); no verbal, memory, or processing speed components; not a substitute for comprehensive cognitive assessment; still shows cultural effects despite being "nonverbal"
EPPP Key Point: Raven's matrices is the best single measure of Spearman's g and fluid intelligence. However, "nonverbal" does not mean "culture-free" -- research shows that cultural factors (familiarity with multiple-choice format, exposure to abstract visual patterns, test-taking attitudes) still influence performance.

Peabody Picture Vocabulary Test (PPVT-5)

The PPVT-5 (Dunn, 2019) measures receptive vocabulary, which is used as an estimate of verbal ability and scholastic aptitude. It requires no reading, writing, or oral responses.

Administration

  • The examiner says a word and the examinee selects the picture (from four options) that best represents that word
  • Ages 2:6 through 90+
  • Takes approximately 10-15 minutes
  • Yields a standard score (M = 100, SD = 15), percentile rank, stanine, and age equivalent

Uses and Limitations

  • Screening for verbal ability, particularly when the examinee cannot produce oral responses (e.g., autism, selective mutism, severe speech impairment)
  • Often paired with the Expressive Vocabulary Test (EVT-3) to compare receptive and expressive language abilities
  • Not a measure of overall intelligence -- it assesses only receptive vocabulary (a narrow aspect of crystallized ability)
  • Significantly influenced by language exposure and educational opportunity
EPPP Note: The PPVT is a measure of receptive vocabulary only, not overall intelligence. Using it as a sole estimate of IQ would overrepresent crystallized verbal ability and miss fluid reasoning, processing speed, working memory, and visual-spatial abilities.

Leiter International Performance Scale, Third Edition (Leiter-3)

The Leiter-3 (Roid, Miller, Pomplun, & Koch, 2013) is a completely nonverbal intelligence test that requires no verbal instructions from the examiner and no verbal responses from the examinee. It uses pantomime and gestural instructions.

Structure

  • Ages 3-75+
  • Two batteries: Cognitive Battery and Attention/Memory Battery
  • Cognitive Battery subtests: Figure Ground, Form Completion, Classification/Analogies, Sequential Order -- yield a nonverbal IQ (M = 100, SD = 15)
  • Attention/Memory Battery: Attention Sustained, Forward Memory, Reverse Memory, Nonverbal Stroop -- yield attention and memory composites

Appropriate Populations

  • Individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing
  • Non-English speakers and English Language Learners
  • Individuals with autism spectrum disorder who have limited verbal abilities
  • Individuals with motor speech disorders
  • Anyone for whom a completely nonverbal assessment is preferred
Leiter-3 vs. Raven's: Both are nonverbal, but the Leiter-3 is more comprehensive, measuring multiple cognitive abilities (classification, sequential reasoning, memory, attention) rather than just matrix reasoning. The Leiter also uses nonverbal administration instructions, making it appropriate when the examinee cannot understand verbal directions.

Cognitive Abilities Test (CogAT)

The CogAT (Lohman & Hagen, 2001) is a group-administered test of cognitive abilities used primarily in educational settings for identifying gifted students, predicting academic achievement, and informing instructional decisions.

Structure

  • Three batteries: Verbal, Quantitative, and Nonverbal
  • Yields a composite score and separate battery scores
  • Available for grades K-12
  • Group administration makes it efficient for school-wide screening

Score Profiles

The CogAT uses a unique ability profile system that describes the pattern of scores across the three batteries:

  • Profile level (A = above average, B = average, etc.)
  • Profile pattern (e.g., V+ indicates verbal strength; N- indicates nonverbal weakness)
  • Profiles are used to suggest differentiated instructional strategies

Culture-Fair and Culture-Free Testing

The concepts of culture-fair and culture-free testing represent different approaches to minimizing cultural bias in cognitive assessment:

Culture-Free Tests

Culture-free tests aim to be completely independent of cultural influence. This is now widely recognized as an impossible ideal because all cognitive tests are affected to some degree by the examinee's cultural background, educational opportunity, familiarity with testing situations, and test-taking strategies.

Culture-Fair Tests

Culture-fair tests (sometimes called "culture-reduced" tests) attempt to minimize cultural bias without claiming to eliminate it entirely. Strategies include:

  • Using nonverbal stimuli and response formats
  • Reducing language demands in both instructions and responses
  • Using abstract geometric or figural content rather than content tied to specific cultural knowledge
  • Providing practice items to ensure the examinee understands the task format
  • Developing norms that include representative samples of diverse populations

Cattell's Culture Fair Intelligence Test (CFIT)

Raymond Cattell developed the CFIT specifically to measure fluid intelligence while minimizing cultural influence. It uses four subtests: Series, Classifications, Matrices, and Conditions, all using abstract figures. Three difficulty levels (Scales 1, 2, 3) are available for different age ranges and ability levels.

EPPP Critical Point: No test is truly culture-free. Even nonverbal tests show group differences related to cultural factors such as familiarity with testing formats, experience with abstract visual patterns, and motivation to perform in testing situations. The appropriate term is "culture-reduced" or "culture-fair," and clinicians must still consider cultural context when interpreting results.

Nonverbal Assessment: Best Practices

When assessing individuals for whom standard verbal-loaded tests are inappropriate, clinicians should follow these guidelines:

  • Select instruments normed on populations that include the examinee's demographic group
  • Consider whether nonverbal instructions (as in the Leiter-3) are necessary, or whether nonverbal test content with verbal instructions (as in Raven's) is sufficient
  • Use multiple measures rather than a single nonverbal test to get a more comprehensive picture
  • Report results with appropriate caveats about the limitations of nonverbal assessment
  • Recognize that nonverbal IQ scores may underrepresent the abilities of verbally strong individuals
  • Avoid using a single nonverbal screening measure as a definitive measure of intellectual functioning

Commonly Tested EPPP Summary

KABC-II: Dual interpretive models. CHC model yields FCI (includes crystallized). Luria model yields MPI (excludes crystallized). Use Luria/MPI for culturally/linguistically diverse children.
Raven's: Prototypical measure of Spearman's g and fluid intelligence. Nonverbal but NOT culture-free.
PPVT: Receptive vocabulary only. Not a substitute for comprehensive IQ testing. Useful when examinee cannot produce oral responses.
Leiter-3: Completely nonverbal (administration and response). Best choice for deaf/hard of hearing or severely language-impaired individuals.
Culture-free vs. culture-fair: No test is culture-free. Culture-fair tests minimize but do not eliminate cultural influence. Always consider cultural context in interpretation.
