Freud and Classical Psychoanalysis

Sigmund Freud developed psychoanalysis in the late 19th century, creating the first comprehensive theory of personality and the first systematic psychotherapy. Psychoanalysis is based on the premise that unconscious conflicts, often originating in childhood, drive psychological symptoms and maladaptive behavior.

Structural Model of the Mind

Freud's structural model posits three mental structures: (1) Id -- operates on the pleasure principle; contains instinctual drives (Eros/life and Thanatos/death); entirely unconscious. (2) Ego -- operates on the reality principle; mediates between id impulses, superego demands, and external reality; spans conscious, preconscious, and unconscious. (3) Superego -- the moral component, incorporating parental and societal standards; includes the conscience (punitive) and the ego-ideal (aspirational).

Defense Mechanisms

Defense mechanisms are unconscious ego strategies for managing anxiety arising from conflicts between the id, superego, and reality. Key defenses include:

DenialRefusing to acknowledge external realityAn alcoholic insisting they have no drinking problem
ProjectionAttributing one's unacceptable feelings to others"I'm not angry at you -- you're angry at me!"

Defense Mechanism Hierarchy

Defenses range from immature/primitive (denial, projection, splitting) to mature (sublimation, humor, suppression). Sublimation is considered the most mature defense. Repression is considered the most fundamental defense -- the cornerstone of all other defenses. For the EPPP, be able to distinguish between similar defenses: projection vs. displacement, repression vs. suppression (suppression is conscious), rationalization vs. intellectualization.

Transference and Countertransference

Transference occurs when the client unconsciously redirects feelings and attitudes from past relationships (especially with parents) onto the therapist. The analysis of transference is considered the central vehicle of change in psychoanalysis. Countertransference refers to the therapist's emotional reactions to the client, which may reflect the therapist's own unresolved conflicts or provide clinically useful information about the client's interpersonal impact.

Key Psychoanalytic Techniques

  • Free association -- The patient says whatever comes to mind without censorship; the "fundamental rule" of psychoanalysis
  • Dream analysis -- Dreams are the "royal road to the unconscious"; manifest content (surface story) vs. latent content (hidden meaning)
  • Interpretation -- The analyst offers explanations of the patient's unconscious meanings
  • Analysis of resistance -- Exploring the patient's opposition to the therapeutic process (e.g., missing sessions, changing topics)
  • Working through -- Repeated examination of unconscious material to produce lasting change

Ego Psychology

Ego psychology shifted the emphasis from the id to the adaptive functions of the ego. Anna Freud systematized the defense mechanisms in The Ego and the Mechanisms of Defense (1936). Heinz Hartmann proposed the concept of the conflict-free ego sphere -- autonomous ego functions (perception, memory, learning) that operate independently of instinctual drives.

Key Ego Psychology Concepts

Ego autonomy -- The ego has its own energy source and can function independently of the id. Adaptation -- The ego's primary function is to facilitate adaptation to the environment. Erik Erikson extended ego psychology across the lifespan with his eight psychosocial stages, emphasizing social and cultural influences on ego development. Erikson's stages emphasize ego identity crises at each developmental period.

Object Relations Theory

Object relations theory focuses on how early relationships (especially with the mother) are internalized as mental representations ("objects") that shape personality and later relationships. The term "object" refers to significant people in the individual's life.

Melanie Klein

Klein proposed that infants experience two fundamental positions in relating to the mother:

  • Paranoid-schizoid position (first few months) -- The infant uses splitting, perceiving the mother as either all-good ("good breast") or all-bad ("bad breast"); characterized by persecutory anxiety
  • Depressive position (from ~6 months) -- The infant integrates good and bad aspects of the mother into a whole object; experiences guilt and concern for having directed aggression at the loved object

Donald Winnicott

Winnicott introduced several influential concepts:

  • Good-enough mother -- The caregiver who adequately (not perfectly) meets the infant's needs, gradually allowing manageable frustrations that promote development
  • Transitional object -- An object (e.g., a blanket, teddy bear) that represents the infant's first "not-me" possession, bridging the space between internal and external reality
  • True self vs. false self -- The true self emerges when the good-enough mother mirrors the infant's spontaneous gestures; the false self develops as a protective compliance when the mother substitutes her own gestures for the infant's
  • Holding environment -- The therapist provides a safe, empathic space analogous to the mother's holding of the infant

Clinical Application

A client with borderline personality features frequently sees the therapist as either "the best therapist ever" or "completely useless." This splitting reflects a failure to achieve Klein's depressive position -- the inability to integrate good and bad qualities into a coherent whole-object representation. Object relations therapy would help the client develop the capacity for ambivalence (holding contradictory feelings simultaneously).

Otto Kernberg

Otto Kernberg integrated object relations theory with ego psychology, creating a comprehensive framework for understanding personality pathology -- particularly borderline and narcissistic personality organization. Kernberg's central construct is the object relations unit: a mental structure consisting of a self-representation, an object-representation, and the affect linking them. These internalized units, formed through early relational experiences, become the building blocks of personality structure.

Three Levels of Personality Organization

Kernberg proposed that personality can be assessed along a structural continuum defined by three criteria: reality testing, identity integration, and predominant defense mechanisms.

Neurotic organization: Intact reality testing, integrated identity (stable and coherent sense of self and others), and reliance on mature/higher-level defenses (repression, rationalization, intellectualization). These individuals experience circumscribed conflicts but maintain a cohesive sense of who they are.

Borderline organization: Reality testing is generally intact but may falter under stress. The hallmark is identity diffusion -- a fragmented, contradictory sense of self and others. Predominant defenses are primitive: splitting, projective identification, idealization, devaluation, and omnipotent control.

Psychotic organization: Impaired reality testing, severe identity diffusion, and reliance on primitive defenses. The individual cannot reliably distinguish internal experience from external reality.

Transference-Focused Psychotherapy (TFP)

Kernberg developed Transference-Focused Psychotherapy (TFP), a manualized, psychodynamic treatment designed primarily for borderline personality disorder. TFP uses the therapeutic relationship as the primary vehicle of change, with particular emphasis on:

  • Confrontation -- Drawing the patient's attention to contradictory or split representations as they emerge in the transference (e.g., "Last week I was the best therapist; today I am useless. Can we look at what is happening?")
  • Interpretation of primitive defenses -- Specifically targeting splitting, projective identification, idealization, and devaluation as they manifest in the here-and-now of the therapeutic relationship
  • Integration of split self and object representations into more coherent, ambivalent wholes -- moving the patient from the paranoid-schizoid position toward the depressive position

Kernberg vs. Kohut: A Critical Distinction

Kernberg and Kohut offer contrasting approaches to narcissistic and borderline pathology, and this distinction is commonly tested on the EPPP. Kernberg views narcissistic and borderline pathology as rooted in aggression and primitive defenses; the therapist actively confronts and interprets defensive operations, particularly splitting and projective identification. Kohut, in contrast, views the same pathology as resulting from empathic failures in development; the therapist provides empathic mirroring and functions as a selfobject, allowing the client to resume arrested development through transmuting internalization. In short: Kernberg confronts defenses; Kohut provides empathic attunement.

Self Psychology (Kohut)

Heinz Kohut developed self psychology, focusing on the development and maintenance of a cohesive sense of self. Kohut argued that narcissistic needs are normal developmental requirements, not pathological. Psychopathology arises from empathic failures by caregivers.

Selfobject Needs

Kohut identified three fundamental selfobject needs -- needs for others to serve psychological functions for the self:

  • Mirroring -- The need to be validated, admired, and recognized ("You are wonderful")
  • Idealization -- The need to look up to and merge with an admired, powerful other ("You are wonderful and I am part of you")
  • Twinship/Alter ego -- The need to feel a sense of essential likeness with others ("We are alike")

Therapeutic Approach in Self Psychology

The therapist functions as an empathic selfobject who provides the mirroring and idealization the client missed in development. Inevitable empathic failures (optimal frustrations) by the therapist, when repaired through empathic understanding, allow the client to develop internal self-soothing capacities through a process called transmuting internalization. The therapist uses empathic immersion (experiencing the world from the client's perspective) as the primary therapeutic stance.

Brief Psychodynamic Therapy

Several clinicians adapted psychoanalytic principles into time-limited formats. These brief psychodynamic therapies typically involve 12-40 sessions and share key features:

  • Identification of a central conflict or focal theme early in treatment
  • More active therapist role compared to classical psychoanalysis
  • Use of transference interpretation focused specifically on the core theme
  • A predetermined time limit that creates urgency and motivation

Core Conflictual Relationship Theme (CCRT)

Luborsky's CCRT method identifies recurring relationship patterns across the client's narratives. Each episode is coded for three components: (1) the client's wish or need (e.g., "I want to be accepted"), (2) the response of the other (e.g., "Others reject me"), and (3) the response of the self (e.g., "I feel depressed and withdraw"). The most frequently occurring pattern becomes the therapeutic focus. This method has been widely used in psychotherapy process research.

EPPP Focus: Commonly Tested Concepts

  • Know all major defense mechanisms and be able to identify them in vignettes; sublimation = most mature, repression = most fundamental
  • Transference = client's feelings toward therapist; countertransference = therapist's feelings toward client
  • Winnicott: transitional object, good-enough mother, true self/false self
  • Klein: paranoid-schizoid and depressive positions, splitting
  • Kernberg: three levels of personality organization (neurotic, borderline, psychotic); Transference-Focused Psychotherapy (TFP); confrontation and interpretation of primitive defenses
  • Kernberg vs. Kohut: Kernberg confronts primitive defenses; Kohut provides empathic mirroring
  • Kohut: selfobject needs (mirroring, idealization, twinship), transmuting internalization, optimal frustration
  • Free association is the fundamental rule of psychoanalysis; dreams are the royal road to the unconscious
