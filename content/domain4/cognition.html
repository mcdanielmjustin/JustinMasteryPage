Through Thoughts

Cognitive restructuring & rational thinking
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Beck's Cognitive Therapy

Aaron T. Beck developed cognitive therapy in the 1960s, originally for depression. Beck proposed that psychological distress results not from events themselves but from individuals' interpretations of those events. The cognitive model holds that distorted thinking influences mood and behavior, and that modifying dysfunctional cognitions produces improvements in affect and functioning.

Levels of Cognition

Beck identified three levels of cognition: (1) Automatic thoughts -- spontaneous, rapid cognitions that arise in response to situations (e.g., "I'm going to fail"); (2) Intermediate beliefs -- conditional rules and assumptions (e.g., "If I don't get an A, I'm worthless"); and (3) Core beliefs (schemas) -- deep, fundamental beliefs about self, others, and the world (e.g., "I am incompetent"). Therapy works from the surface level inward.

Cognitive Distortions

Beck and his colleagues identified systematic errors in thinking called cognitive distortions. These are habitual patterns of biased information processing:

DistortionDefinitionExample
All-or-nothing thinkingViewing situations in only two categories"If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure."
CatastrophizingPredicting the worst possible outcome"If I make a mistake, I'll be fired and lose everything."
Mind readingAssuming you know what others think"She thinks I'm boring."
OvergeneralizationDrawing broad conclusions from a single event"I failed this test; I'll never pass anything."
Selective abstractionFocusing on one detail while ignoring the big pictureDwelling on one critical comment despite many compliments
Magnification/minimizationInflating negatives or shrinking positivesMagnifying a mistake while minimizing achievements
PersonalizationAttributing external events to oneself without basis"My friend is upset; it must be something I did."
Emotional reasoningUsing feelings as evidence of truth"I feel like a fraud, so I must be one."

CBT Techniques

Core techniques in Beck's cognitive therapy include:

  • Socratic questioning -- Guided discovery through a series of questions that help the client examine evidence for and against their beliefs
  • Thought records -- Structured worksheets documenting the situation, automatic thought, emotion, evidence for/against, and alternative balanced thought
  • Behavioral experiments -- Testing predictions derived from dysfunctional beliefs in real-world situations
  • Activity scheduling -- Planning and monitoring daily activities to combat avoidance and increase mastery/pleasure
  • Downward arrow technique -- Probing beneath automatic thoughts to uncover intermediate and core beliefs

Thought Record Example

Situation: Gave a presentation at work. Automatic Thought: "Everyone thought I was terrible." Emotion: Anxiety (85%). Evidence For: One person was on their phone. Evidence Against: Several people asked good questions; boss said "nice job." Balanced Thought: "Most people seemed engaged. One person being distracted doesn't mean I was terrible." Emotion after: Anxiety (35%).

Beck's Cognitive Triad of Depression

Beck proposed that depression involves a negative cognitive triad: negative views of (1) the self ("I'm worthless"), (2) the world ("Everything is terrible"), and (3) the future ("Things will never get better"). These negative schemas are activated by stressful events and maintained through systematic cognitive distortions.

Ellis's Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT)

Albert Ellis developed REBT (originally called Rational Emotive Therapy) in 1955, making it one of the earliest cognitive-behavioral approaches. REBT is based on the premise that emotional disturbance is largely caused by irrational beliefs about events, not by the events themselves.

The ABC Model

A (Activating event) -- The situation or trigger. B (Belief) -- The rational or irrational interpretation. C (Consequence) -- The emotional and behavioral result. Ellis added D (Disputing) -- Challenging the irrational belief, and E (Effective new belief) -- The rational replacement. The core insight: A does not cause C; B causes C.

Irrational Beliefs

Ellis identified several categories of irrational beliefs characterized by rigid, absolutistic demands (musts, shoulds, oughts):

  • Demandingness -- "I absolutely must perform well and win approval" / "Others must treat me fairly" / "Conditions must be comfortable"
  • Awfulizing -- "It's awful, horrible, and terrible when things don't go my way"
  • Low frustration tolerance (LFT) -- "I can't stand it" or "I can't bear this discomfort"
  • Global rating -- Condemning the total self, others, or life based on single instances

Beck vs. Ellis: Key Distinctions for the EPPP

While both are cognitive approaches, there are important differences. Ellis is more directive and confrontational, actively disputing irrational beliefs. Beck uses Socratic questioning and guided discovery. Ellis focuses on irrational beliefs (musts/shoulds); Beck focuses on automatic thoughts, intermediate beliefs, and schemas. Ellis's approach is more philosophical; Beck's is more empirical and research-driven. Both are considered founders of the cognitive-behavioral tradition.

Stress Inoculation Training (SIT)

Stress Inoculation Training was developed by Donald Meichenbaum as a cognitive-behavioral approach to help clients build resilience to stress by developing coping skills before, during, and after stressful events. SIT follows a medical inoculation analogy -- exposure to manageable doses of stress builds resistance to future stress.

Three Phases of SIT

  1. Conceptualization phase -- Educating the client about stress and its effects; identifying stressors and current coping strategies; establishing a collaborative relationship
  2. Skills acquisition and rehearsal phase -- Teaching coping skills (relaxation, cognitive restructuring, self-instructional training, problem-solving); practicing skills through imagery and role-play
  3. Application and follow-through phase -- Gradually applying skills to increasingly stressful situations; booster sessions for maintenance

Meichenbaum's Self-Instructional Training

Meichenbaum also developed self-instructional training (SIT), originally for impulsive children. The client learns to guide their own behavior through internal speech. The sequence follows Vygotsky's developmental model: (1) an adult model performs the task while talking aloud, (2) the child performs the task with the adult's verbal guidance, (3) the child performs while talking aloud to themselves, (4) the child whispers instructions, and (5) the child uses covert (internal) self-instructions. This approach bridges behavioral and cognitive interventions.

Problem-Solving Therapy

Problem-solving therapy (PST), developed by Thomas D'Zurilla and Marvin Goldfried, is a cognitive-behavioral intervention that teaches clients a structured approach to identifying and implementing effective solutions to everyday problems. PST is based on the premise that psychopathology is often related to ineffective problem-solving skills or a negative problem orientation.

Five Steps of Problem Solving

  1. Problem orientation -- Recognizing problems as normal and solvable; adopting an optimistic, rational approach rather than an avoidant or impulsive one
  2. Problem definition and formulation -- Clearly defining the problem in specific, concrete terms; identifying goals
  3. Generation of alternatives -- Brainstorming multiple possible solutions without evaluating them (quantity over quality initially)
  4. Decision making -- Evaluating each alternative based on feasibility and likely consequences; selecting the best option
  5. Solution implementation and verification -- Carrying out the chosen solution, monitoring the results, and adjusting if necessary

EPPP Focus: Commonly Tested Concepts

  • Beck's cognitive triad (negative views of self, world, future) is specific to depression
  • Know the major cognitive distortions and be able to identify them in clinical vignettes
  • Ellis's ABC model: A (Activating event), B (Belief), C (Consequence), D (Disputing), E (Effective new belief)
  • Beck uses Socratic questioning; Ellis uses direct disputation
  • Automatic thoughts are surface-level cognitions; schemas are the deepest level
  • Meichenbaum's Stress Inoculation Training has three phases: conceptualization, skills acquisition, application
  • Problem-solving therapy (D'Zurilla & Goldfried) teaches a structured five-step approach to everyday problems
