Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

ACT (pronounced as the word "act") was developed by Steven C. Hayes and is rooted in Relational Frame Theory (RFT), a behavioral account of language and cognition. ACT is considered a "third-wave" cognitive-behavioral therapy that emphasizes acceptance rather than thought modification.

Psychological Flexibility

The central goal of ACT is to increase psychological flexibility -- the ability to contact the present moment fully as a conscious human being and to change or persist in behavior when doing so serves valued ends. Psychological inflexibility (the opposite) is viewed as the root of psychopathology.

Six Core Processes (The Hexaflex)

ACT targets six interrelated processes arranged in a hexagonal model:

  1. Acceptance -- Willingness to experience unwanted thoughts and feelings without trying to control or eliminate them
  2. Cognitive Defusion -- Reducing the literal impact of thoughts; seeing thoughts as mental events rather than truths (e.g., "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure" rather than "I'm a failure")
  3. Present Moment Awareness -- Mindful contact with the here-and-now
  4. Self-as-Context -- Contacting a transcendent sense of self (the "observing self") distinct from the content of experience
  5. Values -- Clarifying what matters most; chosen life directions that are not goals but ongoing patterns of activity
  6. Committed Action -- Taking concrete steps guided by values, even in the presence of difficult internal experiences

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

DBT was developed by Marsha Linehan originally for the treatment of borderline personality disorder (BPD) and chronically suicidal individuals. DBT combines standard cognitive-behavioral techniques with Eastern mindfulness practices and dialectical philosophy.

The Biosocial Theory

DBT is grounded in the biosocial theory, which proposes that BPD is primarily a disorder of emotion dysregulation resulting from the transaction between (1) a biological vulnerability to emotional sensitivity/reactivity and (2) an invalidating environment -- one that pervasively negates, dismisses, or punishes the child's emotional experiences.

Treatment Modes and Structure

Comprehensive DBT includes four modes of treatment:

  • Individual therapy -- Weekly sessions focusing on the target hierarchy: (1) life-threatening behaviors, (2) therapy-interfering behaviors, (3) quality-of-life-interfering behaviors, (4) skills acquisition
  • Skills training group -- Weekly 2.5-hour group to learn and practice the four skills modules
  • Phone coaching -- Brief between-session calls for in-the-moment skills application
  • Therapist consultation team -- Regular meetings to support therapist motivation and capability

The Dialectical Stance

The core dialectic in DBT is the balance between acceptance and change. The therapist simultaneously validates the client's experience (acceptance) while pushing for behavioral change. This balancing act is reflected in specific strategies: validation strategies (acceptance) and problem-solving strategies (change). The synthesis of these opposites drives therapeutic progress.

Mindfulness-Based Approaches

Several structured interventions incorporate mindfulness meditation into cognitive-behavioral frameworks:

Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)

Developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn at the University of Massachusetts Medical Center, MBSR is an 8-week group program originally designed for chronic pain and stress-related conditions. The program includes body scan meditation, sitting meditation, gentle yoga, and informal mindfulness practices applied to daily activities.

Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)

Developed by Zindel Segal, Mark Williams, and John Teasdale, MBCT integrates MBSR with elements of CBT specifically for the prevention of depressive relapse. MBCT teaches patients to recognize early warning signs of depression and to disengage from ruminative thought patterns by relating differently to their thoughts. Research demonstrates that MBCT reduces relapse rates for patients with three or more prior depressive episodes.

Existential Therapy

Existential therapy draws on existential philosophy (Kierkegaard, Heidegger, Sartre) and focuses on universal human concerns. Major contributors include Rollo May, Irvin Yalom, Viktor Frankl, and Ludwig Binswanger.

Yalom's Four Ultimate Concerns

Irvin Yalom identified four existential givens that form the core of human anxiety:

  1. Death -- Awareness of mortality and the tension between the wish to continue existing and the inevitability of death
  2. Freedom -- The absence of external structure; the terrifying responsibility of being the author of one's own life
  3. Isolation -- Fundamental existential aloneness; no matter how close our relationships, an unbridgeable gap remains
  4. Meaninglessness -- The absence of inherent meaning in the universe; the challenge of creating one's own meaning

Viktor Frankl and Logotherapy

Frankl developed logotherapy ("therapy through meaning"), influenced by his experiences in Nazi concentration camps. Frankl proposed that the primary human motivation is a will to meaning, and that an existential vacuum (a sense of meaninglessness) is the source of much psychopathology. His technique of paradoxical intention involves instructing clients to intentionally engage in the feared behavior, thereby breaking the anticipatory anxiety cycle.

EPPP Focus: Commonly Tested Concepts

  • ACT aims for psychological flexibility, not symptom reduction; uses cognitive defusion rather than cognitive restructuring
  • DBT was developed for borderline personality disorder by Marsha Linehan; know the four modules
  • DBT's DEAR MAN is an interpersonal effectiveness skill; TIPP is for distress tolerance
  • The biosocial theory links biological emotional vulnerability with an invalidating environment
  • MBCT is specifically for relapse prevention in depression (3+ prior episodes)
  • Yalom: four ultimate concerns (death, freedom, isolation, meaninglessness)
  • Frankl: logotherapy, will to meaning, paradoxical intention
