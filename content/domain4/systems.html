Through the System

Family therapy & group dynamics
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Systems Theory Foundation

Family therapy approaches share a foundation in general systems theory, which views the family as an interconnected system rather than a collection of individuals. Key systems concepts include:

  • Circular causality -- Behavior in families is mutually influenced; A affects B, and B affects A simultaneously, rather than in a linear cause-effect chain
  • Homeostasis -- Families tend to maintain stability and resist change; symptoms may serve a homeostatic function in the system
  • Equifinality -- Different initial conditions can lead to the same outcome; many paths lead to the same family pattern
  • Identified patient (IP) -- The family member presented as "the problem"; systemic therapists view the IP's symptoms as reflecting dysfunction in the larger family system

First-Order vs. Second-Order Change

First-order change involves surface-level modifications within the existing system structure (e.g., a family changes which parent enforces rules, but the dysfunctional pattern continues). Second-order change involves a fundamental transformation of the system's structure or rules themselves (e.g., the family reorganizes its hierarchy entirely). Most family therapies aim for second-order change.

Structural Family Therapy (Minuchin)

Salvador Minuchin developed structural family therapy, which focuses on the organization of the family system -- its structure. The therapist actively intervenes to reorganize dysfunctional family structures, particularly boundaries and hierarchies.

Key Structural Concepts

  • Subsystems -- Functional units within the family: spousal/marital subsystem, parental subsystem, sibling subsystem. Each has distinct roles and rules.
  • Boundaries -- Rules defining who participates in a subsystem and how. Range from enmeshed (diffuse, overly permeable boundaries with excessive closeness) to disengaged (rigid boundaries with emotional distance). Healthy families have clear boundaries.
  • Hierarchy -- The distribution of power and authority; healthy families have parents in a position of authority over children
  • Coalitions -- Alliances between family members, often across generational lines (e.g., a parent and child aligning against the other parent)

Structural Techniques

TechniqueDescription
JoiningThe therapist accommodates to the family's style to build rapport; entering the family system before attempting to change it
EnactmentAsking family members to interact with each other in session rather than talking to the therapist, so the therapist can observe actual patterns
Boundary makingStrengthening or loosening boundaries between subsystems (e.g., having enmeshed parent-child dyad sit farther apart)
UnbalancingDeliberately siding with one family member to shift the power structure and disrupt a dysfunctional hierarchy
RestructuringActively reorganizing the family's transactional patterns through directives, seating changes, and task assignments

Clinical Example

A mother and her 14-year-old daughter are enmeshed -- they share everything, the daughter sleeps in the mother's bed, and the father is disengaged from the family. The structural therapist would work to strengthen the parental subsystem boundary (bringing the parents together as a team), create appropriate distance in the mother-daughter subsystem, and draw the father back into the family. The daughter's presenting problem (e.g., school refusal) is understood as reflecting the dysfunctional structure.

Strategic Family Therapy (Haley)

Jay Haley and Cloe Madanes developed strategic family therapy, which focuses on solving the presenting problem through specific directives and interventions. Strategic therapists are less concerned with insight and more focused on changing the behavioral sequences that maintain symptoms.

Key Concepts and Techniques

  • Directives -- The therapist assigns specific tasks for the family to perform between sessions; these may be straightforward or paradoxical
  • Paradoxical interventions -- Prescribing the symptom or instructing the family to continue the problematic behavior. The rationale: if a symptom is involuntary, prescribing it makes it voluntary (and therefore controllable); if the family resists the directive, they must abandon the symptom
  • Reframing -- Offering a new interpretation of behavior that changes its meaning (e.g., reframing a teenager's rebellion as "a healthy attempt at independence")
  • Ordeal therapy -- Assigning a task that is more difficult than the symptom itself, making the symptom less "worth" maintaining

The MRI Group

The Mental Research Institute (MRI) in Palo Alto (Watzlawick, Weakland, Fisch) is closely associated with strategic/brief therapy. Their key concept: problems are maintained by the very attempted solutions people use. Therapy focuses on interrupting the cycle of failed solutions. The MRI group also contributed foundational communication theory, including the axiom that "one cannot not communicate."

Bowenian Family Therapy

Murray Bowen developed one of the most comprehensive family systems theories, emphasizing multigenerational transmission of emotional patterns and the centrality of differentiation of self.

Core Bowenian Concepts

  • Differentiation of self -- The ability to maintain one's own sense of self while in emotional contact with others; the capacity to distinguish between thinking and feeling. Low differentiation = emotional fusion with others
  • Triangulation -- When a two-person relationship becomes stressed, a third person is drawn in to stabilize it (e.g., parents in conflict focus on a child's behavior). Triangles are the basic unit of relationship systems
  • Nuclear family emotional system -- Four patterns of managing anxiety: marital conflict, dysfunction in one spouse, impairment of one or more children, emotional distance
  • Multigenerational transmission process -- Emotional patterns and levels of differentiation are transmitted across generations
  • Emotional cutoff -- Managing unresolved attachment by reducing or cutting off emotional contact with family; appears to resolve the problem but actually increases vulnerability
  • Genogram -- A multigenerational family diagram used to map family patterns, relationships, and emotional processes across at least three generations

Narrative Therapy (White & Epston)

Michael White and David Epston developed narrative therapy, a postmodern approach grounded in social constructionist epistemology. Narrative therapy holds that people make meaning of their lives through stories, and that problems arise when individuals become dominated by oppressive, deficit-saturated narratives about themselves. The central therapeutic task is to help clients separate themselves from problem-saturated stories and construct new, preferred narratives.

Core Philosophy

"The Person Is Not the Problem; the Problem Is the Problem"

This foundational axiom of narrative therapy rejects the idea that pathology resides within the individual. Instead, problems are understood as separate entities that influence people's lives. This philosophical stance shifts the therapeutic conversation from diagnosis and internal deficits to the relationship between the person and the problem, opening space for agency and resistance.

Key Concepts and Techniques

  • Externalization -- The signature technique of narrative therapy. The problem is linguistically separated from the person's identity, turning it into an external entity that can be examined, resisted, and overcome. Externalization uses language that positions the problem as something that acts upon the person rather than something the person is.

Externalizing Conversation

Rather than asking "Why are you so anxious?" a narrative therapist might ask: "When does Anxiety visit you? What tricks does Anxiety use to convince you that you can't cope? Have there been times when you stood up to Anxiety and didn't let it push you around?" This language externalizes the problem (Anxiety) as a separate agent, preserves the client's sense of self as competent, and opens space for identifying moments of agency.

  • Dominant stories -- The prevailing narratives that organize a person's understanding of themselves and their experience. When dominant stories are problem-saturated (e.g., "I am a failure," "Our family is broken"), they filter out evidence of competence, strength, and positive experience.
  • Alternative stories (preferred stories) -- New narratives that are more empowering, multidimensional, and reflective of the person's values and hopes. The therapeutic goal is to help clients re-author their lives according to preferred stories.
  • Unique outcomes -- Exceptions to the dominant problem story -- moments when the person successfully resisted, escaped, or was not influenced by the problem. These are the entry points for constructing alternative narratives. The therapist actively inquires about times when the problem did not dominate.
  • Re-authoring -- The process of weaving unique outcomes into a coherent new narrative about the person's identity, values, and capabilities. Re-authoring conversations help clients thicken the alternative story by connecting unique outcomes to intentions, values, hopes, and commitments.

Narrative Practices

  • Definitional ceremonies / outsider witness practices -- Structured rituals in which an audience (outsider witnesses) listens to the client tell their story, then reflects back what struck them -- without offering interpretations, advice, or judgments. The audience's reflections help thicken the client's preferred story by acknowledging it through multiple perspectives. Drawn from anthropological work on rites of passage.
  • Therapeutic letters and documents -- White and Epston used letters summarizing sessions, certificates of achievement, and other documents to make therapeutic gains tangible and enduring. These documents serve as external records of the client's preferred story and can be revisited long after therapy ends.

Philosophical Foundations

Narrative therapy is deeply influenced by the work of French philosopher Michel Foucault, particularly his analysis of power/knowledge -- the idea that dominant discourses in society (including psychiatric and psychological discourses) shape what is considered "normal" and "pathological." Narrative therapists are attuned to how cultural, institutional, and professional power can produce problem-saturated identities. They adopt a decentered but influential therapeutic stance -- the therapist is curious and facilitative but does not position themselves as the expert on the client's life. The client is always the primary author of their own story.

Application in Family Therapy

In family contexts, narrative therapy externalizes problems that the family has internalized as belonging to one member (often the identified patient). By positioning the problem as external to all family members, the entire family can collaborate against it rather than blaming a single individual. Family members are invited to share their own unique outcomes and to serve as witnesses to each other's preferred stories, strengthening new narratives through collective acknowledgment.

Family Narrative Example

A family presents with their 10-year-old son, who has been labeled "the angry kid" at home and school. The narrative therapist externalizes the problem: "It sounds like Anger has been getting a lot of power in your family. When did Anger first start bossing everyone around? Are there times when your family has teamed up and not let Anger take over dinner time?" The family identifies several "Anger-free evenings" (unique outcomes), and together they explore what made those evenings possible -- collaboration that re-authors the family's story from "broken family with an angry child" to "family that sometimes wins against Anger."

Milan Systemic Family Therapy

The Milan group (Selvini Palazzoli, Boscolo, Cecchin, and Prata) developed a systemic approach that uses specific interviewing and intervention techniques:

  • Circular questioning -- Asking one family member to comment on the relationship between two other family members (e.g., "Who do you think is most upset when Mom and Dad argue?"). This reveals systemic patterns and perceptions that direct questioning cannot access.
  • Hypothesizing -- The therapy team develops tentative hypotheses about the family's relational patterns before and during the session, using these hypotheses to guide questioning
  • Neutrality -- The therapist avoids siding with any family member and maintains curiosity about all perspectives; later revised to "curiosity" by Cecchin
  • Positive connotation -- Reframing the symptom and each family member's behavior as serving a positive, protective function for the family system
  • Rituals and invariant prescriptions -- Assigning ritualized behavioral tasks designed to disrupt the family's repetitive interaction patterns

Experiential Family Therapy

Virginia Satir

Satir emphasized emotional experience and communication patterns. She identified four dysfunctional communication stances people adopt under stress:

  1. Placator -- Agrees with everything to avoid conflict; ignores own needs
  2. Blamer -- Finds fault with others; aggressive and dominating
  3. Super-reasonable (computer) -- Intellectualizes and detaches from feelings
  4. Irrelevant (distracter) -- Changes the subject; uses humor or distraction to deflect

The healthy alternative is the congruent communicator, who expresses feelings directly and honestly while respecting others. Satir used techniques such as family sculpting (having members physically position themselves to represent family dynamics) and family reconstruction.

Carl Whitaker

Whitaker practiced experiential/symbolic family therapy, an intuitive, provocative approach that used humor, absurdity, and the therapist's own emotional reactions. Whitaker believed that growth occurs through emotional experience in the session rather than through insight or education. He often used co-therapy and insisted on treating the entire family.

Group Therapy

Yalom's Therapeutic (Curative) Factors

Irvin Yalom identified 11 therapeutic factors (originally "curative factors") that operate in group therapy:

#FactorDescription
1Instillation of hopeSeeing others improve inspires optimism
2UniversalityRealizing "I am not alone" -- others share similar struggles
3Imparting informationPsychoeducation and advice from therapist and members
4AltruismHelping others enhances one's own self-esteem and sense of purpose
5Corrective recapitulation of the primary family groupThe group re-creates family dynamics, allowing corrective emotional experiences
6Development of socializing techniquesLearning social skills through group interaction
7Imitative behaviorModeling -- observing and imitating adaptive behaviors of others
8Interpersonal learningGaining insight into one's interpersonal impact through feedback
9Group cohesivenessThe sense of belonging and acceptance; the group analog of the therapeutic alliance
10CatharsisEmotional release and expression of feelings in a safe context
11Existential factorsConfronting fundamental issues of existence (responsibility, mortality, meaning)

Stages of Group Development

Groups typically progress through predictable stages of development:

  1. Forming (Orientation) -- Members are polite, anxious, dependent on the leader; establishing ground rules; superficial interaction
  2. Storming (Conflict) -- Struggles for dominance, resistance to the leader and to group norms; interpersonal conflicts emerge; members test boundaries
  3. Norming (Cohesion) -- Group identity develops; trust increases; norms are established; members cooperate and support one another
  4. Working (Performing) -- Productive therapeutic work occurs; high cohesion, mutual support, self-disclosure; members give and receive honest feedback
  5. Termination (Adjourning) -- Consolidation of gains; addressing feelings of loss; reviewing accomplishments; planning for continued growth

Group Leadership Styles

Research suggests that the most effective group leaders balance emotional stimulation (challenging, confronting) with caring (warmth, support). Leaders who are too confrontational without sufficient caring produce casualties. Leaders must also manage group dynamics such as scapegoating (targeting one member), monopolizing (one member dominating), and subgrouping (cliques forming within the group).

Types of Group Therapy

Groups vary widely in their theoretical orientation, structure, and goals:

TypeFocusStructure

Open vs. Closed Groups

Open groups allow new members to join at any time (e.g., drop-in support groups). They provide ongoing accessibility but may disrupt cohesion with each new member. Closed groups have a fixed membership from the start and do not admit new members once the group begins. They tend to develop stronger cohesion and allow for deeper interpersonal work, but a member dropping out can significantly impact group dynamics. Slow-open groups replace departing members gradually, maintaining a balance between stability and accessibility.

Ethical Considerations in Group Therapy

Group therapy raises unique ethical issues: (1) confidentiality is more difficult to ensure because the therapist cannot control what members share outside the group; the therapist must emphasize confidentiality expectations but cannot guarantee them. (2) Informed consent must address the unique risks of group treatment, including potential breaches of confidentiality by other members. (3) The therapist must screen members for suitability -- clients who are acutely suicidal, psychotic, or extremely socially phobic may not be appropriate for group treatment.
