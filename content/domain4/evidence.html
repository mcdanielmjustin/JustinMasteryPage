Efficacy vs. Effectiveness

A fundamental distinction in psychotherapy outcome research is between efficacy and effectiveness studies. Understanding this distinction is critical for evaluating the evidence base for psychological treatments.

Key Definitions

Efficacy -- Does the treatment work under ideal, controlled conditions? Efficacy studies are typically randomized controlled trials (RCTs) conducted in research settings with manualized treatments, homogeneous samples (strict inclusion/exclusion criteria), trained therapists, and comparison to control groups. High internal validity.

Effectiveness -- Does the treatment work in real-world clinical practice? Effectiveness studies examine outcomes in naturalistic settings with diverse patients, typical clinical conditions, and routine care. High external validity (generalizability).

The Dodo Bird Verdict

The Dodo bird verdict refers to the controversial conclusion that all bona fide psychotherapies produce roughly equivalent outcomes. The name derives from the Dodo bird in Alice's Adventures in Wonderland, who declared after a race: "Everybody has won, and all must have prizes."

Saul Rosenzweig (1936) first suggested this idea, and Lester Luborsky and colleagues (1975) provided the first empirical support through a review of comparative outcome studies. Their frequently cited conclusion: "Everyone has won and all must have prizes."

The Debate

Supporters of the Dodo bird verdict (e.g., Wampold, Luborsky) argue that when bona fide treatments are compared, effect size differences are minimal, and that common factors (shared by all therapies) account for most therapeutic change. Critics (e.g., Chambless, Beutler) argue that specific treatments do outperform others for specific disorders (e.g., ERP for OCD, CBT for panic disorder) and that lumping all therapies together obscures meaningful differences. This debate remains one of the most important in the field.

Rosenzweig, S. (1936). Some implicit common factors in diverse methods of psychotherapy. American Journal of Orthopsychiatry, 6(3), 412-415.

Common Factors

The common factors model holds that ingredients shared across all effective therapies are the primary drivers of therapeutic change, rather than technique-specific factors unique to each orientation. Research suggests the following approximate breakdown of outcome variance:

Lambert's Outcome Variance Model

Empirically Supported Treatments (ESTs)

The Empirically Supported Treatment (EST) movement, led by the APA Division 12 (Society of Clinical Psychology) Task Force, aimed to identify specific treatments with demonstrated efficacy for specific disorders.

Criteria for EST Designation

The Chambless criteria (named after Dianne Chambless, who chaired the original task force) established two levels:

  • Well-established treatments -- At least two good group-design experiments by different research teams showing superiority to pill/psychological placebo or equivalence to an established treatment, OR a large series of single-case experiments; conducted with treatment manuals; clearly specified population
  • Probably efficacious treatments -- At least two good experiments showing superiority to a wait-list control, OR one well-designed experiment meeting well-established criteria, OR a small series of single-case experiments

Examples of Well-Established ESTs

CBT for depression and anxiety disorders. ERP for OCD. Behavioral couples therapy for relationship distress. CBT for bulimia nervosa. Interpersonal therapy for depression. Prolonged exposure for PTSD. These treatments have the strongest evidence bases, supported by multiple RCTs from independent research groups.

Criticisms of the EST Movement

The EST approach has been criticized for: (1) overemphasis on manualized treatments at the expense of clinical flexibility; (2) reliance on RCTs that may not generalize to real-world settings; (3) potential exclusion of effective treatments that lack research funding (e.g., psychodynamic, humanistic therapies); (4) treating disorders as discrete entities when comorbidity is the norm; and (5) undervaluing the role of the therapeutic relationship and common factors. The evidence-based practice (EBP) framework was developed partly in response, integrating research evidence with clinical expertise and patient values/preferences.

Dose-Response Research

The dose-response model examines the relationship between the "dose" of therapy (typically measured in number of sessions) and therapeutic outcome.

Howard's Dose-Response Curve

Kenneth Howard and colleagues (1986) found a negatively accelerating (log-linear) relationship between number of sessions and improvement:

  • By session 8: approximately 50% of patients showed measurable improvement
  • By session 26: approximately 75% showed improvement
  • By session 52: approximately 85% showed improvement

The curve shows diminishing returns -- the largest gains occur earliest in treatment, with progressively smaller incremental gains per additional session. This has significant implications for managed care and session limits.

The Phase Model

Howard also proposed a phase model of psychotherapy outcome with three sequential phases:

  1. Remoralization -- Restoration of hope and subjective well-being (occurs earliest)
  2. Remediation -- Reduction of specific symptoms
  3. Rehabilitation -- Improvement in overall functioning and life patterns (occurs latest)
Howard, K. I., Kopta, S. M., Krause, M. S., & Orlinsky, D. E. (1986). The dose-response relationship in psychotherapy. American Psychologist, 41(2), 159-164.

Meta-Analysis Findings

Meta-analysis is a statistical technique for combining results across multiple studies to estimate an overall effect size. It has been transformative for psychotherapy outcome research.

The Smith and Glass (1977) Meta-Analysis

Mary Lee Smith and Gene Glass conducted the landmark first meta-analysis of psychotherapy outcomes, combining 375 studies. Their key findings:

  • The average effect size across all therapies was d = 0.85, meaning the average therapy client was better off than 80% of untreated controls
  • Psychotherapy was clearly effective compared to no treatment
  • Different types of therapy produced broadly similar effect sizes (supporting the Dodo bird verdict)

Subsequent Key Meta-Analytic Findings

  • Wampold (2001) found that specific treatment techniques account for at most 1-8% of outcome variance, while common factors account for substantially more
  • The allegiance effect -- researchers tend to find superior results for their preferred therapy; when allegiance is controlled, differences between therapies shrink
  • Therapy is more effective than placebo, which is more effective than no treatment
  • Individual therapy and group therapy produce broadly comparable outcomes for most conditions

Effect Size Interpretation

Cohen's d: 0.2 = small, 0.5 = medium, 0.8 = large. The overall effect of psychotherapy (d = 0.80-0.85) is considered a large effect -- comparable to many well-accepted medical interventions. This means the average person receiving therapy is better off than approximately 80% of those who do not receive therapy.

Evidence-Based Practice (EBP)

In response to criticisms of the EST movement, the APA Presidential Task Force on Evidence-Based Practice adopted a broader framework. Evidence-based practice in psychology (EBPP) is defined as "the integration of the best available research with clinical expertise in the context of patient characteristics, culture, and preferences" (APA, 2006).

Three Components of EBPP

(1) Best available research evidence -- Including but not limited to RCTs; also systematic case studies, qualitative research, process research, and clinical observation. (2) Clinical expertise -- The clinician's judgment, skill, and experience in applying treatments, including the ability to form a strong therapeutic alliance, assess patient needs, and adapt interventions. (3) Patient characteristics, culture, and preferences -- Individual differences in values, beliefs, cultural background, and treatment preferences that must guide clinical decision-making.

Therapist Effects and Client Variables

Therapist Effects

Research consistently shows that there is significant variability in outcomes across therapists, even when delivering the same manualized treatment. Therapist effects account for approximately 5-9% of outcome variance -- comparable to or larger than the effects of specific treatment techniques. Factors associated with more effective therapists include:

  • Strong facilitative interpersonal skills (empathy, warmth, emotional expressiveness)
  • Ability to form strong therapeutic alliances across diverse clients
  • Flexibility in adapting treatment to individual client needs
  • Engagement in deliberate practice and ongoing professional development

Client Variables as Predictors

Client characteristics are the single largest predictor of therapy outcome (accounting for roughly 40% of variance per Lambert's model). Key client variables include:

  • Severity and chronicity -- More severe and chronic disorders generally require longer treatment
  • Motivation and readiness for change -- Clients in the action stage (Prochaska) tend to benefit more
  • Social support -- Clients with stronger social networks tend to have better outcomes
  • Psychological mindedness -- The capacity for self-reflection and insight
  • Attachment style -- Secure attachment predicts better alliance formation and outcomes

Deterioration and Negative Effects

While therapy is generally effective, research indicates that approximately 5-10% of adult clients and 14-24% of child clients deteriorate during treatment. Lambert developed an early warning system (OQ-45) that tracks client progress session-by-session and alerts therapists when clients are not improving or are deteriorating, allowing for mid-course corrections.

Levels of Prevention

The prevention framework, originally developed in public health and adapted for mental health by Gerald Caplan, organizes prevention efforts into three levels based on when the intervention occurs relative to the onset of disorder.

Caplan's Three Levels of Prevention

Primary prevention -- Interventions directed at the general population or at-risk groups before a disorder develops. Goal: reduce the incidence (new cases) of disorder. Secondary prevention -- Early identification and treatment of a disorder in its initial stages. Goal: reduce the prevalence (total cases) and severity by catching problems early. Tertiary prevention -- Interventions aimed at reducing the disability, relapse, and complications associated with an already-established disorder. Goal: reduce residual effects and prevent recurrence.

LevelTargetGoalExample
PrimaryGeneral population or at-risk groups (before onset)Reduce incidenceSchool-based anti-bullying programs, parenting skills training, Head Start
SecondaryIndividuals showing early signs or at high riskReduce prevalence through early detectionDepression screening in primary care, crisis intervention hotlines, developmental screening for children
TertiaryIndividuals with established disordersReduce disability and prevent relapseRelapse prevention programs, vocational rehabilitation, supported employment for severe mental illness

IOM Classification (Alternative Framework)

The Institute of Medicine (IOM) proposed an alternative classification that further divides primary prevention:

  • Universal prevention -- Targets the entire population regardless of risk (e.g., public service announcements about mental health)
  • Selective prevention -- Targets individuals or groups whose risk is above average (e.g., support groups for children of divorcing parents)
  • Indicated prevention -- Targets individuals showing early signs or subclinical symptoms (e.g., intervention for students with emerging anxiety symptoms)

Distinguishing Prevention Levels

A schoolwide social-emotional learning curriculum delivered to all students = primary/universal prevention. Screening all students for depression and referring those who score high = secondary prevention. A cognitive-behavioral group for students already diagnosed with anxiety = tertiary prevention. The EPPP frequently tests the ability to correctly classify interventions into these levels.

Caplan's Consultation Model

Gerald Caplan developed the most influential model of mental health consultation, which he defined as a process between a professional helper (consultant) and a help-seeker (consultee) who has responsibility for a client or program. The consultant has no direct authority over the consultee, and the relationship is non-hierarchical and voluntary.

Core Principles of Caplan's Model

The consultant works indirectly -- improving the consultee's ability to handle current and future similar cases, rather than directly treating the client. The consultee retains full responsibility for the client. The consultation relationship is coordinate (non-hierarchical), voluntary, and confidential. The goal is to enhance the consultee's professional functioning, not to provide personal therapy.

Four Types of Mental Health Consultation

TypeFocusDescription
Client-centered case consultationThe client/caseThe consultant assesses the client and provides recommendations to the consultee about the case. Most similar to traditional clinical work. The primary goal is to help the client.
Consultee-centered case consultationThe consultee's difficulty with the caseThe focus is on improving the consultee's ability to handle the case. The problem may stem from lack of knowledge, skill, confidence, or objectivity. Most emphasized by Caplan.
Program-centered administrative consultationA program or organizationThe consultant evaluates a specific program or organizational problem and provides recommendations.
Consultee-centered administrative consultationThe consultee's approach to administrationThe focus is on improving the consultee's skills in program development, leadership, or organizational management.

Consultee-Centered Case Consultation in Detail

Caplan regarded consultee-centered case consultation as the most unique and important type. He identified four reasons a consultee may have difficulty with a case:

  1. Lack of knowledge -- The consultee doesn't have sufficient information about the client's condition
  2. Lack of skill -- The consultee lacks the technical ability to intervene effectively
  3. Lack of confidence -- The consultee has the knowledge and skill but lacks self-assurance
  4. Lack of objectivity -- The consultee's personal issues interfere with professional judgment (the most complex and interesting problem)

Theme Interference and Theme Interference Reduction

When a consultee lacks objectivity, Caplan attributed this to theme interference -- the consultee unconsciously links the client's situation to an unresolved personal conflict, leading to a "syllogism" such as: "All women who are assertive end up alone and miserable" (connecting the client to the consultee's personal theme). The consultant addresses this through theme interference reduction, which involves demonstrating that the feared outcome is not inevitable -- typically by examining the specifics of the case rather than by directly addressing the consultee's personal issues. The consultant never provides therapy to the consultee.

Types of Consultation Beyond Caplan

Behavioral Consultation

Behavioral consultation applies behavioral principles (functional analysis, reinforcement, environmental modification) to consultation. The consultant helps the consultee conduct a functional behavioral assessment, identify antecedents and consequences maintaining problem behavior, and design a behavior intervention plan. Common in school settings.

Organizational Consultation

Organizational consultation focuses on the system level, addressing organizational culture, structure, communication, and processes rather than individual cases. Approaches include process consultation (Edgar Schein), where the consultant helps the organization diagnose and solve its own problems rather than providing expert recommendations.

Community Psychology

Community psychology emerged in the 1960s (formalized at the Swampscott Conference in 1965) as a reaction against the limitations of the individual-treatment model. Community psychology shifts the focus from treating individuals to changing systems, environments, and social conditions that contribute to psychological problems.

Core Principles

  • Prevention over treatment -- Addressing problems before they develop rather than waiting to treat them
  • Ecological perspective -- Understanding behavior in the context of multiple environmental systems (individual, family, community, society)
  • Empowerment -- Helping communities and individuals gain control over their lives and circumstances
  • Social justice -- Advocating for equitable access to resources and addressing systemic inequalities
  • Respect for diversity -- Valuing cultural differences and adapting interventions to community contexts
  • Citizen participation -- Involving community members in the design and implementation of programs

Key Community Psychology Concepts

  • Sense of community (McMillan & Chavis) -- Membership, influence, integration and fulfillment of needs, shared emotional connection
  • Ecological analogy (James Kelly) -- Communities function like ecosystems; interventions must consider interdependence, cycling of resources, adaptation, and succession
  • Underpopulated settings (Barker) -- Environments with fewer people than needed to fill available roles, leading to increased participation and role versatility

The Community Mental Health Movement

The Community Mental Health Centers Act (1963), signed by President Kennedy, established federally funded community mental health centers (CMHCs). These centers were required to provide five essential services: (1) inpatient care, (2) outpatient care, (3) partial hospitalization, (4) emergency services, and (5) consultation and education. This legislation was a cornerstone of deinstitutionalization -- the movement to shift care from large state hospitals to community-based settings.

Empowerment and Prevention Programs

Resilience and Protective Factors

Prevention research has increasingly focused on identifying protective factors that buffer individuals against developing psychological disorders, even in the presence of significant risk factors. Key protective factors include:

  • Individual factors -- High intelligence, easy temperament, positive self-esteem, internal locus of control, effective coping skills
  • Family factors -- Warm and supportive parenting, family cohesion, stable home environment, at least one caring adult
  • Community factors -- Strong social support networks, access to quality education, positive school climate, involvement in prosocial organizations

Primary Prevention vs. Psychotherapy

A critical distinction for the EPPP: primary prevention targets populations before disorder onset and aims to reduce incidence (new cases). Psychotherapy is a form of tertiary prevention that treats existing disorders. Screening programs (e.g., PHQ-9 for depression in primary care) are secondary prevention because they aim for early detection. The EPPP frequently asks examinees to classify interventions into the correct prevention level based on the target population and timing.

Crisis Intervention

Crisis intervention is a form of secondary prevention that provides immediate, short-term assistance to individuals experiencing acute psychological distress. Grounded in crisis theory (developed by Erich Lindemann and Gerald Caplan), it assumes that crises are time-limited (typically resolving within 4-6 weeks) and that individuals in crisis are more amenable to change.

Principles of Crisis Intervention

  • Immediacy -- Respond quickly; delay reduces effectiveness
  • Short-term focus -- Typically 1-6 sessions; focus on the here and now
  • Goal: return to pre-crisis functioning -- Not long-term personality change but restoring equilibrium
  • Active, directive approach -- The therapist is more active and directive than in traditional therapy
  • Assess lethality -- Evaluate suicide/homicide risk as a first priority

Psychological First Aid vs. Critical Incident Stress Debriefing

Psychological First Aid (PFA) is the current evidence-informed approach to immediate post-disaster/crisis support. It emphasizes safety, calming, self-efficacy, connectedness, and hope. Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (CISD) -- a structured group intervention conducted within 24-72 hours of a traumatic event -- was once widely used but is no longer recommended. Research (including a Cochrane review) found that CISD does not prevent PTSD and may actually interfere with natural recovery processes.

Self-Help and Mutual Support Groups

Self-help groups (also called mutual support or peer support groups) are voluntary organizations of people who share a common condition or experience. They differ from professionally led group therapy in that they are typically peer-facilitated and free of charge.

Professional Role with Self-Help Groups

Psychologists can serve as consultants to self-help groups, provide referrals, and collaborate with these organizations. However, it is important to recognize that self-help groups are not a substitute for professional treatment in all cases. Research on AA and 12-step facilitation therapy (TSF) suggests that active involvement in AA is associated with improved abstinence outcomes. The Project MATCH study found TSF to be as effective as CBT and motivational enhancement therapy for alcohol use disorder.
