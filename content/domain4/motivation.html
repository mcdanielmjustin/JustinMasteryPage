Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)

Developed by Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg at the Brief Family Therapy Center in Milwaukee, Solution-Focused Brief Therapy represents a radical departure from traditional problem-focused approaches. Rather than exploring the origins or maintenance of problems, SFBT directs attention toward constructing solutions by identifying what clients already do well and amplifying those successes.

Core Philosophy

SFBT operates on several key assumptions: (1) if it isn't broken, don't fix it; (2) if something works, do more of it; (3) if something doesn't work, do something different; (4) small steps can lead to large changes; and (5) the solution is not necessarily related to the problem.

The Miracle Question

The miracle question is the hallmark technique of SFBT. The therapist asks: "Suppose tonight, while you are sleeping, a miracle happens and the problem that brought you here is solved. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what will be different that will tell you a miracle has happened?" This question serves multiple functions:

  • Bypasses the client's sense of hopelessness by creating a vivid vision of a preferred future
  • Identifies concrete, behavioral goals for therapy in the client's own language
  • Shifts focus from problem-saturated narratives to solution-oriented thinking
  • Reveals what the client truly values and wants, rather than simply what they want to eliminate

Scaling Questions

Scaling questions ask clients to rate their current experience on a scale of 1 to 10. For example: "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 10 means you are handling your anxiety as well as you ever could, where would you say you are today?" Follow-up questions explore what has helped the client reach their current number and what a one-point improvement would look like.

Clinical Example

Therapist: "You said you're at a 4 today. What are you doing that has you at a 4 rather than a 1?"

Client: "Well, I have been going on walks and calling my sister more."

Therapist: "That's impressive. What would be different at a 5?"

This questioning identifies existing strengths and generates incremental, achievable goals.

Exception Questions and Coping Questions

Exception questions explore times when the problem could have occurred but did not: "Tell me about a time recently when you expected the panic to happen but it didn't." These questions highlight client competencies and suggest that solutions are already partially in place.

Coping questions are used when clients feel overwhelmed and cannot identify exceptions: "Given how difficult things have been, how have you managed to keep going?" This validates the client's struggle while simultaneously identifying resilience.

SFBT Session Structure

A typical SFBT session includes: (1) establishing what the client wants from the session, (2) exploring exceptions and competencies, (3) using the miracle question or scaling, (4) a consultation break where the therapist reflects, and (5) delivering feedback that includes compliments and a therapeutic task. Sessions are typically limited to 5-8 meetings.

Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Motivational Interviewing was developed by William R. Miller and Stephen Rollnick as a directive, client-centered counseling style for eliciting behavior change by helping clients explore and resolve ambivalence. MI was originally developed for substance use treatment but has since been applied to a wide range of health behaviors.

The Spirit of MI

MI is characterized by four interrelated elements: (1) Partnership -- MI is done "with" and "for" the client, not "to" or "on" the client; (2) Acceptance -- including absolute worth, autonomy support, accurate empathy, and affirmation; (3) Compassion -- actively promoting the client's welfare; and (4) Evocation -- drawing out the client's own motivations and resources rather than installing them.

OARS: Core Skills of MI

The fundamental micro-skills of MI are captured by the acronym OARS:

SkillDescriptionExample
Open-ended QuestionsQuestions that cannot be answered with a simple yes/no"What concerns do you have about your drinking?"
AffirmationsStatements recognizing client strengths and efforts"It took a lot of courage to come here today."
ReflectionsStatements that mirror or rephrase client speech; simple or complex"So you're feeling torn between wanting to quit and worrying about losing your social circle."
SummariesCollecting and linking several client statements together"Let me make sure I'm following -- you've mentioned health worries, your wife's concerns, and the expense..."

Change Talk and Sustain Talk

Change talk is any client speech that favors movement toward a target behavior change. It is organized by the mnemonic DARN-CAT:

  • Desire -- "I want to..." / Ability -- "I could..." / Reasons -- "Because..." / Need -- "I have to..." (Preparatory)
  • Commitment -- "I will..." / Activation -- "I'm ready to..." / Taking steps -- "I actually did..." (Mobilizing)

Sustain talk is client speech favoring the status quo. The MI therapist does not argue against sustain talk but responds with reflections and explores the ambivalence.

The Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change)

MI is closely associated with Prochaska and DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model, which proposes that behavior change unfolds through stages:

  1. Precontemplation -- Not yet considering change; unaware or in denial of the problem
  2. Contemplation -- Aware of the problem but ambivalent; weighing pros and cons
  3. Preparation -- Intending to take action soon; making small preliminary steps
  4. Action -- Actively modifying behavior; requires considerable commitment of time and energy
  5. Maintenance -- Sustaining the change over time; working to prevent relapse

EPPP Clinical Application

A key principle of MI is to avoid the righting reflex -- the therapist's natural tendency to try to fix the client's problem or persuade them to change. When clinicians argue for change, clients are pushed toward arguing against it (sustain talk). MI posits that the client, not the therapist, should be the one voicing the arguments for change. Rolling with resistance, rather than confronting it, is a core MI strategy.

Prochaska, J. O., & DiClemente, C. C. (1983). Stages and processes of self-change of smoking: Toward an integrative model of change. Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 51(3), 390-395.

Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

Interpersonal Therapy was developed by Gerald Klerman and Myrna Weissman in the 1970s, originally as a time-limited treatment for major depression. IPT focuses on resolving interpersonal problems and improving interpersonal functioning, based on the premise that psychiatric symptoms arise in an interpersonal context and that improving relationships will alleviate symptoms.

Four Interpersonal Problem Areas

IPT organizes treatment around one or two of four defined problem areas:

Problem AreaDescriptionTherapeutic Focus
GriefComplicated bereavement following the death of a significant otherFacilitate mourning process, help establish new relationships and interests
Role DisputesConflicts with a significant other due to differing expectationsIdentify the dispute, determine the stage (renegotiation, impasse, dissolution), plan strategies
Role TransitionsDifficulty coping with life changes (e.g., divorce, retirement, diagnosis)Mourn the loss of the old role, develop a positive view of the new role, build mastery
Interpersonal DeficitsImpoverished social networks, chronic loneliness, or poor social skillsReduce social isolation, enhance social skills, form new relationships

Phases of IPT

IPT is typically conducted in 12-16 weekly sessions organized into three phases:

  1. Initial Phase (Sessions 1-3): Assess symptoms, conduct an interpersonal inventory (review of significant relationships), identify the primary problem area, assign the "sick role" (legitimizing the patient's need for help), and establish the treatment contract
  2. Middle Phase (Sessions 4-12): Work on the identified problem area using specific strategies; therapist uses techniques such as communication analysis, role playing, and exploring parallels between relationships
  3. Termination Phase (Sessions 13-16): Consolidate gains, anticipate future difficulties, establish the patient's sense of competence, and address feelings about ending therapy

Evidence Base for IPT

IPT is one of the most well-researched psychotherapies. It has demonstrated efficacy for major depression (comparable to CBT and medication), bulimia nervosa, and is recommended in treatment guidelines by the American Psychiatric Association. The NIMH Treatment of Depression Collaborative Research Program found IPT and CBT were both effective for mild to moderate depression, with IPT showing particular advantages for patients with severe interpersonal difficulties.

Additional Brief Therapy Concepts

MI Processes: The Four-Process Model

Miller and Rollnick (2013) organized MI into four overlapping processes that guide the flow of a session:

  1. Engaging -- Establishing a working relationship and mutual trust; the foundation for everything that follows
  2. Focusing -- Developing and maintaining a specific direction for the conversation about change
  3. Evoking -- Eliciting the client's own motivations for change (change talk); the heart of MI
  4. Planning -- Developing commitment to change and formulating a specific plan of action when the client is ready

SFBT: Compliments and First-Session Tasks

At the end of each SFBT session, the therapist delivers carefully constructed compliments that validate the client's existing strengths and coping strategies. Following compliments, the therapist may assign a first-session task -- most famously the formula first-session task: "Between now and next time we meet, I'd like you to observe what happens in your life that you want to continue to have happen." This observation task shifts attention from problems to strengths.

IPT and the Medical Model

IPT is unique among psychotherapies in its deliberate use of a medical model. Depression is conceptualized as a medical illness, and the therapist explicitly assigns the patient to a sick role during the initial phase. This serves to: (1) reduce guilt and self-blame; (2) legitimize the need for help; and (3) provide temporary relief from overwhelming role expectations. However, the sick role is time-limited -- a key therapeutic goal is helping the patient gradually resume normal role functioning.

Decisional Balance in MI

The decisional balance exercise is a technique in which the client explores four quadrants: (1) benefits of the status quo, (2) costs of the status quo, (3) benefits of change, and (4) costs of change. This structured exploration of ambivalence helps the client articulate their own reasons for change without the therapist arguing for it. The decisional balance is particularly useful in the contemplation stage, where the person is actively weighing pros and cons.

Brief Therapy: Common Principles

Despite their different theoretical backgrounds, brief therapies share several principles that distinguish them from longer-term approaches:

  • Time limitation -- A defined number of sessions creates urgency and focuses the work; the time limit itself is considered therapeutic
  • Focus -- A specific, circumscribed problem or theme is identified early and maintained throughout treatment
  • Therapist activity -- Brief therapy clinicians are generally more active and directive than in long-term approaches
  • Rapid alliance formation -- The therapeutic relationship must be established quickly, as there is limited time
  • Goal orientation -- Clear, measurable goals are established collaboratively in early sessions
  • Emphasis on client strengths -- Identifying and building on existing resources rather than exploring pathology in depth

Who Is Suitable for Brief Therapy?

Brief therapy tends to be most appropriate for clients who present with: (1) a circumscribed, clearly defined problem; (2) at least one prior experience of a meaningful relationship (suggesting capacity for alliance); (3) adequate ego strength; (4) motivation for change; and (5) the ability to engage quickly with the therapist. Clients with chronic personality disorders, severe trauma histories, or active psychosis may require longer-term treatment, though brief interventions can be useful as part of a stepped-care model.

EPPP Focus: Commonly Tested Concepts

  • The miracle question is strongly associated with SFBT and de Shazer/Berg -- know this pairing
  • MI's OARS (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries) and the concept of the righting reflex are frequently tested
  • Know the five stages of change: Precontemplation, Contemplation, Preparation, Action, Maintenance
  • IPT focuses on four problem areas: grief, role disputes, role transitions, interpersonal deficits
  • Distinguish SFBT's exception questions (exploring when the problem is absent) from MI's change talk (evoking the client's own arguments for change)
  • Scaling questions are used in SFBT to quantify progress and identify next steps
  • IPT assigns the sick role in the initial phase to legitimize the patient's need for treatment and reduce self-blame
  • MI was originally developed for substance use disorders but is now applied broadly
