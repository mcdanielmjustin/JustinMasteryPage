Overview of Standard 4: Privacy and Confidentiality

Standard 4 of the APA Ethics Code addresses one of the most fundamental principles of psychological practice: the obligation to protect confidential information obtained through professional work. Confidentiality is central to the therapeutic relationship, as clients must feel safe disclosing sensitive information to benefit from treatment.

This standard is one of the most heavily tested on the EPPP. Questions frequently focus on the limits of confidentiality, the duty to warn or protect, HIPAA regulations, and special populations such as minors.

Privacy vs. Confidentiality vs. Privilege

Privacy is the right of individuals to control access to information about themselves. Confidentiality is the ethical obligation of the psychologist not to disclose information obtained in a professional relationship. Privilege (or testimonial privilege) is a legal right that protects clients from having their confidential communications disclosed in legal proceedings without their consent. Privilege belongs to the client, not the psychologist.

Standard 4.01: Maintaining Confidentiality

Psychologists have a primary obligation to take reasonable precautions to protect confidential information obtained through or stored in any medium. This includes:

  • Information obtained in clinical practice, research, teaching, and consultation
  • Data stored electronically, on paper, or in any other format
  • Information shared orally in sessions, consultations, or supervisory contexts

The duty extends beyond active professional relationships. Psychologists must protect confidential information even after the professional relationship has ended and after the client has died (in most jurisdictions).

Standard 4.02: Discussing the Limits of Confidentiality

Psychologists discuss with persons and organizations the relevant limits of confidentiality and the foreseeable uses of information generated through their psychological activities. This discussion should occur:

  • At the outset of the relationship (preferably during the informed consent process)
  • As new circumstances arise that may affect confidentiality
  • When the psychologist's role changes (e.g., from individual to couples therapy)

Common Limits of Confidentiality

The following situations typically require or permit disclosure of otherwise confidential information:

  1. Danger to self -- When a client poses an imminent risk of suicide or serious self-harm
  2. Danger to others -- When a client poses a credible threat of violence toward an identifiable third party (Tarasoff duty)
  3. Child abuse or neglect -- All 50 states mandate reporting of suspected child abuse or neglect
  4. Elder abuse or abuse of dependent adults -- Most states mandate reporting
  5. Court orders -- A valid court order may require disclosure; a subpoena alone generally does not
  6. Client consent -- Clients may authorize disclosure through a signed release
  7. Insurance and third-party payors -- Information necessary for reimbursement
  8. Consultation -- Psychologists may disclose limited information for consultation purposes, typically without identifying the client
EPPP Tip

A common EPPP trap: A subpoena alone does NOT require disclosure of confidential records. The psychologist should consult an attorney, notify the client, and may assert privilege on the client's behalf. Only a court order (signed by a judge) compels disclosure. Know this distinction well.

The Tarasoff Duty: Duty to Warn and Protect

The landmark case Tarasoff v. Regents of the University of California established the legal foundation for the duty to protect third parties from client violence.

Tarasoff I (1974) -- Duty to Warn

The California Supreme Court initially ruled that therapists have a duty to warn identifiable potential victims when a client communicates a credible threat of violence.

Tarasoff II (1976) -- Duty to Protect

On rehearing, the court expanded the ruling to establish a broader duty to protect, which may be discharged through various means, not solely by warning the potential victim. Protective actions include:

  • Warning the intended victim directly
  • Notifying law enforcement
  • Hospitalizing the client (voluntary or involuntary)
  • Increasing the frequency of therapy sessions
  • Modifying the treatment plan to reduce risk
  • Other reasonable steps to protect the identified victim
Elements Required for Tarasoff Duty

Most jurisdictions require: (1) a special relationship exists between the therapist and the dangerous individual (i.e., a treatment relationship); (2) the client makes a credible, serious threat of physical violence; and (3) the threat is directed toward an identifiable potential victim.

Variations Across Jurisdictions

Not all states have adopted the Tarasoff ruling. Some states have enacted statutes that impose a duty to warn or protect, while others have rejected it entirely. On the EPPP, the general principle tested is awareness that such a duty exists and knowing the basic elements required to trigger it.

Example

A client tells their therapist, "I'm going to kill my ex-wife when she picks up the kids on Friday." This statement involves a serious threat, an identifiable victim, and an apparent timeline. The therapist has a duty to take protective action, which may include warning the ex-wife, contacting police, and potentially seeking involuntary hospitalization for the client.

HIPAA: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

The HIPAA Privacy Rule (2003) established federal standards for protecting individually identifiable health information (Protected Health Information, or PHI). Key provisions for psychologists include:

Key HIPAA Concepts

ConceptDefinition
Protected Health Information (PHI)Any individually identifiable health information transmitted or maintained in any form (electronic, paper, oral)
Covered EntityHealth care providers who transmit health information electronically, health plans, and health care clearinghouses
Minimum Necessary StandardCovered entities must make reasonable efforts to use, disclose, and request only the minimum amount of PHI needed for the purpose
Notice of Privacy PracticesCovered entities must provide clients with a notice describing how their PHI may be used and disclosed
Psychotherapy NotesNotes recorded by a mental health professional documenting or analyzing the contents of a therapy session, maintained separately from the medical record. These receive extra protection under HIPAA.

Psychotherapy Notes Under HIPAA

Psychotherapy notes are afforded greater protection than other PHI. Authorization from the client is generally required for disclosure of psychotherapy notes, even to insurance companies. Psychotherapy notes do NOT include:

  • Medication prescription and monitoring
  • Session start and stop times
  • Treatment modalities and frequencies
  • Results of clinical tests
  • Diagnosis, functional status, treatment plan, symptoms, prognosis, and progress to date
EPPP Tip

HIPAA psychotherapy notes are ONLY the process notes about the content of therapy sessions, kept separately from the medical record. Treatment plans, diagnoses, and progress notes are NOT psychotherapy notes under HIPAA and can be shared for treatment, payment, and healthcare operations without special authorization.

Confidentiality with Minors

Working with minors presents unique confidentiality challenges because the legal right to consent (and access information) typically belongs to the parent or legal guardian, while the therapeutic relationship depends on the minor's trust.

  • Parents generally have the right to access their minor child's treatment records.
  • However, many practitioners negotiate a limited confidentiality agreement at the outset, explaining to both parents and the minor what will and will not be shared.
  • The psychologist should inform the minor about the limits of confidentiality in age-appropriate language.
  • In many states, minors can consent to their own mental health treatment at age 12-16 (depending on the jurisdiction), which may alter confidentiality obligations.
  • Mandatory reporting obligations (e.g., child abuse) always override confidentiality agreements with minors.

Confidentiality in Group Therapy

In group therapy, the psychologist cannot guarantee that other group members will maintain confidentiality. The ethical obligation is to:

  • Discuss the importance of confidentiality with all group members at the outset
  • Establish group rules regarding confidentiality
  • Inform members that the psychologist cannot guarantee that other group members will not disclose information
  • Address breaches of confidentiality if they occur within the group

Mandated Reporting

Psychologists are mandated reporters in all 50 states for suspected child abuse and neglect. Key points:

  • Reporting is based on reasonable suspicion, not certainty. Psychologists do not need to conduct an investigation before reporting.
  • Most states extend mandated reporting to elder abuse and abuse of dependent adults.
  • Failure to report when mandated can result in criminal penalties, civil liability, and ethics violations.
  • Good-faith reports are typically protected by immunity statutes that shield reporters from liability even if the report proves unfounded.
  • Mandated reporting obligations override both confidentiality and privilege.

Standard 4.05: Disclosures

Psychologists may disclose confidential information with the appropriate consent of the client or as mandated or permitted by law for valid purposes including:

  • Providing professional services to the client
  • Obtaining professional consultations
  • Protecting the client, psychologist, or others from harm
  • Obtaining payment for services

Standard 4.06: Consultations

When consulting with colleagues, psychologists do not disclose confidential information that could reasonably lead to the identification of a client unless they have obtained prior consent or the disclosure cannot be avoided. They share only information necessary for the consultation purpose.

Key Takeaways for the EPPP

  • Privilege belongs to the client, not the therapist. Only the client (or their legal representative) can waive privilege.
  • A subpoena alone does not compel disclosure; a court order does. When receiving a subpoena, consult an attorney and notify the client.
  • The Tarasoff duty requires a special relationship, a credible serious threat, and an identifiable victim. The duty to protect can be fulfilled by means other than warning the victim.
  • Under HIPAA, psychotherapy notes (process notes kept separately) receive extra protection and generally require separate authorization for disclosure.
  • With minors, parents generally have access to records, but practitioners should negotiate confidentiality limits at the outset.
  • In group therapy, the psychologist cannot guarantee other members' confidentiality but must establish clear expectations.
  • Mandated reporting (child abuse, elder abuse) overrides confidentiality and privilege, and is based on reasonable suspicion, not certainty.
