Advertising & Public Statements (Std 5)

False statements, testimonials, solicitation
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of Standard 5: Advertising and Other Public Statements

Standard 5 (Advertising and Other Public Statements) of the APA Ethics Code addresses truthfulness in professional representations. Psychologists have an obligation to ensure that all public statements about their services, qualifications, and research are accurate, not deceptive, and do not mislead the public.

This standard is tested on the EPPP because violations in advertising can directly harm the public and undermine trust in the profession.

Standard 5: Advertising and Other Public Statements

5.01: Avoidance of False or Deceptive Statements

Psychologists do not knowingly make public statements that are false, deceptive, or fraudulent concerning their research, practice, or other work activities. This prohibition applies to:

  • Training, experience, or competence
  • Academic degrees and credentials
  • Institutional or association affiliations
  • Services offered and their scientific or clinical basis
  • Fees charged
  • Publications or research findings

Psychologists do not make false, deceptive, or fraudulent statements concerning the degree of success of their services, and they claim only degrees as credentials that were earned from regionally accredited educational institutions or that are the basis for psychology licensure.

Example

A psychologist advertises a "95% success rate" for treating anxiety disorders without any empirical basis for this claim. This violates Standard 5.01 because it is a deceptive statement about the degree of success of services. Vague or unsubstantiated claims of effectiveness are prohibited.

5.02: Statements by Others

Psychologists who engage others to create or place public statements that promote their professional practice retain professional responsibility for such statements. If psychologists learn of deceptive statements about their work made by others, they make reasonable efforts to correct such statements.

  • Psychologists do not compensate employees of press, radio, television, or other communication media in return for publicity in a news item.
  • A paid advertisement must be identified as such.

5.03: Descriptions of Workshops and Non-Degree-Granting Educational Programs

Psychologists responsible for workshops, seminars, and other non-degree programs ensure that descriptions are accurate regarding:

  • The target audience
  • Educational objectives
  • The presenters and their qualifications
  • Fees and any prerequisites

5.04: Media Presentations

When psychologists provide public advice or comment via print, internet, mail, television, radio, or other electronic media, they take precautions to ensure that statements are:

  • Based on appropriate psychological literature and practice
  • Consistent with the Ethics Code
  • Not indicative of a professional relationship with the recipient

5.05: Testimonials

Psychologists do not solicit testimonials from current therapy clients or other persons who, because of their particular circumstances, are vulnerable to undue influence. This reflects the power differential in the therapy relationship and the potential for coercion, even if unintentional.

EPPP Tip

The prohibition on soliciting testimonials applies to current therapy clients and others vulnerable to undue influence. The standard does not explicitly prohibit testimonials from former clients, but careful ethical analysis is warranted because former clients may remain vulnerable to the influence of their former therapist.

5.06: In-Person Solicitation

Psychologists do not engage, directly or through agents, in uninvited in-person solicitation of business from actual or potential therapy clients or other persons who, because of their particular circumstances, are vulnerable to undue influence. This does not prohibit:

  • General marketing or advertising that is not targeted at vulnerable individuals
  • Providing disaster relief or community outreach services
  • Responding to individuals who initiate contact seeking services

Key Takeaways for the EPPP

  • All public statements must be truthful and non-deceptive -- psychologists cannot claim unsubstantiated success rates, misleading credentials, or false affiliations.
  • Psychologists retain responsibility for statements made by others on their behalf (Standard 5.02).
  • Testimonials may not be solicited from current therapy clients or others vulnerable to undue influence.
  • In-person solicitation of vulnerable individuals is prohibited, but general marketing is permissible.
  • Workshop and program descriptions must accurately represent objectives, presenters, fees, and prerequisites.
  • Media presentations must be based on appropriate literature and must not imply a professional relationship with the audience.
