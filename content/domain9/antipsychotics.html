Typical vs atypical, D2 blockade, side effects

Introduction to Antipsychotic Medications

Antipsychotic medications (also called neuroleptics) are the primary pharmacological treatment for schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders. They are also used for bipolar disorder, severe agitation, Tourette's disorder, and as adjuncts for treatment-resistant depression. Antipsychotics are divided into two main classes: first-generation (typical) and second-generation (atypical).

For the EPPP, understanding the differences between these two classes -- particularly their mechanisms of action, side effect profiles, and the major adverse effects (EPS, tardive dyskinesia, NMS, and metabolic syndrome) -- is essential.

The Dopamine Hypothesis of Schizophrenia

The dopamine hypothesis proposes that schizophrenia symptoms result from dysregulated dopamine activity in the brain:

  • Positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought) are associated with excess dopamine activity in the mesolimbic pathway
  • Negative symptoms (flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia) are associated with reduced dopamine activity in the mesocortical pathway (prefrontal cortex)

This helps explain why typical antipsychotics, which broadly block dopamine D2 receptors, effectively treat positive symptoms but often worsen negative symptoms and cause movement disorders (due to dopamine blockade in the nigrostriatal pathway).

Four Dopamine Pathways

Mesolimbic: Excess dopamine leads to positive symptoms. Mesocortical: Reduced dopamine leads to negative and cognitive symptoms. Nigrostriatal: Dopamine blockade here causes EPS and tardive dyskinesia. Tuberoinfundibular: Dopamine blockade here increases prolactin, causing hyperprolactinemia.

First-Generation (Typical) Antipsychotics

First-generation antipsychotics (FGAs) work primarily by blocking dopamine D2 receptors. They are classified by potency, which determines their side effect profile.

High-Potency Typical Antipsychotics

Require lower doses; greater risk of EPS; lower risk of sedation and anticholinergic effects.

Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) are movement disorders caused by dopamine D2 blockade in the nigrostriatal pathway. They are most common with high-potency typical antipsychotics.

Acute dystoniaHours to daysSustained muscle contractions (torticollis, oculogyric crisis, opisthotonus); can be life-threatening if laryngeal muscles affectedAnticholinergics (benztropine/Cogentin, diphenhydramine/Benadryl)

Parkinsonism (pseudoparkinsonism)Weeks to monthsBradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor, shuffling gait, mask-like faciesAnticholinergics (benztropine); amantadine; dose reduction

EPPP Tip

Know the timeline: acute dystonia appears within hours to days, akathisia within days to weeks, parkinsonism within weeks to months, and tardive dyskinesia after months to years of use. TD is the most feared because it may be irreversible and paradoxically may worsen when the offending drug is discontinued.

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

NMS is a rare but life-threatening medical emergency caused by antipsychotic medications (especially high-potency typicals, but can occur with any antipsychotic). Mortality rate is approximately 10-20% if untreated.

Clinical Features of NMS (mnemonic: FEVER)

  • Fever (hyperthermia, often >40C/104F)
  • Encephalopathy (altered mental status, confusion, delirium)
  • Vitals unstable (autonomic instability: tachycardia, labile blood pressure, diaphoresis)
  • Elevated enzymes (elevated creatine phosphokinase/CPK from muscle breakdown)
  • Rigidity (severe "lead pipe" muscle rigidity)

Treatment of NMS

  • Immediate discontinuation of the antipsychotic
  • Supportive care (cooling, IV fluids, cardiac monitoring)
  • Dantrolene (muscle relaxant) for rigidity
  • Bromocriptine (dopamine agonist) to restore dopaminergic activity
  • Benzodiazepines may be used adjunctively
NMS vs. Serotonin Syndrome

Both involve hyperthermia and altered mental status, but NMS features lead-pipe rigidity and develops over days, while serotonin syndrome features hyperreflexia, clonus, and myoclonus and develops within hours. NMS is caused by dopamine blockade; serotonin syndrome by serotonin excess.

Second-Generation (Atypical) Antipsychotics

Atypical antipsychotics block both dopamine D2 receptors and serotonin 5-HT2A receptors. This dual mechanism, known as serotonin-dopamine antagonism (SDA), is thought to reduce EPS risk and improve negative symptoms compared to typical antipsychotics.

EPPP Tip

Key facts to know: Clozapine = treatment-resistant schizophrenia + agranulocytosis risk + mandatory blood monitoring. Olanzapine = worst metabolic side effects. Aripiprazole = unique partial D2 agonist mechanism. Risperidone = highest prolactin elevation among atypicals.

Metabolic Syndrome

Second-generation antipsychotics (especially clozapine and olanzapine) carry significant risk of metabolic syndrome, which includes:

  • Weight gain (often 10-30+ pounds)
  • Dyslipidemia (elevated triglycerides, decreased HDL)
  • Hyperglycemia / Type 2 diabetes
  • Hypertension

Metabolic monitoring (fasting glucose, lipid panel, weight, waist circumference, blood pressure) is recommended at baseline and at regular intervals for all patients on atypical antipsychotics.

Metabolic Risk by Medication (High to Low)

  1. Clozapine and Olanzapine (highest risk)
  2. Quetiapine and Risperidone (moderate risk)
  3. Aripiprazole, Ziprasidone, and Lurasidone (lowest risk)

Key Takeaways for the EPPP

  • Typical antipsychotics work via D2 blockade; atypicals combine D2 and 5-HT2A blockade.
  • High-potency typicals (haloperidol) = high EPS risk, low sedation. Low-potency typicals (chlorpromazine) = low EPS risk, high sedation.
  • EPS timeline: acute dystonia (hours-days), akathisia (days-weeks), parkinsonism (weeks-months), tardive dyskinesia (months-years).
  • NMS is a medical emergency: hyperthermia, lead-pipe rigidity, autonomic instability, elevated CPK. Treat with dantrolene and bromocriptine.
  • Clozapine is the gold standard for treatment-resistant schizophrenia but requires blood monitoring for agranulocytosis.
  • Olanzapine has the highest metabolic risk among commonly used atypicals.
  • Aripiprazole is unique as a partial D2 agonist.
  • Metabolic monitoring is recommended for all patients on atypical antipsychotics.
  • Tardive dyskinesia may be irreversible and can worsen when the offending drug is stopped.
