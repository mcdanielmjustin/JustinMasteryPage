Nature vs. Nurture

Genetics, epigenetics, gene-environment interactions
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Overview of the Nature-Nurture Debate

The nature-nurture question is one of the oldest and most fundamental issues in psychology: To what extent are human characteristics determined by genetic inheritance (nature) versus environmental experience (nurture)? Contemporary developmental psychology has moved well beyond this dichotomy, recognizing that virtually all traits arise from complex interactions between genes and environments.

For the EPPP, candidates must understand behavioral genetics methodology, the concept of heritability, epigenetic mechanisms, and the specific types of gene-environment interactions and correlations that shape development.

Behavioral Genetics

Behavioral genetics is the field that examines the relative contributions of genetic and environmental factors to individual differences in behavior and psychological traits. It relies on several key research designs to disentangle genetic from environmental influences.

Twin Studies

Twin studies compare monozygotic (MZ) twins (who share 100% of their DNA) with dizygotic (DZ) twins (who share approximately 50% of their segregating genes, like any siblings). The logic is straightforward: if MZ twins are more similar on a trait than DZ twins, genetic factors are implicated.

Key Terminology

Concordance rate: The probability that both twins exhibit a given trait when one twin has it. Higher concordance in MZ vs. DZ twins suggests genetic influence.

Equal environments assumption: The critical assumption in twin studies that MZ and DZ twins share environmental influences to the same degree. Violations of this assumption can inflate heritability estimates.

The landmark Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart (Bouchard et al., 1990) found that MZ twins raised in different families showed remarkably similar IQ scores (correlation ~.70), personality traits, and even behavioral quirks, providing compelling evidence for strong genetic influences on many psychological characteristics.

Adoption Studies

Adoption studies compare adopted children with both their biological parents (who share genes but not environment) and their adoptive parents (who share environment but not genes). If adopted children more closely resemble their biological parents on a trait, genetic factors are implicated; if they more resemble their adoptive parents, environmental factors are suggested.

The Colorado Adoption Project (Plomin et al., 1997) followed adopted and non-adopted children longitudinally, revealing that the correlation between adopted children and their biological parents on cognitive measures actually increases with age, while correlations with adoptive parents decrease -- a finding consistent with increasing genetic influence over time.

Clinical Example: Adoption Study Interpretation

A researcher examines schizophrenia rates among adults who were adopted at birth. The biological mothers of the adopted-away children had schizophrenia. Results show that these adoptees have elevated schizophrenia rates (~10-15%) compared to adoptees whose biological parents had no psychiatric history (~1-2%), despite being raised by non-schizophrenic adoptive parents. This classic design (Heston, 1966; Kety et al., 1968) demonstrates genetic vulnerability while also highlighting that the majority of at-risk adoptees do not develop the disorder, pointing to the importance of environmental factors.

Heritability

Heritability (h-squared) is a statistic that estimates the proportion of variance in a trait within a population that is attributable to genetic differences among individuals. It ranges from 0.0 (no genetic contribution to individual differences) to 1.0 (all variation due to genetic differences).

Critical Misunderstandings About Heritability

Heritability applies to populations, not individuals. A heritability of .50 for intelligence does NOT mean that 50% of an individual's intelligence is genetic.

Heritability does not mean immutability. A highly heritable trait can still be strongly influenced by environmental intervention (e.g., PKU is nearly 100% genetic but intellectual disability is prevented by dietary modification).

Heritability is context-dependent. It changes across populations, ages, and environments. When environments are more uniform, heritability estimates are higher because less environmental variation exists.

Heritability cannot explain group differences. Even if a trait is highly heritable within groups, differences between groups may be entirely environmental in origin (Lewontin, 1970).

Heritability estimates for major psychological traits include approximately .50 for general intelligence (with estimates increasing from childhood to adulthood), .40-.60 for personality traits (Big Five), and .60-.80 for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder.

Shared vs. Nonshared Environment

Behavioral genetics partitions environmental influence into two components:

  • Shared environment (c-squared): Environmental factors that make siblings more similar to each other (e.g., family SES, parenting style, neighborhood). Surprisingly, shared environment accounts for relatively little variance in most traits by adulthood.
  • Nonshared environment (e-squared): Environmental factors that make siblings different from each other (e.g., differential parental treatment, unique peer groups, random events, measurement error). This component is typically the largest environmental contributor to individual differences.

Plomin and Daniels (1987) published a highly influential paper asking "Why are children in the same family so different from one another?" Their conclusion -- that nonshared environmental influences dominate over shared ones for most psychological traits -- reshaped how psychologists think about family influence.

Epigenetics

Epigenetics refers to changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the DNA sequence itself. These modifications determine whether specific genes are "turned on" or "turned off" and can be influenced by environmental factors including stress, nutrition, toxins, and early caregiving experiences.

Key Terminology

DNA methylation: The addition of methyl groups to DNA, typically silencing gene expression. Increased methylation of a gene promoter region generally reduces that gene's activity.

Histone modification: Chemical changes to the histone proteins around which DNA is wound, affecting how tightly DNA is packaged and thus how accessible genes are for transcription.

Transgenerational epigenetic inheritance: The transmission of epigenetic marks from one generation to the next without changes in DNA sequence.

A groundbreaking study by Meaney and colleagues (Weaver et al., 2004) demonstrated that rat pups who received high levels of maternal licking and grooming showed altered methylation patterns at the glucocorticoid receptor gene, resulting in lower stress reactivity throughout life. This effect was reversible through cross-fostering, demonstrating a direct mechanism by which early caregiving shapes gene expression and stress physiology.

Clinical Implications

Epigenetics provides a biological mechanism for understanding how early adversity (e.g., childhood abuse, neglect) can have lasting effects on stress reactivity, mental health, and even physical health. Studies of human suicide victims with histories of childhood abuse have found increased methylation of the glucocorticoid receptor gene in the hippocampus (McGowan et al., 2009), paralleling the animal findings. This research supports the importance of early intervention and suggests that environmental enrichment may help reverse adverse epigenetic modifications.

Gene-Environment Interactions and Correlations

Gene-Environment Interaction (GxE)

Gene-environment interaction occurs when the effect of a gene depends on the environment (or vice versa). The same genetic variant may lead to different outcomes depending on environmental conditions.

The classic example is Caspi et al. (2003), who found that individuals with the short allele of the 5-HTTLPR serotonin transporter gene were more likely to develop depression following stressful life events than those with the long allele. The gene alone did not cause depression, nor did stress alone -- it was the interaction that mattered. (Note: This finding has been debated in subsequent replication attempts, highlighting the complexity of GxE research.)

Gene-Environment Correlations (rGE)

Scarr and McCartney (1983) described three types of gene-environment correlation, which explain how genes and environments become linked:

TypeDescriptionExampleAge Trend
Passive rGEParents provide both genes and environment; the child passively receives bothMusically gifted parents pass on musical genes AND provide instruments and lessonsStrongest in early childhood
Evocative rGEThe child's genetically influenced traits elicit particular responses from the environmentA temperamentally easy baby elicits more positive parenting than a difficult babyStable across development
Active rGEThe child actively selects and creates environments compatible with genetic predispositions (also called "niche-picking")An intellectually curious child seeks out libraries, stimulating peers, and academic challengesIncreases with age as autonomy grows

Clinical Example: Diathesis-Stress Model

The diathesis-stress model is a specific form of gene-environment interaction in which a genetic vulnerability (diathesis) is expressed as a disorder only when triggered by environmental stress. For example, an individual may carry genetic risk for anxiety disorders but only develop symptoms following significant life stressors. The more recently proposed differential susceptibility hypothesis (Belsky & Pluess, 2009) extends this concept, suggesting that the same genetic variants that confer vulnerability to adversity also confer enhanced sensitivity to positive environments -- making some individuals "orchids" (highly sensitive to environment, for better or worse) and others "dandelions" (resilient regardless of environment).

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Heritability is a population statistic, not an individual measure -- it cannot explain group differences and does not imply immutability
  • Know the three types of gene-environment correlation (passive, evocative, active) and their developmental trajectories (passive decreases, active increases with age)
  • Distinguish between gene-environment interaction (GxE) and gene-environment correlation (rGE)
  • Understand the logic of twin studies (MZ vs. DZ comparisons) and adoption studies (biological vs. adoptive parent comparisons)
  • Know that nonshared environment is typically more important than shared environment for most psychological traits (Plomin & Daniels, 1987)
  • Understand the diathesis-stress model and the differential susceptibility hypothesis as models of gene-environment interaction
  • Be familiar with epigenetics as a mechanism by which environment alters gene expression without changing DNA sequence
  • Heritability of IQ increases with age (from ~.40 in childhood to ~.60-.80 in adulthood) -- a counterintuitive finding
  • The equal environments assumption is a critical methodological issue in twin studies
