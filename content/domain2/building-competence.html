Building Competence — Middle Childhood

Domain 2: Lifespan & Developmental Stages ~25 min read
Flashcards Quick Quiz: Fundamental Advanced Learning

Piaget's Concrete Operational Stage

The concrete operational stage (approximately ages 7–11) represents a major cognitive watershed. Children now think logically about concrete, tangible events. The hallmark limitations of preoperational thought—egocentrism, centration, irreversibility—give way to more flexible, organized reasoning. However, children at this stage still struggle with abstract or hypothetical problems.

Conservation

Conservation

The understanding that certain physical properties of objects (number, length, liquid quantity, mass, area, weight, volume) remain unchanged despite changes in their outward appearance. Conservation develops gradually, with number and length typically mastered first (around age 6–7) and volume last (around age 11–12). This staggered acquisition is called horizontal décalage.

Classification and Seriation

Classification is the ability to group objects according to shared characteristics and to understand class inclusion relationships (e.g., understanding that "dogs" is a subset of "animals"). Seriation is the ability to arrange objects in a logical order along a quantitative dimension (e.g., shortest to tallest). Transitivity—the ability to infer the relationship between two items based on their relationships with a third item (if A > B and B > C, then A > C)—also emerges during this period.

Exam Example

A child is shown a set of sticks of varying lengths and asked to arrange them from shortest to longest. A preoperational child may use trial-and-error, while a concrete operational child systematically selects the shortest stick first, then the next shortest, demonstrating seriation ability.

Reversibility and Decentration

Reversibility—the ability to mentally reverse an action or operation—is a key achievement that enables conservation. The concrete operational child understands that pouring water back into the original container would restore the original appearance. Decentration—the ability to consider multiple aspects of a situation simultaneously—allows the child to attend to both height and width when judging volume.

Piaget, J., & Inhelder, B. (1969). The psychology of the child. Basic Books.

Kohlberg's Theory of Moral Development

Lawrence Kohlberg (1927–1987) expanded upon Piaget's earlier work on moral judgment to propose a comprehensive stage theory of moral reasoning. Using moral dilemmas (most famously the Heinz dilemma), Kohlberg identified three levels of moral development, each containing two stages.

Preconventional (ages ~4–10)Stage 1: Punishment & ObedienceAvoiding punishment; "might makes right"
Stage 2: Instrumental PurposeSelf-interest; "what's in it for me?"; reciprocal exchange
Conventional (ages ~10–adult)Stage 3: Interpersonal Conformity"Good boy/good girl" orientation; meeting others' expectations; maintaining relationships
Stage 4: Law & OrderMaintaining social order; following rules and laws; duty to society
Postconventional (not reached by all adults)Stage 5: Social ContractRights, democratic principles; laws serve the greater good but can be changed

The Heinz Dilemma

A man's wife is dying of cancer. A druggist has discovered a cure but is charging $2,000—ten times what the drug costs to make. Heinz can only raise $1,000. Should he steal the drug? Kohlberg's scoring focuses not on whether the person says Heinz should or should not steal, but on the reasoning behind the judgment.

Exam Example

A child says Heinz should not steal the drug because "he'll get in trouble and go to jail." This reasoning reflects Stage 1 (preconventional), where morality is based on avoidance of punishment. In contrast, an adult who says Heinz should steal because "the right to life supersedes the right to property" is reasoning at Stage 6 (postconventional).

Gilligan's Ethic of Care

Carol Gilligan (1982) critiqued Kohlberg's theory as reflecting a male-centered justice orientation and proposed an alternative ethic of care. Gilligan argued that women's moral reasoning tends to emphasize relationships, compassion, and responsibility to others, which Kohlberg's framework undervalued. She proposed three levels of moral development in women: (1) self-interest, (2) self-sacrifice and caring for others, and (3) a balanced ethic of care that includes both self and others.

Clinical Consideration

Empirical research has produced mixed support for Gilligan's claim of systematic gender differences in moral reasoning. Meta-analyses have found that when differences appear, they tend to be small. However, Gilligan's work importantly highlighted the role of cultural context and relational orientation in moral development, broadening the field beyond a purely justice-based framework.

Kohlberg, L. (1984). Essays on moral development: Vol. 2. The psychology of moral development. Harper & Row.

Gilligan, C. (1982). In a different voice. Harvard University Press.

Self-Concept and Self-Esteem

During middle childhood, children develop increasingly complex and realistic self-representations. Unlike the overly positive and concrete self-descriptions of early childhood ("I'm fast," "I have a dog"), school-age children begin to describe themselves in terms of psychological traits, social comparisons, and competencies across multiple domains.

Erikson's Industry vs. Inferiority

Industry vs. Inferiority

Erikson's fourth psychosocial stage (approximately ages 6–11). Children develop a sense of industry—competence and mastery—through productive work in school, sports, hobbies, and social activities. Failure to develop competence leads to feelings of inferiority and inadequacy. The virtue that emerges from successful resolution is competence.

Susan Harter's Self-Perception Model

Susan Harter demonstrated that by middle childhood, self-esteem becomes differentiated into distinct domains: academic competence, social acceptance, athletic competence, physical appearance, and behavioral conduct. Global self-worth depends on both the child's self-perceived competence in each domain and the importance they attach to that domain. A child who values academics and performs well will have higher self-esteem than one who values academics but struggles.

Exam Example

A 9-year-old who excels at reading but is poor at sports has high self-esteem if she values academic achievement and places less importance on athletics. According to Harter, the discrepancy between perceived competence and the importance of a domain predicts self-esteem.

Harter, S. (2012). The construction of the self: Developmental and sociocultural foundations (2nd ed.). Guilford Press.

Peer Relationships and Friendship

Peer relationships become increasingly important during middle childhood, as children spend more time with age-mates and less time under direct adult supervision. The nature of friendships shifts from proximity-based and activity-based connections to relationships grounded in mutual trust, loyalty, and reciprocity.

Selman's Stages of Perspective-Taking

Robert Selman proposed a stage model of social perspective-taking that parallels cognitive development:

  1. Stage 0 (3–6 years): Egocentric; undifferentiated perspective-taking.
  2. Stage 1 (5–9 years): Social-informational; recognizes that others may have different information.
  3. Stage 2 (7–12 years): Self-reflective; can step into another's shoes and reflect on own behavior from their perspective.
  4. Stage 3 (10–15 years): Mutual; can simultaneously consider own and other's perspective.
  5. Stage 4 (14–adult): Societal; understands that third-party or societal perspectives exist.

Peer Status Categories

Sociometric research has identified five peer status categories based on peer nominations: popular (many liked, few disliked), rejected (few liked, many disliked), neglected (few liked, few disliked), controversial (many liked, many disliked), and average. Rejected children are at highest risk for later adjustment difficulties, particularly rejected-aggressive children, who show elevated rates of externalizing problems and delinquency.

Clinical Application

Chronic peer rejection in middle childhood is one of the strongest predictors of later maladjustment, including school dropout, delinquency, and substance use. Social skills training interventions targeting rejected children aim to improve social cognition, emotion regulation, and behavioral repertoire to break the cycle of rejection.

Clinical Note: Bullying

Bullying peaks during the transition to middle school. Olweus (1993) identified characteristics of both bullies (dominant, low empathy, positive attitudes toward aggression) and victims (anxious, insecure, physically weaker). Bully-victims—children who are both perpetrators and targets—show the worst psychological outcomes. Evidence-based prevention programs (e.g., Olweus Bullying Prevention Program) address the school climate systemically.

Learning Disabilities and ADHD

Middle childhood is the period when learning disabilities and attention difficulties are most commonly identified, as the academic demands of formal schooling expose underlying cognitive processing weaknesses.

Specific Learning Disorders

The DSM-5 uses the umbrella term Specific Learning Disorder with specifiers for the affected academic domain: reading (dyslexia), written expression (dysgraphia), and mathematics (dyscalculia). Key diagnostic features include academic skills substantially below age expectations despite adequate instruction, not better explained by intellectual disability, sensory deficits, or psychosocial adversity.

Dyslexia

The most common specific learning disorder, characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition, poor spelling, and poor decoding. Research implicates deficits in phonological processing—the ability to identify and manipulate the sound structures of language. The prevalence is approximately 5–15% of school-age children, with higher rates in boys.

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

ADHD is characterized by a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning. The DSM-5 identifies three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, and combined. The prevalence is approximately 5–7% of children, with boys diagnosed more frequently than girls (though girls are more likely to have the inattentive presentation, which may be underidentified).

Exam Example

A teacher reports that an 8-year-old girl daydreams frequently, loses her homework, and has difficulty following multi-step instructions, but is not disruptive in class. Her grades have been declining despite average intelligence. This presentation is most consistent with ADHD, predominantly inattentive presentation—a subtype that is more common in girls and often missed due to the absence of hyperactive-impulsive behaviors.

American Psychiatric Association. (2013). Diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders (5th ed.).

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Concrete operations: Conservation (horizontal décalage), classification, seriation, transitivity, reversibility, and decentration. Children reason logically about concrete but not abstract problems.
  • Kohlberg's three levels and six stages: Know the reasoning (not just the answer) that defines each stage. Preconventional = self-interest; Conventional = social expectations; Postconventional = abstract principles.
  • Gilligan's critique: Kohlberg's theory may undervalue care-based reasoning. Gilligan proposed an ethic of care orientation, though empirical evidence for systematic gender differences in moral stage is mixed.
  • Industry vs. Inferiority (Erikson): Stage 4; virtue = competence. Children develop self-efficacy through mastering school, sports, and social tasks.
  • Peer status: Popular, rejected, neglected, controversial, average. Rejected-aggressive children are at highest risk for later maladjustment.
  • Harter's self-esteem model: Self-esteem = discrepancy between perceived competence and domain importance. Multiple domains differentiate in middle childhood.
  • Specific Learning Disorders: Dyslexia (phonological processing deficit), dyscalculia, dysgraphia. Discrepancy between ability and achievement.
  • ADHD presentations: Inattentive, hyperactive-impulsive, combined. Know that the inattentive presentation is more common in girls and often underdiagnosed.
