Overview of Attachment Theory

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and extended by Mary Ainsworth, is one of the most influential frameworks in developmental psychology. It describes the deep emotional bond between an infant and caregiver and explains how early relationship experiences shape socioemotional development, internal representations of self and others, and relationship patterns across the lifespan.

For the EPPP, attachment theory is a commonly tested topic that intersects with developmental psychology, personality, psychopathology, and psychotherapy. Candidates must understand Bowlby's theoretical framework, Ainsworth's classification system, internal working models, and the extension of attachment concepts to adult relationships.

Bowlby's Attachment Theory

John Bowlby (1907-1990) drew on ethology, psychoanalysis, and systems theory to propose that attachment is an evolved behavioral system designed to maintain proximity between an infant and caregiver, thereby ensuring the infant's survival. Bowlby challenged the then-dominant psychoanalytic view that infant-mother bonding was based on feeding (drive reduction) and the behaviorist view that attachment was a secondary drive.

Key Terminology

Attachment behavioral system: An innate system that motivates infants to seek proximity to an attachment figure, especially under conditions of threat, illness, or distress. This system operates as a "goal-corrected" feedback loop.

Attachment figure: A specific person (typically the primary caregiver) toward whom attachment behaviors are directed. Not all caregivers are attachment figures -- the key requirement is consistent, responsive caregiving.

Secure base: The attachment figure serves as a base from which the child explores the environment. When the child feels safe, exploration increases; when threatened, attachment behavior is activated.

Safe haven: The attachment figure as a source of comfort and security to which the child returns when distressed.

Bowlby's Phases of Attachment Development

  1. Pre-attachment phase (birth to 6 weeks): Infant signals (crying, grasping, smiling) are directed at anyone; no specific attachment
  2. Attachment-in-the-making (6 weeks to 6-8 months): Infant begins to show differential responding to familiar caregivers but does not protest separation
  3. Clear-cut attachment (6-8 months to 18-24 months): Specific attachment is evident; separation anxiety and stranger anxiety emerge; the child actively seeks proximity and protests when the attachment figure leaves
  4. Goal-corrected partnership (24 months onward): The child begins to understand the caregiver's goals and motives and can negotiate plans, enabling more flexible attachment behavior

Harlow's Monkey Studies

Harry Harlow's (1958) research with rhesus monkeys provided powerful evidence against drive-reduction theory. Infant monkeys separated from their mothers were given a choice between a wire "mother" that provided food and a cloth "mother" that provided no food but was covered in soft terrycloth. The monkeys overwhelmingly preferred the cloth mother, spending most of their time clinging to it and running to it when frightened. This demonstrated that contact comfort, not feeding, was the primary basis of attachment.

Monkeys raised in isolation showed severe and lasting social deficits, including inability to mate, neglect of offspring, and self-injurious behavior -- demonstrating the long-term consequences of attachment deprivation.

Ainsworth's Strange Situation

Mary Ainsworth (1913-1999) developed the Strange Situation procedure to systematically classify infant attachment patterns. This laboratory paradigm involves a series of eight 3-minute episodes in which a 12-18 month old infant is observed with the caregiver, with a stranger, and alone. The critical behaviors observed are the infant's response to separation from and reunion with the caregiver.

Ainsworth originally identified three attachment patterns; Main and Solomon (1986) later added a fourth:

Clinical Example: The Strange Situation in Practice

During a Strange Situation assessment, a 14-month-old is observed. When his mother leaves, he cries briefly and looks toward the door. When she returns, he crawls to her, reaches up to be held, and quickly calms when picked up, then returns to playing with toys within a minute. This infant demonstrates a secure attachment pattern -- he shows appropriate distress at separation, actively seeks reunion, is effectively soothed by the caregiver's return, and uses her as a secure base to resume exploration. In contrast, a disorganized infant might approach the returning parent but then suddenly freeze mid-approach, turn in circles, or fall prone on the floor -- behaviors reflecting the unresolvable conflict when the attachment figure is both the source of safety and the source of fear.

Internal Working Models

Bowlby proposed that through repeated interactions with caregivers, infants develop internal working models -- mental representations of the self, the attachment figure, and relationships. These models include:

  • Model of self: "Am I worthy of love and care?" (securely attached: "yes"; insecurely attached: "no" or "only sometimes")
  • Model of others: "Are others reliable, responsive, and trustworthy?" (securely attached: "yes"; insecurely attached: "no" or "unpredictable")

Internal working models are hypothesized to operate largely outside conscious awareness and to influence expectations, emotions, and behavior in subsequent relationships. While they tend to be stable over time, they are not immutable -- significant relationship experiences (positive or negative) can alter internal working models, a concept central to attachment-based psychotherapy.

Clinical Implications

Internal working models are the theoretical bridge between childhood attachment experiences and adult relationship patterns. In psychotherapy, the therapeutic relationship itself can serve as a "corrective emotional experience" that modifies insecure internal working models. Attachment-based therapies (e.g., Emotionally Focused Therapy for couples, Circle of Security for parent-child relationships) explicitly target internal working models by providing a secure therapeutic base from which clients can explore and revise their relational expectations. The concept of "earned security" refers to individuals who had insecure childhood attachments but developed secure adult attachment through therapy or other significant relationships.

Adult Attachment

Hazan and Shaver (1987) extended attachment theory to adult romantic relationships, proposing that romantic love is an attachment process. Adults' attachment orientations parallel the infant patterns:

Categorical Model (Hazan & Shaver)

  • Secure: Comfortable with intimacy and interdependence; trusting
  • Avoidant: Uncomfortable with closeness; values independence; suppresses attachment needs
  • Anxious-ambivalent: Preoccupied with relationships; fears abandonment; hyperactivates attachment system

Dimensional Model (Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991)

Bartholomew proposed a four-category model based on two dimensions: model of self (positive/negative) and model of others (positive/negative):

Adult Attachment Interview (AAI)

The Adult Attachment Interview, developed by Mary Main and colleagues (George, Kaplan, & Main, 1985), assesses adults' "state of mind with respect to attachment" through a semi-structured interview about childhood attachment experiences. Classification is based not on what happened but on how coherently and collaboratively the person narrates their attachment history:

  • Autonomous/Secure (F): Coherent, balanced narrative; values attachment relationships
  • Dismissing (Ds): Minimizes attachment; idealizes or dismisses parents; brief, incomplete narratives that contradict general positive statements
  • Preoccupied (E): Entangled with past attachment experiences; angry, confused, or passive narratives; excessive detail
  • Unresolved/Disorganized (U/d): Lapses in monitoring of reasoning or discourse when discussing loss or trauma (e.g., speaking of a deceased person as if alive)

Robust research demonstrates intergenerational transmission: a parent's AAI classification predicts their infant's Strange Situation classification with approximately 75% accuracy (van IJzendoorn, 1995).

Cross-Cultural Considerations

While the distribution of attachment patterns varies across cultures, the core patterns (especially secure attachment) are found universally. Van IJzendoorn and Kroonenberg (1988) meta-analyzed Strange Situation studies across cultures and found:

  • Secure attachment is the modal pattern in all cultures studied (~65% globally)
  • Western European/individualistic cultures tend to show higher rates of avoidant attachment
  • Japanese and Israeli kibbutz samples show higher rates of ambivalent/resistant attachment (possibly reflecting cultural practices rather than insecurity per se)
  • Within-culture variation is greater than between-culture variation

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Bowlby proposed attachment as an evolved behavioral system for survival; Harlow's monkeys showed contact comfort (not feeding) drives attachment
  • Know all four attachment types and their associated caregiver behavior: Secure (sensitive/responsive), Avoidant (rejecting), Ambivalent (inconsistent), Disorganized (frightening/frightened)
  • The Strange Situation classifies attachment based on reunion behavior, not separation behavior
  • Disorganized attachment is most strongly associated with maltreatment and is the greatest risk factor for later psychopathology
  • Internal working models are mental representations of self and others formed through early attachment experiences
  • The AAI classifies adults based on coherence of narrative, not content of childhood experiences
  • Intergenerational transmission: parent AAI classification predicts infant Strange Situation classification (~75% match)
  • Bartholomew's four-category model crosses model of self (positive/negative) with model of others (positive/negative)
  • Secure attachment is the majority pattern across all cultures studied
  • Separation anxiety and stranger anxiety emerge around 6-8 months, coinciding with the clear-cut attachment phase
  • "Earned security" -- adults with insecure childhood attachment who develop secure adult attachment -- demonstrates that working models can change
