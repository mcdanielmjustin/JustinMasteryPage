Foundations of Genetics

Human development begins at conception, when a sperm cell fertilizes an ovum to form a single-celled zygote containing 46 chromosomes—23 from each parent. Understanding the basic principles of genetics is essential for the EPPP, particularly as they relate to the inheritance of traits, genetic disorders, and the interplay between genotype and phenotype.

Genotype and Phenotype

Genotype vs. Phenotype

Genotype refers to the specific genetic makeup of an organism—the particular alleles inherited for a given trait. Phenotype refers to the observable characteristics that result from the interaction of the genotype with the environment. The same genotype can produce different phenotypes depending on environmental conditions.

Patterns of Inheritance

In dominant-recessive inheritance, a single dominant allele is sufficient to produce the trait in the phenotype, whereas the recessive trait appears only when the individual is homozygous recessive (carries two copies of the recessive allele). Examples include Huntington's disease (dominant) and cystic fibrosis (recessive).

Many traits follow more complex patterns, including codominance (both alleles fully expressed, as in AB blood type), incomplete dominance (blended expression), and polygenic inheritance (traits influenced by many genes, such as intelligence and temperament).

Phenylketonuria (PKU)

An autosomal recessive metabolic disorder in which the enzyme needed to break down phenylalanine is absent. If untreated, the accumulation of phenylalanine leads to severe intellectual disability. PKU is a classic example of how environment (diet) can modify genetic expression—a restricted diet begun in infancy prevents cognitive impairment.

Clinical Relevance

PKU screening is performed via the newborn heel-prick test (Guthrie test) in all U.S. states. It illustrates a core principle: a genetic condition does not inevitably produce disability if the environmental trigger is removed early. This concept is central to understanding the gene-environment interaction framework.

Plomin, R., DeFries, J. C., Knopik, V. S., & Neiderhiser, J. M. (2016). Top 10 replicated findings from behavioral genetics. Perspectives on Psychological Science, 11(1), 3–23.

Stages of Prenatal Development

Prenatal development unfolds across three distinct periods, each characterized by unique milestones and vulnerabilities. The timing of harmful exposures determines which structures are most affected, a principle known as organogenesis timing.

Germinal Period (Weeks 0–2)

The germinal period begins at fertilization and extends through implantation in the uterine wall (approximately days 10–14). During this phase, the zygote undergoes rapid cell division (cleavage), forming a blastocyst. The inner cell mass will become the embryo, while the outer layer (trophoblast) develops into support structures including the placenta. Approximately 50–60% of fertilized ova fail to implant successfully.

Embryonic Period (Weeks 3–8)

The embryonic period is the most critical phase of prenatal development. During these six weeks, all major organ systems begin to form through a process called organogenesis. The three germ layers—ectoderm (nervous system, skin), mesoderm (muscles, bones, circulatory system), and endoderm (digestive and respiratory systems)—differentiate into specialized tissues. The neural tube, which will become the brain and spinal cord, closes by approximately day 28.

Clinical Note

Neural tube defects (e.g., spina bifida, anencephaly) result from failure of the neural tube to close properly during weeks 3–4. Adequate maternal folic acid intake before and during early pregnancy significantly reduces this risk, which is why supplementation is recommended before conception.

Fetal Period (Weeks 9–Birth)

The fetal period is characterized by rapid growth, refinement of organ systems, and increasing behavioral complexity. By week 12, the fetus can move its limbs. By weeks 22–26, the age of viability is approached—the point at which survival outside the womb becomes possible with intensive medical support. During the third trimester, the brain undergoes rapid development, with extensive synapse formation and the beginnings of myelination.

Teratogens and Prenatal Risk Factors

A teratogen is any environmental agent that can cause damage to the developing embryo or fetus. The impact of a teratogen depends on several factors: the dose and duration of exposure, the genetic susceptibility of the organism, the timing of exposure (organ systems are most vulnerable during their period of most rapid formation), and the presence of other risk or protective factors.

Principles of Teratogenic Effects

1. Susceptibility depends on the developmental stage at the time of exposure.
2. Each teratogen has a characteristic pattern of abnormalities (specificity).
3. The effect is dose-dependent: greater exposure typically produces more severe effects.
4. Teratogens differ in their mechanisms of action at the cellular level.
5. The genotype of the mother and the embryo/fetus influences susceptibility.

Alcohol: Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders

Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) represent a continuum of effects resulting from prenatal alcohol exposure. The most severe form, Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS), is characterized by facial dysmorphology (smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, short palpebral fissures), growth retardation, and central nervous system dysfunction. Alcohol is the leading known preventable cause of intellectual disability in the Western world.

Exam Example

A child presents with a smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, small head circumference, and significant learning difficulties. The mother reports heavy alcohol use during pregnancy. This presentation is most consistent with Fetal Alcohol Syndrome, the most severe manifestation of prenatal alcohol exposure.

Other Teratogens

Additional teratogens of note include: tobacco (associated with low birth weight, preterm delivery, and SIDS risk); cocaine (vasoconstriction leading to placental abruption, preterm birth); prescription medications such as thalidomide (limb malformations), isotretinoin/Accutane (craniofacial, cardiac, and CNS defects), and certain antiepileptic drugs (e.g., valproic acid, associated with neural tube defects); and environmental toxins such as lead and mercury.

Clinical Application

Clinicians should be aware that the effects of prenatal substance exposure may not be fully apparent at birth but can emerge as developmental delays in cognition, language, and behavior during childhood. A thorough developmental history should always include questions about prenatal exposures.

Moore, K. L., Persaud, T. V. N., & Torchia, M. G. (2019). The developing human: Clinically oriented embryology (11th ed.). Elsevier.

Birth and Neonatal Assessment

The transition from intrauterine to extrauterine life is a critical period. Complications during labor and delivery can have lasting developmental consequences, making rapid assessment of the newborn essential.

Birth Complications

Key complications include anoxia (oxygen deprivation), which can lead to brain damage if prolonged; preterm birth (before 37 weeks gestation), associated with increased risk for respiratory distress, intraventricular hemorrhage, and later developmental delays; and low birth weight (below 2,500 grams), which can result from either prematurity or intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR).

Apgar Score

Developed by Virginia Apgar in 1952, this rapid assessment is performed at 1 and 5 minutes after birth. Five criteria are each scored 0–2: Appearance (skin color), Pulse (heart rate), Grimace (reflex irritability), Activity (muscle tone), and Respiration. Total scores range from 0–10, with 7–10 indicating good condition, 4–6 requiring some intervention, and 0–3 indicating a critical need for resuscitation.

Exam Example

A pediatric neurologist evaluates a 10-month-old and notes that the Babinski reflex is still present. While the Babinski reflex is normal in infants, its persistence beyond approximately 12 months (or its reappearance in adults) may indicate upper motor neuron damage and warrants further neurological investigation.

Exam Example

A neonate scored 3 at 1 minute and 6 at 5 minutes on the Apgar. The low initial score indicated the need for immediate medical intervention, while the improvement to 6 suggests partial response to resuscitation. The 5-minute score is the stronger predictor of long-term outcomes.

Maternal Factors Affecting Prenatal Development

Beyond specific teratogens, a range of maternal characteristics and conditions influence prenatal development. These factors interact with one another and with genetic susceptibility to produce cumulative effects on the developing organism.

Maternal Age

Advanced maternal age (typically defined as 35 or older at delivery) is associated with increased risk of chromosomal abnormalities, particularly Down syndrome, due to age-related changes in oocyte quality. The risk of trisomy 21 rises from approximately 1 in 1,250 at age 25 to 1 in 100 at age 40. Adolescent mothers face increased risks related to inadequate prenatal care, nutritional deficiencies, and social stressors.

Maternal Stress

Chronic maternal stress during pregnancy elevates cortisol levels, which can cross the placental barrier and affect fetal development. Research has linked prenatal stress to preterm birth, low birth weight, and later behavioral and emotional difficulties in offspring, including increased reactivity of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.

Nutrition

Adequate maternal nutrition is essential for optimal prenatal development. Key nutrients include folic acid (prevents neural tube defects), iron (supports oxygen transport and brain development), iodine (critical for thyroid function and cognitive development), and omega-3 fatty acids (support neural development). Severe maternal malnutrition is associated with low birth weight, cognitive impairment, and increased vulnerability to later disease.

Clinical Application

When conducting a comprehensive developmental history, clinicians should inquire about maternal nutrition, stress, substance use, infections, and complications during pregnancy and delivery. These factors form an essential part of the biopsychosocial formulation for any developmental concern.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Genotype vs. phenotype: Genotype is the genetic code; phenotype is what is expressed. PKU demonstrates that phenotype can be altered by environmental intervention even when genotype is fixed.
  • Three prenatal stages: Germinal (0–2 weeks, implantation), embryonic (3–8 weeks, organogenesis, most vulnerable to teratogens), fetal (9 weeks–birth, growth and refinement).
  • Teratogen principles: Timing is critical. The embryonic period is the most vulnerable. Effects are dose-dependent and influenced by genetic susceptibility.
  • FAS features: Smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, short palpebral fissures, microcephaly, intellectual disability. It is the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability.
  • TORCH infections: Know the acronym and the key prenatal effects of each, especially rubella (first trimester exposure is most dangerous).
  • Apgar scoring: Five criteria (Appearance, Pulse, Grimace, Activity, Respiration), scored 0–2 each at 1 and 5 minutes. The 5-minute score is more predictive of outcomes.
  • Neonatal reflexes: Know the major reflexes (rooting, sucking, Moro, Babinski, palmar grasp), their stimuli, responses, and when they should disappear. Persistence beyond expected age suggests neurological concern.
  • Chromosomal abnormalities: Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner (45,X), Klinefelter (47,XXY), Fragile X (FMR1 mutation). Know key features and inheritance patterns.
