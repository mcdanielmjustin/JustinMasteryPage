Overview of Temperament and Personality Development

Temperament refers to constitutionally based individual differences in emotional reactivity and self-regulation that appear early in life and show moderate stability across development. Personality development, particularly as conceptualized by Erikson's psychosocial stages and Marcia's identity statuses, describes how the self evolves across the lifespan in response to both biological maturation and social experience.

For the EPPP, candidates must understand the major temperament classification systems (Thomas & Chess, Kagan), how temperament interacts with environment to shape developmental outcomes, and Erikson's eight psychosocial stages in detail. These topics appear frequently and have direct relevance to clinical assessment and intervention.

Thomas and Chess: The New York Longitudinal Study

Alexander Thomas and Stella Chess (1977) conducted the landmark New York Longitudinal Study (NYLS), following 133 children from infancy into adulthood. They identified nine dimensions of temperament and classified children into three broad temperament types, plus a mixed category.

Nine Dimensions of Temperament

Three Temperament Types

Temperament Classifications

Easy temperament (~40% of sample): Regular biological rhythms, positive approach to new situations, high adaptability, generally positive mood, mild to moderate intensity of reactions. These children are generally a pleasure to parent and adjust readily to new routines.

Difficult temperament (~10% of sample): Irregular biological rhythms, withdrawal from new situations, slow adaptability, predominantly negative mood, intense reactions. These children are at higher risk for behavioral problems, particularly when there is poor goodness of fit with caregivers.

Slow-to-warm-up temperament (~15% of sample): Initially withdrawn and negative in response to new stimuli, but gradually adapt with repeated exposure. Low activity level and mild reactions. May be mislabeled as "shy" but can adjust well given patience and gentle encouragement.

Mixed/Unclassifiable (~35%): Did not fit neatly into any category, showing combinations of traits from multiple types.

Goodness of Fit

Thomas and Chess's most important clinical contribution was the concept of goodness of fit -- the match between a child's temperament and the demands and expectations of the environment (particularly the parents). When there is a good fit, development proceeds smoothly; when there is a poor fit, the child is at increased risk for behavioral and emotional problems.

Clinical Example: Goodness of Fit

A mother describes her 3-year-old as "impossible" -- the child has irregular sleep patterns, intense emotional reactions, and withdraws from new situations. The mother, who values structure and punctuality, feels frustrated and incompetent. Assessment reveals the child has a difficult temperament, and the mother's rigid expectations create a poor goodness of fit. Intervention focuses not on changing the child's temperament but on helping the mother adjust her expectations and parenting strategies: establishing predictable routines (accommodating the child's slower adaptability), preparing the child for transitions, allowing extra time for new situations, and reframing the child's intensity as a sign of passion rather than defiance. This approach exemplifies how temperament-informed parenting can improve outcomes without requiring temperament change.

Kagan's Behavioral Inhibition

Jerome Kagan (1994) conducted extensive longitudinal research on behavioral inhibition -- a temperamental trait characterized by heightened vigilance and fearfulness in response to unfamiliar people, objects, and situations. Approximately 15-20% of infants show consistently high reactivity (vigorous limb movements, crying, arching of back) to novel stimuli at 4 months of age.

Biological Basis

Kagan proposed that behavioral inhibition has a biological basis in a low threshold for arousal in the amygdala and associated limbic circuits. Behaviorally inhibited children show:

  • Higher resting heart rates and less heart rate variability
  • Greater cortisol reactivity to stress
  • More right frontal EEG asymmetry (associated with withdrawal motivation)
  • Larger pupil dilation in response to novel stimuli

Developmental Trajectory

Approximately two-thirds of highly reactive 4-month-olds become behaviorally inhibited toddlers, and roughly one-third to one-half of behaviorally inhibited toddlers go on to develop social anxiety disorder in adolescence (Kagan & Snidman, 2004). However, the trajectory is not deterministic:

  • Sensitive, supportive parenting can reduce the behavioral expression of inhibition
  • Overprotective or anxious parenting can exacerbate it
  • The biological reactivity often persists even when behavioral inhibition decreases, suggesting that inhibited children learn to manage (not eliminate) their reactivity

Clinical Implications

Behavioral inhibition is one of the strongest temperamental predictors of anxiety disorders, particularly social anxiety disorder. Early identification of behaviorally inhibited children allows for preventive intervention. Research supports the use of graduated exposure to novel situations, parent training to avoid accommodation of avoidance, and cognitive-behavioral strategies. Importantly, parents should be counseled that the goal is not to eliminate the child's temperamental sensitivity but to help the child develop coping skills that prevent the temperament from developing into a clinical disorder.

Erikson's Psychosocial Stages

Erik Erikson (1902-1994) proposed eight stages of psychosocial development spanning the entire lifespan. Each stage presents a central psychosocial crisis -- a conflict between two opposing tendencies that must be resolved for healthy development. Successful resolution results in a basic virtue or strength; unsuccessful resolution results in maladaptive tendencies.

Stage Details for EPPP

Trust vs. Mistrust: Resolved through consistent, responsive caregiving. Corresponds closely to Bowlby's attachment theory. When caregivers are unreliable, the infant develops mistrust of the world.

Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt: Toddlers need to explore and assert independence. Overly controlling or punitive parenting leads to shame and self-doubt. Corresponds to Piaget's sensorimotor/early preoperational period and the development of self-will.

Initiative vs. Guilt: Preschoolers initiate activities and assert power through play. If punished for initiative or made to feel that their desires are always wrong, excessive guilt develops.

Industry vs. Inferiority: School-age children develop competence through academic and social achievements. Failure or negative comparison leads to feelings of inferiority.

Identity vs. Role Confusion: The central crisis of adolescence. Successful resolution results in a coherent sense of self; failure leads to identity confusion. This stage has been extensively elaborated by James Marcia.

Generativity vs. Stagnation: Middle-aged adults seek to contribute to the next generation through parenting, mentoring, or creative work. Failure leads to self-absorption and stagnation.

Identity Development: Marcia's Identity Statuses

James Marcia (1966) operationalized Erikson's identity concept by proposing four identity statuses based on two dimensions: whether the individual has experienced an identity crisis (a period of active exploration of alternatives) and whether they have made a commitment (a firm decision about identity elements such as occupation, ideology, and relationships).

StatusCrisis (Exploration)CommitmentDescription
Identity AchievementYes (resolved)YesHas explored alternatives and made firm commitments. Associated with the best psychological outcomes: high self-esteem, mature reasoning, and strong relationships.
MoratoriumCurrently in crisisNo (not yet)Actively exploring identity alternatives but has not yet committed. Often characterized by anxiety and questioning, but this is a healthy and necessary process.
ForeclosureNoYesHas made commitments without exploration -- typically adopting parents' or authority figures' values without question. Appears stable but may be rigid and defensive when beliefs are challenged.
Identity DiffusionNoNoNo exploration and no commitment. May appear apathetic or aimless. Associated with the poorest outcomes when it persists. In early adolescence, this is normative; in late adolescence/adulthood, it is concerning.

Clinical Example: Assessing Identity Status

A college counseling center psychologist works with a 20-year-old pre-med student who reports feeling lost and questioning everything. She originally chose medicine because "my parents are both doctors and it's what I've always been expected to do," but she is now intensely interested in art and environmental science. She feels anxious, guilty, and uncertain. This student appears to be transitioning from foreclosure (committed without exploration, based on parental expectations) to moratorium (actively exploring alternatives, experiencing productive anxiety). The psychologist normalizes this process as healthy identity work rather than pathologizing the distress, while helping the student explore her values, interests, and the distinction between her own goals and her parents' expectations.

Clinical Implications

Identity development continues beyond adolescence, and many adults revisit identity questions during major life transitions (career change, divorce, retirement). Marcia's framework is useful in clinical assessment for understanding clients' developmental challenges. Moratorium, while anxious, is a necessary precursor to identity achievement. Foreclosed individuals may present for therapy when life events challenge their unexamined commitments, and identity-diffused adults may present with depression, purposelessness, or personality pathology. Erikson noted that societies need to provide a psychosocial moratorium -- a sanctioned period of exploration (e.g., college years) -- to facilitate healthy identity development.

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Know Thomas and Chess's three temperament types (easy, difficult, slow-to-warm-up) and their approximate prevalence rates (40%, 10%, 15%)
  • Goodness of fit is the match between temperament and environmental demands -- outcomes depend on the interaction, not temperament alone
  • Kagan's behavioral inhibition: biologically based (amygdala reactivity), predicts social anxiety; parenting can moderate the trajectory
  • Know all eight of Erikson's psychosocial stages, their age ranges, crises, and virtues
  • Trust vs. Mistrust (infancy) parallels attachment theory; resolved through consistent caregiving
  • Identity vs. Role Confusion is the central crisis of adolescence
  • Generativity vs. Stagnation (middle adulthood) involves contributing to the next generation
  • Know Marcia's four identity statuses and the two dimensions (exploration/crisis and commitment)
  • Identity Achievement (explored + committed) has the best outcomes; Identity Diffusion (no exploration + no commitment) has the worst outcomes when persistent
  • Foreclosure involves commitment without exploration -- often based on adopting parental values unquestioningly
  • Moratorium (currently exploring, not yet committed) is necessary for achieving identity and is normative in late adolescence
  • Temperament is moderately stable across development but is NOT immutable -- environment shapes its expression
