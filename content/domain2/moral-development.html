Overview of Moral Development

Moral development encompasses the growth of an individual's ability to distinguish right from wrong, to develop a personal moral code, and to behave in accordance with ethical principles. This chapter covers the major theoretical frameworks: Piaget's foundational work on moral reasoning, Kohlberg's influential stage theory, Gilligan's feminist critique, and research on prosocial behavior and empathy development.

For the EPPP, moral development is a consistently tested topic. Candidates must know Kohlberg's six stages in detail, understand the criticisms leveled against his theory, and be able to apply these concepts to clinical and forensic contexts.

Piaget's Theory of Moral Development

Jean Piaget (1932) studied moral development by observing children playing games (especially marbles) and presenting them with moral dilemmas. He identified two broad stages of moral reasoning:

Piaget's Two Stages

Heteronomous morality (Moral Realism) -- approximately ages 4-7: Rules are viewed as absolute, unchangeable, and handed down by authority. Moral judgments are based on objective consequences rather than intentions. A child who breaks 15 cups accidentally is judged as "naughtier" than a child who breaks 1 cup while trying to steal cookies. This stage is characterized by immanent justice -- the belief that punishment automatically follows wrongdoing.

Autonomous morality (Moral Relativism) -- approximately age 10 and onward: Rules are understood as social agreements that can be changed by mutual consent. Moral judgments are based on intentions and motives rather than outcomes alone. The concept of reciprocity and fairness becomes central. Punishment should "fit the crime" rather than be arbitrary.

Piaget's key contribution was demonstrating that moral reasoning develops in a predictable sequence tied to cognitive development. The shift from heteronomous to autonomous morality parallels the transition from preoperational to concrete operational thinking. Peer interaction (particularly disagreement and negotiation among equals) was seen as the primary driver of moral development, more so than adult instruction.

Kohlberg's Theory of Moral Development

Lawrence Kohlberg (1927-1987) extended Piaget's work by proposing a more detailed six-stage model of moral reasoning. Kohlberg assessed moral development by presenting participants with hypothetical moral dilemmas and analyzing the reasoning (not the content) behind their judgments. His most famous dilemma involves Heinz, whose wife is dying and who must decide whether to steal an unaffordable drug to save her life.

Level 1: Preconventional Morality (Most children under 9; some adolescents and adults)

Moral reasoning is based on direct consequences to the self. Rules are external and imposed by others.

StageOrientationDescriptionHeinz Dilemma Example
Stage 1Obedience and PunishmentActions are judged right or wrong based on whether they lead to punishment. Moral behavior = avoiding punishment. Might makes right."He shouldn't steal because he'll go to jail." OR "He should steal because he'll be in trouble if his wife dies."
Stage 2Individualism and Exchange (Instrumental Purpose)Right action satisfies one's own needs and occasionally others' needs. Reciprocity is understood as "you scratch my back, I'll scratch yours" -- a concrete, self-interested exchange."He should steal it because his wife might return the favor someday." OR "He shouldn't steal because he might get caught and the punishment isn't worth it."

Level 2: Conventional Morality (Most adolescents and adults)

Moral reasoning is based on conforming to social expectations, maintaining relationships, and upholding the social order.

StageOrientationDescriptionHeinz Dilemma Example
Stage 3Good Interpersonal Relationships ("Good Boy/Nice Girl")Being moral means living up to the expectations of people close to you. Emphasis on being "good," gaining approval, and maintaining relationships. Intentions begin to matter."He should steal it because that's what a good husband would do." OR "He shouldn't steal because people will think he's a criminal."
Stage 4Maintaining Social Order (Law and Order)Moral behavior means obeying laws, respecting authority, and fulfilling one's duties to maintain social order. Laws exist for the good of society."He shouldn't steal because if everyone stole, society would break down." OR "He should steal because he has a duty to protect his wife, but he should accept the legal consequences."

Level 3: Postconventional (Principled) Morality (A minority of adults; rare before age 20)

Moral reasoning is based on self-chosen ethical principles that may transcend specific laws or social conventions.

StageOrientationDescriptionHeinz Dilemma Example
Stage 5Social Contract and Individual RightsLaws are social contracts that should serve the greatest good. When laws conflict with individual rights, the rights take precedence. Laws can and should be changed through democratic means."The law is important, but the right to life supersedes the pharmacist's right to profit. The law should be changed to prevent such situations."
Stage 6Universal Ethical PrinciplesMoral reasoning is guided by self-chosen, abstract ethical principles (justice, equality, human dignity) that apply universally. One acts according to these principles even at personal cost."He should steal the drug because human life has inherent value that transcends any law. Failing to act would violate the principle of justice."

Clinical Example: Assessing Moral Reasoning Stage

A forensic psychologist evaluates a 16-year-old who committed shoplifting. When asked why stealing is wrong, the teen responds: "Because you get in trouble and your parents get mad at you." This response reflects Stage 1 reasoning (avoidance of punishment). Another teen might say: "Because stores would go out of business and that would hurt everyone" -- reflecting Stage 4 reasoning (maintaining social order). The distinction matters clinically: interventions for the Stage 1 reasoner might focus on developing perspective-taking and empathy, while the Stage 4 reasoner may benefit from exploring the social and personal factors that led to behavior incongruent with their moral understanding.

Clinical Implications

Kohlberg's theory has important applications in forensic psychology and moral education. Research shows that delinquents and antisocial individuals tend to show lower levels of moral reasoning (predominantly Stages 1-2), though the relationship between moral reasoning and moral behavior is modest. Kohlberg advocated "Just Community" programs in schools and prisons that use group discussion of moral dilemmas to stimulate moral development -- a person can be advanced by approximately one stage through exposure to reasoning one stage above their current level (the "+1 convention").

Criticisms of Kohlberg's Theory

Gilligan's Ethics of Care

Carol Gilligan (1982), in her influential book In a Different Voice, argued that Kohlberg's theory was biased toward a justice orientation (emphasizing individual rights, abstract principles, and impartiality) that reflects typically male moral reasoning. Gilligan proposed that women more often use a care orientation (emphasizing relationships, compassion, responsibility to others, and contextual sensitivity), which Kohlberg's scoring system systematically undervalued.

Gilligan's Three Levels of Care-Based Morality

Level 1 -- Orientation to Self-Survival: Focus on self-interest and individual survival.

Transition: Recognition that this orientation is selfish.

Level 2 -- Goodness as Self-Sacrifice: Focus on caring for others, often at the expense of self. Being "good" means being responsible for others.

Transition: Recognition that neglecting one's own needs is also problematic.

Level 3 -- Morality of Nonviolence: Balance between care for self and care for others. The injunction against hurting anyone (including oneself) becomes the guiding principle.

Subsequent research (Walker, 1984; Jaffee & Hyde, 2000) has generally found only small or no gender differences in moral reasoning on Kohlberg's scale, and both men and women use both justice and care orientations depending on the context of the dilemma. Nevertheless, Gilligan's contribution was valuable in highlighting that moral reasoning involves more than justice considerations alone.

Other Criticisms

  • Cultural bias: Kohlberg's model reflects Western, individualistic values. Postconventional reasoning as he defined it may not represent the highest moral development in collectivist cultures where community obligation is prioritized over individual rights (Snarey, 1985)
  • Moral reasoning vs. moral behavior: Kohlberg assessed reasoning, not action. The correlation between moral reasoning stage and actual moral behavior is moderate at best
  • Stage 6 rarity: Kohlberg eventually acknowledged that Stage 6 was more of a theoretical ideal than an empirically observed stage; virtually no research participants consistently scored at Stage 6
  • Overemphasis on cognition: The theory neglects the roles of emotion, intuition, and automatic moral judgments. Haidt's (2001) social intuitionist model argues that moral judgments are typically made through rapid intuitive processes, with reasoning serving primarily as post-hoc justification

Prosocial Behavior and Empathy

Prosocial behavior refers to voluntary actions intended to benefit others, including sharing, helping, comforting, and cooperating. The development of prosocial behavior is closely tied to the development of empathy and perspective-taking.

Developmental Progression of Empathy

Martin Hoffman (2000) described a developmental sequence of empathic responding:

  1. Global empathy (newborn): Reactive crying in response to another infant's distress -- a reflexive, undifferentiated response
  2. Egocentric empathy (~1 year): Child recognizes another's distress but offers comfort based on what would comfort the self (e.g., bringing own teddy bear to a crying adult)
  3. Empathy for another's feelings (~2-3 years): Child begins to understand that others have different feelings and can offer appropriate comfort
  4. Empathy for another's life condition (late childhood/adolescence): Can feel empathy for others' general life situations, including people not immediately present (e.g., empathy for the poor, for victims of injustice)

Clinical Example: Empathy Assessment

A school psychologist observes a 5-year-old during play. When a classmate falls and begins crying, the child runs over, pats the classmate on the back, and says "It's okay, do you want me to get the teacher?" This response demonstrates empathy for another's feelings (Level 3 in Hoffman's model) -- the child recognizes the other child's distress and offers comfort appropriate to the other child's needs rather than their own. In contrast, a child at the egocentric empathy stage might offer their own favorite toy, not recognizing that the hurt child needs different forms of comfort.

Factors Influencing Prosocial Development

  • Parenting: Inductive discipline (explaining why behavior is wrong, how it affects others) promotes prosocial behavior more than power assertion or love withdrawal (Hoffman, 1970)
  • Modeling: Children who observe prosocial behavior in parents and other adults are more likely to behave prosocially
  • Perspective-taking ability: Advances in cognitive development that enable understanding others' viewpoints facilitate prosocial behavior
  • Temperament: Children with higher dispositional empathy and lower aggression show more prosocial behavior

EPPP Exam Focus Points

  • Know all six of Kohlberg's stages, their levels (preconventional, conventional, postconventional), and what each stage's reasoning looks like
  • Kohlberg assessed moral reasoning (not moral content or moral behavior) -- the same answer can reflect different stages depending on the reasoning
  • Stage 3 is the most common stage among adolescents; Stage 4 is the most common among adults; postconventional stages are rare
  • Gilligan criticized Kohlberg for gender bias: justice orientation (Kohlberg) vs. care orientation (Gilligan). However, research shows minimal gender differences in Kohlberg's scoring
  • Piaget: heteronomous morality (rules fixed, consequences matter) vs. autonomous morality (rules negotiable, intentions matter)
  • Piaget's concept of immanent justice: young children believe punishment automatically follows wrongdoing
  • Cultural criticism: Kohlberg's stages may reflect Western individualistic values rather than universal moral development
  • Haidt's social intuitionist model challenges the primacy of reasoning in moral judgment
  • Hoffman's empathy stages: global empathy, egocentric empathy, empathy for another's feelings, empathy for another's life condition
  • Inductive discipline (reasoning with children about effects of behavior) promotes moral development more than power assertion
  • The "+1 convention": exposure to reasoning one stage above one's current level promotes moral growth
